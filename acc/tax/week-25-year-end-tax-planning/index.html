    Week 25: Year-End Tax Planning — The Conversations That Happen Before December 31¶

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    • Conduct a proactive year-end planning call with every client — by November 30
    • Calculate and communicate exactly how much income a client should target before year-end
    • Guide RRSP, TFSA, FHSA, and RESP contribution decisions before December 31
    • Execute tax-loss harvesting conversations with clients who have investment portfolios
    • Advise business owners (self-employed and incorporated) on year-end income strategies
    • Time charitable donation claims to maximize the credit
    • Identify the Ontario-specific year-end planning items most preparers miss
    • Position yourself as a year-round advisor — not just a tax season filer
    • Build the year-end planning call into your practice as a systematic, billable service

    The practice-defining insight of this week

    The average tax preparer sees clients in February and April. The exceptional tax preparer calls every client in October and November. Those two phone calls — 15 minutes each — identify planning opportunities that can save clients $2,000–$15,000 before the year ends. Once you do this for a client and they experience the savings, they will never go to anyone else. The year-end planning call is not an extra service. It is the service that justifies every fee you charge and every referral you generate. This week teaches you how to conduct it with authority.

    25.1 Why Year-End Planning Matters More Than Tax Filing¶

    By the time a client sits in your office in February handing you their T4, the tax year is closed. You can file accurately. You can optimize existing claims. But you cannot change what happened. The income was earned. The RRSP contribution either happened or it didn't. The capital loss either was or wasn't harvested.

    Year-end planning is the only time you have agency over the outcome.

    FEBRUARY (Tax Filing Season)
→ You record what happened
→ You optimize within what exists
→ You cannot change income, contributions, or timing
→ Value: accuracy and capture of existing opportunities

NOVEMBER (Year-End Planning)
→ You influence what will happen
→ You create opportunities that didn't exist before
→ You recommend actions that directly change the tax bill
→ Value: 10× higher per hour than tax season filing

    The most successful tax preparers in Canada are not people who file returns faster than anyone else. They are people who speak to clients before December 31 and help them make decisions that change outcomes. Every other preparer the client could hire does what you do in February. Almost none of them do what you do in November.

    25.2 The Year-End Planning Call — Structure and Scripts¶

    Who Gets the Call¶

    Priority 1 — Call everyone: - Clients with self-employment income (year-end income planning critical) - Clients with investment accounts (tax-loss harvesting, dividend timing) - Incorporated clients (salary vs. dividend decisions, bonus declaration) - Senior clients (OAS clawback management, RRSP conversion deadline) - Clients who had a major life change (marriage, baby, new job, divorce, home purchase)

    Priority 2 — Email with action items: - Simple T4 employment clients (RRSP timing reminder, donation stacking) - Students (tuition timing, RRSP room check)

    The 15-Minute Year-End Call — The Script¶

    Opening (2 minutes): "Hi [Client name] — I'm calling with my annual year-end check-in. I do this every year with all my clients in November because there are often things we can do before December 31 that can meaningfully reduce your tax bill. Do you have about 15 minutes?"

    [If yes, proceed. If not, schedule a specific callback time.]

    Current year income check (3 minutes):

    For employees: "Based on your current pay, where will your total employment income land for 2025? Have there been any changes — a raise, a bonus, a new income source like freelance work or rental income?"

    For self-employed: "How has the year gone business-wise? Do you have a rough sense of your net income so far? Any large invoices still outstanding?"

    For investors: "Have you made any dispositions in your investment account this year — sold any stocks, funds, or property?"

    The four planning questions (8 minutes):

    1. RRSP/TFSA/FHSA: "Do you know your RRSP room for this year? Have you made any contributions? There's still time before December 31 — or March 1 for RRSP. Based on your income, the tax savings from maximizing your RRSP are [amount]."

    2. Investment portfolio: "If you have a non-registered account — have you looked at whether any positions are sitting at a loss? There may be an opportunity to harvest those losses before December 31 to offset gains you've had this year."

    3. Major decisions before December 31: "Anything happening before year-end I should know about? A home sale, a business sale, a large bonus, an inheritance?"

    4. Ontario-specific: "Have you made any home accessibility improvements, or had significant medical expenses this year? Are you set up for the Ontario Trillium Benefit if you're renting?"

    Closing (2 minutes): "Based on what you've told me, here's what I'd recommend before December 31: [1–3 specific actions]. I'll send you a summary by email. Is there anything else on your mind about your tax situation this year?"

    The Follow-Up Email¶

    Within 24 hours of the call, send a brief email:

    Subject: Your 2025 Year-End Tax Planning Summary

[Client name],

Great talking with you earlier. Here's a summary of what we discussed:

RECOMMENDED ACTIONS BEFORE DECEMBER 31, 2025:
1. [Specific action — RRSP contribution of $X]
2. [Specific action — Harvest loss in [security], estimated tax saving $X]
3. [Specific action — Donate securities directly instead of cash]

BEFORE MARCH 1, 2026:
1. RRSP contribution of up to $X (can still apply to 2025)

ESTIMATED TAX SAVINGS FROM THESE ACTIONS: approximately $X

Let me know if you have questions. I'll be in touch in January to 
confirm everything before we file.

[Your name]

    This email does three things: 1. Confirms the advice (CYA protection) 2. Reminds the client to actually act 3. Demonstrates the quantified value of your service

    25.3 RRSP and TFSA — The Year-End Contribution Decisions¶

    The December 31 vs. March 1 Decision¶

    TFSA contributions: Deadline is December 31. New room opens January 1 of the following year. If a client has unused TFSA room and available cash — contribute before December 31.

    RRSP contributions: Technically due March 1 of the following year — but there are strategic reasons to make them before December 31:

    1. The money grows inside the plan from December rather than March — 2 extra months of tax-sheltered compounding
    2. Clients forget. Telling a client "contribute by March 1" means some will forget or procrastinate until it's too late. Encouraging December action has a much higher follow-through rate
    3. Deduction timing — the contribution earns the deduction from the year specified (your choice) but the plan starts earning tax-free from day of contribution. Contribute in December, decide on deduction timing in February

    The RRSP Bracket Strategy¶

    The most powerful RRSP decision isn't just "contribute as much as possible." It's contributing exactly enough to reach the next bracket boundary.

    Case — Sandra's Bracket Strategy

    Sandra earns $72,000 in 2025. She wants to know how much to contribute.

    Current position: $72,000 net income. Marginal combined rate: 29.65% (in the $57,376–$102,894 combined bracket).

    Bracket boundary targets: - Drop to $57,375 (next federal bracket): requires $72,000 − $57,375 = $14,625 contribution. Every dollar of this $14,625 saves 29.65 cents - Drop below $57,375 (into 20.05% combined bracket): Any dollar above $14,625 now saves only 20.05 cents — still valuable, but less so

    Additionally: - If Sandra drops to $44,325 or below: she enters the full Age Amount range (she's 64 this year — turning 65 next year, but the Age Amount doesn't apply yet) - If she drops to $36,502 or below: CCB phase-out territory (no children, doesn't apply)

    Your recommendation: "Sandra, your first priority is contributing $14,625 to your RRSP. Every dollar of that saves you 29.65 cents — that's $4,336 in immediate combined tax savings. After that, contributions still save you 20 cents on the dollar. You have $24,400 in RRSP room available. If you can contribute more than $14,625, do it — but that first $14,625 is the highest-value investment you can make before year-end."

    TFSA Optimization¶

    The TFSA has a 2025 contribution limit of $7,000 plus all unused room since 2009.

    Clients who should prioritize TFSA contributions before December 31: - Low-income clients (below $50,000 net income) where RRSP deduction is worth less - Clients who have maximized RRSP or have no RRSP room - Clients who need accessible savings (TFSA withdrawals are tax-free and add room back January 1 the following year) - Seniors at or near OAS clawback thresholds (TFSA withdrawals don't affect net income)

    FHSA — Open Now if Not Already Open¶

    For any client under 71 who might buy a first home and hasn't opened an FHSA: open before December 31. The room accumulates from the year the account is opened. A client who opens in December 2025 and doesn't contribute generates $8,000 in carryforward room to contribute in 2026 ($16,000 total room available).

    Cost of opening: $0. Cost of not opening: permanent loss of $8,000 in tax-sheltered room.

    Your year-end reminder for first-time home-buyer clients: "If you might buy a home in the next 10 years and you haven't opened an FHSA yet — open one before December 31. It costs nothing and preserves your contribution room."

    25.4 Tax-Loss Harvesting — The Non-Registered Portfolio Conversation¶

    Tax-loss harvesting is the practice of selling investments at a loss before year-end to generate capital losses that can offset capital gains realized during the year.

    The Basic Framework¶

    Capital gains realized this year:         $18,000
Capital losses available to harvest:      $12,000
                                          ────────
Net capital gain after harvesting:         $6,000
Taxable capital gain (×50%):               $3,000
Tax saved by harvesting:    $12,000 × 50% × 46.16% = $2,769

    The Rules to Remember¶

    1. Capital losses can only offset capital gains — not employment or other income
    2. Superficial loss rule: If you sell an investment at a loss and repurchase the same or identical investment within 30 days before or after the sale, the loss is denied. The clock resets on the 31st day
    3. The December 31 deadline is firm — settlement takes 2 days for equities. For a loss to count in 2025, the trade must settle by December 31 — meaning the stock must be sold by approximately December 29 (allowing for settlement)
    4. Losses carry back 3 years and forward indefinitely

    Case — Robert's Portfolio Review Call

    Robert has a non-registered investment account. During your November call:

    "Robert, you mentioned earlier this year you sold some RBC shares at a gain of about $22,000. Have you looked at whether anything in your portfolio is currently underwater — sitting at a loss? If we can identify $22,000 or more in unrealized losses and sell them before December 31, those losses offset your gains and you pay zero capital gains tax this year."

    Robert logs into his brokerage. He has: - Shopify shares: bought at $68, now at $41. 200 shares. Unrealized loss: ($68 − $41) × 200 = $5,400 - US tech ETF: bought at $120, now at $89. 100 units. Unrealized loss: ($120 − $89) × 100 = $3,100 - Canadian energy stock: bought at $24, now at $17. 500 shares. Unrealized loss: ($24 − $17) × 500 = $3,500 - Total harvestable losses: $12,000

    "Robert, if you sell these three positions by December 29, you generate $12,000 in capital losses. Applied against your $22,000 in gains, you pay tax on only $10,000 in net capital gains rather than $22,000. At your marginal rate, that saves you approximately $2,307 in combined tax.

    Now — the superficial loss rule. If you want to buy back Shopify, you need to wait 31 days. You can buy a similar tech company or tech ETF immediately — just not the same security. Would you like me to show you the after-tax impact on your return?"

    Robert sells all three positions by December 27. He buys a different tech ETF immediately (not Shopify — to avoid the superficial loss). He waits 31 days and then rebuys Shopify.

    Tax saved: $2,307 from a 15-minute November phone call.

    The Important Client Conversation About Investment Decisions¶

    You are a tax preparer, not a licensed investment advisor (unless separately registered). Your role:

    You can: Explain the tax consequences of selling specific securities, the superficial loss rule, and how harvested losses affect the tax return.

    You cannot: Recommend which securities to buy, hold, or sell based on their investment merit. If a client asks "should I sell Shopify?" from an investment perspective — direct them to their financial advisor.

    Your framing: "From a tax perspective, selling these positions before December 31 would generate [X] in losses. Whether selling makes sense from an investment perspective — your financial advisor is the right person to ask. I'm just showing you the tax math."

    25.5 Self-Employed Year-End Income Strategies¶

    For self-employed clients, December is a month of significant financial decisions that directly affect next year's tax bill.

    The Income Deferral Strategy¶

    A self-employed client can defer income by delaying invoicing until after December 31. Income is generally recognized on a cash basis for most sole proprietors when received.

    When this makes sense: - The client is in a higher bracket this year than expected (unexpected large contract) - Next year's income is expected to be significantly lower - The client has a specific reason to reduce this year's net income (OAS clawback threshold, benefit phase-out, spousal credit optimization)

    Case — The Consultant Who Waited Three Weeks

    Ahmed is a freelance consultant. By November 30, his net business income is $89,000 — just above the OAS clawback threshold ($90,997). He has a $14,000 invoice outstanding to a client.

    Scenario A — Invoice before December 31: Net income: $89,000 + $14,000 = $103,000 OAS: $8,300. Clawback: ($103,000 − $90,997) × 15% = $1,801. He keeps $6,499 of his OAS.

    Scenario B — Invoice and receive payment in January 2026: 2025 net income: $89,000 — below the clawback threshold 2025 OAS: full $8,300 — no clawback Clawback saved: $1,801

    Additionally, the tax on $14,000 at 29.65% combined: $4,151 — deferred one year.

    By waiting three weeks to invoice, Ahmed saves $1,801 in OAS and defers $4,151 in tax. The invoice is paid in January — he doesn't lose the money, just delays receiving it slightly.

    Important: Ahmed must actually delay invoicing, not just delay depositing the cheque. CRA looks at when income was invoiced (when legally earned) for accrual-basis businesses. For most sole proprietors who report on cash basis, the payment receipt date matters. Verify the client's accounting method before recommending this.

    The Expense Acceleration Strategy¶

    The mirror image of income deferral: accelerating deductible expenses into the current year.

    What can be purchased before December 31 to create 2025 deductions:

    Expense Timing Opportunity
    Software subscriptions (annual) Pay in December vs. January — 12 months earlier deduction
    Professional development Book and pay for courses before December 31
    Equipment (under $500, Class 12 at 100%) Buy small tools, office supplies before year-end
    Marketing and advertising Prepay advertising campaigns for Q1
    Professional fees Pay your accountant's annual fee in December
    Vehicle expenses Fill the tank December 31 — small but real

    The limitation: Prepaid expenses must relate to the period being paid for. You can't prepay rent 5 years in advance and deduct it this year. But prepaying an annual software subscription covers the next 12 months — deductible in the year paid.

    The RRSP Contribution Maximization for Self-Employed¶

    Self-employed clients often underestimate their RRSP room because their earned income fluctuates year to year. The year-end call is the moment to calculate this precisely.

    "Based on your estimated 2025 net business income of $76,000, your 2026 RRSP room will be $76,000 × 18% = $13,680. But more importantly — have you maxed out your 2025 RRSP room? Your 2024 NOA shows $19,400 in available room. You've contributed $5,000 this year. That means you still have $14,400 in room you can use by March 1, 2026. At your combined 29.65% rate, that's $4,267 in tax savings."

    25.6 Incorporated Client Year-End — Salary, Bonus, and Dividend Decisions¶

    For incorporated business owners, the most consequential year-end decision is how to extract income from the corporation before or after December 31.

    The Corporate Fiscal Year vs. Personal Calendar Year¶

    The corporation's fiscal year (often not December 31 for some corporations — though most small businesses use December 31) matters for when expenses can be deducted corporately. For the personal T1, only income received (salary) or declared (dividends) affects the client's personal return.

    The Bonus Timing Decision¶

    A management bonus declared by the corporation before December 31 is deductible by the corporation in the current fiscal year — even if it's paid up to 180 days after the fiscal year-end.

    This creates a specific planning opportunity:

    The corporation earns $200,000 this year. The owner wants to take some of it out. If the corporation declares a $40,000 bonus by December 31 (even paid in March), the corporation deducts it from 2025 corporate income — saving approximately $4,880 in corporate tax (12.2% × $40,000). The owner includes it in 2025 personal income when they receive the T4.

    Your year-end call to an incorporated client:

    "[Client], the corporation has earned approximately $[X] this year. Before December 31, we should decide how much you want to declare as a bonus — it reduces the corporation's tax bill this year even if it's paid to you in the new year. Have you discussed with your accountant what the optimal amount is? I can work with whatever they recommend on the personal return side."

    Coordinate with the T2 preparer for bonus declarations

    This decision has both corporate and personal tax implications. Your role on the T1 is to receive the T4 or T5 and file accordingly. The bonus amount should be determined by the client and their T2 preparer. You can explain the concept and the personal tax impact — but the corporate mechanics are the CPA's domain.

    The Year-End Dividend Declaration¶

    Dividends must be formally declared by the corporation's board of directors before year-end to be included in the current calendar year on the personal T1.

    A dividend declared on December 31, 2025 is included on the 2025 T1. A dividend declared on January 2, 2026 is on the 2026 T1.

    The year-end call question: "Has your accountant been in touch about whether you want to declare any dividends before December 31? This affects both this year's and next year's personal tax bills. I can model both scenarios for you — just need your estimated 2025 income and your plans for 2026."

    25.7 Charitable Donation Timing — Maximizing the Credit¶

    The December 31 Deadline¶

    Charitable donations must be made by December 31 to qualify for the current year's tax credit. A donation made on January 2 doesn't count for the prior year regardless of what the receipt says.

    Your year-end reminder: "If you're planning to make any charitable donations this year — for your church, hospital foundation, food bank, or any registered charity — make sure the donation is made and processed before December 31. A credit card donation processed December 31 counts for 2025."

    The Stacking Reminder¶

    From Week 13: biennial donation stacking generates more credit by keeping donations in the 29%+ rate zone. For clients who give small amounts ($200–$600/year):

    "If you gave $500 last year and $500 this year — and you haven't claimed either yet — you could combine both on this year's return for $1,000 total. The first $200 is credited at 15%; the remaining $800 at 29%. That's more credit than two separate $500 claims."

    The Gift of Securities — Year-End Urgency¶

    From Week 13: donating appreciated securities directly to charity eliminates the capital gain. This is time-sensitive — securities must be transferred to the charity's account before December 31.

    The problem: Transferring securities to a charity takes time — the broker must process the transfer and the charity's brokerage account must receive them. Allow at least 10–15 business days for this to complete. This means clients who want to donate securities in 2025 should initiate the transfer by approximately December 12 to ensure completion by December 31.

    Case — Margaret's RBC Shares Donation

    Margaret holds RBC shares bought in 2008 for $8,000, now worth $28,000 — an accrued gain of $20,000. She wants to donate $28,000 to her hospital foundation.

    If she sells and donates cash: Capital gain: $20,000. Tax at 46.16% combined × 50%: $4,616. Donation receipt: $28,000 − $4,616 (net proceeds): effectively donating $23,384 after tax.

    If she donates shares directly: Capital gain: $0 (exempt on direct donation of publicly traded securities). Donation receipt: $28,000 (full FMV). Donation credit: $200 × 15% + $27,800 × 29% = $8,092 federal alone.

    Difference: $4,616 in capital gains tax eliminated + larger donation receipt.

    Your November call to Margaret: "Margaret, I noticed you own those RBC shares with a significant gain. Have you thought about how you want to handle the donation this year? If you donate the shares directly — without selling — you eliminate the capital gains tax entirely and get a receipt for the full value. But the transfer needs to be initiated at least two weeks before year-end. If this is something you want to do, we should act soon."

    Margaret initiates the transfer on December 10. It settles December 22. The 2025 donation receipt is for $28,000.

    25.8 Ontario-Specific Year-End Planning Items¶

    Ontario Trillium Benefit — ON-BEN Planning¶

    Clients who change living situations in December: If a client is moving from rented to owned accommodation (or vice versa) on December 1 or later — their December 31 situation determines the OTB filing. Advise them on what documentation they'll need for ON-BEN based on their year-end status.

    Clients who need to document landlord information: If a renting client can't remember their landlord's name and address — find it before year-end while the lease agreement is accessible. Missing landlord information on ON-BEN requires an amendment.

    Senior Clients — OAS Clawback Pre-Planning¶

    The year-end call for seniors earning near the $90,997 OAS clawback threshold is one of the highest-value conversations in tax practice.

    The tool: RRSP contributions, pension splitting, and income deferral can all reduce net income below $90,997 — preserving the full OAS of approximately $8,300/year.

    Case — The November Call That Saved $1,245 in OAS

    George, 73, calls you for his year-end check-in. He estimates his 2025 income:

    • RRIF minimum: $52,000
    • CPP: $14,000
    • OAS: $8,300
    • Small pension: $18,000
    • Estimated total: $92,300

    OAS clawback: ($92,300 − $90,997) × 15% = $1,303 × 15% = $195 clawed back

    "George, your income is running about $1,303 above the OAS clawback threshold. If we can reduce your net income by $1,304 before year-end, you preserve your full OAS. Are there any legitimate deductions we haven't maximized — carrying charges, home office if applicable? Also, are you and your wife still filing together? Pension income splitting with her could reduce your net income."

    George and his wife do a $2,000 pension income split they hadn't optimized. George's net income drops to $90,300 — below the threshold. OAS clawback: $0.

    Annual saving from this call: $195 × (likely 10+ remaining years) = $1,950+ in cumulative OAS preservation.

    Plus: the pension split also restores part of George's Age Amount — worth an additional $248 in federal credit annually.

    The Ontario LIFT Credit — Income Level Check¶

    For clients with employment income who are near the $30,000 LIFT Credit threshold:

    If they're slightly above $30,000: An RRSP contribution that brings net income below $30,000 doesn't help LIFT (LIFT is based on employment income, not net income). But it reduces overall tax.

    If they're just below $30,000: Advise against taking on additional employment income in December that would push them above the threshold and create Ontario tax where there was none.

    Home Accessibility Tax Credit — December 31 Deadline¶

    Eligible renovations for seniors or DTC recipients must be paid for by December 31. If a client has outstanding renovation invoices — pay them before year-end.

    Up to $20,000 in eligible expenses generates a $3,000 federal credit (15% × $20,000). If the client has a large renovation nearly complete, ensure the final invoice is paid in December.

    25.9 The Year-End Planning Calendar — What to Do When¶

    Timing Action
    October 1–15 Review prior year returns for all clients. Identify who needs a call. Prepare draft "planning notes" for each
    October 15–November 15 Conduct year-end planning calls — priority clients first (self-employed, investors, seniors, incorporated)
    November 15–November 30 Send year-end planning emails to all remaining clients. Follow up on any open items from earlier calls
    December 1–10 Donation securities transfer deadline — any client donating appreciated securities must initiate transfer now
    December 1–15 RRSP contributions for highest-income clients who want compounding to start
    December 15 Last chance to contact clients about year-end actions with meaningful lead time
    December 29 Last trading day for tax-loss harvesting — sales must settle by December 31. Most equities settle T+2
    December 31 All charitable donations, TFSA contributions, FHSA contributions, and year-end income events must occur by end of day
    March 1 RRSP contribution deadline for 2025 tax year

    25.10 The Year-End Planning Service — How to Price and Deliver It¶

    Is This Billable?¶

    Yes — if you position it correctly.

    Option A — Included in annual fee: "My fee for your return includes a year-end planning call each November. This is part of the service." This approach justifies a higher annual fee ($250+ for simple returns rather than $150) and creates loyalty. Clients who receive a planning call don't shop around.

    Option B — Separate planning fee: "Tax return preparation: $175. Year-end planning review (October/November): $95." Some clients who don't value the call won't pay for it — this filters them out. Those who pay for it are your most loyal and referral-active clients.

    Option C — Strategic advisory retainer: For incorporated clients and high-net-worth individuals: "My advisory retainer is $1,200/year. This includes your T1 return, a year-end planning call, RRSP optimization advice, and unlimited questions throughout the year." At 50 clients × $1,200 = $60,000/year before additional returns. This is how solo practices become genuinely profitable.

    The Value Conversation¶

    Always quantify the value of the planning call before or during it:

    "Last November, after our call, you made the RRSP contribution and harvested those two investment losses. Combined, that saved you approximately $4,800 in tax. My fee for the return was $280. The planning call essentially paid for itself 17 times over. That's what I try to deliver every year."

    Clients who hear this number don't question your fee. They wonder how they operated without you.

    25.11 Case Studies — Year-End Planning Conversations That Changed Outcomes¶

    Case A — The Self-Employed Consultant's November Call¶

    Client: Patricia, 48, self-employed marketing consultant. November 15 call.

    Income snapshot: Net business income estimated at $112,000 for 2025. Prior year RRSP room: $26,400. She's contributed $0 this year.

    Your analysis during the call:

    At $112,000, Patricia is in the 37.16% combined bracket (between $102,894 and $150,000).

    "Patricia, let's look at your RRSP. You have $26,400 in room. Every dollar you contribute saves you 37 cents right now — that's the combined federal and Ontario rate at your income level. If you contribute $26,400 to your RRSP, you save $9,823 in combined tax.

    Beyond that — are there any large expenses you could accelerate into this year? Software renewals, professional development, any equipment you've been planning to buy?"

    Patricia mentions she's been planning to buy a new MacBook ($2,800) and upgrade her video conferencing setup ($1,400) for 2026.

    "If you buy both before December 31 instead of January, those CCA deductions (the MacBook at Class 50/55%, the AV equipment at Class 8/20%) are available against 2025 income instead of 2026. The first-year CCA on the MacBook alone is $2,800 × 55% × 50% half-year = $770. That saves another $286 in tax at your rate."

    Patricia also has a Shopify ETF at a $4,200 unrealized loss. She had no capital gains this year — no immediate harvesting benefit. But she notes it for future reference.

    Actions recommended before December 31: 1. Buy MacBook and AV equipment: additional tax saving $286 2. RRSP contribution of $26,400 by March 1 (or December if she has the cash): $9,823 saving

    Total first-year tax saving from this 15-minute call: $10,109

    Patricia acts on both recommendations. Her 2025 tax bill is $10,109 lower than it would have been. Your fee was $325.

    She refers two colleagues to you in January.

    Case B — The Investment Couple's December Call¶

    Client: Victor and Helen, ages 55 and 52. Victor earns $98,000 employment. Helen earns $42,000 part-time employment. They have a combined non-registered investment portfolio of $380,000.

    November 18 call:

    "Victor, a few things to think about before year-end. First — I noticed you sold some mutual funds in June at a gain of $14,000. Did you realize any other gains or losses in your portfolio this year?"

    Victor checks — he has a US tech ETF sitting at a $9,200 loss and a Canadian energy stock at a $3,800 loss. Total harvestable: $13,000.

    "If you sell those two positions before December 29, you generate $13,000 in capital losses against your $14,000 in gains. You'd pay tax on only $1,000 in net capital gain instead of $14,000. At your combined rate, that saves approximately $2,891.

    The superficial loss rule means you can't buy back the exact same funds within 30 days. But you can buy similar alternatives immediately — for example, if you sell the US tech ETF, you could buy a different US broad market ETF today and maintain your exposure."

    Victor confirms he wants to proceed. He calls his broker.

    "Second item — Helen's TFSA. Do you know if she's maximized her TFSA room? She had unused room of approximately $11,500 from prior years. With the new $7,000 in 2025 room, she has up to $18,500 available. If she has the cash, TFSA contributions before December 31 eliminate all future tax on that growth."

    Helen contributes $11,500 to her TFSA before year-end.

    "Third — charitable donations. You mentioned St. Joseph's Health Care. If you have any appreciated securities you were going to sell anyway, donating shares directly saves the capital gain. Does your portfolio have any long-held positions with large gains?"

    Victor has TD Bank shares bought at $42, now at $94 — 200 shares. Gain: $10,400. He was planning to eventually sell some.

    "If you donate 100 of those shares directly to St. Joseph's, you receive a donation receipt for $9,400 (current value), eliminate a $5,200 capital gain, and generate a donation credit. At your rate, the combined benefit is approximately $3,940."

    Victor initiates the share transfer on December 8.

    Total identified savings from this November call: - Tax-loss harvesting: $2,891 - Helen's TFSA growth (no current-year tax saving but future income tax elimination): long-term value - Security donation: $3,940 combined saving

    Total: $6,831 from a 20-minute call.

    Case C — The Retired Couple at the OAS Threshold¶

    Client: Margaret (70) and George (72). November 20 call.

    Estimated 2025 income: Margaret: RRIF $48,000, CPP $11,000, OAS $8,300 = $67,300 George: DB pension $55,000, CPP $13,000, OAS $8,300 = $76,300

    Both are below the OAS clawback threshold. Good. But are they optimized?

    "Margaret and George — you're both below the $90,997 OAS threshold, so no clawback this year. But I want to look at the pension splitting. George, your DB pension of $55,000 is eligible for splitting. Margaret's marginal rate is lower. If you split $10,000 of George's pension to Margaret — George moves a portion into a lower bracket and Margaret's tax increases slightly at her lower rate. Let's model this."

    Without splitting: George: $76,300 net income. Combined Ontario tax: approximately $14,800. Margaret: $67,300 net income. Combined Ontario tax: approximately $11,200. Total combined: $26,000.

    With splitting $10,000 to Margaret: George: $66,300. Tax: approximately $11,800. Margaret: $77,300. Tax: approximately $14,100. Total combined: $25,900.

    Splitting saves $100 in this case — modest, because their incomes are reasonably balanced. But the exercise confirms they're near-optimally split.

    "The pension split helps modestly this year — about $100. More importantly, your current structure keeps both of you below the OAS clawback threshold, which is the priority. I'd recommend maintaining the current split. What I do want to flag: George, your RRIF mandatory minimum will increase as you age. By 76, it will be pushing your income to approximately $92,000 — above the clawback threshold. We should discuss a gradual withdrawal strategy over the next few years to keep you below the threshold for as long as possible. Can we set aside 30 minutes in January for that conversation?"

    George and Margaret agree. The January call is booked. The year-end call identified a future problem and established the relationship as ongoing — not just transactional.

    25.13 Week 25 Summary¶

    What you learned this week

    • Year-end planning is your highest-value service — it creates opportunities that are closed forever once December 31 passes. The year-end call is what separates you from every other preparer
    • The 15-minute year-end call structure: Current year income check → four planning questions (registered accounts, investment losses, year-end decisions, Ontario specifics) → written follow-up with quantified savings
    • RRSP bracket strategy: Don't just contribute "the maximum" — contribute exactly enough to reach the next bracket boundary first. Every dollar in the 29.65% bracket saves more than every dollar in the 20.05% bracket
    • Tax-loss harvesting: Capital losses offset capital gains. Trades must settle by December 31 (execute by December 29). The superficial loss rule requires 31 days before repurchasing the identical security — but similar securities can be bought immediately
    • Self-employed year-end strategies: Income deferral (delay invoicing to January), expense acceleration (buy equipment/supplies before December 31), RRSP maximization before year-end or by March 1
    • Incorporated clients: Bonus declaration before December 31 is deductible corporately this year even if paid in the new year. Dividend timing (before vs. after December 31) affects which personal tax year it falls in. Coordinate with the T2 preparer
    • Charitable donation timing: December 31 hard deadline. Securities donations to charity eliminate the capital gain — but must initiate transfer by approximately December 12. Biennial stacking generates more credit for small annual givers
    • Ontario-specific year-end: OAS clawback management through RRSP or pension splitting (most valuable for seniors near $90,997 threshold). Home Accessibility Tax Credit expenses paid before December 31. FHSA account opening deadline
    • Year-end planning is billable — either as part of a premium annual fee, a separate line item, or a full advisory retainer. Quantify the savings to the client. They never question the fee when you've identified $6,831 in savings

    ✅ Week 25 Quiz¶

    When you're ready, ask Claude: "Quiz me on Week 25" for an interactive test.

    1. Sandra earns $74,000 in employment income in 2025 and has $18,000 in available RRSP room. What is the exact RRSP contribution that maximizes her tax savings per dollar contributed, and what combined rate applies to that first portion?

    Sandra is in the combined 29.65% bracket (federal 20.5% + Ontario 9.15%) on income between $57,376 and $102,894. The bracket boundary below her is $57,375. To move from $74,000 to $57,375 requires: $74,000 − $57,375 = $16,625 in RRSP contributions. This entire $16,625 is sheltered at the 29.65% combined rate, saving $16,625 × 29.65% = $4,929 in combined tax. Any contribution above $16,625 (up to her $18,000 room) drops into the 24.15% bracket (federal 15% + Ontario 9.15%), saving only $1,375 × 24.15% = $332. The optimal strategy: prioritize the $16,625 that saves 29.65 cents per dollar first, then contribute the remaining $1,375 for additional (though smaller) savings.

    2. Robert has a non-registered portfolio. He realized $22,000 in capital gains from selling RBC shares in June. He has three positions at unrealized losses: Tech ETF ($8,400 loss), Energy stock ($5,200 loss), Financials ETF ($3,600 loss). He wants to harvest losses. (a) What is the maximum tax saving if he harvests all three? (b) What is the December deadline for executing the trades? (c) Can he immediately buy back the Tech ETF?

    (a) Tax saving: Total harvestable losses: $8,400 + $5,200 + $3,600 = $17,200. Applied against $22,000 in gains: net capital gain = $22,000 − $17,200 = $4,800. Without harvesting: taxable capital gain = $22,000 × 50% = $11,000. With harvesting: taxable capital gain = $4,800 × 50% = $2,400. Tax saving on $8,600 difference at Robert's 43.41% combined rate: $8,600 × 50% × 43.41% = $1,867 in combined tax saved. (b) Deadline: Most equities settle T+2 (trade date + 2 business days). For trades to settle by December 31, they must be executed by approximately December 29 (allowing for the 2-day settlement window). Execute no later than December 27 to be safe. (c) Tech ETF buyback: No — not immediately. The superficial loss rule denies the capital loss if the identical security is repurchased within 30 days before or after the sale. He must wait 31 days before buying back the same Tech ETF. However, he CAN immediately buy a different but similar ETF (e.g., a different US tech index ETF) to maintain market exposure without triggering the superficial loss rule.

    3. Ahmed is self-employed with estimated net income of $89,500 for 2025 (just below the $90,997 OAS clawback threshold). He has a $14,000 outstanding invoice for work completed in November. His client will pay whenever he invoices. What is the tax benefit of waiting until January 2026 to send the invoice, and what should you verify before recommending this?

    Tax benefit: If Ahmed invoices in January 2026, the $14,000 is received in 2026 — not 2025. His 2025 net income stays at $89,500 — below the $90,997 OAS threshold. He keeps his full OAS of approximately $8,300/year with $0 clawback in 2025. Additionally, the income tax on $14,000 at his combined 29.65% rate ($4,151) is deferred to his 2026 return. What to verify before recommending: (1) Accounting method: Does Ahmed report on a cash basis (income when received) or accrual basis (income when earned)? Most sole proprietors use cash basis, where the invoice date isn't relevant — receipt date is. For cash basis: delaying receipt to January works. For accrual basis: income may be recognized when the work was completed in November regardless of invoice date — the strategy may not work. (2) Business relationship: The client must actually pay in January. If the client pays in December regardless of the invoice date — the strategy fails. Verify Ahmed's accounting method before making this recommendation.

    4. Margaret holds 300 RBC shares purchased in 2010 for $8,000 (approximately $26.67/share). The current price is $94/share — total value $28,200. She wants to donate $28,200 to her hospital foundation this year. Walk through both scenarios (cash donation vs. share donation) and calculate the total tax advantage of donating shares directly.

    Scenario A — Sell shares, donate cash: Proceeds: $28,200. ACB: $8,000. Capital gain: $20,200. Taxable capital gain (50%): $10,100. At Margaret's 43.41% rate: $10,100 × 43.41% = $4,384 capital gains tax. After-tax cash to donate: $28,200 − $4,384 = $23,816. Donation receipt: $23,816. Credit (above $200 at 29%): $23,616 × 29% = $6,849 federal credit. Scenario B — Donate shares directly: Capital gain: $0 (exempt on direct donation of publicly traded securities to a registered charity). Donation receipt: $28,200 (full FMV). Donation credit: $200 × 15% + $28,000 × 29% = $30 + $8,120 = $8,150 federal credit. Total advantage of donating shares directly: Capital gains tax avoided: $4,384. Additional donation credit: $8,150 − $6,849 = $1,301. Total advantage: $5,685 — from the same charitable intent, simply by transferring shares rather than selling first. Margaret must initiate the share transfer to the charity's brokerage account by approximately December 12 to ensure it settles before December 31.

    5. For a self-employed client who normally buys a new laptop in February, what is the specific tax advantage of buying it in December instead? The laptop costs $2,400 and qualifies as Class 50 (55% CCA rate). Show the calculation.

    CCA in Year 1 (December purchase, 2025 tax year): $2,400 × 55% rate × 50% (half-year rule) = $660 CCA deduction in 2025. The remaining UCC of $1,740 continues to be depreciated in subsequent years. If purchased in February 2026: The $660 CCA deduction happens in 2026 — one year later. The tax advantage: The client gets the $660 deduction one year earlier. At their combined rate of (example) 33%: $660 × 33% = $218 in tax saved one year sooner. Additionally, there is the time value of paying $218 less in tax in April 2026 (the 2025 filing) vs. April 2027 (the 2026 filing) — approximately one year of deferred payment. For high-bracket clients (43%+): $660 × 43% = $284 saved a year earlier. The compounding effect over a practice lifetime of buying equipment December vs. January is meaningful. Advise clients to accelerate all business equipment purchases to December if year-end income supports the deduction.

    6. You are conducting a year-end planning call with Victor, 73, who has a RRIF of $850,000, a DB pension of $58,000, CPP of $14,000, and OAS of $8,300. His mandatory RRIF minimum at age 73 is 5.40% = $45,900. His estimated 2025 net income is $126,200. What is his OAS clawback, and what strategy should you discuss with him for the next several years?

    2025 OAS clawback: ($126,200 − $90,997) × 15% = $35,203 × 15% = $5,280 clawed back annually. Victor keeps only $8,300 − $5,280 = $3,020 of his OAS this year. Over a 10-year remaining retirement, if income stays the same: $5,280 × 10 = $52,800 in lost OAS. The strategy discussion: Victor's RRIF balance of $850,000 will generate increasing mandatory minimums as he ages (at 80: 6.82% × remaining balance, at 85: 8.51%). This problem compounds unless the balance is reduced. Discuss: (1) Voluntary RRIF withdrawals above the minimum in lower-income years — but at $126,200, he's already well above the threshold, so additional withdrawals only worsen the clawback. (2) Pension income splitting with his spouse — if his wife has lower income, allocating up to 50% of eligible pension income (RRIF qualifies at age 65+) to her could reduce his net income. If wife has $25,000 income: splitting $18,000 from RRIF to her reduces his income to $108,200 (still above threshold but reduced clawback). (3) RRIF melt-down over the next few years — this ship may have already sailed, but maximizing TFSA contributions from excess RRIF withdrawals shelters future growth. (4) Charitable giving from RRIF assets — withdrawing and donating directly, or discussing legacy giving. This is the critical conversation: "Victor, I want to flag that your RRIF is large enough that mandatory withdrawals will keep you well above the OAS clawback threshold for many years. The earlier we had this conversation — ideally before age 71 — the more we could have done. Going forward, let's model whether pension splitting with your wife can partially reduce the clawback, and explore whether charitable giving might be part of your estate planning."

    7. It is November 20. A client has not made any charitable donations yet this year. She gives $400/year to charity normally, and she didn't claim the 2024 donations either (she lost the receipts). She has her 2024 receipts now. What is her optimal donation strategy and the resulting federal credit?

    Strategy: Stack 2024 and 2025 donations on the 2025 return. The 2024 receipts can be carried forward and claimed in 2025 (donation receipts can be carried forward up to 5 years). Combined 2024 + 2025 donation: $400 + $400 = $800. Federal donation credit: $200 × 15% + $600 × 29% = $30 + $174 = $204. Versus claiming each year separately: Each year: $200 × 15% + $200 × 29% = $30 + $58 = $88/year × 2 years = $176 combined. Stacking advantage: $204 − $176 = $28 more — not enormous, but real. Additionally, if she plans to continue giving $400/year: by stacking every two years (claim $800 in even years, nothing in odd years), she consistently gets the $204 credit every two years instead of $88 per year, saving $28 per two-year cycle indefinitely. The time to implement this: give her the stacking instruction today so she claims both years on the 2025 return.
