    Week 2: The Chart of Accounts — Organizing Every Dollar¶

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    • Explain the five account types (Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Revenue, Expenses) and what each contains
    • Identify the normal balance of any account without hesitation
    • Build a chart of accounts from scratch for any small Ontario service or product business
    • Decide whether to create a new account or use an existing one — the discipline that separates a clean set of books from a messy one
    • Recognize the Ontario-specific accounts every business needs: HST Payable, HST Receivable, WSIB Payable, EHT Payable, Vacation Pay Accrual
    • Set up the chart of accounts in QuickBooks Online correctly from day one
    • Review an existing chart of accounts and identify what's missing, redundant, or wrongly classified

    Why the chart of accounts is the most underrated skill in bookkeeping

    The chart of accounts is the structural foundation of every set of books. A well-designed chart makes bookkeeping fast, reports meaningful, and tax preparation straightforward. A badly designed chart makes everything harder — from daily data entry to year-end. Employers can tell within 30 seconds of opening a QuickBooks file whether the bookkeeper who set it up knew what they were doing. This week, you become that bookkeeper.

    2.1 What a Chart of Accounts Really Is¶

    Imagine a filing cabinet for every dollar a business touches.

    Every transaction — every sale, every bill, every payroll deposit, every cheque — must be filed somewhere. The chart of accounts is the list of folders in that cabinet. Each folder has a number and a name, and every transaction gets placed in exactly one folder.

    When the business owner asks "how much did we spend on fuel this year?" — the bookkeeper pulls the Fuel folder and counts. When CRA asks "how much HST did you collect?" — the bookkeeper pulls the HST Payable folder. When the bank asks "what's the business's net profit?" — the bookkeeper adds up all the revenue folders and subtracts all the expense folders.

    The chart of accounts isn't just organizational housekeeping. It is the lens through which the business sees itself financially.

    Why It Matters More Than Beginners Think¶

    Two businesses with identical transactions but different charts of accounts will produce completely different-looking financial statements. One might show "Marketing Expense — $18,000" as a single line. Another might break the same $18,000 across Google Ads, Facebook Ads, Trade Shows, Print Advertising, and Promotional Materials. Both are correct. But the second business can answer the question "what's our ROI on Google Ads?" instantly. The first business has to dig through invoices.

    Your job as a bookkeeper is to design a chart of accounts that gives the owner the information they need to run their business — no more, no less.

    2.2 The Five Account Types¶

    Every account in every business belongs to one of exactly five types. Memorize these. Understand them. You will categorize every account you ever see.

    1. Assets¶

    What the business owns or is owed.

    Assets are further divided by how quickly they can be converted to cash:

    Current Assets (convertible to cash within 12 months): - Cash in bank - Petty cash - Accounts receivable (money customers owe) - Inventory (if the business sells products) - Prepaid expenses (insurance, rent paid in advance) - HST Receivable / Input Tax Credits (what CRA owes back on purchases)

    Non-Current Assets (long-term, used for more than 12 months): - Equipment - Vehicles - Buildings - Land - Accumulated Depreciation (a contra-asset — we cover this in Week 5)

    Asset accounts are typically numbered 1000–1999.

    2. Liabilities¶

    What the business owes.

    Liabilities are also split by timing:

    Current Liabilities (due within 12 months): - Accounts payable (bills owed to suppliers) - Credit card payable - HST Payable (what the business owes CRA on collected HST) - Wages Payable / Vacation Pay Accrual - Source Deductions Payable (CPP, EI, income tax withheld from employee pay, not yet remitted) - WSIB Payable (Ontario's workplace safety insurance) - EHT Payable (Ontario's Employer Health Tax) - Current portion of long-term debt

    Non-Current Liabilities (due beyond 12 months): - Long-term bank loans - Vehicle loans - Shareholder loans (when the owner has lent money to the corporation)

    Liability accounts are typically numbered 2000–2999.

    3. Equity¶

    The owner's stake in the business — what's left after liabilities are subtracted from assets.

    For sole proprietors: - Owner's Capital (initial and ongoing investment) - Owner's Draws (money taken out for personal use) - Current Year Earnings (profit or loss accumulating during the year)

    For corporations: - Share Capital (what shareholders paid for their shares) - Retained Earnings (accumulated profits not distributed as dividends) - Dividends Declared (distributions to shareholders)

    Equity accounts are typically numbered 3000–3999.

    4. Revenue (Income)¶

    What the business earns from its core activities.

    • Sales Revenue (or Service Revenue for service businesses)
    • Consulting Income
    • Rental Income (for landlord clients)
    • Interest Income
    • Other Income (tips, miscellaneous)

    Revenue accounts are typically numbered 4000–4999.

    Most small businesses have just 1-3 revenue accounts. More only if the owner genuinely wants to see revenue broken out — otherwise you're creating clutter.

    5. Expenses¶

    What the business spends to generate revenue.

    Expenses are often divided into two groups:

    Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) — direct costs of producing or delivering the product/service: - Materials and supplies purchased for resale - Direct labour (for trades and manufacturers) - Freight and shipping on goods purchased - Subcontractor costs (for contractors)

    Operating Expenses — everything else required to run the business: - Rent - Utilities - Insurance - Professional fees - Office supplies - Marketing and advertising - Vehicle expenses - Telephone and internet - Wages and salaries - Employer CPP, EI, WSIB, EHT contributions - Bank charges - Interest expense - Depreciation

    COGS accounts are typically numbered 5000–5999. Operating expenses are 6000–7999 or 6000–8999, depending on the numbering scheme.

    The Five Types at a Glance¶

    Type Represents Typical Numbering Examples
    Assets What is owned or owed to the business 1000–1999 Cash, AR, Equipment, Inventory
    Liabilities What is owed by the business 2000–2999 AP, HST Payable, Loan Payable
    Equity Owner's stake 3000–3999 Capital, Retained Earnings, Draws
    Revenue Income earned 4000–4999 Sales, Service Revenue
    Expenses Costs incurred 5000–8999 Rent, Wages, Insurance

    2.3 The Account Numbering Convention¶

    There's no single required numbering system in Canada — but there is a widely-accepted convention, and every Ontario bookkeeper recognizes it. Use it.

    The Standard Range¶

    1000 – 1999  ASSETS
2000 – 2999  LIABILITIES
3000 – 3999  EQUITY
4000 – 4999  REVENUE
5000 – 5999  COST OF GOODS SOLD
6000 – 7999  OPERATING EXPENSES

    Some charts extend to: 

    8000 – 8999  OTHER INCOME/EXPENSE (interest earned, gains on sale, etc.)
9000 – 9999  INCOME TAX

    Finer Organization Within Each Range¶

    Within each range, convention groups related accounts together:

    Within Assets (1000–1999): - 1000–1099 Current Assets - Cash - 1100–1199 Accounts Receivable - 1200–1299 Inventory - 1300–1399 Prepaid Expenses, HST Receivable - 1500–1899 Fixed Assets (Equipment, Vehicles, Buildings) - 1900–1999 Accumulated Depreciation

    Within Liabilities (2000–2999): - 2000–2099 Accounts Payable - 2100–2199 HST, Source Deductions Payable - 2200–2299 Wages Payable, Vacation Accrual - 2300–2499 Credit Cards, Short-term Loans - 2500–2999 Long-term Loans, Shareholder Loan

    This structure serves two purposes: accounts appear in a logical order on reports, and you can tell what type an account is just from its number.

    Why Leave Gaps?¶

    Always number in increments of 5 or 10, never 1. If your first cash account is 1000, the second should be 1010, not 1001.

    Why? Because you'll add new accounts over time, and you want them to slot in logically. If you number sequentially from 1, you'll eventually want to add "TD Savings" but the next number is already "Accounts Receivable" — and renumbering existing accounts in QBO is a nightmare.

    Leave room. Your future self will thank you.

    2.4 Normal Balances — The Logic, Not the Memorization¶

    Every account has a normal balance — either debit or credit. When the account increases, you post to the normal side. When it decreases, you post to the opposite side.

    Beginners memorize this. Professionals understand it.

    The Logic¶

    The normal balance of any account is whichever side increases the account. That's it. That's the whole rule.

    Now — which side increases which account? There are two fundamental facts to know:

    Fact 1: The left side (debit) of the accounting equation: 

    ASSETS = LIABILITIES + EQUITY
  ↑
(this side)

    Accounts on the left side of the equation — Assets — increase with a debit.

    Fact 2: The right side (credit) of the accounting equation: 

    ASSETS = LIABILITIES + EQUITY
          ↑          ↑
         (this side)

    Accounts on the right side — Liabilities and Equity — increase with a credit.

    That's the core rule. Everything else follows.

    Extending to Revenue and Expenses¶

    Revenue and Expenses are not part of the basic equation directly — they are sub-categories of Equity, because they change equity during the period.

    • Revenue increases equity (you earned more). Equity increases with a credit. So revenue also increases with a credit.
    • Expenses decrease equity (you spent money). To decrease a credit-normal account (Equity), you debit. So expenses increase with a debit.

    The Full Normal Balance Table¶

    Account Type Normal Balance Increased By Decreased By
    Assets Debit Debit Credit
    Liabilities Credit Credit Debit
    Equity Credit Credit Debit
    Revenue Credit Credit Debit
    Expenses Debit Debit Credit

    Why This Matters for the Chart of Accounts¶

    When you set up the chart of accounts, you designate each account's type. That designation determines the normal balance — and therefore how transactions flow through the account. Getting the type wrong corrupts every report.

    A classic beginner error: setting up "Owner's Draws" as an Expense (because it feels like the owner is "taking money out"). Wrong — Draws is an Equity account (a reduction of equity, but equity nonetheless). If you code it as an expense, it reduces net income, which is doubly wrong and throws off both the P&L and the tax filings.

    The most common account type mistakes

    • Owner's Draws → Expense: Wrong. It's an Equity account (a contra-equity reducing Capital).
    • HST Payable → Expense: Wrong. HST collected is a Liability to CRA, not an expense.
    • Loan Proceeds → Revenue: Wrong. Receiving a loan creates a Liability, not revenue.
    • Purchase of Equipment → Expense: Wrong. Equipment is an Asset. It becomes expense over time through depreciation.
    • Bank fees charged on a customer payment → Reduction of Revenue: Wrong. Bank fees are an Expense, full stop.

    2.5 The Ontario-Specific Accounts Every Bookkeeper Must Know¶

    Ontario bookkeeping has specific accounts that don't appear in U.S. textbooks and sometimes get overlooked in generic Canadian ones. These must be in every Ontario chart of accounts.

    HST Payable (2100)¶

    Collected from customers on taxable sales. Owed to CRA. Always a Liability.

    When you sell $1,000 of taxable services in Ontario: - Cash or AR increases by $1,130 (total including 13% HST) - Revenue increases by $1,000 - HST Payable (liability) increases by $130

    That $130 does not belong to the business. It belongs to CRA. The HST Payable account tracks it until the HST return is filed and the money is remitted.

    HST Receivable / ITC (Input Tax Credits) (1300)¶

    Paid to suppliers on taxable purchases. Claimable back from CRA. Always an Asset.

    When you buy $226 of office supplies (with 13% HST): - Office Supplies expense increases by $200 - HST Receivable (asset) increases by $26 - Cash or AP increases by $226

    The $26 is money you can recover from CRA by offsetting it against HST Payable on your HST return.

    The Math on the HST Return¶

    HST Payable balance     (what you owe CRA)
MINUS HST Receivable    (what you can claim back)
=  NET HST REMITTANCE   (what you actually send to CRA)

    If HST Payable is $5,000 and HST Receivable is $1,800, you remit $3,200 to CRA. If purchases exceeded sales and HST Receivable is larger, you get a refund.

    We cover the complete HST process in Week 7.

    Source Deductions Payable (2110)¶

    CPP, EI, and income tax withheld from employee paycheques, not yet remitted to CRA. Liability.

    When you run payroll and withhold $400 of income tax, $150 of CPP, and $95 of EI from an employee: - Wages Expense captures the gross pay - Source Deductions Payable increases by $645 (the amounts withheld) - Cash decreases by the net pay to the employee

    When you remit these deductions to CRA (along with the employer's matching CPP and EI), Source Deductions Payable goes back to zero.

    CRA is extremely strict about source deductions. Late remittances trigger automatic penalties. Unremitted source deductions are a director's personal liability — meaning directors can be held personally responsible for unpaid source deductions even after bankruptcy.

    WSIB Payable (2120)¶

    Ontario's Workplace Safety and Insurance Board premium. Liability.

    WSIB is mandatory for most Ontario employers (with some exceptions for specific industries and for corporations where only the owner works and has no employees). The rate is assigned by WSIB based on industry classification and ranges roughly from 0.21% to 20.05% of insurable earnings.

    When you calculate WSIB for a payroll period: - WSIB Expense increases (operating expense) - WSIB Payable (liability) increases until remitted

    EHT Payable (2130)¶

    Ontario's Employer Health Tax. Liability.

    EHT is charged to Ontario employers whose total Ontario payroll exceeds a threshold (currently $1 million for private sector employers, with the first $1M exempt). Public sector and registered charities have different thresholds.

    Most small businesses with payroll under $1 million don't pay EHT — but a growing business might cross the threshold mid-year. Know the rule.

    Vacation Pay Accrual (2200)¶

    Ontario's Employment Standards Act requires a minimum vacation pay of 4% of gross wages for employees with less than 5 years of service and 6% for those with 5+ years.

    If the business pays vacation pay out with each paycheque, there's no accrual — it goes directly to the employee. If the business accrues vacation pay (the employee takes vacation later), every payroll must record the 4% or 6% as a liability:

    • Wages Expense increases by gross wages
    • Vacation Pay Accrual (liability) increases by 4% or 6% of gross wages

    When the employee takes vacation, the accrual is reduced and paid out. We cover this in Week 11.

    The Full Ontario-Specific Account Block¶

    Here are the accounts every Ontario chart of accounts should include. Drop any of these and the books will be incomplete.

    Account # Account Name Type
    1300 HST Receivable / ITCs Asset
    2100 HST Payable Liability
    2110 Source Deductions Payable Liability
    2120 WSIB Payable Liability
    2130 EHT Payable Liability
    2200 Vacation Pay Accrual Liability
    6100 Wages Expense Expense
    6110 Employer CPP Expense Expense
    6120 Employer EI Expense Expense
    6130 WSIB Expense Expense
    6140 EHT Expense Expense

    2.6 Signature Example — Northbrook Landscaping, Barrie¶

    Let's build a complete chart of accounts from scratch for a real Ontario business.

    The Business¶

    Northbrook Landscaping is owned by James Reid, operating in Barrie, Ontario. It's incorporated as a small Ontario corporation. James is the sole shareholder-employee.

    The business provides: - Residential landscape design and installation (taxable) - Lawn maintenance contracts (taxable) - Snow removal (taxable)

    It owns: - A 2023 Ford F-250 truck - A utility trailer - Lawn mowers, blowers, trimmers, and other equipment - A small storage yard (leased, not owned)

    It employs: - James (owner-employee, paid T4 salary) - Two seasonal labourers (April to November) - One part-time administrator (year-round)

    It uses: - RBC Business Chequing - RBC Visa Business credit card - A line of credit with RBC - A truck loan with Ford Credit

    The Complete Chart of Accounts¶

    Here's the full chart — 42 accounts, every one intentional.

    1000-series: ASSETS¶

    # Account Type Purpose
    1000 RBC Chequing Bank Main operating account
    1010 RBC Savings Bank Emergency/tax reserve
    1020 Petty Cash Bank $200 physical cash for small purchases
    1100 Accounts Receivable AR Customer invoices outstanding
    1200 Inventory - Landscape Materials Inventory Seed, fertilizer, mulch, stones held for projects
    1300 HST Receivable (ITCs) Other Current Asset HST paid on purchases, recoverable
    1310 Prepaid Insurance Other Current Asset Annual insurance premiums paid in advance
    1320 Prepaid Rent Other Current Asset Last month's rent held by landlord
    1500 Equipment Fixed Asset Mowers, blowers, trimmers, tools
    1510 Vehicles Fixed Asset F-250 truck and trailer
    1900 Accumulated Depreciation - Equipment Fixed Asset (contra) Lifetime depreciation of equipment
    1910 Accumulated Depreciation - Vehicles Fixed Asset (contra) Lifetime depreciation of vehicles

    Notes on this section: - James doesn't have multiple AR accounts — one is enough for a small business. If he had a government contract subject to different payment terms, we might split. - Inventory only tracks materials held for projects. Equipment is a fixed asset, not inventory. - The two accumulated depreciation accounts are "contra assets" — they have a credit balance and reduce the related asset to its book value. We cover these fully in Week 5.

    2000-series: LIABILITIES¶

    # Account Type Purpose
    2000 Accounts Payable AP Supplier invoices owed
    2100 HST Payable Other Current Liability HST collected from customers
    2110 Source Deductions Payable Other Current Liability CPP, EI, income tax withheld
    2120 WSIB Payable Other Current Liability WSIB premium owing
    2130 EHT Payable Other Current Liability Not applicable currently — payroll under $1M
    2200 Vacation Pay Accrual Other Current Liability Accrued vacation for the admin employee
    2300 RBC Visa Business Credit Card Business credit card balance
    2400 Line of Credit - RBC Credit Card Revolving line of credit
    2500 Truck Loan - Ford Credit Long-term Liability Balance on F-250 financing
    2600 Shareholder Loan - James Reid Long-term Liability Informal money movement between James and the corp

    Notes on this section: - We keep the EHT Payable account even though it's currently $0 — if payroll crosses $1M, we're ready without setup delay. - The Shareholder Loan is critical. When James takes cash from the company for personal use outside of his regular payroll (or injects personal money into the business), it flows through this account. We flag the balance for the accountant before year-end. - The truck loan has both a current portion (the 12 months of payments due in the next year) and a long-term portion. Many small businesses don't split these at month-end — the accountant does it at year-end.

    3000-series: EQUITY¶

    # Account Type Purpose
    3000 Common Shares Equity Par value of issued shares (James holds 100 common shares at $1 each = $100)
    3100 Retained Earnings Equity Accumulated profits from prior years
    3500 Dividends Declared Equity Any dividends James takes (distinct from salary)
    3900 Current Year Earnings Equity Net income for the current year (QBO usually creates this automatically)

    Notes on this section: - For a corporation, we don't use "Owner's Capital" — we use "Common Shares" and "Retained Earnings." - If this were a sole proprietorship, we'd have "Owner's Capital" and "Owner's Draws" instead.

    4000-series: REVENUE¶

    # Account Type Purpose
    4000 Landscape Design & Installation Income Large project revenue — design & build
    4100 Lawn Maintenance Contracts Income Ongoing monthly service revenue
    4200 Snow Removal Income Winter service revenue
    4900 Other Income Income Miscellaneous (tips, gear sold, etc.)

    Notes on this section: - James specifically wants to see landscape installation vs. maintenance vs. snow removal — three very different profit centres with different seasonality. Splitting revenue this way lets him see which segment is most profitable. - "Other Income" is the catch-all. Keep it around but keep it small — anything accumulating here in significant amounts should get its own account.

    5000-series: COST OF GOODS SOLD¶

    # Account Type Purpose
    5000 Materials - Landscape Projects COGS Plants, soil, stone, mulch consumed on projects
    5100 Materials - Maintenance COGS Fertilizer, seed, weed control for contract work
    5200 Subcontractor Costs COGS Specialized subcontractors (electrical, irrigation)
    5300 Direct Labour - Seasonal COGS Wages for the two seasonal labourers (direct field work)

    Notes on this section: - COGS captures the direct costs of delivering services. By splitting into Installation materials vs. Maintenance materials, James can see which revenue stream has better margins. - Seasonal labour is COGS because these employees work directly on client projects. The administrator, by contrast, is an operating expense (below). - Some bookkeepers would combine materials into one account. For a landscaping business where revenue is segmented, keeping materials segmented too is valuable.

    6000-series: PAYROLL-RELATED EXPENSES¶

    # Account Type Purpose
    6000 Salary - Owner (James) Expense James's annual T4 salary
    6010 Wages - Administrative Expense Part-time administrator wages
    6100 Employer CPP Expense Expense Employer portion of CPP
    6110 Employer EI Expense Expense Employer portion of EI
    6120 WSIB Expense Expense Assessed WSIB premiums
    6130 Employee Benefits Expense Health insurance premiums (if offered)
    6140 Vacation Pay Expense Expense Vacation pay accrued during the year

    Notes on this section: - We separate James's salary from admin wages so the owner can see their own compensation cost distinctly. Direct labour (the seasonal crew) is in COGS above. - The employer's share of CPP, EI, WSIB, and EHT (if applicable) are all expenses in their own right — separate from the employee's paycheque.

    7000-series: OPERATING EXPENSES¶

    # Account Type Purpose
    7000 Rent Expense Yard lease
    7010 Utilities Expense Hydro, gas, water at the yard
    7020 Telephone & Internet Expense Business cell phones and office internet
    7100 Vehicle - Fuel Expense Gasoline and diesel
    7110 Vehicle - Repairs & Maintenance Expense Truck and trailer maintenance
    7120 Vehicle - Insurance Expense Commercial vehicle insurance
    7200 General Insurance Expense Liability and property insurance
    7300 Office Supplies Expense Paper, pens, small office items
    7310 Small Tools & Supplies Expense Hand tools, consumables under $500
    7400 Advertising & Marketing Expense Website, Google Ads, signage, flyers
    7500 Professional Fees Expense Accountant, lawyer, bookkeeper fees
    7600 Bank Charges Expense Monthly fees, wire transfer fees, NSF fees
    7610 Credit Card Fees Expense Merchant fees on customer credit card payments
    7700 Meals & Entertainment Expense 50% deductible per ITA — keep separate for easy tax calculation
    7800 Training & Education Expense Courses, certifications, industry association dues

    Notes on this section: - Vehicle expenses are broken into fuel, maintenance, and insurance. James wants to know what the truck is actually costing him. - Meals & Entertainment is kept as a distinct account because CRA only allows 50% of it as a deduction. Keeping it separate makes the year-end tax adjustment trivial instead of requiring a forensic review. - "Small Tools" is distinct from "Equipment" (asset). The rule of thumb: purchases under $500 go to expense; purchases over that are considered for capitalization as equipment assets. This isn't a hard CRA rule but is a common bookkeeping convention.

    8000-series: OTHER INCOME AND EXPENSE¶

    # Account Type Purpose
    8000 Interest Income Income Interest earned on savings
    8100 Interest Expense Expense Interest on truck loan, line of credit, credit card
    8200 Depreciation Expense Expense Annual depreciation on equipment and vehicles

    Notes on this section: - Depreciation is booked monthly (for book purposes) or annually (for simpler businesses). We cover depreciation fully in Week 5.

    The Final Count: 42 Accounts¶

    That's the complete chart of accounts for Northbrook Landscaping. Every account serves a purpose. Nothing is missing. Nothing is redundant.

    If James someday expands into commercial landscape maintenance, we add a new revenue account (4110 — Commercial Maintenance Contracts). If he adds a second truck, the existing Vehicle account still works — we don't need to split by vehicle. If he starts importing plants from the US, we might add a Foreign Exchange Gain/Loss account in the 8000 series.

    The chart grows with the business — but it never grows for no reason.

    2.7 The Discipline: When to Create a New Account (and When Not To)¶

    The single biggest bookkeeping mess is a chart of accounts with too many accounts — most of them used once and then forgotten, cluttering every report for years.

    The Rule of Thumb¶

    Create a new account only if the answer to at least one of these is yes:

    1. Does the owner need to see this category separately on reports?
    2. Does CRA require it to be tracked separately (like HST, M&E, source deductions)?
    3. Will the category have recurring transactions?
    4. Does an industry-specific convention exist that this should be separate?

    Do NOT create a new account if:

    • It's a one-time transaction — put it in an existing catch-all account
    • It's similar to an existing account — expand the existing one
    • You're "not sure" where to put something — ask the owner or accountant

    A Real Situation¶

    A client buys a $45 wall calendar and some sticky notes for the office. A rookie bookkeeper creates a new account called "Calendars and Small Supplies." Three years later, that account has six transactions totalling $220 and clutters every report.

    The right move: Code it to Office Supplies. That's what Office Supplies exists for.

    Another case: a client hires a videographer for a one-time corporate video, costing $4,800. Should we create "Video Production Expense"? Probably not. It's a one-time cost. Code it to Advertising & Marketing with a clear description in the memo field ("Corporate brand video — Jan 2025"). If the owner ever wants to find it, a memo search takes 5 seconds.

    The test: If you're only going to use an account once or twice, it probably doesn't need to exist.

    Ontario Industry-Specific Accounts You'll Encounter¶

    Different industries have specific accounts you'll see:

    Construction and Trades: - Holdbacks Receivable / Holdbacks Payable (under the Construction Act) - Job Costing by project (advanced — usually through QBO Plus with class tracking)

    Retail: - Sales Returns and Allowances - Cash Over/Short - Inventory Shrinkage

    Professional Services (Law, Accounting, Consulting): - Work in Progress (WIP) - Trust Account Payable (for lawyers) - Unbilled Revenue

    Restaurants: - Food Costs (separate from Beverage Costs) - Tips Payable (if pooled or distributed)

    Not-for-Profit: - Grant Income (restricted vs. unrestricted) - Fund Accounting structures

    These come up when you're hired into these industries. You don't need to know them all now — but knowing they exist prevents you from being surprised on the job.

    2.8 Setting Up the Chart of Accounts in QuickBooks Online¶

    QBO ships with a default chart of accounts. It's a starting point — but rarely sufficient as-is for an Ontario business. You'll need to modify it.

    The Setup Process¶

    Step 1 — Choose Industry During Setup When you create a new QBO company, QBO asks you to select an industry. Choose the closest match. QBO will pre-populate a chart based on typical accounts for that industry. This saves 30 minutes of setup.

    Step 2 — Review the Default Chart Go to Settings → Chart of Accounts. Review every account. Some will be irrelevant (delete or hide them). Others will be missing (add them). Pay special attention to:

    • HST settings — QBO creates HST accounts automatically when you enable sales tax. Enable HST for Ontario (13%) during setup.
    • Opening balance accounts — if you're migrating from another system, QBO creates an "Opening Balance Equity" account. This should be zeroed out after migration is complete.
    • Uncategorized Asset / Liability / Income / Expense — these are catch-alls QBO creates. In professional bookkeeping, you never use them for more than 5 minutes. Any transaction in these accounts at month-end is wrong and must be recoded.

    Step 3 — Add Missing Ontario Accounts Add any Ontario-specific accounts not already present: EHT Payable, Vacation Pay Accrual, WSIB Payable, etc.

    Step 4 — Set Up Account Numbers QBO allows account numbering, but it's off by default. Turn it on: Settings → Account and Settings → Advanced → Chart of Accounts → Enable account numbers: ON

    Once enabled, renumber each account according to the convention (1000s for assets, 2000s for liabilities, etc.). This makes reports significantly cleaner and allows accounts to sort in a predictable order.

    Step 5 — Use Sub-Accounts Intentionally QBO allows sub-accounts (Equipment → Computers, Equipment → Office Furniture). Use these sparingly. They're useful for fixed asset categories but quickly become noise if overused for operating expenses.

    Step 6 — Lock Irrelevant Default Accounts You can't delete an account with transactions in it — but you can mark it inactive. Do this for all unused default accounts to keep the chart clean.

    What a Clean QBO Chart of Accounts Looks Like¶

    When you open a well-organized QBO file, the chart of accounts:

    • Has account numbers enabled and visible
    • Is ordered 1000s → 8000s
    • Has 30–60 accounts (fewer for very simple businesses, more for complex ones)
    • Has zero "Uncategorized" balances
    • Has all Ontario-specific tax accounts present and correctly categorized
    • Has no orphaned or duplicate accounts

    When you open a messy one, you'll see 80+ accounts with bizarre names ("John's Misc," "Old Bank," "Don't Use"), transactions scattered across "Uncategorized Expense," duplicate vendors creating duplicate accounts, and no numbering at all.

    Your first job with any new client is often to clean up the chart. We cover cleanup in Week 13.

    2.9 Reviewing an Existing Chart of Accounts — The Diagnostic Skill¶

    An entry-level bookkeeper is often handed an existing QBO file and asked to "just keep the books going." Before you do anything else, review the chart of accounts.

    The 10-Minute Diagnostic¶

    Open the Chart of Accounts. Ask yourself:

    1. Are account numbers enabled? If not, turn them on.
    2. Are all accounts numbered according to convention? If not, note which ones are off.
    3. Is HST correctly set up? Check HST Payable and HST Receivable — are they being used? Any balances that don't make sense?
    4. Are there "Uncategorized" accounts with balances? Every dollar in these needs to be recoded.
    5. Is there "Opening Balance Equity" with a non-zero balance? This is a migration leftover that should be cleared.
    6. Are the revenue accounts logical? Does the breakdown match how the owner thinks about their business?
    7. Are there duplicate accounts? (Two "Office Supplies" accounts, one capitalized differently.)
    8. Are there unused accounts? Ones with zero balance and no recent activity — consider inactivating.
    9. Does the chart include Ontario-specific accounts? WSIB, EHT, Vacation Pay Accrual — all present?
    10. Are any expense accounts misclassified? (Owner's Draws showing up as an expense, for example.)

    The answers tell you what cleanup work is needed before you can do accurate day-to-day bookkeeping.

    2.10 Common Mistakes in Chart of Accounts Design¶

    Your chart of accounts is a living document. Here are the traps to avoid.

    Mistake 1 — Too Many Accounts¶

    A chart with 120 accounts for a simple service business is a red flag. Reports become unreadable. Categorization decisions become impossible. Half the accounts never get used.

    Fix: Consolidate. Merge similar accounts. Aim for 30–60 accounts for most small businesses.

    Mistake 2 — Too Few Accounts¶

    The opposite problem: everything goes into "Miscellaneous Expense." The owner has no idea where money is going.

    Fix: Interview the owner. What decisions do they want to make? What categories matter to them? Build accounts to support those decisions.

    Mistake 3 — Inconsistent Naming¶

    "Office Supplies," "office expenses," "Office - Supplies," "General office." All four exist in the same chart.

    Fix: Pick one naming convention and enforce it. Proper case is preferred. No abbreviations unless universal.

    Mistake 4 — Personal Expenses Mixed In¶

    Particularly common in sole proprietor books — the owner has personal coffee purchases, family groceries, and personal Netflix subscriptions flowing into the business chart.

    Fix: Code personal expenses to Owner's Draws (for sole proprietors) or Shareholder Loan (for corporations — see Week 19). They are NEVER business expenses.

    Mistake 5 — HST Account Confusion¶

    A client accidentally has three HST accounts: "HST Payable," "Sales Tax Payable," and "GST/HST Owing." None of them reconcile to the HST returns.

    Fix: Use QBO's built-in sales tax module. Don't manually create HST accounts. QBO auto-creates and maintains them if you set up sales tax properly.

    Mistake 6 — No Meals & Entertainment Separation¶

    M&E is only 50% deductible for tax. If it's lumped in with other expenses, year-end requires forensic work to extract it.

    Fix: Always have a separate Meals & Entertainment account.

    Mistake 7 — No Depreciation or Amortization Accounts¶

    Fixed assets exist, but there's no monthly depreciation being recorded. The P&L shows unrealistic profit.

    Fix: Book monthly depreciation (even if your client's business is small, accountants prefer to see it handled throughout the year rather than at year-end). We cover depreciation entries in Week 5.

    2.11 Quiz — 12 Questions¶

    Question 1 Name the five account types.

    Answer

    Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Revenue, Expenses.

    Question 2 What is the normal balance of an Accounts Receivable account? Why?

    Answer

    Debit — because Accounts Receivable is an asset, and assets are on the left side of the accounting equation. Assets increase with a debit, so their normal balance is a debit balance.

    Question 3 A client's chart of accounts has "HST Payable" set up as an Expense account. What's the problem, and what's the impact?

    Answer

    Problem: HST Payable is a liability (it's money owed to CRA), not an expense. If it's set up as an expense: - It appears on the P&L, inflating expenses and understating net income - Every HST collection reduces "expenses" (which makes no sense) - The HST return won't reconcile to the books - Year-end financial statements will be wrong

    Fix: Delete the incorrect account (after moving all transactions), or reclassify it to Liability. If this was set up at the start of the year, you may need to prepare correcting entries going back to January.

    Question 4 A sole proprietor has a "Travel - Personal" expense account in their chart of accounts. What's wrong?

    Answer

    Personal travel is never a business expense. It doesn't belong in the chart of accounts at all as an expense. If the owner paid for personal travel from the business account, the entry should be:

    • Debit Owner's Draws
    • Credit Cash

    This reflects the reality: the owner took money out of the business for personal use. The account should be removed from the chart.

    Question 5 Why should account numbers be spaced in increments of 5 or 10, not 1?

    Answer

    To leave room for future accounts to be inserted in logical order. If you number 1001, 1002, 1003 and then need to add a new cash account, there's no logical number between 1001 and 1002. Numbering 1000, 1010, 1020 allows you to add 1005, 1015 etc. later without renumbering the entire chart.

    Question 6 What's the difference between HST Payable and HST Receivable (ITCs)?

    Answer

    HST Payable (Liability, account ~2100): HST the business has collected from customers on sales. This money belongs to CRA and must be remitted.

    HST Receivable / Input Tax Credits (Asset, account ~1300): HST the business has paid to suppliers on purchases. This is recoverable from CRA by offsetting against HST Payable on the HST return.

    On the HST return, the two are netted: - If HST Payable > HST Receivable → Pay CRA the difference - If HST Receivable > HST Payable → Receive a refund

    Question 7 A small Ontario corporation has payroll of $450,000 for the year. Do they owe EHT?

    Answer

    No. EHT (Employer Health Tax) applies to private-sector Ontario employers only once their Ontario payroll exceeds $1,000,000. At $450,000, the business is well below the threshold and owes no EHT.

    That said, the EHT Payable account should still exist in the chart of accounts — so the system is ready if the business crosses the threshold.

    Question 8 A new client asks you to create a separate expense account for "Coffee and Lunches for Team" because the owner wants to track it. What do you do?

    Answer

    Clarify with the owner: "Coffee and lunches for the team" is Meals & Entertainment — which is subject to the 50% deductibility rule. Creating a separate account is fine, but:

    • Make sure the account is classified under Meals & Entertainment (not Office Supplies or similar)
    • Confirm the transactions are genuinely M&E — if it's employee meals during travel, that may be fully deductible under different rules
    • Keep a general Meals & Entertainment account for other M&E that doesn't fit this category

    The broader principle: creating accounts at owner request is fine as long as you understand the tax treatment. The accountant will thank you for keeping M&E isolated at year-end.

    Question 9 You inherit a QBO file where the chart of accounts has 18 different vendors listed as expense accounts ("Bell Canada Expense," "Hydro Expense," "Enbridge Gas Expense," etc.). Is this correct?

    Answer

    No — this is wrong. Vendors are not account categories. Vendors are who you paid; accounts are what you paid them for.

    The correct structure: - Telephone & Internet (account) ← Bell Canada (vendor) bills coded here - Utilities (account) ← Hydro One (vendor), Enbridge Gas (vendor), City of Toronto Water (vendor) bills coded here

    Fix: Consolidate the 18 accounts into 3–4 proper expense categories (Telephone & Internet, Utilities, etc.), move all transactions to the correct account, and inactivate the vendor-named accounts. This is a common mess in self-done bookkeeping.

    Question 10 In a corporate chart of accounts, where does the owner-shareholder's T4 salary appear? Where does a dividend paid to the same shareholder appear?

    Answer

    Salary: Expense account — typically "Salary - Owner" or "Wages - Officer" (~6000 range). Reduces corporate net income. The employee-shareholder reports it on their personal T1 as employment income.

    Dividend: Equity account — "Dividends Declared" (~3500 range). Does NOT reduce corporate net income (dividends are paid from after-tax profits). The shareholder reports the dividend separately on their personal T1 with the appropriate dividend gross-up and tax credit.

    Getting this wrong — recording dividends as salary, or vice versa — is a significant tax error. For the corporation, the distinction affects corporate tax. For the shareholder, it affects personal tax. Always verify with the client and the accountant which payments are salary and which are dividends.

    Question 11 You've been asked to set up a new QBO file for a construction contractor. Which Ontario-specific accounts should you add that are NOT in QBO's default industry template?

    Answer

    Key additions for Ontario construction:

    • HST Payable and Receivable (QBO handles if sales tax is enabled)
    • Source Deductions Payable (CPP, EI, income tax)
    • WSIB Payable (mandatory for Ontario construction)
    • EHT Payable (if payroll will exceed $1M)
    • Vacation Pay Accrual (common for field labour)
    • Holdbacks Receivable (contract holdbacks owed to the contractor under the Construction Act)
    • Holdbacks Payable (amounts the contractor holds back from subcontractors)
    • Subcontractor Costs (a COGS account for subcontracted work)
    • Tools - Small (for hand tools under the capitalization threshold)
    • Equipment (for capitalized tools and machinery)

    Construction bookkeeping is detailed — and holdbacks in particular are unique to the Construction Act. Missing these sets the client up for major year-end pain.

    Question 12 True or false: A chart of accounts should be finalized on day one and never changed.

    Answer

    False. A chart of accounts is a living document. As the business evolves — new revenue streams, new expense categories, changed reporting needs — the chart should evolve with it.

    That said, changes should be deliberate: - Don't create accounts casually mid-year for one transaction - Plan major restructuring for year-end, so prior year comparisons stay consistent - Inactivate (don't delete) old accounts so historical data is preserved - Coordinate chart changes with the accountant, especially around year-end

    The goal: a chart that serves the business today, adaptable for tomorrow, with clean history preserved throughout.
