    Week 1: How Bookkeeping Works — The Big Picture¶

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    • Explain what bookkeeping is and how it differs from accounting
    • State the accounting equation and explain why it can never be broken
    • Describe every step of the bookkeeping cycle, from source document to financial statement
    • Distinguish between cash-basis and accrual-basis bookkeeping — and know which Ontario businesses use each
    • Explain the bookkeeping differences between a sole proprietor and an incorporated company
    • Describe what a bookkeeper does daily, monthly, and at year-end — and where the job ends and the accountant's begins
    • Identify the core tools every Ontario bookkeeper uses: QuickBooks Online, CRA My Business Account, and source documents

    Why this week is the foundation of everything

    Most bookkeeping courses rush into debits and credits. That's a mistake. Without understanding the system as a whole — why businesses need bookkeeping, what the output is for, and how the pieces connect — the mechanics feel like meaningless rules. This week, you build the mental model. By the end, when you record your first debit in Week 3, you will understand exactly why it's a debit and what it's accomplishing. Every other concept in this course hangs off the framework you build this week.

    1.1 What Bookkeeping Actually Is¶

    Bookkeeping is the systematic recording of every financial event that affects a business, in a way that makes the business's financial story understandable to anyone who needs to read it.

    That's it. That's the whole job.

    But the word systematic is doing enormous work in that sentence. Anyone can scribble down "sold $200 of products today" in a notebook. Bookkeeping requires a structured system — a specific method — that turns scattered transactions into organized data that can answer questions like:

    • How much did we earn last month?
    • How much do customers still owe us?
    • How much HST do we owe CRA this quarter?
    • Can we afford to hire another employee?
    • Is the business actually profitable, or just busy?

    A bookkeeper who records transactions without understanding how they feed into those answers is just a data-entry clerk. A bookkeeper who understands the system is the person a business owner calls before making any financial decision.

    The Bookkeeper's Output¶

    Every day, transactions happen in a business. Sales occur. Bills get paid. Employees get paid. Inventory moves in and out. Equipment wears out.

    Bookkeeping converts all of that activity into three primary outputs:

    1. Financial statements that show the business's profitability and position
    2. Tax filings that satisfy CRA requirements (HST returns, T4s, corporate tax, personal tax schedules)
    3. Decision-making information that the owner uses to run the business

    Everything you learn in this course is ultimately in service of producing those three outputs reliably.

    1.2 Bookkeeping vs. Accounting — Where Your Job Ends¶

    New bookkeepers frequently confuse these two roles. Ontario employers do not. Knowing the boundary precisely will make you sound professional in any interview.

    The Clean Division of Labor¶

    Bookkeeping is the recording of financial transactions as they happen. It is continuous, operational, and detailed.

    Accounting is the interpretation of bookkeeping data — analyzing it, preparing year-end statements, performing tax planning, advising on strategy, and signing off on financial reports.

    Think of it like this: the bookkeeper cooks the meal every day, using the recipes and ingredients the business generates. The accountant is the chef consultant who reviews the dishes at year-end, critiques them, adjusts the recipes for next year, and signs the health inspector's form.

    What a Bookkeeper Does¶

    Frequency Tasks
    Daily Enter sales invoices, record bill payments, code bank transactions, deposit cheques
    Weekly Pay bills, follow up on overdue receivables, run payroll
    Monthly Reconcile bank and credit card accounts, file HST (if monthly), produce P&L and balance sheet, close the month
    Quarterly File HST (if quarterly), remit source deductions (depending on employer type), review AR aging
    Annually Prepare year-end working papers, produce T4s, assist with year-end close, hand off file to accountant

    What an Accountant Does (That a Bookkeeper Doesn't)¶

    • Corporate year-end financial statements with disclosure notes
    • Corporate tax returns (T2)
    • Complex personal tax returns (though some bookkeepers prepare simple T1s)
    • Audits, reviews, and Notice to Reader engagements
    • Tax planning: salary-vs-dividend decisions, corporate structure advice, estate planning
    • CRA audit representation
    • Strategic financial advice and business valuations

    Where the Line Gets Blurry¶

    In small Ontario businesses, the lines blur. A seasoned bookkeeper may prepare the year-end package almost to financial statement level, leaving the accountant only the tax filings and final review. An early-career bookkeeper may do only daily entry under supervision.

    In every case, the bookkeeper should know exactly where their scope ends. When a client asks you a question that crosses into accounting or tax advice, the professional answer is: "That's a question for your accountant — let me note it so we can flag it for them." Answering questions outside your scope is how bookkeepers get sued.

    The scope question to ask yourself before answering any client question

    Am I being asked to record something that has already happened? → Bookkeeper's job.

    Am I being asked to advise on whether to do something, or interpret the tax consequences of something? → Accountant's job. Refer the question.

    1.3 The Accounting Equation — The One Rule That Never Breaks¶

    Every single transaction you will ever record — in every business, in every industry, in every country — must keep this equation in balance:

    ASSETS = LIABILITIES + EQUITY

    That's the foundation. Before we break down what each term means, understand this: if you can explain this equation in plain language, you already understand more bookkeeping theory than most people who do bookkeeping for years.

    What Each Term Means¶

    Assets are what the business owns or is owed.

    Asset Category Examples
    Cash Chequing account, savings account, petty cash
    Accounts Receivable Amounts customers owe the business for invoices issued
    Inventory Products the business has purchased to resell
    Prepaid Expenses Amounts paid in advance (annual insurance, six-month rent)
    Equipment Computers, vehicles, tools, machinery
    Buildings and Land Property the business owns

    Liabilities are what the business owes to others.

    Liability Category Examples
    Accounts Payable Amounts owed to suppliers for bills not yet paid
    HST Payable HST collected from customers, owed to CRA
    Wages Payable Wages earned by employees but not yet paid
    Loans Payable Bank loans, credit lines
    Credit Card Payable Balance owing on business credit cards

    Equity is what the business is worth to its owner(s) — the leftover after liabilities are subtracted from assets.

    Equity Category Examples
    Owner's Capital (sole proprietor) The owner's investment in the business
    Share Capital (corporation) Money shareholders paid for shares
    Retained Earnings Accumulated profits not yet distributed to owners
    Owner's Draws (sole proprietor) Money the owner has taken out for personal use
    Dividends (corporation) Distributions of profit to shareholders

    Why the Equation Must Always Balance¶

    The logic is simple: everything the business owns (assets) must have come from somewhere. Either it was borrowed (liabilities) or it was contributed by the owner or generated as profit (equity). There is no third source.

    Imagine you have $1,000 in your bank account. Where did it come from? Either you borrowed it, or someone gave it to you, or you earned it. Those are the only possibilities. The same is true for every dollar of every asset in every business.

    The Equation Explained with $25,000

    Maria starts a physiotherapy clinic. She deposits $25,000 of personal savings into the business bank account.

    ASSETS           =    LIABILITIES   +    EQUITY
$25,000 (Cash)   =    $0             +    $25,000 (Owner's Capital)

    Now she takes a $15,000 bank loan to buy equipment.

    ASSETS                       =    LIABILITIES          +    EQUITY
$25,000 (Cash)                    $15,000 (Loan)            $25,000 (Capital)
$15,000 (Cash from loan)
= $40,000 total                   = $15,000                 = $25,000

$40,000 = $15,000 + $25,000 ✓

    She buys $12,000 of equipment with some of that cash.

    ASSETS                       =    LIABILITIES          +    EQUITY
$28,000 (Cash)                    $15,000 (Loan)            $25,000 (Capital)
$12,000 (Equipment)
= $40,000 total                   = $15,000                 = $25,000

$40,000 = $15,000 + $25,000 ✓

    Notice: the total didn't change when she bought equipment. Cash went down by $12,000, equipment went up by $12,000. She traded one asset for another. The equation still balances — because she didn't gain or lose net value, she just reorganized what she had.

    This is the mental model. Every transaction you will ever record is a rearrangement of these three categories, and the equation always holds.

    1.4 The Bookkeeping Cycle — From Event to Financial Statement¶

    Bookkeeping isn't a single task — it's a cycle that runs continuously, with every business transaction traveling the same path from the moment it happens to the moment it appears on a financial report.

    Understanding this cycle at a conceptual level is critical because it tells you where you are in the process whenever you're doing any bookkeeping task.

    The Five Stages¶

    1. SOURCE DOCUMENT  →  2. JOURNAL ENTRY  →  3. GENERAL LEDGER  →  4. TRIAL BALANCE  →  5. FINANCIAL STATEMENTS

    Stage 1 — Source Document Every transaction starts with evidence: a sales invoice, a supplier bill, a bank deposit slip, a cheque stub, a payroll register, a cash receipt. This is the proof that the transaction happened. Source documents are the raw material of bookkeeping. Without them, you have nothing to record.

    Ontario employers and CRA require source documents to be kept for at least 6 years after the fiscal year they relate to.

    Stage 2 — Journal Entry The bookkeeper converts the source document into a formal record: a journal entry. This entry identifies which accounts are affected, whether they increased or decreased, and the date of the transaction. Every journal entry has at least two sides (debit and credit) that must balance. This is Week 3's topic — the mechanism you'll master.

    Stage 3 — General Ledger Individual journal entries are posted to the general ledger — the master record of all transactions, organized by account. Every account (Cash, AR, Sales, Rent, etc.) has its own running history. This is Week 4's topic.

    Stage 4 — Trial Balance At the end of a period (usually monthly), the bookkeeper prepares a trial balance: a list of every account and its balance at that moment. The trial balance confirms that total debits equal total credits. If it doesn't balance, there's an error somewhere that must be found before proceeding. Also Week 4.

    Stage 5 — Financial Statements From the trial balance, the bookkeeper prepares the financial statements: the Income Statement (which shows profitability over a period) and the Balance Sheet (which shows financial position at a point in time). These are the primary outputs of the entire process. Week 5's topic.

    Why the Cycle Matters to You Daily¶

    When you sit down to do bookkeeping, every task you perform is at some point in this cycle. Ask yourself: where in the cycle am I right now?

    Task Stage
    Entering invoices in QuickBooks Stage 2 (Journal Entry)
    Filing bank statements Stage 1 (Source Documents)
    Running a P&L report Stage 5 (Financial Statements)
    Checking that debits equal credits in QBO Stage 4 (Trial Balance)
    Looking up when a customer was invoiced Stage 3 (General Ledger)

    When you lose the plot on a task — "wait, why am I doing this?" — return to this cycle. It will always tell you where you are and what comes next.

    1.5 Cash Basis vs. Accrual Basis — How We Record Timing¶

    Here's a question that seems simple but gets to the heart of bookkeeping: when do we record a transaction?

    Two businesses could do the exact same work and have completely different profits, depending on which accounting method they use.

    Cash Basis Accounting¶

    Cash basis records transactions when money actually moves.

    • Revenue is recorded when cash is received.
    • Expenses are recorded when cash is paid.

    Example: You invoice a client for $5,000 on December 15. They pay the invoice on January 20.

    Under cash basis: the $5,000 of revenue is recorded in January — when the cash arrived.

    Accrual Basis Accounting¶

    Accrual basis records transactions when the economic event happens, regardless of when cash moves.

    • Revenue is recorded when it is earned (when the service is performed or the product is delivered).
    • Expenses are recorded when they are incurred (when the benefit is received, even if the bill comes later).

    Example: Same facts. You invoice a client for $5,000 on December 15. They pay on January 20.

    Under accrual basis: the $5,000 of revenue is recorded in December — when you earned it by delivering the service. In January, when the cash arrives, you simply move it from Accounts Receivable to Cash. No new revenue is recorded.

    Why It Matters — Which Method Ontario Businesses Use¶

    Sole proprietors and partnerships are allowed to use cash basis for tax purposes in very limited circumstances (mainly farming and fishing businesses under specific CRA rules). Nearly all other businesses — including all corporations — must use accrual basis.

    The vast majority of Ontario businesses use accrual accounting. This is because:

    • Accrual gives a truer picture of profitability (matching revenues to the expenses that generated them)
    • CRA requires accrual for most businesses
    • Accountants and banks assume accrual when reviewing statements
    • Accrual is what GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles) and ASPE (Accounting Standards for Private Enterprises) require

    This course teaches accrual accounting because that's what you'll use in 98% of Ontario bookkeeping jobs.

    A common new-bookkeeper mistake

    New bookkeepers sometimes record revenue only when the client pays (cash basis thinking) even in an accrual system. This understates revenue in the period earned and messes up the AR aging. In accrual bookkeeping, invoicing creates revenue — the payment that arrives later just moves money from AR to Cash. Master this distinction early.

    1.6 Sole Proprietor vs. Corporation — Ontario Realities¶

    Every Ontario business you bookkeep for is structured as one of two main types: a sole proprietorship (or partnership) or an incorporated company. The bookkeeping differs in specific, important ways.

    Sole Proprietor (and Partnership)¶

    A sole proprietor is an individual running a business under their own name or a registered trade name. Legally, the owner and the business are the same entity.

    Bookkeeping implications:

    • The owner's equity account is called Owner's Capital (or partners' capital for a partnership).
    • When the owner takes money out for personal use, it's a draw (not a salary) — recorded in an Owner's Draws account.
    • The owner does NOT pay themselves a salary through payroll. They take draws.
    • Business income flows to the owner's personal tax return on Schedule T2125 (Statement of Business Activities).
    • Year-end: no separate corporate tax return. The business income appears on the owner's personal T1.

    Corporation (Incorporated Company)¶

    A corporation is a separate legal entity from its owner(s). Shareholders own the corporation; the corporation owns the business.

    Bookkeeping implications:

    • The equity section includes Share Capital (money paid for shares) and Retained Earnings (accumulated profits).
    • The owner, if they work in the business, is an employee — they get a T4 and payroll deductions just like any other employee.
    • The owner can also take money out as dividends (distributions of profit to shareholders) — recorded through Retained Earnings.
    • There's often a Shareholder Loan account tracking money moving between the owner personally and the corporation.
    • Year-end: a separate corporate tax return (T2) is required, with financial statements.

    The Shareholder Loan — The Most Misunderstood Account¶

    In small Ontario corporations, the shareholder loan account tracks money that moves informally between the owner personally and the corporation — the owner lends the company money, or takes money out without a formal dividend or payroll. It's tracked like an IOU between the two parties.

    CRA has strict rules on shareholder loans. If the owner owes money to the corporation at fiscal year-end, and doesn't repay it within one year of the following year-end, the full amount becomes personal taxable income to the owner. This is the source of enormous CRA trouble for small business owners who treat company cash like their own.

    Your job as a bookkeeper: track the shareholder loan account meticulously and flag the balance to the accountant before year-end. You don't advise on the fix — but you must surface the issue.

    Quick Comparison¶

    Feature Sole Proprietor Corporation
    Legal structure Owner = Business Separate entity
    Owner pays themselves Draws Salary (T4) or Dividends
    Equity account Owner's Capital Share Capital + Retained Earnings
    Shareholder loan N/A Common — must be tracked
    Tax return T1 with Schedule T2125 T2 corporate return
    Limited liability No Yes
    Ideal bookkeeper skill level Entry to intermediate Intermediate to senior

    As an entry-level bookkeeper, you'll likely start with sole proprietor clients or simpler corporations. This course teaches both — but emphasizes the corporate bookkeeping that dominates mid-size Ontario small businesses.

    1.7 The Ontario Bookkeeper's Toolkit¶

    Every Ontario bookkeeper uses the same core tools. Get comfortable with them now and you eliminate a learning curve later.

    QuickBooks Online (QBO)¶

    QBO is the accounting software used by roughly 80% of Ontario small businesses. If you can use QBO confidently, you can work almost anywhere.

    What it does: Records all transactions, produces financial statements and reports, handles HST calculations, integrates with bank feeds, produces customer invoices, tracks AR and AP, supports payroll (extra module).

    What you need to know by the end of this course: Setting up a company, entering invoices and bills, recording payments, bank feed categorization, bank reconciliation, running reports, and correcting errors.

    Cost: Plans range from $30 to $100+/month for the business. As a bookkeeper working for an employer, you don't pay — they do. As an independent bookkeeper, you can access a free QuickBooks Online Accountant (QBOA) portal that lets you manage all your clients' QBO files. Week 13 covers the full QBO workflow.

    CRA My Business Account¶

    The CRA's online portal for businesses — used for HST filings, viewing payroll remittances, communicating with CRA, and tracking balances.

    What every bookkeeper needs: A Representative Identifier (RepID) that allows you to access client accounts with their authorization. Apply at the CRA website early in your career — it takes weeks to process and is required for representing clients.

    Source Document Management¶

    Every transaction must be supported by documentation. Modern bookkeepers manage this digitally:

    • Dext (formerly Receipt Bank) — captures receipts via photo or email, extracts data automatically, pushes to QBO
    • Plooto — for electronic payments to suppliers
    • QBO's built-in receipt capture — adequate for many small businesses

    The rule: no source document, no entry. If a client tells you to "just expense $500 for supplies" with no receipt, say no. Without documentation, the expense is not deductible, and CRA will disallow it on audit.

    Banking Access¶

    You don't need bank signing authority — in fact you should not have it (segregation of duties — Week 10 and Week 20 topics). But you do need view-only access to bank accounts so you can reconcile and categorize transactions. Most Canadian banks offer a view-only user role for bookkeepers.

    Excel (or Google Sheets)¶

    Every bookkeeper still uses Excel constantly for: - Working papers and schedules - Reconciliations that QBO doesn't handle well - Payroll tracking - Custom reports

    Your existing admin/Excel background gives you an advantage here — you're already halfway up this curve.

    1.8 Signature Example — Maple Ridge Physiotherapy, Hamilton¶

    Let's apply everything from this week to one real business story.

    Background¶

    Maria Santos, a physiotherapist, has been an employee at a clinic in Hamilton for 8 years. In January 2025 she decides to open her own practice: Maple Ridge Physiotherapy.

    She's doing it as a sole proprietorship to start — incorporating may come later if the business grows.

    Week 1 — What Happens and How We Record It¶

    Day 1 (January 6, 2025): Maria opens a business bank account at RBC and deposits $25,000 from her personal savings.

    Accounting equation perspective: 

    ASSETS              =    LIABILITIES   +    EQUITY
$25,000 (Cash)      =    $0             +    $25,000 (Owner's Capital)

    Source document: Bank deposit slip + confirmation from RBC. Where in the cycle: Stage 1 → Stage 2 (we'll convert this to a journal entry in Week 3).

    Day 3 (January 8): Maria takes a $20,000 bank loan to purchase treatment equipment.

    The money lands in the business bank account.

    Accounting equation: 

    ASSETS              =    LIABILITIES       +    EQUITY
$45,000 (Cash)      =    $20,000 (Loan)    +    $25,000 (Capital)
$45,000             =    $45,000 ✓

    Note: Even though the assets just grew by $20,000, equity did NOT change. The $20,000 came from borrowing, not from owner contribution or earnings. The equation stays perfectly balanced because liabilities grew to match the asset growth.

    Day 5 (January 10): Maria buys treatment tables, electronic therapy equipment, and office furniture for $18,000 total.

    She pays from the business bank account.

    Accounting equation: 

    ASSETS                           =    LIABILITIES       +    EQUITY
$27,000 (Cash)                        $20,000 (Loan)         $25,000 (Capital)
$18,000 (Equipment)
$45,000 total                    =    $20,000                +    $25,000
$45,000 = $45,000 ✓

    Notice: Total assets didn't change. Cash went down $18,000, equipment went up $18,000. She traded one asset for another. This is the most common pattern you'll see — asset substitution leaves both sides of the equation unchanged.

    Day 7 (January 12): Maria signs a lease for clinic space. The lease requires first and last month's rent upfront: $6,000 ($3,000 × 2). She pays from the bank account.

    • January rent: $3,000 — this is an expense for January
    • Last month's rent: $3,000 — this is a prepaid asset (she won't use it until the end of the lease)

    Accounting equation: 

    ASSETS                           =    LIABILITIES       +    EQUITY
$21,000 (Cash)                        $20,000 (Loan)         $25,000 (Capital)
$18,000 (Equipment)                                            ($3,000) (Rent Expense)
$3,000 (Prepaid Rent)
$42,000 total                    =    $20,000                +    $22,000
$42,000 = $42,000 ✓

    Notice: Equity went DOWN by $3,000. When the business incurs an expense, equity decreases. Why? Because expenses reduce the owner's wealth in the business. Maria's capital is now effectively $22,000 — she's spent $3,000 of it running the business.

    This is critical and often missed by beginners: expenses reduce equity. Revenues increase equity. This is how profit ends up on the balance sheet.

    Day 10 (January 15): Maria sees her first 4 patients. Total revenue: $560 + HST of $72.80 = $632.80 collected in cash and Interac.

    Accounting equation: 

    ASSETS                           =    LIABILITIES       +    EQUITY
$21,632.80 (Cash)                     $20,000 (Loan)         $25,000 (Capital)
$18,000 (Equipment)                   $72.80 (HST Payable)    $19,000 (after rent)
$3,000 (Prepaid Rent)                                         + $560 (Revenue)
$42,632.80 total                 =    $20,072.80             +    $22,560
$42,632.80 = $42,632.80 ✓

    What just happened:

    • Cash went up by $632.80 (what she physically collected)
    • HST Payable (liability) went up by $72.80 — because she collected HST on behalf of CRA; she doesn't keep it
    • Revenue (equity) went up by $560 — the actual income she earned

    Notice how HST never touches revenue or equity. HST flows through the business — collected from customers, eventually paid to CRA. It's always a liability on collection and an asset on purchase (ITCs). This is your introduction to HST — we master it in Week 7.

    What This Example Has Shown You¶

    In one week at Maple Ridge Physiotherapy, you've seen:

    • Owner investment (equity goes up)
    • Loans (liabilities go up, assets go up)
    • Asset substitution (assets stay the same, just shift between categories)
    • Prepaid expenses (an asset for future use)
    • Revenue (increases equity)
    • HST (always a liability, separate from revenue)
    • Expenses (decreases equity)

    You've seen every major type of transaction a small Ontario service business encounters in its first week. And every single transaction kept the accounting equation perfectly in balance.

    When you internalize that every transaction must preserve Assets = Liabilities + Equity, you've built the mental model that will support every other week of this course.

    1.9 The Bookkeeper's Daily Life¶

    Before we close Week 1, let's ground the theory in reality. What does a day in an entry-level Ontario bookkeeping job actually look like?

    A Typical Day at an Accounting Firm (Junior Bookkeeper, 2-3 Clients Assigned)¶

    9:00 AM — Open QBO. Check the inbox: three clients have sent receipts for capture. Upload to Dext for auto-processing.

    9:30 AM — Open Client A's QBO. Review yesterday's bank feed imports. Categorize 18 transactions: 11 are obvious (recurring rent, utilities, regular suppliers), 5 need coding decisions (was this dinner a client meeting or office supplies?), 2 need clarification (you email the client with a question).

    10:30 AM — Enter Client B's weekly supplier bills: 6 bills totaling $4,200. Three of them have quantity variances from the POs — you flag these with a note for Client B's approval before payment.

    11:30 AM — Reconcile Client A's credit card statement for the previous month. Statement balance: $3,842.17. Book balance: $3,795.22. Investigate the $46.95 difference.

    12:30 PM — Lunch.

    1:30 PM — Run AR aging report for Client C. Three invoices are 60+ days overdue. Send friendly follow-up emails to those customers on behalf of the client.

    2:30 PM — Client B's HST return is due next week. Run the HST report in QBO, verify the numbers against the sales and purchases reports, prepare the return for the senior bookkeeper to review.

    4:00 PM — Client A calls with a question about whether their new van purchase is deductible and how. You tell them: "That's a great question for your tax accountant — let me note it on the year-end review list so we're sure they know to discuss it with you." Scope discipline.

    4:30 PM — End-of-day cleanup. Save working files. Send daily timesheet entries. Close QBO.

    5:00 PM — Done.

    Key Observations¶

    • The work is detail-oriented but not tedious once you have rhythm.
    • You interact with clients, but you don't advise — you coordinate.
    • Every question has a "where in the cycle am I?" answer.
    • Accuracy matters more than speed. Speed comes from experience; accuracy comes from discipline.
    • The senior bookkeeper or accountant reviews your work initially — this is normal and good.

    As an entry-level bookkeeper, you'll start with one or two clients under supervision. Within 6 months you'll be handling four or five independently. Within two years, you could be supervising new entry-level bookkeepers yourself.

    1.10 Quiz — 10 Questions¶

    Question 1 State the accounting equation.

    Answer

    Assets = Liabilities + Equity

    Every transaction in every business must keep this equation in balance. If it doesn't, there's an error.

    Question 2 A business buys a computer for $2,000 cash. Which parts of the accounting equation are affected, and in what way?

    Answer

    Assets only.

    • Cash (asset) decreases by $2,000
    • Equipment (asset) increases by $2,000
    • Net change in total assets: $0

    This is asset substitution. Liabilities and equity are unaffected. The equation stays balanced because both changes are on the same side.

    Question 3 The owner of a sole proprietorship takes $3,000 from the business bank account for personal use. What type of transaction is this, and what account does it affect?

    Answer

    This is a draw — not a salary. Sole proprietors cannot pay themselves a salary through payroll; they take draws.

    It affects the Owner's Draws account (which reduces equity) and Cash (which reduces assets).

    If this were a corporation, the same transaction would be either a salary (taxable to the employee), a dividend (paid from after-tax profits), or a shareholder loan advance (creating an IOU). Very different treatment.

    Question 4 A client tells you they don't want to do accrual accounting because "cash basis is simpler." You bookkeep for a small Ontario corporation. How do you respond?

    Answer

    Politely but clearly: "For an Ontario corporation, CRA requires accrual accounting. Cash basis is not an option for us. I'll make sure we record transactions when they're earned or incurred — this also gives us much better information about what the business is actually earning each month."

    Cash basis is permitted only in very narrow circumstances (like farming businesses). For virtually all Ontario corporations and most other small businesses, accrual is mandatory.

    Question 5 What is a source document, and why does CRA require them to be kept?

    Answer

    A source document is the original evidence that a transaction occurred — an invoice, a receipt, a cheque stub, a bank statement. Without source documents, bookkeeping entries cannot be verified, and on audit, CRA will disallow expenses or income that lack supporting documentation.

    CRA requires source documents to be kept for 6 years after the fiscal year they relate to. This is non-negotiable for any business.

    Question 6 A client asks you: "Should I buy this new truck as a Class 10 or Class 10.1 CCA asset?" What do you say?

    Answer

    "That's a great tax question — I'll make sure your accountant weighs in on it before you purchase. I'll note it for the year-end review."

    CCA class determination is tax advisory work — accountant scope, not bookkeeper scope. Overstepping here is how bookkeepers get sued when the CCA class turns out to be wrong.

    Question 7 A bookkeeping cycle has five stages. Name them in order.

    Answer
    1. Source Document
    2. Journal Entry
    3. General Ledger
    4. Trial Balance
    5. Financial Statements

    Question 8 A business invoices a customer for $1,000 in December. The customer pays in January. Under accrual accounting, in which month is the revenue recorded?

    Answer

    December — when the service was delivered and the invoice was issued. The revenue is earned in December, not when the cash arrives.

    In January, when the cash is received, no new revenue is recorded. Instead, Accounts Receivable (an asset) goes down by $1,000 and Cash (also an asset) goes up by $1,000 — asset substitution.

    Question 9 What's the difference between a sole proprietor's Owner's Capital account and a corporation's Share Capital account?

    Answer

    Owner's Capital (sole proprietor): Represents the owner's direct investment in the business. The owner and business are legally the same entity. There's one capital account per owner.

    Share Capital (corporation): Represents money shareholders paid to the corporation in exchange for shares. The corporation is a separate legal entity from its shareholders. Share capital is typically set at a small amount ($100 of common shares is typical) and dividends are the primary way profits flow back to shareholders.

    The corporation also maintains a Retained Earnings account separately, which accumulates undistributed profits — a concept that doesn't exist in sole proprietor bookkeeping.

    Question 10 A client in Ontario sells $226 of taxable services, collects $29.38 of HST, and deposits $255.38 in the bank. Break down what happens to each part of the accounting equation.

    Answer
    • Cash (asset) increases by $255.38 (the total deposited)
    • HST Payable (liability) increases by $29.38 (this money belongs to CRA, not the business)
    • Revenue (equity) increases by $226.00 (the actual income the business earned)

    Notice: HST is never revenue. It flows through the business as a liability and will eventually be remitted to CRA. One of the most common errors new bookkeepers make is including HST in revenue — which overstates income and creates problems at year-end.
