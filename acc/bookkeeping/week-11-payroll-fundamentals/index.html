    Week 11: Payroll Fundamentals — CPP, EI, Income Tax, and CRA Remittances¶

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    • Distinguish employees from contractors using CRA's four-factor test
    • Calculate gross pay for salary, hourly, and overtime employees under Ontario's Employment Standards Act
    • Calculate the three statutory deductions (CPP, EI, income tax) for any employee using CRA's payroll tools
    • Calculate the employer's matching contributions and additional costs (CPP, EI, EHT, WSIB)
    • Determine net pay accurately
    • Prepare the payroll journal entry correctly
    • Determine the correct CRA remittance schedule and meet the deadline
    • Complete the PD7A remittance form and submit payment to CRA
    • Understand the personal liability that comes with unremitted source deductions
    • Identify the most common payroll errors and how to prevent them

    Why payroll is the highest-stakes topic in Canadian bookkeeping

    CRA pursues payroll non-compliance more aggressively than any other small business issue. Why? Because source deductions are not the company's money — they're CPP, EI, and income tax withheld from the employee's pay, held in trust for CRA. Late or missing remittances can result in:

    • Penalties of 10% of the unremitted amount (or 20% for repeat offenders)
    • Daily compound interest
    • Director personal liability — corporate directors are personally on the hook for unremitted source deductions even after corporate bankruptcy
    • CRA's collection powers including bank account garnishments

    A bookkeeper who handles payroll cleanly — accurate calculations, on-time remittances, perfect records — is worth their entire salary in avoided penalties. Master this week.

    11.1 The Foundation — Employee vs. Contractor¶

    Before you do any payroll work, you must verify the worker is actually an employee. Misclassifying employees as contractors is the most common — and most expensive — mistake in Canadian small business hiring.

    Why It Matters¶

    If a worker is an employee, the business must: - Withhold CPP, EI, and income tax - Match CPP contributions - Pay 1.4× the employee's EI - Cover WSIB premiums - Pay EHT (if applicable) - Issue T4 slips at year-end - Remit source deductions on schedule

    If a worker is a contractor, the business simply pays the invoice. No deductions. No T4. No remittance.

    The cost difference is substantial. A $50,000 employee actually costs the employer about $56,000-$58,000 (with benefits, employer contributions, WSIB). The same person as a "contractor" appears to cost only $50,000.

    This temptation has led many small businesses to label workers as contractors when they're really employees. CRA aggressively challenges this — and reassessments come with penalties going back years.

    The CRA Four-Factor Test¶

    CRA examines the actual working relationship, not what the parties call it. The four factors:

    Factor 1: Control Who decides when, where, and how the work is done?

    • Employee: The business directs the work. Sets hours. Assigns tasks. Provides instructions.
    • Contractor: Independent. Sets their own schedule. Decides their own methods. Works for multiple clients.

    Factor 2: Tools and Equipment Who provides the tools needed for the work?

    • Employee: The business provides computer, vehicle, equipment, software.
    • Contractor: Provides their own.

    Factor 3: Subcontracting and Helpers Can the worker hire others to do the work?

    • Employee: Cannot subcontract. Must do the work personally.
    • Contractor: Can hire helpers, subcontract portions of the work, run their own crew.

    Factor 4: Financial Risk Does the worker bear business risk and have profit potential?

    • Employee: Receives a fixed wage regardless of business performance. No financial risk.
    • Contractor: Can profit (efficient work) or lose (inefficient work). Bears business risk. May own business assets, carry insurance.

    Applying the Test¶

    CRA looks at the overall relationship — no single factor decides. But the closer the relationship looks to traditional employment (controlled, on-site, full-time, no other clients, business provides everything), the more likely it's classified as employment.

    Common Misclassification Scenarios¶

    Scenario 1: The "Independent Contractor" Office Worker A small business hires "Marcus, IT contractor" who works 9-5 in their office, uses their computers, takes direction from the business owner, and has no other clients. Marcus is an employee, regardless of what the contract says.

    Scenario 2: The Long-Term Sub-Trade A construction company has used the same drywall contractor for 5 years. The contractor only works for them, the company supplies materials, schedules every job. Likely an employee under CRA's view.

    Scenario 3: The Genuine Contractor A bookkeeper services 12 small businesses, sets their own hours, works from home, brings their own laptop, has business insurance, and chooses which clients to take. Genuine contractor.

    The Bookkeeper's Role¶

    When onboarding a new client, ask: - How are workers classified? - Are any "contractors" full-time, on-site, with no other clients?

    If misclassification is likely, flag it for the accountant. A reclassification audit can result in retroactive payroll tax assessments going back several years, with penalties.

    11.2 Gross Pay — The Starting Point¶

    Every payroll calculation starts with gross pay — the amount the employee earns before any deductions.

    Salary Employees¶

    A salaried employee earns a fixed annual amount, paid in regular installments.

    Example: Sarah earns $72,000/year, paid bi-weekly.

    Bi-weekly gross pay = $72,000 ÷ 26 pay periods = $2,769.23

    For salaried employees: - 26 pay periods per year (bi-weekly) - 24 pay periods per year (semi-monthly — 1st and 15th) - 12 pay periods per year (monthly) - 52 pay periods per year (weekly)

    The pay frequency matters because it affects how income tax is calculated (CRA's tables are pay-period specific).

    Hourly Employees¶

    Hourly employees earn a rate per hour worked.

    Example: Marcus earns $22/hour, worked 80 hours in a bi-weekly period.

    Gross pay = 80 hours × $22 = $1,760.00

    Ontario Overtime Rules¶

    Under Ontario's Employment Standards Act (ESA), overtime applies after 44 hours per week (not 40 like some U.S. states).

    Overtime rate: 1.5× the regular hourly rate.

    Example: Marcus worked 50 hours in one week. 

    Regular hours: 44 × $22 = $968.00
Overtime hours: 6 × ($22 × 1.5) = 6 × $33 = $198.00
Total: $1,166.00

    Important Ontario ESA Rules¶

    Beyond overtime, Ontario's ESA sets minimum standards:

    Item Rule
    Minimum wage (general) $17.20/hour as of October 1, 2024 (verify current)
    Minimum wage (students under 18, ≤28 hours/week) $16.20/hour
    Maximum work week 48 hours (with limited exceptions requiring agreement)
    Daily rest At least 11 consecutive hours off between shifts
    Weekly rest At least 24 consecutive hours off per week
    Vacation Minimum 2 weeks (4% pay) under 5 years; 3 weeks (6% pay) at 5+ years
    Public holidays 9 paid holidays per year
    Termination notice 1 week per year of service (up to 8 weeks)

    These rules apply even if the employment contract says otherwise — the ESA sets minimums.

    What's Included in Gross Pay¶

    Gross pay typically includes:

    • Regular wages or salary
    • Overtime pay
    • Vacation pay (when paid out)
    • Bonuses
    • Commissions
    • Statutory holiday pay
    • Taxable benefits (we cover these in Week 12)

    What's NOT Gross Pay¶

    • Reimbursements for business expenses (separate from payroll)
    • Per diem allowances within CRA limits
    • Non-taxable benefits (e.g., employer-paid health premiums in some cases)

    11.3 The Three Statutory Deductions¶

    Every employee paycheque has three mandatory deductions: CPP, EI, and federal/provincial income tax.

    Deduction 1: Canada Pension Plan (CPP)¶

    CPP is Canada's national retirement pension. Both employees and employers contribute.

    For 2025 (verify current rates each year):

    Element Amount
    Maximum pensionable earnings (YMPE) $71,300
    Basic exemption $3,500
    Maximum contributory earnings $67,800 ($71,300 − $3,500)
    Employee contribution rate (CPP) 5.95%
    Employee maximum CPP contribution $4,034.10
    CPP2 (additional contribution above YMPE) 4.00%
    YMPE2 ceiling for CPP2 $81,200
    Maximum CPP2 contribution $396

    Employee CPP calculation:

    Per pay period:
1. Pensionable earnings = Gross pay (excluding non-pensionable items)
2. Subtract pay-period exemption ($3,500 ÷ pay periods per year)
3. Multiply by 5.95%

    Bi-weekly CPP exemption: $3,500 ÷ 26 = $134.62 per pay period

    Example — Sarah, $2,769.23 bi-weekly:

    Pensionable earnings: $2,769.23
Less basic exemption: $134.62
Contributory earnings: $2,634.61
× 5.95% = $156.76 CPP

    CPP2 (additional contribution) only kicks in once an employee crosses the $71,300 YMPE for the year. For most employees, you'll only deal with regular CPP.

    Deduction 2: Employment Insurance (EI)¶

    EI provides temporary income for unemployed workers.

    For 2025:

    Element Amount
    Maximum insurable earnings $65,700
    Employee EI rate 1.64%
    Maximum employee contribution $1,077.48
    Employer EI rate 1.4× employee rate = 2.296%

    Employee EI calculation:

    EI = Insurable earnings × 1.64%

    There's no exemption for EI — it's calculated on every dollar of insurable earnings.

    Example — Sarah, $2,769.23 bi-weekly:

    EI = $2,769.23 × 1.64% = $45.42

    Deduction 3: Income Tax (Federal + Provincial)¶

    Income tax is the most complex deduction because it depends on: - Pay period frequency - Province of employment - The employee's TD1 forms (federal and provincial) - Other adjustments (extra deductions requested, RRSP contributions)

    You don't manually calculate income tax — you use CRA's payroll tools:

    1. CRA Payroll Deductions Online Calculator (PDOC) — free web tool
    2. Payroll Deductions Tables (T4032) — Ontario edition for manual lookup
    3. Payroll software — QuickBooks Payroll, Wagepoint, Ceridian, ADP

    These tools incorporate all current rates and brackets. Manual calculation is error-prone — always use one of the official tools.

    The TD1 Forms¶

    When an employee starts, they complete:

    • TD1 (Federal): Establishes their federal personal tax credits
    • TD1ON (Ontario): Establishes their Ontario tax credits

    These tell the employer how much tax to withhold. If an employee doesn't file TD1 forms, the employer must withhold based on Claim Code 1 (basic personal amount only).

    The TD1 captures: - Basic personal amount - Spouse or common-law partner amount - Eligible dependants - Disability amount - Tuition (transferred from family member) - Other credits

    Putting It Together — Sarah's Bi-Weekly Pay¶

    Sarah, $72,000/year, bi-weekly, basic TD1 (Claim Code 1).

    Using CRA's PDOC for Ontario, bi-weekly pay of $2,769.23:

    Gross pay:                         $2,769.23
CPP contribution:                     156.76
EI contribution:                       45.42
Federal tax:                          307.50  (approximate from PDOC)
Provincial tax (Ontario):             127.95  (approximate from PDOC)
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Total deductions:                     637.63
Net pay:                            $2,131.60

    Note: Income tax amounts above are illustrative — always use PDOC or current tax tables for actual calculations. Tax brackets change annually.

    11.4 The Employer's Additional Costs¶

    Beyond paying the employee's net pay, the employer has additional payroll costs.

    Employer CPP — 1:1 Match¶

    The employer contributes the same amount as the employee.

    For Sarah: 

    Employee CPP: $156.76
Employer CPP: $156.76 (matching)
Total CPP per pay period: $313.52

    Employer EI — 1.4× the Employee Amount¶

    The employer pays 1.4 times what the employee pays.

    For Sarah: 

    Employee EI: $45.42
Employer EI: $45.42 × 1.4 = $63.59
Total EI per pay period: $109.01

    Workplace Safety and Insurance Board (WSIB) — Ontario¶

    WSIB premiums are mandatory for most Ontario employers. Rates vary by industry:

    Industry Type Approximate WSIB Rate (per $100 of payroll)
    Office work, professional services $0.21 - $0.40
    Retail $0.50 - $1.00
    Manufacturing $2.00 - $5.00
    Construction $5.00 - $20.00 (varies widely by trade)

    For Sarah (assume office worker, 0.30%): 

    WSIB = $2,769.23 × 0.30% = $8.31 per pay period

    WSIB is paid by the employer only — never deducted from the employee.

    Employer Health Tax (EHT) — Ontario¶

    EHT applies to Ontario employers with payroll exceeding $1 million. Below that threshold, no EHT.

    For 2025: - Exemption: First $1,000,000 of Ontario payroll (for private-sector employers) - Rate: 1.95% on payroll over $1M (for most private employers) - Some healthcare/charitable organizations have different rules

    For most small businesses (Sarah's employer with payroll well under $1M), no EHT is owed. But the EHT Payable account should still exist on the chart of accounts in case the threshold is crossed.

    Total Employer Cost — Sarah's Bi-Weekly Pay¶

    Gross pay (Sarah's salary):           $2,769.23
+ Employer CPP:                          156.76
+ Employer EI:                            63.59
+ WSIB (assumed 0.30%):                    8.31
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Total employer cost per pay period:   $2,997.89

    Annual cost (× 26): - $72,000 in salary - $77,945 total employer cost

    That's about 8.3% above the salary in employer costs. Construction businesses with high WSIB rates can pay 15-25% above salary in employer costs.

    11.5 The Payroll Journal Entry¶

    There are several ways to structure the payroll entry. Here's the professional standard.

    The Two-Entry Approach¶

    Entry 1 — The Employee's Pay:

    Dr. Wages Expense                   2,769.23   (Sarah's gross pay)
     Cr. CPP Payable                          156.76   (employee deduction)
     Cr. EI Payable                            45.42   (employee deduction)
     Cr. Income Tax Payable                   435.45   (combined federal + provincial)
     Cr. Bank Account                       2,131.60   (Sarah's net pay)
Memo: Bi-weekly payroll - Sarah Chen - period ending Oct 31

    This entry expenses the gross pay, captures the deductions as liabilities (owed to CRA), and pays Sarah her net.

    Entry 2 — The Employer's Costs:

    Dr. Employer CPP Expense              156.76
Dr. Employer EI Expense                63.59
Dr. WSIB Expense                        8.31
     Cr. CPP Payable                          156.76   (employer match)
     Cr. EI Payable                            63.59   (employer 1.4×)
     Cr. WSIB Payable                           8.31
Memo: Employer payroll costs - Sarah Chen - period ending Oct 31

    This entry captures the employer's additional costs as expenses, with corresponding liabilities owed to CRA and WSIB.

    Combined View¶

    After both entries:

    Account Effect
    Wages Expense +$2,769.23
    Employer CPP +$156.76
    Employer EI +$63.59
    WSIB Expense +$8.31
    Total payroll expense +$2,997.89
    CPP Payable (employee + employer) +$313.52
    EI Payable (employee + employer) +$109.01
    Income Tax Payable +$435.45
    WSIB Payable +$8.31
    Total liabilities +$866.29
    Bank Account -$2,131.60 (net pay only)

    The remittance to CRA (covered next) clears the CPP, EI, and Income Tax liabilities.

    11.6 The CRA Remittance — When and How¶

    The source deductions you withheld and the employer's matching contributions must be remitted to CRA on a schedule determined by your remitter type.

    Remittance Schedules¶

    CRA assigns each employer to one of four remitter types based on their average monthly withholding (AMWA):

    Remitter Type AMWA Remittance Frequency Due Date
    Quarterly Less than $1,000 (and certain other criteria) Quarterly 15th of month after quarter
    Regular Less than $25,000 Monthly 15th of following month
    Threshold 1 (Accelerated) $25,000 - $99,999 Twice monthly Various
    Threshold 2 (Accelerated) $100,000+ Up to 4× per month 3 days after pay

    For most small Ontario businesses (Sarah's employer): - AMWA under $25,000 = Regular remitter - October's deductions are due November 15 - November's deductions are due December 15 - And so on

    What Gets Remitted¶

    The remittance includes: - Employee CPP withheld - Employer CPP (matching) - Employee EI withheld - Employer EI (1.4×) - Federal and provincial income tax withheld

    Important: WSIB and EHT are remitted separately, on different schedules, to different agencies. We cover those in Week 12.

    The PD7A Form¶

    CRA sends each employer a PD7A — a payroll remittance form/voucher — for each remittance period. The PD7A is pre-filled with the employer's account information.

    Modern small businesses don't typically use the paper PD7A. They remit: - Online through CRA My Business Account (most common) - Online banking bill payment (CRA is a payee at most banks) - Pre-authorized debit - In person at a financial institution (with the PD7A)

    When using online banking, the bill payment to "CRA Payroll Deductions" requires the 15-character payroll account number (BN + RP0001 suffix).

    Recording the Remittance¶

    When you remit on November 15 for October's payroll:

    Dr. CPP Payable                       313.52
Dr. EI Payable                        109.01
Dr. Income Tax Payable                435.45
     Cr. Bank Account                          857.98
Memo: October source deductions remittance to CRA

    This clears the three CRA liability accounts to zero (assuming this was the only payroll for October).

    If you have multiple pay periods in a month, the liabilities accumulate during the month and clear with one remittance.

    The CRA Remittance Penalty Structure¶

    Late remittances trigger automatic penalties:

    • First-time late or up to 3 days late: 3% of unremitted amount
    • 4-5 days late: 5%
    • 6-7 days late: 7%
    • More than 7 days late or repeat offender: 10%

    After consecutive late remittances within 12 months, penalties can climb to 20%.

    Plus daily compound interest on the unremitted amount.

    These are HARSH penalties. A $5,000 remittance, 10 days late, costs an additional $500 + interest. Multiply across multiple months and the cost compounds quickly.

    Why Late Remittances Are So Costly¶

    The economic logic from CRA's view: source deductions are not the company's money. The company withheld this from employees as their CPP/EI/tax. By being late, the company is effectively borrowing from CRA — and CRA charges a steep penalty for that.

    The legal logic: under the Income Tax Act, directors of a corporation are personally liable for unremitted source deductions. Even after corporate bankruptcy, CRA can pursue directors personally for these amounts. There's no "limited liability" protection for unremitted source deductions.

    This is why payroll on time matters more than almost anything else in small business bookkeeping.

    11.7 Signature Example — Durham Digital Marketing, Oshawa¶

    Let's run a complete bi-weekly payroll for a realistic Ontario small business.

    The Business¶

    Durham Digital Marketing Inc. is a small marketing agency in Oshawa with four employees and one owner-shareholder.

    The owner, Lisa Park, runs the business and pays herself a T4 salary (since the business is incorporated). She has three other employees:

    • Sarah Chen — Senior digital strategist, $72,000/year salary, bi-weekly
    • Marcus Rodriguez — Web developer, $22/hour, hourly
    • Priya Patel — Account manager, $55,000/year, bi-weekly
    • Thomas Lee — Junior copywriter, $18/hour, part-time

    For this pay period (Oct 18-31, 2025):

    • Sarah: 80 hours (full-time salary)
    • Marcus: 80 hours regular + 4 hours overtime
    • Priya: 80 hours (full-time salary)
    • Thomas: 75 hours (no overtime — under 44/week)

    Calculating Each Employee's Gross Pay¶

    Sarah (salary): 

    $72,000 ÷ 26 = $2,769.23

    Marcus (hourly with overtime): 

    Regular: 80 × $22 = $1,760.00
Overtime: 4 × ($22 × 1.5) = 4 × $33 = $132.00
Total: $1,892.00

    Priya (salary): 

    $55,000 ÷ 26 = $2,115.38

    Thomas (hourly, no OT): 

    75 × $18 = $1,350.00

    Calculating CPP for Each Employee¶

    Bi-weekly CPP exemption: $134.62

    Sarah: 

    Pensionable earnings: $2,769.23
- Exemption: $134.62
= Contributory: $2,634.61
× 5.95% = $156.76

    Marcus: 

    $1,892.00 - $134.62 = $1,757.38
× 5.95% = $104.56

    Priya: 

    $2,115.38 - $134.62 = $1,980.76
× 5.95% = $117.86

    Thomas: 

    $1,350.00 - $134.62 = $1,215.38
× 5.95% = $72.32

    Calculating EI for Each Employee¶

    EI = insurable earnings × 1.64%

    Sarah: $2,769.23 × 1.64% = $45.42 Marcus: $1,892.00 × 1.64% = $31.03 Priya: $2,115.38 × 1.64% = $34.69 Thomas: $1,350.00 × 1.64% = $22.14

    Calculating Income Tax (PDOC)¶

    Using CRA's PDOC for Ontario, bi-weekly pay, Claim Code 1 (basic TD1 — illustrative amounts):

    Employee Gross Federal Tax Provincial Tax Total Income Tax
    Sarah $2,769.23 $307.50 $127.95 $435.45
    Marcus $1,892.00 $158.20 $69.85 $228.05
    Priya $2,115.38 $193.10 $84.20 $277.30
    Thomas $1,350.00 $89.55 $42.10 $131.65

    These are illustrative amounts — always use PDOC for actual current calculations.

    Net Pay Calculation¶

    Employee Gross CPP EI Tax Total Ded Net Pay
    Sarah $2,769.23 $156.76 $45.42 $435.45 $637.63 $2,131.60
    Marcus $1,892.00 $104.56 $31.03 $228.05 $363.64 $1,528.36
    Priya $2,115.38 $117.86 $34.69 $277.30 $429.85 $1,685.53
    Thomas $1,350.00 $72.32 $22.14 $131.65 $226.11 $1,123.89
    Totals $8,126.61 $451.50 $133.28 $1,072.45 $1,657.23 $6,469.38

    The Employer's Side¶

    Employer CPP (1:1 match): $451.50 Employer EI (1.4× employee): $133.28 × 1.4 = $186.59

    WSIB (assume 0.45% — marketing/professional services): $8,126.61 × 0.45% = $36.57

    EHT: Annual payroll well under $1M — none owed.

    Total employer cost this pay period: 

    Gross wages:              $8,126.61
+ Employer CPP:               451.50
+ Employer EI:                186.59
+ WSIB:                        36.57
────────────────────────────────────
Total:                    $8,801.27

    The Journal Entries¶

    Entry 1 — Employee Pay (combined for all four):

    Oct 31    Wages Expense                     8,126.61
               CPP Payable                                451.50
               EI Payable                                 133.28
               Income Tax Payable                       1,072.45
               Bank Account                             6,469.38
          Memo: Bi-weekly payroll Oct 18-31 (4 employees)

    Entry 2 — Employer Costs:

    Oct 31    Employer CPP Expense                451.50
          Employer EI Expense                  186.59
          WSIB Expense                          36.57
               CPP Payable                                451.50
               EI Payable                                 186.59
               WSIB Payable                                36.57
          Memo: Employer payroll costs Oct 18-31

    After both entries posted:

    • CPP Payable balance: $903.00 (employee + employer)
    • EI Payable balance: $319.87 (employee + employer)
    • Income Tax Payable balance: $1,072.45
    • Total to remit to CRA: $2,295.32
    • WSIB Payable: $36.57 (separate, remitted to WSIB)

    The Remittance¶

    If Durham Digital is a Regular remitter (AMWA under $25,000), the October remittance is due November 15.

    Nov 15    CPP Payable                         903.00
          EI Payable                          319.87
          Income Tax Payable                1,072.45
               Bank Account                            2,295.32
          Memo: October source deductions remittance to CRA

    If there were also a pay period earlier in October, both pay periods' withholdings would accumulate in the liability accounts and remit together.

    WSIB is remitted on its own schedule (typically quarterly or annually for small employers) directly to WSIB — not CRA.

    11.8 The TD1 Form — In Detail¶

    When an employee starts, they complete two TD1 forms — one federal, one provincial.

    What the TD1 Captures¶

    The TD1 lists tax credits the employee is entitled to. The total of these credits determines the claim amount — the income tax-free threshold.

    Credit Line What It Is
    Basic personal amount The base amount everyone gets — $15,705 federal (2025), $12,747 ON (2025)
    Age amount If 65 or older with income under threshold
    Pension income amount If receiving eligible pension income
    Spouse/common-law partner amount Supporting a partner with low income
    Eligible dependant Single parent with qualifying dependant
    Disability amount Self or dependant
    Tuition (transferred) Tuition transferred from a child or spouse

    The employee claims credits they're entitled to. The total becomes their claim amount.

    Claim Codes¶

    CRA's payroll tables use Claim Codes that correspond to ranges of total claim amounts:

    • Claim Code 0: No claim (additional tax requested)
    • Claim Code 1: Basic personal amount only ($15,705 federal in 2025)
    • Claim Codes 2-10: Higher claim amounts as more credits are added
    • Claim Code X: Custom claim amount specified

    When you enter an employee in payroll software, you input their claim code. PDOC and tax tables use this code to determine income tax to withhold.

    What If an Employee Doesn't Submit TD1s?¶

    The employer must withhold tax based on Claim Code 1 (basic personal amount only). This is the most conservative — it withholds the most tax. The employee can update later if they're entitled to more credits.

    Updating TD1s Throughout Employment¶

    Employees should update their TD1 when their situation changes: - Marriage or common-law relationship - Child born or becoming a dependant - Student starting eligible studies - Disability status change

    The employer should keep the most recent TD1 on file.

    The Bookkeeper's Responsibility¶

    For each new hire: 1. Provide federal and provincial TD1 forms (or direct them to CRA's website) 2. Receive completed forms before first pay 3. Enter the claim code (or claim amount) in payroll 4. File the TD1 in the employee's payroll file 5. Update if the employee submits a revised TD1

    You don't advise on what credits to claim — that's a tax question. But you ensure the form is completed and applied correctly.

    11.9 Common Payroll Errors and How to Prevent Them¶

    Error 1 — Wrong Pay Period Frequency¶

    Setting up an employee as monthly when they're paid bi-weekly produces wrong tax calculations.

    Prevention: Verify pay frequency at setup. PDOC requires you to specify it.

    Error 2 — Forgetting CPP Exemption¶

    The basic exemption ($3,500/year, $134.62 bi-weekly) is often forgotten. Result: over-deducting CPP.

    Prevention: Use PDOC or payroll software which applies the exemption automatically.

    Error 3 — Missing Overtime¶

    Hourly employees who worked >44 hours in a week but were paid at regular rate.

    Prevention: Track weekly hours separately. Ontario's 44-hour threshold is per week, not per pay period.

    Error 4 — Not Remitting on Time¶

    Late remittances trigger automatic penalties.

    Prevention: Set calendar reminders. CRA's "Pay later" online doesn't extend deadlines.

    Error 5 — Treating Contractors as Employees (or Vice Versa)¶

    Misclassification leads to retroactive reassessment.

    Prevention: Apply CRA's four-factor test. Document the analysis. Flag uncertain cases for the accountant.

    Error 6 — Wrong WSIB Rate¶

    Using a generic rate instead of the rate assigned by WSIB based on actual industry classification.

    Prevention: Get the WSIB rate from the actual WSIB account confirmation. Industry classifications are specific.

    Error 7 — Including Non-Pensionable/Non-Insurable Items¶

    Some payments aren't subject to CPP or EI (severance pay treatment, certain benefits).

    Prevention: Consult CRA's T4001 Employers' Guide for complex situations. Or use payroll software that handles these distinctions.

    Error 8 — Forgetting Vacation Pay Accrual¶

    Ontario requires vacation pay (4% or 6% of wages). If paid out as taken (not as part of every paycheque), it must be accrued.

    Prevention: Include vacation pay accrual in the payroll process. We cover this in Week 12.

    Error 9 — Missing Statutory Holidays¶

    Ontario has 9 statutory holidays. Employees are entitled to "stat pay" calculated as the average of regular wages over the prior 4 weeks.

    Prevention: Track stat holidays in the payroll calendar. Calculate stat pay correctly.

    Error 10 — Source Deductions Not Trust Funds¶

    Some businesses treat CRA remittances as "spendable" cash, using payroll withholdings for operating expenses temporarily. This is the most dangerous payroll error.

    Prevention: Treat source deductions as trust funds. Set up a separate account if needed. Never use them for anything else.

    11.10 Payroll Software vs. Manual Calculation¶

    Most professional bookkeepers use payroll software rather than calculating manually.

    Manual Calculation (CRA PDOC)¶

    When to use: - Small business, 1-3 employees - Simple pay structures (no benefits, complex deductions) - Owner-shareholder paying themselves only

    How: 1. Go to canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/services/e-services/payroll-deductions-online-calculator-pdoc.html 2. Select province (Ontario), pay frequency, employee details 3. Enter gross pay 4. PDOC calculates CPP, EI, tax 5. Manually create journal entry in QBO

    Pros: Free, no subscription Cons: Time-consuming, error-prone, no T4 generation, no record-keeping

    Payroll Software¶

    Common Canadian payroll providers:

    • QuickBooks Online Payroll — integrates with QBO bookkeeping
    • Wagepoint — popular Canadian solution
    • Ceridian Powerpay — full-service
    • ADP — enterprise-level
    • Wave Payroll — simple/free for very small businesses
    • PaymentEvolution — Canadian, basic plans free

    What they do: - Calculate CPP, EI, tax automatically - Direct deposit to employee accounts - Generate pay stubs - Track vacation, stat holidays, sick days - Calculate ROEs (Records of Employment) - Generate T4s at year-end - File T4 Summary with CRA - Track WSIB and remittances - Generate remittance vouchers

    Cost: Typically $20-50/month per employee, depending on features.

    When to Recommend Software¶

    For any business with 3+ employees, payroll software is worth the cost. The time saved alone justifies the subscription. Beyond that, it dramatically reduces errors and audit risk.

    For a 1-employee company (owner only), manual calculation through PDOC may be adequate.

    The bookkeeper's role: know which solution the client uses, ensure it's set up correctly, and reconcile its output to the books.

    11.11 Quiz — 15 Questions¶

    Question 1 Apply CRA's four-factor test to: A worker comes to the office Monday-Friday 9-5, uses the company's computer, takes direction from the manager, has worked there for 3 years, and has no other clients. Are they an employee or a contractor?

    Answer

    Employee. Despite any "contractor" labels: - Control: Strong employer control (set hours, set location) - Tools: Provided by employer - Subcontracting: Cannot substitute (must do work personally) - Risk: No business risk; receives wage regardless

    All four factors point to employment. CRA would reclassify and reassess back payroll taxes.

    Question 2 What's Ontario's overtime threshold under the ESA?

    Answer

    44 hours per week. Overtime rate is 1.5× the regular hourly rate for hours worked beyond 44 in a single week.

    Note: This differs from many U.S. states (40 hours) — Ontario uses 44.

    Question 3 Marcus earns $25/hour. In one week he works 50 hours. Calculate his gross pay.

    Answer 
    Regular hours: 44 × $25 = $1,100.00
Overtime hours: 6 × ($25 × 1.5) = 6 × $37.50 = $225.00
Total gross: $1,325.00

    Question 4 For 2025, what's the basic CPP exemption per pay period for a bi-weekly employee?

    Answer

    $134.62 ($3,500 annual ÷ 26 bi-weekly periods).

    This amount is exempt from CPP contribution. Only earnings above this threshold are subject to CPP.

    Question 5 Calculate the employee CPP contribution for an employee earning $2,500 bi-weekly.

    Answer 
    Pensionable earnings: $2,500.00
- Bi-weekly exemption: $134.62
= Contributory earnings: $2,365.38
× 5.95% = $140.74 CPP

    Question 6 What's the employer's EI contribution rate?

    Answer

    1.4× the employee's EI contribution.

    If the employee EI rate is 1.64%, the employer rate is 1.4 × 1.64% = 2.296% of insurable earnings.

    Per pay period: if employee EI is $40, employer EI is $40 × 1.4 = $56.

    Question 7 A new employee hasn't submitted TD1 forms. How much income tax do you withhold?

    Answer

    Use Claim Code 1 (basic personal amount only — federally and provincially).

    This is the most conservative withholding. The employee can update later if they're entitled to more credits.

    Question 8 A small Ontario corporation has annual payroll of $620,000. Does it owe EHT?

    Answer

    No. EHT applies only when Ontario payroll exceeds $1 million for private-sector employers. At $620,000, the business is well below the threshold.

    However, the EHT Payable account should still exist on the chart of accounts in case payroll grows past $1M.

    Question 9 Is WSIB an employee deduction or an employer cost?

    Answer

    Employer cost only. WSIB is paid entirely by the employer — never deducted from the employee's pay. Rates vary by industry and can range from 0.21% to over 20% of payroll.

    Question 10 For a Regular remitter, when is October's source deductions remittance due?

    Answer

    November 15. Regular remitters (AMWA under $25,000) remit by the 15th of the month following the payroll period.

    For October's payroll deductions, the deadline is November 15.

    Question 11 What's the penalty for a remittance that's 8 days late?

    Answer

    10% of the unremitted amount (for Regular remitters who are more than 7 days late).

    This is on top of daily compound interest.

    For a $5,000 remittance, 8 days late = $500 penalty + interest. Late remittances are extremely costly.

    Question 12 Why are unremitted source deductions a particular problem for corporate directors?

    Answer

    Personal liability. Under the Income Tax Act, directors of a corporation are personally liable for unremitted source deductions.

    Even after corporate bankruptcy, CRA can pursue the directors personally. Limited liability protection that exists for most corporate debts does NOT apply to source deductions.

    This is why on-time remittance is non-negotiable — even if the company is struggling.

    Question 13 Write the journal entry for an employee earning $2,000 gross with $120 CPP, $33 EI, and $250 income tax withheld.

    Answer 
    Dr. Wages Expense              2,000.00
     Cr. CPP Payable                        120.00
     Cr. EI Payable                          33.00
     Cr. Income Tax Payable                 250.00
     Cr. Bank Account                     1,597.00
Memo: Bi-weekly payroll - [Employee name]

    Question 14 For the same employee, write the journal entry for the employer's matching contributions (assume WSIB rate 0.5%).

    Answer 
    Dr. Employer CPP Expense       120.00     (1:1 match)
Dr. Employer EI Expense         46.20     (33 × 1.4)
Dr. WSIB Expense                10.00     (2,000 × 0.5%)
     Cr. CPP Payable                        120.00
     Cr. EI Payable                          46.20
     Cr. WSIB Payable                        10.00
Memo: Employer payroll costs - [Employee name]

    Question 15 A client says "I'd rather pay my workers as contractors to avoid all this payroll hassle." How do you respond?

    Answer

    Cautiously and clearly:

    "I understand the appeal, but the classification depends on the actual working relationship — not what we call it. CRA applies a four-factor test (control, tools, subcontracting, financial risk). If the workers fail that test, CRA can reclassify them as employees retroactively, and the company is liable for unpaid payroll taxes plus penalties going back several years.

    Misclassification is one of CRA's top audit areas for small businesses. The risk often exceeds the apparent savings. Let's work with your accountant to evaluate whether each worker genuinely qualifies as a contractor — and document the analysis. For workers who don't qualify, proper payroll setup protects you from a much bigger problem later."

    Don't help a client misclassify employees. The legal and financial consequences are severe.
