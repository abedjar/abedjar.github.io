    Week 6: Phase 1 Capstone — The Full Accounting Cycle¶

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    • Take a new Ontario business from zero to complete financial statements, entirely on your own
    • Build a chart of accounts tailored to the business
    • Record a full month of transactions as journal entries
    • Post entries to the general ledger
    • Prepare an accurate trial balance
    • Produce a complete Income Statement and Balance Sheet
    • Self-diagnose errors at any stage of the cycle
    • Know whether you're ready to move on to Phase 2

    Why this week is the real test

    Phase 1 has given you every piece of the foundation. This week pulls them together. No new concepts. Just the full cycle — the way every month of real bookkeeping works. If you can complete this week's simulation without prompts, you are genuinely ready for Phase 2 — the real daily work. If you struggle, the struggle tells you exactly where to review. This is where self-assessment becomes possible.

    6.1 How This Week Works¶

    Most weeks in this course introduce new material. Week 6 is different — it's a working exercise.

    The structure:

    • Section 6.2 — The simulation brief. You're given the story: a new Ontario business opens, 30 transactions happen over one month, and you need to produce the financial statements.
    • Section 6.3 — Your work. You attempt the full cycle on your own. Build the chart of accounts. Record journal entries. Produce the trial balance. Produce the statements.
    • Section 6.4 — The detailed walkthrough. The complete answer key. Every journal entry explained, the trial balance shown, the statements prepared with reasoning.
    • Section 6.5 — The self-diagnostic. Questions to ask yourself about where you struggled — and what that tells you about what to review.
    • Section 6.6 — The Phase 1 quiz. 15 questions integrating everything from Weeks 1-5.

    Suggested approach:

    1. Read Section 6.2 carefully. Understand the business.
    2. Attempt Section 6.3 yourself before reading any further. Use a spreadsheet, notebook, or blank QBO file. Block out 3-4 hours.
    3. Only after your attempt, compare to Section 6.4.
    4. Use Section 6.5 honestly. Don't defend; diagnose.
    5. Complete Section 6.6's quiz.

    6.2 The Simulation Brief — Thornbury Café, Collingwood¶

    The Business¶

    Thornbury Café is opening in January 2025 in Collingwood, Ontario. The owner is Sarah McPherson, a former marketing professional who's always dreamed of owning a coffee shop.

    The business is structured as a sole proprietorship. Sarah has registered "Thornbury Café" as a business name in Ontario. She'll eventually consider incorporating, but for year one she's going simple.

    The café will: - Serve coffee, tea, pastries, and light lunches (sandwiches and salads) - Sell branded merchandise (mugs, t-shirts) - Operate 7 days a week, 7am to 5pm - Employ Sarah plus one part-time employee (Liam) who works 20 hours/week

    Business Particulars¶

    • HST registration: Registered from day one (Sarah anticipates exceeding the $30,000 threshold quickly)
    • HST filing frequency: Quarterly (based on CRA's default for new registrants)
    • Banking: TD Business Chequing, TD Business Visa
    • Accounting software: QuickBooks Online Essentials (she'll start with Simple Start but you recommend Essentials for proper sales tax handling)

    Your Task¶

    Sarah has hired you as her bookkeeper. She'll handle daily POS (point of sale) and bring you her documents at month-end. Your job: take the 30 transactions from January 2025 and produce a complete set of books, including:

    1. A chart of accounts tailored to Thornbury Café
    2. All journal entries for January
    3. A trial balance as at January 31, 2025
    4. An Income Statement for January
    5. A Balance Sheet as at January 31, 2025

    Adjustment C — Cost of Goods Sold (Periodic Method)

    At month-end, we need to calculate COGS. Using the periodic method:

    Beginning Inventory  +  Purchases  =  Available for Sale
Available for Sale − Ending Inventory = COGS

    For coffee/beverages: - Beginning: $0 (new business) - Purchases: $2,800 (Entry 8) + $850 (Entry 17) = $3,650 - Ending (physical count): $1,850 - COGS — Coffee: $3,650 − $1,850 = $1,800

    For pastries: - Beginning: $0 - Purchases: $1,600 + $1,200 = $2,800 - Ending: $150 - COGS — Pastries: $2,650

    For merchandise: - Beginning: $0 - Purchases: $1,500 - Ending: $980 - COGS — Merchandise: $520

    For supplies (treated as consumables, expensed as used): - Purchases: $420 - Assume all consumed in month (simpler; adjusting policy going forward if needed) - COGS — Supplies: $420

    In practice, the bookkeeper would re-trace every entry, check each ledger balance, and find the mismatch. The typical approach: 1. Total the debits on the trial balance — confirm they match the sum of asset and expense accounts plus owner's draws 2. Total the credits — confirm they match the sum of liability, equity (capital, retained earnings), and revenue accounts 3. Find any transaction that was posted on only one side 4. Find any transaction where the debit and credit amounts don't match 5. Correct and re-verify

    6.6 Phase 1 Quiz — 15 Questions¶

    This quiz integrates everything from Weeks 1-5. A score of 12/15 or better indicates Phase 1 mastery.

    Question 1 A business buys a $3,000 laptop with cash. Write the journal entry.

    Answer 
    Dr. Equipment (or Computer Equipment)     3,000
     Cr. Cash                                    3,000

    Assuming no HST context in the question. If HST was paid separately: 

    Dr. Equipment                              3,000
Dr. HST Receivable (ITC)                     390
     Cr. Cash                                    3,390

    Question 2 A sole proprietor owner takes $2,000 for personal use. What account do you debit?

    Answer

    Owner's Draws — a contra-equity account. It is NOT an expense. It reduces the owner's equity in the business.

    Question 3 State the accounting equation.

    Answer

    Assets = Liabilities + Equity

    Question 4 A customer's $850 cheque bounces (NSF). The original sale included $850 + $110.50 HST, paid by that cheque. Write the entry to reverse the receipt.

    Answer 
    Dr. Accounts Receivable     960.50
     Cr. Cash                         960.50

    We reverse the original receipt. The original AR had been cleared when the cheque was received; now we must put it back. HST Payable is NOT reversed — the supply still occurred; the HST is still owed to CRA. If the bank also charged an NSF fee, that's a separate entry as a bank charge expense.

    Question 5 What is the normal balance of each? (a) Prepaid Insurance (b) HST Payable (c) Retained Earnings (d) Depreciation Expense

    Answer

    (a) Prepaid Insurance — Debit (asset) (b) HST Payable — Credit (liability) (c) Retained Earnings — Credit (equity) (d) Depreciation Expense — Debit (expense)

    Question 6 A business pays $2,400 for a 12-month insurance policy upfront. What's the correct entry?

    Answer 
    Dr. Prepaid Insurance       2,400
     Cr. Cash                       2,400

    The full $2,400 is an asset (Prepaid Insurance) at purchase. Each month thereafter, $200 is expensed: 

    Dr. Insurance Expense         200
     Cr. Prepaid Insurance          200

    Question 7 Your trial balance shows debits of $87,450 and credits of $87,360. The difference is $90. What diagnostic test applies?

    Answer

    $90 is divisible by 9, suggesting a transposition error — digits were reversed somewhere (e.g., $54 entered as $45, or $180 entered as $810).

    Scan the trial balance for any amount that might be the reversed form of a different amount. Common transpositions between $90 apart include: - $54 vs. $45 - $18 vs. $81 - $180 vs. $810 - $54.00 vs. $45.00 (if decimals)

    Question 8 A client has Net Income of $28,000 but her bank account only grew by $5,000. Name three possible reasons.

    Answer

    Several valid answers:

    1. Accounts Receivable grew — customers invoiced but haven't paid
    2. Inventory purchased — cash spent on stock, shown as asset not expense
    3. Equipment purchased — capital assets, not expensed
    4. Principal payments on loans — reduce cash but don't affect profit
    5. Owner's draws or dividends — cash out, doesn't touch Income Statement
    6. Prepaid expenses — cash paid in advance for future benefit

    And reverse factors: - Depreciation was deducted from profit but didn't reduce cash - AP grew (expenses recorded, bills not paid) — profit lower than cash drop

    Question 9 An error of principle vs. an error of commission — what's the difference?

    Answer

    Error of commission: Transaction recorded to the wrong account, but correct type (e.g., coded to "Utilities" when it should have been "Telephone & Internet" — both are expenses). Trial balance still balances.

    Error of principle: Transaction recorded to the wrong TYPE of account (e.g., capital equipment coded as office supplies — an asset recorded as an expense). Trial balance still balances, but balance sheet and income statement are materially distorted.

    Error of principle is much more serious.

    Question 10 A business incorporated as an Ontario corporation has its owner working as an employee. The owner earns $5,000/month gross. Which account on the chart of accounts captures this cost?

    Answer

    Salary Expense (or "Salary - Owner") — because the owner-employee is paid through payroll, the corporation records:

    Dr. Salary Expense          5,000
     Cr. Source Deductions Payable       X
     Cr. Cash                           Y

    Where X is the CPP + EI + income tax withheld, and Y is the net pay.

    This is different from a sole proprietor, who would use "Owner's Draws" when taking money out (no payroll, no source deductions).

    Question 11 What's the difference between Cash Basis and Accrual Basis accounting? Which do most Ontario businesses use?

    Answer

    Cash Basis: Revenue recorded when cash is received. Expenses recorded when cash is paid. Simple but distorts timing.

    Accrual Basis: Revenue recorded when earned (when service is delivered or product shipped). Expenses recorded when incurred (when the benefit is received). Reflects economic reality.

    Most Ontario businesses use accrual. Required by CRA for corporations and most businesses. Cash basis is permitted only in limited cases (farming/fishing businesses, some very small operations).

    Question 12 A business buys $900 of office supplies on credit. Write the journal entry with HST.

    Answer

    $900 + $117 HST = $1,017 total on account.

    Dr. Office Supplies         900.00
Dr. HST Receivable (ITC)    117.00
     Cr. Accounts Payable           1,017.00

    Note: We do NOT debit Cash because the purchase was made on account (credit purchase). Later, when the invoice is paid: 

    Dr. Accounts Payable      1,017.00
     Cr. Cash                       1,017.00

    Question 13 A business records this journal entry:

    Dr. Rent Expense             3,000
     Cr. Cash                        3,000

    The actual bill was $3,000 + HST of $390. What's wrong?

    Answer

    The HST Receivable (ITC) is missing. The correct entry should be:

    Dr. Rent Expense          3,000
Dr. HST Receivable         390
     Cr. Cash                      3,390

    By not recording the HST, the business has: - Overstated Rent Expense by $390 (if they recorded the full $3,390 to rent) OR - Credited Cash incorrectly (if they only recorded $3,000 when $3,390 left the bank)

    Either way, the books are wrong and a $390 ITC is lost unless corrected.

    Question 14 The Balance Sheet shows Total Assets = $142,500 but Total Liabilities + Equity = $141,800. What does this tell you?

    Answer

    The Balance Sheet is out of balance by $700. This should not happen in QBO — the software enforces it. Likely causes:

    1. An opening balance or data migration issue
    2. A manual journal entry that was entered with unequal debits and credits (and somehow accepted)
    3. An account was inappropriately classified on one statement but differently on the other
    4. A deleted account left a residual balance somewhere

    Action: Do not publish. Investigate by: - Running the trial balance — does it balance? - If trial balance balances, the issue is in how accounts are classified on the Balance Sheet - Compare the Balance Sheet to the trial balance account-by-account to find the discrepancy

    Question 15 You're reviewing an Ontario small business P&L. Revenue is $280,000. Gross Profit is $165,000. Total Operating Expenses are $148,000. Interest Expense is $4,200. Depreciation is $8,000. Calculate:

    (a) Gross Profit Margin (b) Operating Income (c) Net Income (ignoring tax)

    Answer

    (a) Gross Profit Margin = $165,000 / $280,000 = 58.9%

    (b) Operating Income = Gross Profit − Operating Expenses = $165,000 − $148,000 = $17,000

    (c) Net Income = Operating Income − Interest − Depreciation = $17,000 − $4,200 − $8,000 = $4,800

    A 58.9% gross margin is reasonable for a service business. But with only $4,800 net income on $280,000 revenue (1.7% net margin), this is a struggling business — or one with significant owner compensation built into operating expenses that makes the "net" look small. The owner should review whether the business is viable at this scale.
