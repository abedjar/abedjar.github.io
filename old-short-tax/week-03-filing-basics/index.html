    Week 3: Filing Basics — Deadlines, Residency, SINs & What Happens After You File¶

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    • Identify who must file a return and who should file even when not required
    • Explain residency rules and how they affect a client's return
    • Know every key filing deadline and the exact cost of missing them
    • Understand what happens between the moment you submit and the NOA arriving
    • Handle SIN-related issues confidently

    3.1 Who Has to File a Tax Return?¶

    Not everyone is legally required to file — but many people should file even when they don't have to. As a preparer, you need to know the difference.

    Required to File¶

    A Canadian resident must file a T1 if any of the following apply:

    Situation Why
    They owe tax to the CRA Balance owing must be reported and paid
    They sold capital property (e.g. stocks, real estate) Must report disposition regardless of gain or loss
    They have a capital gains exemption to claim Must file to claim it
    They received a demand to file from CRA Legal obligation to comply
    They received Working Income Tax Benefit (WITB/CWB) advance payments Must reconcile
    They disposed of their principal residence Must report even if fully exempt
    They have self-employment income with CPP obligations CPP on self-employment must be reported
    They have an amount to repay (HBP or LLP) Must report repayments or they're added to income

    Should File (Even If Not Required)¶

    This is a huge value-add you offer clients

    Many people — especially low-income earners, students, and newcomers — don't realize they're leaving money on the table by not filing. You will find these clients and fix this for them.

    Reason to File Anyway Benefit
    Earned any income Builds RRSP contribution room
    Low or no income Qualifies for GST/HST credit (up to ~$500/year)
    Has children Triggers Canada Child Benefit (CCB)
    Age 19+ with low income Canada Workers Benefit (CWB)
    Paid tuition Carries forward unused tuition credits
    Had tax withheld at source Get a refund of over-withheld amounts
    Ontario resident with low income Ontario Trillium Benefit

    3.2 Residency — The Most Misunderstood Concept in Canadian Tax¶

    Residency determines whether Canadian tax law applies to someone and how much of their income is taxable in Canada. Get this wrong and the entire return is wrong.

    The Four Residency Statuses¶

    Who: Lived in Canada all of January 1 through December 31.

    Tax treatment: Reports and pays tax on worldwide income — every dollar earned anywhere on earth.

    Forms: Standard T1, full-year provincial tax.

    Example: Priya lives in Toronto, works for a Canadian company, has a bank account in India earning interest. She must report both her Canadian employment income and her Indian interest income on her T1.

    Worldwide income — no exceptions

    Full-year residents often forget or deliberately omit foreign income. CRA has information-sharing agreements with dozens of countries. Foreign income that isn't reported will eventually be found.

    Who: Either arrived in Canada during the year (newcomer, returning Canadian) or left Canada during the year (emigrant).

    Tax treatment:

    • Reports Canadian and worldwide income only for the period they were resident
    • For the non-resident period, only Canadian-source income is taxable

    Key line: Line 10100 still captures employment income, but the period covered matters. A notation is required indicating the date of entry or departure.

    Example — Arrival: Carlos moved from Brazil to Vancouver on July 1. He worked in Brazil Jan–June (earning the equivalent of $30,000 CAD) and in Canada July–December (earning $42,000).

    • Canadian period income: $42,000 → fully taxable in Canada
    • Brazilian period income: $30,000 → not taxable in Canada
    • Carlos files a T1 as a part-year resident and only reports the $42,000

    Example — Departure: Linda moves to the UK permanently on March 31. She earned $18,000 in Canada before leaving.

    • Reports $18,000 on her T1
    • May have a deemed disposition of assets on departure date (more on this in advanced topics)
    • Claims credits pro-rated based on days resident (91 days ÷ 365 = 24.9%)

    Pro-ration of personal credits for part-year residents

    Non-refundable credits like the Basic Personal Amount are pro-rated by the number of days the person was resident in Canada. This catches many preparers off guard.

    Who: Lives outside Canada but earned Canadian-source income (rental income from a Canadian property, employment performed in Canada, etc.)

    Tax treatment: Only pays Canadian tax on Canadian-source income.

    Forms: Not a standard T1 — requires a T1 Non-Resident or Section 216/217 election depending on income type.

    Non-residents are a specialty area

    Non-resident returns are complex and beyond the scope of your first tax season. Refer these to a specialist until you're comfortable with the rules.

    Who: Doesn't meet the standard residency test but has strong enough ties to Canada that CRA treats them as a resident (e.g., government employees abroad, military personnel).

    Also: The 183-day rule — a person who is NOT ordinarily a Canadian resident but spends 183 or more days in Canada in a calendar year may be deemed a resident.

    Tax treatment: Same as full-year resident — worldwide income.

    Residency Determination — The "Significant Ties" Test¶

    CRA doesn't just count days. They look at ties to Canada:

    Primary ties (very strong indicators of residency):

    • Home available for use in Canada
    • Spouse or common-law partner in Canada
    • Dependants in Canada

    Secondary ties (supporting evidence):

    • Canadian bank accounts
    • Canadian driver's licence
    • Canadian health card
    • Vehicle registered in Canada
    • Canadian club memberships

    Case — Did Ahmed leave Canada?

    Ahmed tells you he "moved to Dubai" in May. But he kept his condo in Toronto (renting it out), his wife stayed in Canada, and he still has an Ontario driver's licence and bank accounts.

    CRA's view: Ahmed likely never stopped being a Canadian resident. His primary ties (home, spouse) never left. He likely owes Canadian tax on his worldwide income for the full year, including his Dubai salary.

    This is the kind of situation where you need to ask probing questions during intake — and potentially refer to a specialist.

    3.3 Social Insurance Numbers (SINs)¶

    The SIN is a 9-digit number issued by Service Canada. It's the unique identifier CRA uses to link every slip, return, and benefit to a specific person.

    SIN Rules You Must Know¶

    Rule Detail
    Every client must have a SIN No SIN = cannot file electronically
    Temporary residents get a SIN starting with 9 These expire — check the expiry date
    SINs starting with 9 are tied to work permits When the permit expires, the SIN expires
    You must verify ID Best practice: photocopy government-issued ID for your records
    Never share or store SINs insecurely Privacy law obligation — treat like a password

    Expired SIN starting with 9

    If your client has a SIN beginning with 9 and it has expired, they need to renew their temporary resident status before filing. CRA will reject a return filed with an expired SIN. Flag this during intake — don't wait until you're ready to file.

    SIN fraud — protect your clients

    As a preparer, you become a target for identity theft schemes. Never email SINs in plain text. Use encrypted portals or secure document collection tools. If a client says their SIN was compromised, direct them to Service Canada immediately.

    3.4 Filing Deadlines — Know These Cold¶

    These are the dates that govern your entire tax season. Missing them costs your clients real money.

    Standard Deadlines¶

    Deadline Date Who It Applies To
    Filing deadline — individuals April 30 Most Canadians
    Filing deadline — self-employed June 15 Self-employed individuals and their spouses/partners
    Payment deadline — self-employed April 30 Even if you can file by June 15, tax owed is due April 30
    Filing deadline — deceased (died Jan 1–Oct 31) 6 months after date of death Estate/executor
    Filing deadline — deceased (died Nov 1–Dec 31) 6 months after date of death Estate/executor
    RRSP contribution deadline 60 days after Dec 31 (≈ March 1) Anyone contributing to reduce prior year's taxes

    The self-employed trap

    This catches clients every year. Self-employed people think "I have until June 15" — but any balance owing is still due April 30. If they pay on June 1, they'll be charged interest from May 1. The June 15 extension is only for the paperwork, not the payment.

    What Happens If You Miss the Deadline?¶

    Late-filing penalty:

    5% of balance owing
+ 1% per additional month late (up to 12 months)
= Maximum 17% penalty on top of the balance owing

    Repeat offenders (filed late in any of the previous 3 years):

    10% of balance owing
+ 2% per additional month (up to 20 months)
= Maximum 50% penalty

    Interest: CRA charges compound daily interest on both the unpaid balance AND the penalties, at the prescribed rate (currently ~8–9% annually). Interest starts accruing May 1.

    Case — The Cost of Filing Late

    Client: Marcus owes $4,000 in taxes. He filed his return 3 months late. He has filed late once before in the last 3 years.

    Penalty calculation (repeat offender rate):

    Component Calculation Amount
    Base penalty 10% × $4,000 $400
    Monthly additions 2% × 3 months × $4,000 $240
    Total penalty $640

    Plus daily compound interest on $4,640 from May 1 onward.

    The lesson: Even if a client can't pay, file the return on time. The penalty is on the balance owing — if there's no balance owing, there's no late-filing penalty. Always file on time.

    No balance owing? File anyway — no penalty

    If a client is getting a refund or owes nothing, there is no late-filing penalty. But their refund is just sitting with CRA, and benefit programs like GST/HST credits may be paused until the return is filed.

    3.5 The Filing Process — What Happens After You Submit¶

    Understanding this timeline helps you set client expectations and catch problems early.

    The Journey of a T1 Return¶

    YOU FILE (EFILE)
     ↓
CRA receives return (usually within minutes for EFILE)
     ↓
Automated processing (1–2 weeks typically)
     ↓
Notice of Assessment (NOA) issued
  → Refund sent (direct deposit: 1–2 weeks | cheque: 4–6 weeks)
  → Balance owing notification (payment due if not already paid)
     ↓
CRA may select return for review (weeks to months later)
     ↓
If reviewed: CRA requests documentation → you respond → revised NOA

    EFILE vs. Paper Filing¶

    Method Processing Time Required For
    EFILE (electronic) 2 weeks typical Mandatory if you prepare 6+ returns for pay
    NETFILE (client self-files online) 2 weeks typical Client files their own return
    Paper 8–16 weeks Last resort only

    EFILE is not optional for you as a paid preparer

    Once you prepare more than 5 returns for compensation in a calendar year, CRA requires you to EFILE. You register for EFILE through CRA — we cover this in Week 23 (Business Setup). As a practical matter, EFILE every return from day one.

    3.6 Adjusting a Filed Return — T1-ADJ¶

    Mistakes happen. A client brings you a slip after you already filed. Here's what to do.

    You cannot simply re-file the return. Instead, you submit a T1 Adjustment Request (T1-ADJ).

    How to Adjust¶

    • Online: Through CRA My Account ("Change my return") — fastest method
    • By mail: Send T1-ADJ form with supporting documents
    • Through EFILE: Most tax software supports ReFILE for recent years

    Adjustment Rules¶

    Rule Detail
    How far back can you adjust? 10 years
    Who can request an adjustment? The taxpayer, or their authorized representative (you, with RepID)
    How long does CRA take? 2–4 weeks online; 8+ weeks by mail
    Can CRA adjust in their favour? Yes — CRA can also reassess (increase tax owed) within 3 years of the NOA

    Case — The Forgotten T5

    You file Jennifer's return in February. In April she calls you — she found a T5 from a GIC that matured, showing $1,200 in interest she forgot to report.

    What you do: 1. Log into CRA My Account as Jennifer's representative 2. Use "Change my return" to add $1,200 to line 12100 3. CRA recalculates — Jennifer owes an additional ~$360 in tax 4. CRA sends a Revised NOA with the updated balance

    What you tell Jennifer: "We need to correct this — it's better we catch it now than have CRA find it during a review. The additional tax is about $360, plus a small amount of interest since it should have been filed originally."

    3.7 Voluntary Disclosure Program (VDP)¶

    What if a client comes to you with years of unfiled returns or unreported income?

    CRA's Voluntary Disclosure Program allows taxpayers to come forward proactively to correct past errors or omissions — with reduced or eliminated penalties.

    Key Rules¶

    Rule Detail
    Must be voluntary CRA cannot have already contacted the client about the issue
    Must be complete Cannot disclose partially — must disclose everything
    Must include payment Must pay estimated taxes owing at time of application
    Penalty relief Penalties waived in most cases; interest may be partially waived
    Criminal prosecution Typically waived for VDP applicants

    VDP is a specialized service

    In your first tax season, if a client comes to you with 5+ years of unfiled returns or significant unreported foreign income, refer them to a tax professional experienced with VDP. The process is detailed and the stakes are high.

    That said — know it exists. Many clients feel shame about unfiled years and avoid dealing with it. Knowing you can point them toward a solution (and potentially help them) is valuable.

    3.8 Case Studies¶

    Case A — The New Immigrant (Part-Year Resident)¶

    Client: Mei arrived from China on September 15. Before arriving, she earned the equivalent of $48,000 CAD in China. After arriving, she found work and earned $22,000 in Canada.

    What you report:

    Income Amount Taxable in Canada?
    Chinese employment (Jan–Sep 14) $48,000 ❌ No — pre-residency
    Canadian employment (Sep 15–Dec 31) $22,000 ✅ Yes

    Total income on T1: $22,000

    Credits: Pro-rated based on 108 days resident ÷ 365 = 29.6% of Basic Personal Amount.

    Benefit: Mei should still file — she'll build RRSP room on the $22,000 and may qualify for GST/HST credit.

    Case B — Should I File? (Low Income Student)¶

    Client: Tyler, 20, is a full-time student. He earned $9,000 from a part-time job. His employer withheld $850 in tax.

    Does he have to file? No — his income is below the Basic Personal Amount ($16,129), so he technically owes no tax.

    Should he file? Absolutely yes:

    Reason Benefit
    Tax withheld Gets $850 refund
    Builds RRSP room $9,000 × 18% = $1,620 in new RRSP room
    GST/HST credit Qualifies for ~$350/year
    Tuition credits Carries forward for future years when he earns more

    Tyler should always file.

    Case C — Filed Late, Owes Nothing¶

    Client: Rosa, 34, forgot to file last year. She was getting a refund of $1,100.

    Penalty: $0 — no late-filing penalty because there was no balance owing.

    Consequence: Her GST/HST credit payments were paused for the months CRA didn't have a return on file. She may have missed 1–2 quarterly payments (~$150–200).

    What you do: File the late return now. CRA will recalculate her benefit entitlements and issue any missed payments retroactively.

    3.9 Week 3 Summary¶

    What you learned this week

    • Not everyone must file, but most people should — leaving money on the table by not filing is common
    • Residency status (full-year, part-year, non-resident, deemed) determines what income is taxable in Canada
    • Province of residence = where the client lived on December 31
    • Individual filing deadline: April 30 | Self-employed filing: June 15 | But tax owed always due April 30
    • Late-filing penalty: 5% + 1%/month (or 10% + 2%/month for repeat offenders)
    • No balance owing = no late-filing penalty — but benefits may be paused
    • EFILE is mandatory for paid preparers filing 6+ returns
    • Mistakes are corrected via T1-ADJ, not by re-filing
    • The VDP exists for clients with years of unfiled returns or unreported income

    ✅ Week 3 Quiz¶

    When you're ready, ask Claude: "Quiz me on Week 3" for an interactive test.

    1. A self-employed client calls you on May 20 saying they haven't filed yet and owe $3,500. What do you tell them?

    File immediately and pay the balance now. While self-employed taxpayers have until June 15 to file, the tax owed was due April 30. CRA has been charging daily compound interest on the $3,500 since May 1. Every additional day of delay increases the interest owing. There is no late-filing penalty yet (that starts if they miss June 15), but interest is already running.

    2. A client moved from Manitoba to British Columbia on November 5. Which province's tax rates apply for the full year?

    British Columbia. Province of residence for tax purposes is determined by where the client lived on December 31 of the tax year — that's BC, regardless of when they moved.

    3. What is the late-filing penalty for a first-time offender who owes $5,000 and files 4 months late?

    5% + (1% × 4 months) = 9% of $5,000 = $450 penalty, plus daily compound interest on both the $5,000 and the $450 from May 1.

    4. Your client earned $8,500 from part-time work and had $700 withheld in tax. They say they don't need to file because their income is low. What do you tell them?

    They're right that they don't have to file — but they should. Filing gets them their $700 refund, builds RRSP contribution room, qualifies them for the GST/HST credit (~$350/year), and preserves any tuition credits. Not filing means leaving real money with CRA.

    5. A client arrives from South Korea on April 10 and earns $55,000 in Canada for the rest of the year. They also had $20,000 in Korean income before arriving. What goes on their T1?

    Only the $55,000 Canadian income goes on the T1. Pre-residency foreign income is not taxable in Canada for part-year residents. Non-refundable credits are pro-rated based on the number of days they were resident (265 days ÷ 365 ≈ 72.6% of credits).

    6. You filed a client's return and they later found a missed T4 slip. What do you do?

    Do not re-file. Submit a T1 Adjustment Request (T1-ADJ) — either through CRA My Account ("Change my return") or by mail. CRA will recalculate the return and issue a Revised NOA. Going back to correct errors voluntarily is always better than waiting for CRA to find them.

    7. A client hasn't filed taxes in 7 years and has unreported income. What program exists to help them?

    The Voluntary Disclosure Program (VDP). It allows taxpayers to proactively correct past errors. If CRA hasn't already contacted them about the issue, they can apply for VDP, pay the taxes owing, and typically have penalties waived. Criminal prosecution is also typically waived. This is a specialized service — in your first season, refer these clients to a VDP-experienced professional.
