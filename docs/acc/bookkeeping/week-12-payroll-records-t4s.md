# Week 12: Payroll Records and T4s — Year-End Payroll

!!! info "Learning Objectives"
    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    - Maintain employee payroll records that satisfy CRA retention requirements
    - Manage mid-year payroll events: new hires, terminations, leaves, raises
    - Issue a Record of Employment (ROE) when required
    - Recognize taxable benefits and calculate them correctly
    - Apply Ontario vacation pay rules (4% or 6%) using accrual or pay-out methods
    - Produce accurate T4 slips with every box correctly populated
    - Distinguish T4 from T4A and know when each applies
    - Prepare and file the T4 Summary
    - Reconcile annual T4s to monthly remittances — the year-end check
    - Recognize T4 errors that trigger CRA reviews

!!! tip "Why year-end payroll is the most important compliance moment"
    T4 slips must be filed by the **last day of February** following the calendar year. They report total earnings, deductions, and benefits for every employee — and CRA cross-references them against the employee's personal tax return AND the employer's monthly remittance summary. Discrepancies trigger automated CRA review letters. A bookkeeper who delivers clean, reconciled T4s on time saves the client weeks of CRA correspondence — and embarrasses no one. This week, you become that bookkeeper.

## 12.1 Payroll Records — What to Keep, Where, and For How Long

CRA requires employers to keep complete payroll records for **6 years** after the end of the year they relate to. That's the hard rule. Many bookkeepers keep records for 7+ years to be safe.

### What Records Must Be Maintained

For each employee:

- **Personal information:** Full legal name, address, SIN, date of birth, date of hire
- **TD1 forms:** Federal and provincial, including any updates
- **Payroll register:** All pay periods showing gross, deductions, and net
- **Hours records:** For hourly employees and overtime tracking
- **Vacation tracking:** Accrued, taken, paid out
- **ROEs issued:** When applicable (see Section 12.4)
- **Remittance records:** PD7A confirmations, online payment confirmations
- **T4 slips and T4 Summary:** Year-end reports filed with CRA
- **Employee handbook acknowledgment, employment contract, etc.**

### How to Organize

In a digital age, most bookkeepers maintain:
- A digital folder per employee (e.g., shared drive: `Payroll/2025/Sarah Chen/`)
- Inside: TD1, employment contract, pay stubs, T4, ROE, any other relevant documents
- A separate folder for company-level records: monthly remittances, T4 Summary, etc.

In QBO, much of this is captured in the Employee record. But payroll software shouldn't be the ONLY copy — back up regularly.

### The SIN Rule

A **Social Insurance Number (SIN)** is required for every employee. The employer must:

- Request the SIN at the time of hire
- Verify it's a real SIN (the employee's SIN card or government correspondence)
- Record it accurately for T4 and remittance purposes
- Keep it confidential — SINs must be protected as sensitive personal information

If an employee can't or won't provide a SIN, the employer must request it within 3 days of hire and document the request.

**Important:** SINs starting with "9" are temporary SINs issued to non-permanent residents. These have expiry dates. The employer must verify the SIN is still valid when issuing T4s.

## 12.2 The Payroll Register — The Running Record

The **payroll register** is the cumulative record showing every pay period for every employee throughout the year.

### What It Tracks

For each employee, each pay period:

- Pay period dates
- Gross earnings (regular, overtime, bonus, vacation paid)
- CPP withheld (year-to-date)
- EI withheld (year-to-date)
- Income tax withheld (year-to-date)
- Other deductions (RRSP, union dues, garnishments)
- Net pay
- Vacation accrued and taken

### Why It Matters

The payroll register is the source of truth for T4s. Every box on the T4 comes from cumulative numbers in the register.

It's also essential for:
- Tracking whether an employee has hit annual maximums (CPP and EI both have annual caps)
- Issuing ROEs (which require pay-period-specific data)
- CRA audit response (auditors will request the register)

### Annual Maximums to Watch

**CPP — 2025 (verify current year):**
- Maximum employee contribution: $4,034.10
- Once an employee hits this maximum, no more CPP is withheld for the year
- Maximum employer matching: $4,034.10
- CPP2 (additional contribution above YMPE) kicks in for higher earners

**EI — 2025:**
- Maximum employee contribution: $1,077.48
- Once hit, no more EI is withheld for the year
- Employer continues to pay 1.4× employee maximum

**The implications for your work:**
- High earners hit CPP and EI maximums mid-year (typically October-November)
- After the maximum, gross pay continues but deductions stop
- Your payroll software handles this automatically; manual calculation requires year-to-date awareness

### Sample Payroll Register Entry

For Sarah Chen, October 31 pay period (a few months into the year):

```
Employee: Sarah Chen
Pay Period: Oct 18-31, 2025
YTD Gross (through this period): $46,769.23
YTD CPP: $2,651.18 (still under $4,034.10 max)
YTD EI: $766.97 (still under $1,077.48 max)
YTD Income Tax: $7,355.65

This Period:
  Gross: $2,769.23
  CPP: $156.76
  EI: $45.42
  Income Tax: $435.45
  Net: $2,131.60

YTD After This Period:
  Gross: $49,538.46
  CPP: $2,807.94
  EI: $812.39
  Income Tax: $7,791.10
  Net: $38,127.03
```

This rolling record is what's used at year-end to populate the T4.

## 12.3 Mid-Year Payroll Events

Throughout the year, employees experience changes that affect payroll. Each requires specific handling.

### New Hire

When a new employee starts:

1. **Onboarding paperwork:**
   - Personal info form (SIN, address, banking for direct deposit)
   - TD1 federal and provincial
   - Employment contract or offer letter signed

2. **Payroll setup:**
   - Add to payroll system with claim code
   - Set pay frequency, rate, deductions
   - Activate direct deposit (test with $1 if possible)

3. **First pay period:**
   - Pro-rate if mid-period start
   - Verify all deductions calculate correctly
   - Confirm net pay matches expectations

**Documentation requirements:** First-day documents go in the employee file immediately. If TD1 isn't provided, document the request and use Claim Code 1 default.

### Pay Raise

When an employee gets a raise:

1. **Document the change:** Letter or email from owner, signed by employee
2. **Update payroll system:** New pay rate effective from a specific date
3. **Adjust withholdings:** Income tax may change tax bracket — payroll software handles automatically
4. **Update employment contract** if substantial

The bookkeeper isn't authorized to change pay rates — only to implement changes authorized by the owner.

### Leave of Absence

Common leave types in Ontario:

| Leave Type | Duration | Pay |
|---|---|---|
| Pregnancy/Parental Leave | Up to 78 weeks combined | Unpaid (EI benefits available) |
| Personal Emergency Leave | 3 paid days + 7 unpaid days/year (50+ employees) | Mixed |
| Sick Leave | Varies by ESA and employer policy | Varies |
| Bereavement | 2 unpaid days/year | Unpaid |

**During leave:**
- Employee is still on the books (no termination)
- Pay temporarily suspended
- Benefits may continue (employer's choice for some)
- Vacation accrual continues (usually) per ESA
- ROE issued at start of leave

**Returning from leave:**
- Restore pay
- Resume vacation accrual from where it stopped
- No re-onboarding required

### Termination

When an employee leaves the company (voluntary or involuntary):

1. **Final pay calculations:**
   - Wages earned through last day
   - Accrued vacation pay (if accrued, not paid out each pay)
   - Termination pay (if terminated without cause)
   - Severance pay (if entitled under ESA — 5+ years, $2.5M+ payroll)

2. **Issue Record of Employment (ROE):**
   - Required within 5 days of last day worked
   - Filed electronically through Service Canada (or by mail)

3. **Final T4 will be issued at year-end:**
   - Don't issue a T4 mid-year for a terminated employee
   - The annual T4 will reflect their full year of earnings

### Vacation Pay-Out on Termination

Ontario ESA requires that all accrued vacation pay be paid out at termination. If the employee was accruing vacation but hadn't taken any:

```
Vacation accrued: 4% of gross wages for the year (or 6% for 5+ years)
Vacation paid out: must clear the accrued balance
```

The vacation pay-out is subject to CPP, EI, and income tax — it goes through payroll just like regular wages.

## 12.4 The Record of Employment (ROE)

The **Record of Employment** is the document Service Canada uses to determine an employee's eligibility for EI benefits.

### When an ROE Is Required

You must issue an ROE whenever there's an **interruption of earnings**:

- Termination (voluntary or involuntary)
- Layoff
- Pregnancy leave start
- Parental leave start
- Sick leave (extended)
- Compassionate care leave
- Death of the employee

### Deadlines

- **Electronic ROE:** Within 5 calendar days of the interruption
- **Paper ROE:** Within 5 calendar days OR within 15 days of the date the form is created (whichever is sooner)

Most modern Canadian payroll software files ROEs electronically, automatically pulling from the payroll register.

### What the ROE Shows

Key boxes on the ROE:

- **Box 11:** Last day for which paid
- **Box 12:** Final pay period ending date
- **Box 14:** Insurable earnings for last 53 weeks (for EI calculation)
- **Box 15A:** Total insurable earnings (sum)
- **Box 15B:** Last 53 weeks of insurable earnings broken down
- **Box 16:** Reason for issuing ROE (with codes — e.g., A=shortage of work, M=dismissal, E=quit)
- **Box 17:** Vacation pay (if any)
- **Box 18:** Comments

The reason code in Box 16 matters — it can affect the employee's EI eligibility. The employer must report accurately.

### Common ROE Errors

- **Wrong reason code** — selecting "shortage of work" when the employee was dismissed for cause (or vice versa)
- **Missing pay period** — not including the final partial period
- **Vacation pay confusion** — including or excluding incorrectly
- **Late filing** — penalty risk and delays employee's EI claim

If an error is discovered, the employer can issue an amended ROE. But it's better to get it right the first time.

## 12.5 Vacation Pay — Ontario Rules

Ontario's ESA mandates vacation pay. The rules are specific.

### Minimum Requirements

| Years of Service | Vacation Time | Vacation Pay |
|---|---|---|
| Under 5 years | 2 weeks per year | 4% of gross wages |
| 5+ years | 3 weeks per year | 6% of gross wages |

**Some industries/contracts** offer more — but these are minimums. The employer must comply with the higher of: ESA minimums, employment contract, or collective agreement.

### Two Methods of Handling Vacation Pay

**Method 1: Pay With Each Pay Period**

Each pay cheque includes vacation pay calculated as 4% (or 6%) of that period's wages.

**Example:** Employee earns $1,500 gross in a pay period. Vacation pay = 4% × $1,500 = $60. Total gross including vacation pay = $1,560.

When the employee takes a vacation, they don't receive additional pay (because they got it spread throughout the year). They use earned time off.

**Pros:**
- No vacation pay liability accumulating on the books
- Employee receives benefit in real-time
- Simpler at year-end

**Cons:**
- Employee must save for vacation (not always reliable)
- Can be unfamiliar to employees who expect "vacation pay" as a separate amount

**Method 2: Accrue Throughout the Year, Pay Out When Vacation Taken**

Each pay period, vacation pay is **accrued as a liability** but not paid. When the employee takes vacation, they receive their accrued vacation pay.

**Example:** Employee earned $1,500 this period. Accrue 4% = $60.

```
Dr. Wages Expense                  1,500.00 (regular wages)
Dr. Vacation Pay Expense              60.00 (accrued vacation)
     Cr. CPP Payable                          [calc]
     Cr. EI Payable                           [calc]
     Cr. Income Tax Payable                   [calc]
     Cr. Vacation Pay Accrual                  60.00
     Cr. Bank Account                       [net pay]
```

When the employee takes 1 week of vacation worth $1,500:

```
Dr. Vacation Pay Accrual          1,500.00
     Cr. CPP Payable                          [calc on $1,500]
     Cr. EI Payable                           [calc]
     Cr. Income Tax Payable                   [calc]
     Cr. Bank Account                       [net pay]
```

The vacation pay accrual liability decreases. Wages expense is NOT recorded (because we already expensed it as it accrued).

**Pros:**
- Matches actual time-off usage
- Employee receives a "vacation cheque" when they take time off
- Common in salaried positions

**Cons:**
- Liability accumulates on the books
- More complex tracking
- Year-end reconciliation needed

### Which Method to Use

- **Pay-with-each-cheque method:** Common for hourly workers, especially seasonal/part-time
- **Accrual method:** Common for salaried full-time staff

Either is acceptable under ESA. The employment contract should specify which method applies.

### Payout on Termination

Regardless of method, upon termination, any unpaid vacation must be paid out. With the accrual method, the accrued balance is paid. With the pay-with-each-cheque method, accrued vacation is generally already in the employee's hands.

## 12.6 Taxable Benefits

A **taxable benefit** is something of value the employer provides to the employee that the CRA considers part of their compensation. These must be added to the T4 and tax must be withheld.

### Common Taxable Benefits

| Benefit | Taxable? | Where Reported on T4 |
|---|---|---|
| Group health/dental premiums (employer-paid) | Sometimes (private health = not taxable; employer-paid extended health = depends) | Box 40 if taxable |
| Personal use of company vehicle | Yes | Box 14 + Box 34 |
| Cell phone (personal use) | Sometimes | Box 40 |
| Gift cards | Yes (as cash) | Box 14 |
| Birthday gifts (non-cash, modest) | No (within limits) | Not reported |
| Parking | Sometimes | Box 40 |
| Stock options | Yes | Box 38 |
| Tuition reimbursement | Sometimes | Box 14 if taxable |
| Free meals at work | Often no | Not reported |

### The General Test

If the employer pays for something that personally benefits the employee, it's likely taxable. Exceptions exist (e.g., uniforms, safety equipment, working condition benefits) but the default is "taxable."

### Calculating the Benefit

For most benefits, the taxable amount is the **fair market value** to the employee.

**Example: Personal Use of Company Vehicle**

If an employee uses a company car for personal trips:
- **Standby charge:** Calculated based on the car's cost and personal kilometres
- **Operating cost benefit:** Calculated based on personal kilometres × CRA rate (usually around $0.30/km for 2025)

These are detailed calculations — CRA's Employer's Guide T4130 has the specifics. For complex benefits, consult the accountant.

### Recording Taxable Benefits in Payroll

The taxable benefit increases the employee's gross income for the period:

```
Dr. Wages Expense                    [regular wages]
Dr. Vehicle Benefit Expense          [benefit amount]
     Cr. CPP Payable                          [calc on combined]
     Cr. EI Payable                           [calc on combined]
     Cr. Income Tax Payable                   [calc on combined]
     Cr. Bank Account                         [net pay - cash only, not benefit]
```

The benefit is taxable but NOT cash to the employee. Tax is withheld (which feels like a paycut to the employee), but the benefit value is reported as income.

### The T4 Reporting

Most taxable benefits go in **Box 40** (Other taxable benefits) AND are added into Box 14 (Employment income). Specific benefits have their own boxes (e.g., Box 34 for personal use of vehicle).

We'll cover T4 boxes in detail next.

## 12.7 The T4 Slip — Every Box Explained

The **T4 slip** is the year-end document showing total employment income and deductions for each employee. It must be filed with CRA by the **last day of February** following the calendar year.

### T4 Slip Layout

```
T4 — Statement of Remuneration Paid

Box 10: Province of employment           ON
Box 12: Social Insurance Number          XXX XXX XXX
Box 14: Employment income                $XX,XXX.XX
Box 16: Employee's CPP contributions     $X,XXX.XX
Box 18: Employee's EI premiums           $X,XXX.XX
Box 22: Income tax deducted              $X,XXX.XX
Box 24: EI insurable earnings            $XX,XXX.XX
Box 26: CPP/QPP pensionable earnings     $XX,XXX.XX
Box 28: Exempt — CPP, EI, PPIP           (rarely used)
Box 29: Employment code                  (specific situations)
Box 34: Personal use of employer's car   $X,XXX.XX (if applicable)
Box 36: Interest-free and low-interest loan benefit (if applicable)
Box 38: Security options benefits        (stock options)
Box 39: Security options deduction
Box 40: Other taxable benefits           $X,XXX.XX (if applicable)
Box 42: Employment commissions           (if commission-based)
Box 44: Union dues                       $X,XXX.XX (if applicable)
Box 46: Charitable donations             (if employer-deducted)
Box 50: Pension plan number              (RPP)
Box 52: Pension adjustment                $X,XXX.XX (if RPP)
Box 55: Employee's PPIP premiums         (Quebec only)
Box 56: PPIP insurable earnings          (Quebec only)

Other Information (footnotes, codes 30-99 for specific items)
```

### The Critical Boxes — In Plain Language

**Box 14 — Employment Income:**
The total compensation received during the year. Includes:
- Regular wages and salary
- Overtime
- Vacation pay
- Bonuses
- Commissions
- Taxable benefits (also separately reported in Box 40)

Box 14 is the "headline" number — what the employee reports as employment income on their personal tax return.

**Box 16 — Employee CPP:**
Total CPP contributions deducted during the year. Does NOT include employer matching.

**Box 18 — Employee EI:**
Total EI premiums deducted. Does NOT include employer's 1.4× portion.

**Box 22 — Income Tax Deducted:**
Total federal AND provincial income tax withheld. Combined into one number on the T4.

**Box 24 — EI Insurable Earnings:**
The earnings on which EI was calculated. Usually equals Box 14 unless the employee hit the EI maximum during the year.

**Box 26 — CPP Pensionable Earnings:**
The earnings on which CPP was calculated. Usually equals Box 14 minus non-pensionable items.

**Box 40 — Other Taxable Benefits:**
The total of taxable benefits reported. Note: these are ALSO included in Box 14. Box 40 just identifies the portion that came from benefits rather than direct wages.

**Box 50 — Pension Plan Number:**
If the employer has a Registered Pension Plan (RPP), the plan's CRA-issued number.

**Box 52 — Pension Adjustment (PA):**
If the employer contributes to an RPP, the calculated pension adjustment that affects the employee's RRSP room.

### How T4 Boxes Get Populated

Every box on the T4 comes from the cumulative payroll register for the year:

- Box 14 = Sum of all gross pay (including taxable benefits)
- Box 16 = Sum of all employee CPP withheld
- Box 18 = Sum of all employee EI withheld
- Box 22 = Sum of all federal + provincial tax withheld
- Etc.

Payroll software automates this. Manual T4 preparation requires careful summation.

### The Cross-Check

Before filing the T4, verify:

```
Box 14 (Employment income) ≈ Box 26 (Pensionable earnings) + non-pensionable items
Box 24 (Insurable earnings) ≤ $65,700 (the 2025 EI maximum)
Box 16 (Employee CPP) ≤ $4,034.10 (the 2025 CPP maximum)
Box 18 (Employee EI) ≤ $1,077.48 (the 2025 EI maximum)
```

If any of these numbers exceed the maximums, there's an error — likely too much CPP or EI was withheld during the year. Refund and correct.

## 12.8 The T4A — When You Use It Instead

The **T4A** is a different slip used for non-employment income.

### When to Use T4A

| Situation | Slip |
|---|---|
| Employee receiving wages | T4 |
| Self-employed contractor receiving fee for services | **No slip required** (the contractor reports business income on their own tax return) |
| Pension or retirement income | T4A |
| Scholarships or bursaries | T4A |
| Medical research grants | T4A |
| Lump-sum pension payments | T4A |
| Annuities | T4A |
| Self-employed commissions (limited situations) | T4A |
| Honoraria, fees for services (in specific situations like a board fee) | T4A |

### The T4A vs. Contractor Question

Many small businesses get confused about T4As for contractors. The general rule:

- **Pure self-employed contractor (a real business):** No T4A required. They invoice you, you pay, they report the income on their own business tax return.
- **Honorarium, board fee, or payment for services to a non-business individual:** May require T4A. Box 048 captures fees for services in some scenarios.

**The conservative position:** When in doubt, consult the accountant. T4As are an evolving area with CRA guidance changes.

### T4A Filing

T4As have similar filing deadlines (last day of February) and similar boxes (income type, total amount). The T4A Summary is filed alongside.

## 12.9 The T4 Summary

When you file T4 slips, you also file a **T4 Summary** (T4SUM) — a one-page form that totals all T4 information for the company.

### What the Summary Reports

```
T4 Summary

Box 14: Total employment income (sum of all employee Box 14s)
Box 16: Total employee CPP
Box 18: Total employee EI
Box 19: Total employer EI premiums   (1.4× total Box 18)
Box 22: Total income tax deducted
Box 27: Total employer CPP contributions
Box 80: Total reported on T4s

Total of: CPP (employee + employer) + EI (employee + employer) + Income Tax
                                                                  =
                                                          Total remitted to CRA
```

### The Critical Reconciliation

The T4 Summary must reconcile to the year's actual remittances:

```
Total remittances during the year (from PD7As/online payments)
    =
Total CPP + EI + Income Tax on T4 Summary
```

If they don't match, CRA will issue a review letter requesting an explanation.

### Common Reconciliation Issues

**Issue 1: Late Remittances**
A December remittance paid in February of the following year. CRA records both as separate transactions in different years. You must adjust to ensure the T4 Summary captures the correct year's deductions.

**Issue 2: CRA Mis-Posting**
Sometimes a remittance gets posted to the wrong account. Verify by checking CRA My Business Account.

**Issue 3: Employer Calculation Errors**
The bookkeeper calculated employer CPP/EI incorrectly throughout the year. The T4 Summary requires the corrected amounts.

**Issue 4: Fraudulent Withholding**
Theoretically — withholdings reported but not remitted. Rare, but CRA reviews look for this pattern.

### The Reconciliation Worksheet

Most professional bookkeepers maintain a year-end reconciliation worksheet:

```
PAYROLL RECONCILIATION 2025
Employer: Durham Digital Marketing Inc.

YEAR-END T4 TOTALS
Employee CPP (sum of Box 16s):              $9,580.50
Employer CPP (matching):                    $9,580.50
Total CPP:                                 $19,161.00

Employee EI (sum of Box 18s):               $3,255.20
Employer EI (Total Box 18 × 1.4):           $4,557.28
Total EI:                                   $7,812.48

Employee Income Tax (sum of Box 22s):      $42,892.40
Total Income Tax:                          $42,892.40

GRAND TOTAL FOR T4 SUMMARY:                $69,865.88

REMITTANCES MADE TO CRA:
January 15:                                  $5,710.40
February 15:                                 $5,755.30
March 15:                                    $5,795.80
... [continue through December] ...
December 15:                                 $6,128.10
TOTAL REMITTED:                            $69,865.88

DIFFERENCE:                                       $0.00 ✓
```

If the difference is zero, you're ready to file. If not, investigate before filing.

## 12.10 Filing T4s and the T4 Summary

T4s and T4 Summaries are filed with CRA by the **last day of February** following the calendar year.

### Filing Methods

**Method 1: Online Through Payroll Software (Most Common)**

QuickBooks Online Payroll, Wagepoint, Ceridian, ADP, and most other Canadian payroll providers can file T4s electronically with one click. The software:
- Generates T4 slips for each employee
- Generates the T4 Summary
- Submits to CRA via XML file
- Provides confirmation

**Method 2: CRA Web Forms**

CRA's online portal allows you to enter T4 information manually for up to 100 slips. Useful for very small employers without payroll software.

**Method 3: XML Upload to CRA**

For larger employers or those using non-integrated systems, T4 data can be exported as XML and uploaded to CRA's Internet File Transfer (IFT) system.

**Method 4: Paper Filing (Rare)**

Paper T4s and T4 Summary can be mailed to CRA. CRA strongly discourages this — most employers must file electronically. Paper filing for more than 50 T4s incurs penalties.

### Distribution to Employees

Employees must receive their T4 by the same deadline (last day of February). Distribution methods:

- **Email** (with employee consent — most common)
- **Mail** (printed copy)
- **Pick-up in person**
- **Online employee portal** (in payroll software)

The employee uses the T4 to file their personal income tax return.

### Late Filing Penalties

CRA imposes penalties for late T4 filing:

| Number of T4s Late | Penalty |
|---|---|
| 1-5 | $100 minimum |
| 6-10 | $250 |
| 11-50 | $500 |
| 51-500 | $1,500 |
| 501-2,500 | $2,500 |
| 2,501-10,000 | $3,500 |
| 10,001+ | $7,500 |

Additional penalties for repeat offenders. File on time.

## 12.11 Signature Example — Durham Digital Marketing's Year-End

Let's complete the year-end for the four employees from Week 11.

### Recall: The Four Employees

- **Sarah Chen** — $72,000/year, full year employed
- **Marcus Rodriguez** — $22/hour, but received a raise to $25/hour on July 1
- **Priya Patel** — $55,000/year, took 12-week medical leave (July - September)
- **Thomas Lee** — $18/hour, terminated October 15

Each presents a different year-end scenario.

### Sarah's T4 — The Simple Case

Sarah worked the full year at $72,000.

**Year-end calculations:**
- 26 pay periods × $2,769.23 = $72,000.00 (Box 14)
- CPP withheld throughout year: $4,034.10 (capped at maximum)
- EI withheld throughout year: $1,077.48 (capped at maximum)
- Income tax withheld: approximately $11,260 (varies based on PDOC)

**Sarah's T4:**

```
Box 14: Employment income          $72,000.00
Box 16: Employee CPP                $4,034.10
Box 18: Employee EI                 $1,077.48
Box 22: Income tax deducted        $11,260.00
Box 24: EI insurable earnings      $65,700.00 (capped at max)
Box 26: CPP pensionable earnings   $71,300.00 (capped at YMPE)
```

Note: Sarah's earnings exceeded both EI ($65,700) and CPP ($71,300) maximums. The Box 24 and Box 26 reflect the maximums, not her gross.

### Marcus's T4 — The Mid-Year Raise

Marcus worked all year, but rate changed mid-year.

**First half ($22/hour, January 1 - June 30):**
- 13 bi-weekly periods at average 80 hours = 1,040 hours
- Plus 30 overtime hours total
- Earnings: 1,040 × $22 + 30 × $33 = $22,880 + $990 = $23,870

**Second half ($25/hour, July 1 - December 31):**
- 13 bi-weekly periods at average 80 hours = 1,040 hours
- Plus 25 overtime hours total
- Earnings: 1,040 × $25 + 25 × $37.50 = $26,000 + $937.50 = $26,937.50

**Total annual:**
- Box 14: $50,807.50
- Box 16: ~$2,803 (5.95% on contributory earnings less basic exemption)
- Box 18: ~$833 (1.64% × $50,807.50)
- Box 22: ~$5,180 (estimated)

### Priya's T4 — The Medical Leave

Priya was on unpaid medical leave for 12 weeks (mid-July to mid-October). During leave: no wages, no source deductions.

**Pre-leave (January - mid-July, 14 pay periods of $2,115.38):**
- $29,615.32

**Post-leave (mid-October - December, 6 pay periods):**
- $12,692.28

**Total annual:**
- Box 14: $42,307.60
- Box 16: ~$2,283
- Box 18: ~$694
- Box 22: ~$3,580

**Box 28 (Exempt — CPP/EI):** Mark "X" if she earned no wages during certain periods. Actually, you only mark exempt if specific employment categories apply (some agricultural, certain religious orders) — not for routine leave. Standard leave just means no earnings during leave; the T4 simply reflects her actual pay.

**Did Priya receive any disability benefits?** If the company paid sick pay or short-term disability through payroll, those would be reported on the T4. If she received EI sickness benefits, those come from Service Canada and don't appear on the employer's T4 (separate T4E from Service Canada).

### Thomas's T4 — Terminated Mid-Year

Thomas worked January 1 through October 15 — about 10.5 months.

**Pay periods worked:** ~22 bi-weekly periods at 75 hours × $18/hour
**Total earnings:** ~$29,700

**Plus accrued vacation pay-out at termination:**
- Vacation accrued: 4% × $29,700 = $1,188
- Paid out on final cheque: $1,188

**Total Box 14:** $30,888

**Plus termination notice or pay in lieu, if applicable:**
- Under ESA, after 3 months but less than 1 year of service, 1 week of notice required
- If paid in lieu of notice: $18 × 75 = $1,350 (one week's pay)
- This goes in Box 14 as employment income (not severance)

**Final Box 14:** $32,238 (approximately)

**Note:** Thomas's ROE was issued at termination on October 15. The T4 reflects the full year's earnings.

### Preparing the T4 Summary

Sum each box across all four employees:

```
T4 SUMMARY — DURHAM DIGITAL MARKETING INC.
Year: 2025

Box 14 totals (Employment income):
  Sarah:     $72,000.00
  Marcus:    $50,807.50
  Priya:     $42,307.60
  Thomas:    $32,238.00
  Lisa (owner): $80,000.00 [add owner's salary]
  TOTAL:    $277,353.10

Box 16 totals (Employee CPP):
  Sarah:     $4,034.10  (max)
  Marcus:    $2,803.00
  Priya:     $2,283.00
  Thomas:    $1,672.00
  Lisa:      $4,034.10  (max)
  TOTAL:    $14,826.20

Box 18 totals (Employee EI):
  TOTAL:    [sum of all individual Box 18s]

Box 22 totals (Income Tax):
  TOTAL:    [sum of all individual Box 22s]

Box 19: Employer EI = Total Box 18 × 1.4
Box 27: Employer CPP = Total Box 16 (matching)

Total to reconcile to remittances:
  Employee CPP + Employer CPP + Employee EI + Employer EI + Income Tax
```

### The Reconciliation

Compare T4 Summary totals to the sum of monthly remittances throughout the year.

If they match exactly: file confidently.

If they don't match: investigate before filing. Common causes:
- A pay period missed in remittance calculation
- A correction during the year not reflected in remittance
- Employer cost calculation error

### Filing

In QuickBooks Online Payroll, Wagepoint, or similar:
1. Run year-end review
2. Generate T4s (one per employee)
3. Generate T4 Summary
4. Submit to CRA electronically
5. Distribute T4s to employees (email or mail)

Save copies of everything in the year-end folder.

## 12.12 Common T4 Errors That Trigger CRA Reviews

CRA's automated systems flag certain patterns. Avoid these.

### Error 1 — Box 14 Doesn't Match Box 24 + Box 26

If insurable earnings (Box 24) plus pensionable earnings (Box 26) don't logically reconcile to Box 14, CRA flags it.

**Common cause:** Including a non-pensionable item in Box 14 but treating it as pensionable in payroll.

### Error 2 — Box 16 Exceeds the CPP Maximum

If total employee CPP shown exceeds $4,034.10 (the 2025 maximum), CRA detects the over-deduction.

**Common cause:** Manual calculation error, or two part-time jobs with each employer not knowing the other.

### Error 3 — Box 18 Exceeds the EI Maximum

Same issue with EI ($1,077.48 maximum for 2025).

### Error 4 — T4 Summary Doesn't Match Remittances

If total CPP + EI + tax on T4 Summary doesn't equal year's actual remittances, CRA reviews.

### Error 5 — Missing T4s

If an employee on payroll doesn't get a T4 (terminated mid-year or otherwise), CRA cross-references to remittances and notices.

### Error 6 — SIN Errors

Wrong SIN, missing SIN, expired SIN — all trigger employee tax filing problems and CRA letters.

### Error 7 — Box 26 (Pensionable Earnings) Anomalies

If pensionable earnings exceed the YMPE ($71,300 for 2025), CRA flags it because CPP shouldn't have been calculated on amounts above YMPE.

### Error 8 — Late Filing

Filing after the last day of February triggers automatic late penalties.

## 12.13 Quiz — 15 Questions

**Question 1**
How long must payroll records be kept under CRA rules?

??? answer
    **6 years** after the end of the year they relate to. Many bookkeepers keep records for 7+ years to be safe.

---

**Question 2**
A new employee starts October 15. They haven't provided a SIN. What do you do?

??? answer
    1. **Request the SIN immediately** (within 3 days of hire is the requirement)
    2. **Document the request** in writing (email or signed acknowledgment)
    3. **Inform the employee** they must provide it for legal compliance
    4. **Continue payroll** (you can't pay without setting up the employee in payroll, but you also can't refuse to hire someone awaiting SIN if they have legal work authorization)

    If the SIN doesn't arrive, escalate to the owner. Persistent refusal to provide a SIN is grounds for ending employment in some cases (consult legal advice).

---

**Question 3**
What's the difference between paying vacation pay with each pay period vs. accruing it?

??? answer
    **Pay With Each Period:**
    - Add 4% (or 6%) to each paycheque
    - No vacation pay liability accumulates
    - Employee receives benefit in real-time

    **Accrual Method:**
    - Vacation pay accrued as a liability each period
    - Paid out when vacation is taken
    - More complex tracking
    - Common for salaried employees

    Both are acceptable under Ontario ESA. The employment contract should specify which applies.

---

**Question 4**
When must an ROE be issued?

??? answer
    Within **5 calendar days** of an interruption of earnings (termination, layoff, leave start, etc.). For electronic ROEs, the same 5-day deadline applies.

    Late ROEs delay the employee's EI claim and trigger penalties.

---

**Question 5**
A new employee earns $50,000 and uses a company-provided cell phone for personal calls. The employer pays $80/month for the phone. Is this taxable?

??? answer
    **Yes, the personal-use portion is a taxable benefit.**

    Calculate the taxable benefit:
    - $80/month × 12 = $960/year total cost
    - If 50% personal use: $480 is taxable

    **T4 reporting:**
    - Add $480 to Box 14 (employment income)
    - Also report in Box 40 (other taxable benefits)
    - Calculate CPP, EI, and income tax on the additional $480

    The employee receives the benefit (personal use of phone) but doesn't receive cash. Tax is withheld effectively from their other wages.

---

**Question 6**
Sarah's gross pay this year was $72,000. What goes in Box 14, 24, and 26 of her T4?

??? answer
    - **Box 14 (Employment income):** $72,000.00
    - **Box 24 (EI insurable earnings):** $65,700.00 (capped at the 2025 EI maximum)
    - **Box 26 (CPP pensionable earnings):** $71,300.00 (capped at the 2025 YMPE)

    Sarah's earnings exceeded both maximums, so Box 24 and 26 reflect the maximums rather than her gross.

---

**Question 7**
A T4 must be filed and distributed by what date?

??? answer
    **The last day of February** following the calendar year (or the next business day if Feb 28 falls on a weekend).

    For 2025 T4s, that's **February 28, 2026**.

    The deadline applies to both filing with CRA AND distributing slips to employees.

---

**Question 8**
True or False: A self-employed contractor receives a T4 from the company that paid them.

??? answer
    **False.** A self-employed contractor does NOT receive a T4. They invoice the company for services rendered, and they report the income on their own business tax return (T2125 for sole proprietors, T2 for incorporated contractors).

    A T4 is only for employees. If a "contractor" actually meets the employee test, they should have been on payroll all along.

---

**Question 9**
The T4 Summary's total CPP + EI + Income Tax is $52,400. The total remittances during the year were $51,800. What does this tell you?

??? answer
    There's a **$600 discrepancy** that must be investigated before filing.

    Possible causes:
    - One remittance was missed or under-paid by $600
    - A late December remittance was posted to the wrong year
    - Calculation error in either the T4s or the remittances

    **Action:** Reconcile carefully before filing. CRA will flag this discrepancy and request an explanation if it's filed without correction.

---

**Question 10**
A terminated employee has $1,400 of accrued but unpaid vacation. What happens at termination?

??? answer
    The accrued vacation must be **paid out on the final cheque** under Ontario ESA.

    The vacation pay-out is subject to:
    - CPP (with the basic exemption applied)
    - EI
    - Income tax

    It's reported on the T4 in **Box 14** (as employment income). Some employers also report it in **Box 17** of the ROE.

---

**Question 11**
What's the penalty for filing 4 T4 slips one week late?

??? answer
    **Minimum $100 penalty** (for 1-5 T4s late, applied per filing).

    Plus interest on the late filing.

    The penalty applies regardless of whether the slips were correct — late = penalty.

---

**Question 12**
A bookkeeper realizes in March (after T4s are filed) that one employee's T4 had Box 16 understated by $50. What do they do?

??? answer
    **File an amended T4** (T4-AMD) for that employee.

    Steps:
    1. Prepare a corrected T4 with the right numbers
    2. Mark it as "AMENDED" (or use the appropriate code in payroll software)
    3. File with CRA
    4. Issue a corrected T4 to the employee
    5. Inform employee they may need to file an amended personal tax return

    Don't ignore — CRA cross-references T4s with employee tax returns. The discrepancy will eventually surface.

---

**Question 13**
An employee took 12 weeks of unpaid medical leave during the year. How does this affect their T4?

??? answer
    The T4 reports **only what was actually paid** during the year:
    - Pre-leave wages
    - Post-leave wages
    - Total = Box 14

    No special notation is needed for unpaid leave. The T4 doesn't show what wasn't paid; it shows what was.

    However:
    - The employee's overall earnings for the year will be lower than their salary suggests
    - CPP and EI maximums likely won't be reached
    - The employee may receive reduced employer-paid benefits during the year
    - Vacation accrual continues (unless ESA exception) — paid out per the company's vacation method

    The employee may also have received **EI sickness benefits** during leave. Those are reported on a separate **T4E** issued by Service Canada — not on the employer's T4.

---

**Question 14**
An employee's Box 16 shows $4,200 of CPP. What's wrong?

??? answer
    The **2025 CPP maximum is $4,034.10**. An amount of $4,200 exceeds the maximum, indicating over-deduction.

    Possible causes:
    - Manual calculation error
    - Failure to stop CPP withholding when the maximum was reached
    - Including non-pensionable earnings in CPP calculation

    **Action:**
    - Refund the over-deducted amount to the employee (with their next pay or as a separate cheque)
    - Reduce the employer's CPP claim accordingly
    - Adjust Box 16 to $4,034.10 maximum
    - Don't file the T4 with the wrong number

---

**Question 15**
Why is the T4 reconciliation to remittances so important?

??? answer
    Three critical reasons:

    1. **CRA cross-references automatically** — discrepancies trigger review letters and audits
    2. **It validates the year's payroll work** — if reconciliation works, the books are likely right
    3. **It catches missed remittances** — if total remittances don't equal total T4 amounts, payroll cash that should have gone to CRA may have stayed in the company (a serious problem)

    A clean reconciliation is the year-end stamp of approval on twelve months of payroll work. A mismatch can unwind months of work and trigger penalties.
