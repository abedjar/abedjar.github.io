## 5.1 The Three Core Financial Statements

Every business produces three primary financial statements:

1. **Income Statement** (also called Profit & Loss Statement, or P&L) — measures profitability over a period
2. **Balance Sheet** (also called Statement of Financial Position) — measures financial position at a point in time
3. **Statement of Cash Flows** — explains how cash moved through the business during a period

This week focuses on the first two — the Income Statement and Balance Sheet — because these are what bookkeepers produce every month. The Statement of Cash Flows is typically prepared only at year-end by the accountant, and it's a more advanced topic we don't cover in this 14-week course.

### The Relationship Between the Two Statements

These two statements are connected:

```
INCOME STATEMENT (for the PERIOD)          BALANCE SHEET (at a POINT IN TIME)

    Revenue                                   Assets
    − Expenses                                Liabilities
    = Net Income     ──────────────→          Equity (includes Retained Earnings)
                                                                      ↑
                                                                Net Income flows here
```

The Income Statement shows what happened over a period of time (usually a month, quarter, or year). The Balance Sheet shows where the business stands at a specific moment (usually the last day of a period).

The **Net Income** calculated on the Income Statement flows into **Retained Earnings** on the Balance Sheet. This is the bridge between the two statements, and understanding it is the key to understanding financial reporting.

## 5.2 The Income Statement — Step by Step

The Income Statement answers one question: **how profitable was the business during this period?**

### The Basic Structure

```
REVENUE (what we earned)
−  COST OF GOODS SOLD (direct costs of earning it)
=  GROSS PROFIT

−  OPERATING EXPENSES (indirect costs of running the business)
=  OPERATING INCOME

+  OTHER INCOME
−  OTHER EXPENSES (interest, depreciation often)
=  NET INCOME BEFORE TAX

−  INCOME TAX EXPENSE
=  NET INCOME (the bottom line)
```

Each subtotal tells a different part of the story. Let's break them down.

### Section 1 — Revenue

Revenue is what the business earned during the period from its core business activities.

For a service business:
- Service revenue
- Consulting fees
- Rental income (if they're a landlord)

For a product business:
- Sales revenue
- Product sales

For most Ontario small businesses, you'll see 1-3 revenue lines on the Income Statement.

**Example — Maple Ridge Physiotherapy:**
```
REVENUE
  Patient Revenue — Direct Pay             $45,200
  Patient Revenue — Insurance Billing       28,400
  Total Revenue                           $73,600
```

Two revenue lines because Maria wants to see the split between patients who pay directly vs. those whose insurance is billed. This is useful decision-making information.

### Section 2 — Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

For product businesses and trades, COGS captures the direct costs of producing the goods or services sold. For pure service businesses, there may be no COGS section at all — everything goes into operating expenses.

**Example — Northbrook Landscaping:**
```
COST OF GOODS SOLD
  Materials - Landscape Projects          $38,500
  Subcontractor Costs                       8,200
  Direct Labour - Seasonal                 42,000
  Total COGS                              $88,700

GROSS PROFIT                              $61,300
```

Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS. It's the money left after producing what you sold but before paying to run the business (rent, admin, marketing, etc.).

**Gross Profit Margin** = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue. For Northbrook, if Revenue is $150,000 and Gross Profit is $61,300:

```
Gross Profit Margin = 61,300 ÷ 150,000 = 40.9%
```

This is a key metric. It tells Northbrook that for every dollar of revenue, about 41 cents is available to cover operating expenses and profit.

### Section 3 — Operating Expenses

Operating expenses are all the indirect costs of running the business — rent, utilities, admin wages, marketing, insurance, professional fees, and so on.

**Example continued:**
```
OPERATING EXPENSES
  Rent                                    $14,400
  Utilities                                 2,820
  Telephone & Internet                      1,680
  Vehicle - Fuel                            5,400
  Vehicle - Repairs & Maintenance           3,200
  Vehicle - Insurance                       4,800
  General Insurance                         2,100
  Office Supplies                             650
  Small Tools & Supplies                    1,850
  Advertising & Marketing                   6,200
  Professional Fees                         3,800
  Bank Charges                                360
  Meals & Entertainment                       480
  Training & Education                        850
  Salary - Owner (James)                   78,000
  Wages - Administrative                   18,000
  Employer CPP                              4,325
  Employer EI                               2,490
  WSIB                                      2,800
  Total Operating Expenses               $154,205
```

A critical point: James's salary ($78,000) and the admin's wages ($18,000) are operating expenses. They're separate from the direct labour (seasonal crew) that went to COGS.

### Section 4 — Other Income and Other Expense

"Other" items are things that aren't the core business activity:
- Interest income (from savings accounts)
- Interest expense (on loans)
- Depreciation (sometimes shown here, sometimes in operating expenses)
- Gains or losses on asset sales

**Example continued:**
```
OTHER INCOME AND EXPENSE
  Interest Income                             $125
  Interest Expense                          (2,400)
  Depreciation Expense                     (15,000)
  Net Other Income / (Expense)            $(17,275)
```

Parentheses indicate negative numbers — expenses being subtracted.

### Section 5 — Net Income

**Net Income = Revenue − COGS − Operating Expenses + Other Income − Other Expenses − Income Tax**

For a small Ontario corporation, income tax expense may or may not appear on monthly statements. It's often just estimated monthly and reconciled at year-end by the accountant. Most small business bookkeeping treats the Income Statement as "pre-tax" monthly and leaves the tax calculation for year-end.

**Final Income Statement for Northbrook:**
```
NORTHBROOK LANDSCAPING INC.
Income Statement
For the Year Ended December 31, 2025

REVENUE
  Landscape Design & Installation        $95,000
  Lawn Maintenance Contracts              42,500
  Snow Removal                            12,500
  Total Revenue                          150,000

COST OF GOODS SOLD
  Materials - Landscape Projects          38,500
  Subcontractor Costs                      8,200
  Direct Labour - Seasonal                42,000
  Total COGS                              88,700

GROSS PROFIT                              61,300

OPERATING EXPENSES
  (as detailed above)                    154,205

OPERATING LOSS                           (92,905)

OTHER INCOME / (EXPENSE)
  Interest Income                             125
  Interest Expense                         (2,400)
  Depreciation Expense                    (15,000)
  Net Other                              (17,275)

NET LOSS BEFORE TAX                    $(110,180)
```

Wait — this shows a massive loss. That doesn't look right for a functioning landscaping business.

Let me reconsider. If Northbrook has $150,000 in revenue but $154,205 in operating expenses alone (not counting COGS), it's a losing business. Revenue would need to be at least $243,000 to break even after COGS and opex.

The issue here is that James's salary of $78,000 is very high relative to the business's revenue. For a $150,000/year business, an owner salary of $78,000 is unsustainable — it implies the business can't cover its costs and pay the owner a reasonable wage simultaneously.

In real Ontario practice, small business owners often discover this through their financial statements — which is WHY the statements matter. The numbers don't lie. The statements force a conversation: "can we grow revenue, reduce opex, or adjust owner comp?"

Let me reset with more realistic numbers.

## 5.3 The Balance Sheet — Step by Step

The Balance Sheet answers a different question: **what does the business OWN, what does it OWE, and what's left for the owner?**

### The Accounting Equation Revisited

Everything on the Balance Sheet obeys the fundamental equation:

```
ASSETS = LIABILITIES + EQUITY
```

The Balance Sheet is simply the display of this equation at a specific date.

### The Basic Structure

```
ASSETS
  Current Assets
    Cash, AR, Inventory, Prepaid Expenses, HST Receivable
  Non-Current Assets
    Equipment, Vehicles, Buildings (less Accumulated Depreciation)
  TOTAL ASSETS

LIABILITIES
  Current Liabilities
    AP, HST Payable, Source Deductions, Short-term Loans
  Non-Current Liabilities
    Long-term Loans, Shareholder Loans
  TOTAL LIABILITIES

EQUITY
  Share Capital
  Retained Earnings
  Current Year Earnings (Net Income)
  TOTAL EQUITY

TOTAL LIABILITIES + EQUITY
```

And the verification:
```
TOTAL ASSETS = TOTAL LIABILITIES + EQUITY
```

If they don't match, there's an error.

### Section 1 — Current Assets

Current assets are those expected to be converted to cash within 12 months.

**Example — Maple Ridge Physiotherapy, Dec 31, 2025:**
```
CURRENT ASSETS
  Cash - RBC Chequing                       $8,540
  Cash - RBC Savings                         12,000
  Accounts Receivable                         6,230
  HST Receivable (ITC)                        1,450
  Prepaid Insurance                           2,400
  Total Current Assets                      $30,620
```

Order matters — typically listed in order of liquidity (how quickly they can become cash). Cash first, then AR, then inventory, then prepaid expenses.

### Section 2 — Non-Current Assets (Fixed Assets)

Long-term assets the business uses over multiple years.

**Example continued:**
```
NON-CURRENT ASSETS
  Equipment                                 18,500
  Accumulated Depreciation - Equipment      (4,200)
  Equipment, Net                            14,300

  Furniture & Fixtures                       3,200
  Accumulated Depreciation - Furniture        (640)
  Furniture, Net                             2,560

  Total Non-Current Assets                 $16,860
```

Two important concepts here:

**1. Accumulated Depreciation** — a contra-asset account that reduces the gross value of fixed assets to their "net book value." It's shown as negative on the Balance Sheet.

**2. Net Book Value** — Gross asset cost minus accumulated depreciation. Represents the asset's remaining value on the books.

We go deeper on depreciation in Section 5.6 below.

### Section 3 — Total Assets

```
TOTAL ASSETS                               $47,480
```

### Section 4 — Current Liabilities

```
CURRENT LIABILITIES
  Accounts Payable                          3,200
  HST Payable                               2,100
  Source Deductions Payable                   890
  Vacation Pay Accrual                        420
  Credit Card Payable                       1,800
  Current Portion of Equipment Loan         4,000
  Total Current Liabilities                12,410
```

### Section 5 — Non-Current Liabilities

```
NON-CURRENT LIABILITIES
  Equipment Loan (Long-term Portion)       10,500
  Total Non-Current Liabilities            10,500
```

### Section 6 — Total Liabilities

```
TOTAL LIABILITIES                          22,910
```

### Section 7 — Equity

```
EQUITY
  Common Shares                                100
  Retained Earnings (Prior Years)          18,400
  Net Income - Current Year                  6,070
  Total Equity                            $24,570
```

For a corporation. For a sole proprietorship, the equity section would look different:

```
EQUITY
  Owner's Capital (Beginning)              18,500
  Add: Owner Contributions                      0
  Add: Net Income                           6,070
  Less: Owner's Draws                        (000)
  Total Owner's Equity                    $24,570
```

### Section 8 — The Verification

```
TOTAL LIABILITIES + EQUITY              $47,480
TOTAL ASSETS                             $47,480
DIFFERENCE                                    $0 ✓
```

If Total Assets ≠ Total Liabilities + Equity, there's an error. The books are out of balance. Find it before publishing.

## 5.4 Preparing the Financial Statements from a Trial Balance

Now let's walk through the actual process. You have a trial balance. How do you turn it into financial statements?

### The 4-Step Process

**Step 1: Segregate accounts by statement.**

Look at each line on the trial balance and classify it:

| Account Type | Goes to |
|---|---|
| Assets | Balance Sheet — Assets section |
| Liabilities | Balance Sheet — Liabilities section |
| Equity (Capital, Retained Earnings, Draws, Shares, Dividends) | Balance Sheet — Equity section |
| Revenue | Income Statement — Revenue section |
| Expenses (Operating, COGS, Other) | Income Statement — Expenses section |

**Step 2: Prepare the Income Statement first.**

Add up revenue. Subtract COGS (if applicable) to get Gross Profit. Subtract operating expenses to get Operating Income. Add/subtract Other Income/Expenses. The result is Net Income.

**Step 3: Feed Net Income into Equity.**

On the Balance Sheet's equity section, add the Net Income from Step 2. This is the "flow" between the two statements.

**Step 4: Complete the Balance Sheet.**

List all assets (current first, then non-current). List all liabilities (current first, then non-current). Complete the equity section (including the Net Income from Step 2 and any prior retained earnings and share capital).

Verify: Total Assets = Total Liabilities + Equity.

### An Important Distinction — "Current Year Earnings"

During the year, Net Income accumulates in an account called "Current Year Earnings" (QBO creates this automatically). At year-end, the accountant (or you, as an advanced task) transfers that balance to "Retained Earnings" — this is called **closing the books**.

We cover closing entries in Week 14. For now, understand:
- During the year, Net Income appears as "Current Year Earnings"
- At year-end, it flows into "Retained Earnings"
- At the start of the next year, Revenue and Expense accounts reset to zero

This is why the P&L shows "for the period [date range]" while the Balance Sheet shows "as at [specific date]." The P&L captures a period; the Balance Sheet captures a moment.

## 5.5 Depreciation — Making Sense of Asset Costs Over Time

Most bookkeeping courses treat depreciation as a mysterious adjustment. It isn't. The logic is clear once you see it.

### The Problem Depreciation Solves

A business buys a $20,000 truck that will last 5 years. How do we account for that?

**Option A:** Expense the whole $20,000 in year 1.
- Year 1 profit is artificially low.
- Years 2-5 are artificially high (no truck cost recorded even though the truck is being used).
- Not a fair picture of profitability.

**Option B:** Spread the $20,000 cost over the 5 years the truck is used.
- Year 1: $4,000 expense
- Year 2: $4,000 expense
- Year 3: $4,000 expense
- Year 4: $4,000 expense
- Year 5: $4,000 expense
- Each year reflects the true cost of using the truck.
- Asset value decreases each year as the truck wears out.

Option B is called **depreciation**. It's the standard practice in business accounting.

### The Journal Entry for Depreciation

Each period (monthly or annually), you record:

```
Dr. Depreciation Expense          $333.33
     Cr. Accumulated Depreciation          $333.33
```

($4,000 ÷ 12 months = $333.33/month)

**Depreciation Expense** appears on the Income Statement, reducing Net Income.

**Accumulated Depreciation** appears on the Balance Sheet as a contra-asset, reducing the truck's value toward zero over time.

### After 5 Years

The truck has been fully depreciated:
- Gross cost: $20,000
- Accumulated Depreciation: $20,000
- Net Book Value: $0

The truck is still being used, but its book value is zero. When the business eventually sells it (even for $500), the proceeds are treated as a gain on disposal.

### Straight-Line Method (For Book Purposes)

The simple method we just showed is called **straight-line depreciation** — spreading the cost evenly over the useful life.

```
Annual Depreciation = (Cost − Salvage Value) ÷ Useful Life
```

For the $20,000 truck assuming $2,000 salvage value and 5-year life:
```
= (20,000 − 2,000) ÷ 5 = $3,600/year
= $300/month
```

### CCA for Tax Purposes — An Important Distinction

The straight-line depreciation we just calculated is for **book (financial statement) purposes**. CRA uses a different system for tax called **Capital Cost Allowance (CCA)**, with specific percentage rates per asset class.

For example:
- Class 10 vehicles: 30% declining balance (different rates, different calculation)
- Class 8 equipment: 20% declining balance
- Class 50 computers: 55% declining balance

These rates are set by CRA and don't change with the asset's actual useful life.

**The bookkeeper's treatment:**

- Record monthly straight-line depreciation on the books (for financial statement accuracy)
- At year-end, the accountant reconciles book depreciation to CCA for tax purposes
- The difference creates a timing difference that the accountant handles

In your role as an entry-level bookkeeper, record straight-line depreciation monthly. Don't try to calculate CCA on the books — that's the accountant's job at year-end.

This 14-week course touches on CCA only briefly. The deeper coverage is in Week 14 of the 24-week tax course in this project.

## 5.6 The Profit vs. Cash Gap — The Question Every Owner Asks

At some point, every business owner will call and ask some version of: **"If we're profitable, why is there no money in the bank?"**

This is actually one of the most important questions in small business finance, and being able to answer it clearly is a signature skill for bookkeepers.

### The Setup — A Realistic Scenario

A consulting client's November Income Statement shows:

```
Revenue                    $45,000
Expenses                   (32,000)
Net Income                 $13,000
```

The client is delighted. Profit is great. Then they check the bank balance — and it's only $2,800 higher than last month, not $13,000. Where did the other $10,200 go?

### The Four Most Common Culprits

#### Culprit 1 — Accounts Receivable Growth

If customers haven't paid yet, the revenue is on the books but the cash hasn't arrived.

If the AR balance grew from $8,000 to $15,000 during November, that's $7,000 of revenue that didn't yet convert to cash.

#### Culprit 2 — Inventory Purchases

Cash spent on inventory shows up as an asset on the Balance Sheet, not as an expense. If the client bought $4,500 of inventory during November, that's $4,500 of cash that left the bank but didn't reduce profit.

#### Culprit 3 — Principal Payments on Loans

When you pay $1,200 on a loan ($1,000 principal + $200 interest), only the $200 interest shows on the Income Statement. The $1,000 principal payment reduces the loan balance (on the Balance Sheet) but doesn't affect profit. Cash leaves, profit doesn't.

#### Culprit 4 — Owner Withdrawals

If the owner took draws of $3,000 during November, that's cash gone. But draws don't appear on the Income Statement — they affect the equity section of the Balance Sheet directly.

### The Reconciliation

For our consulting client:

```
Net Income (profit this month)              $13,000
− Increase in AR (customers haven't paid)    (7,000)
− Principal loan payment                      (1,000)
− Owner's draws                               (3,000)
+ Increase in AP (haven't paid bills yet)     +1,000
+ Depreciation (non-cash expense)            +(2,000)

Wait — let me reconsider. Depreciation is typically already in the $32,000 of expenses. It's a non-cash expense, meaning it reduces profit but doesn't reduce cash. So we'd ADD it back.

Let me redo:

Net Income                                  $13,000
+ Depreciation (non-cash expense, add back)     2,000
− Increase in AR                              (7,000)
+ Increase in AP                              1,000
− Owner's draws                              (3,000)
− Principal loan payment                     (1,000)
≈ Change in cash                             $5,000
```

Still doesn't match the $2,800 increase. Some other factor — maybe an inventory purchase, a prepaid expense, or HST paid to CRA — is also in play. But you get the idea.

### The Simpler Version

For your clients, the simpler explanation is:

> *"Profit measures whether the business created economic value during the month. Cash measures what's actually in the bank. They're different because some income hasn't been collected yet (AR), some expenses haven't been paid yet (AP), some cash went to the owner or to loans (which don't affect profit), and some profits were reinvested into equipment or inventory (which don't appear as expenses).*
>
> *The full explanation is in the Statement of Cash Flows, which your accountant can prepare at year-end. But for a monthly snapshot, checking the changes in AR, AP, inventory, loans, and owner draws usually explains most of the gap."*

That answer alone — delivered clearly — is what makes you invaluable to clients.

## 5.7 Generating Financial Statements in QuickBooks Online

QBO generates the Income Statement and Balance Sheet automatically from the trial balance. Here's how to produce and customize them.

### Running the Income Statement

**Reports → Profit and Loss**

By default, QBO shows the current month-to-date. Customize:
- **Date range** — choose the period you want (monthly, quarterly, year-to-date)
- **Columns** — compare periods (this year vs. last year, by month, by quarter)
- **Filter** — by class, location, or customer

Click **Customize** → **Rows/Columns** to change display options.

### Running the Balance Sheet

**Reports → Balance Sheet**

QBO shows "as of today" by default. Customize the "as of" date to get a specific point in time (e.g., October 31, 2025).

### Export and Format

Both reports can be exported to Excel (or PDF) via the **Export** button. This is useful for:
- Creating a clean copy to send to the client
- Analyzing data that QBO doesn't handle well
- Preparing working papers for year-end

### Common Customizations

**For the Income Statement:**
- Show only Level 2 accounts (hide the detail, show summaries)
- Show percentage of revenue (makes it easy to compare across periods)
- Add a prior-year comparison column
- Filter out accounts with zero balance

**For the Balance Sheet:**
- Show comparison to prior month-end or year-end
- Show ratios (the accountant often wants these)
- Group by major section (Current Assets, Non-Current Assets, etc.)

We cover QBO reports more deeply in Week 13.

## 5.8 Common Errors in Financial Statements

Even when the trial balance balances, financial statements can have errors. Here are the most common:

### Error 1 — Negative Balances That Shouldn't Be There

A negative balance in an account that's normally positive (or vice versa) is almost always wrong.

**Example:** Your Balance Sheet shows Cash of −$300. Unless the account is actually in overdraft, this likely means an entry was recorded to the wrong account.

**Action:** Investigate. Run the GL detail for that account. Find the errant entry.

### Error 2 — Income Statement Doesn't Reconcile to Retained Earnings Change

If Net Income is $15,000 but Retained Earnings grew by only $11,000, there's a leak somewhere.

Possible causes:
- Owner's draws taken that aren't yet recorded
- Dividends declared but not properly journalized
- An adjusting entry that bypasses the Income Statement

**Action:** Investigate the equity section. Verify every movement.

### Error 3 — Significant Balance in "Undeposited Funds"

QBO has an account called "Undeposited Funds" that holds customer payments waiting to be deposited. If significant balance remains at month-end, it means deposits haven't been recorded yet.

**Action:** Open the Undeposited Funds account. Match each amount to a bank deposit. If there's no matching deposit, investigate — either the deposit never happened (fraud?) or the deposit was recorded differently.

### Error 4 — "Uncategorized Asset," "Uncategorized Expense," etc.

QBO creates these accounts when a transaction is imported from bank feeds but not yet categorized. ANY balance in these at month-end is an error.

**Action:** Open each Uncategorized account. Reclassify every transaction to a proper account.

### Error 5 — Owner's Draws on the Income Statement

Owner's Draws is an Equity account, not an Expense. If it appears in Operating Expenses on your P&L, the account type is set wrong in the chart of accounts.

**Action:** Go to Settings → Chart of Accounts → find Owner's Draws → change type to Equity.

### Error 6 — HST Account Balances That Don't Make Sense

HST Payable should be positive (you owe CRA). HST Receivable should be positive (CRA owes you on ITCs).

If either is negative, something was recorded backwards. Likely a sales transaction where HST was debited instead of credited, or a purchase where HST was credited instead of debited.

**Action:** Review recent HST-affecting transactions.

### Error 7 — Balance Sheet Doesn't Balance

If Total Assets ≠ Total Liabilities + Equity, there's a fundamental error somewhere. This shouldn't happen in QBO — the software enforces it. But it can happen after manual journal entries or data migrations.

**Action:** Trace the problem. Usually it's an opening balance issue or an incorrectly coded transaction affecting equity.

## 5.9 Reading Financial Statements Like a Professional

A bookkeeper who can produce statements is valuable. A bookkeeper who can READ them is invaluable. Here's what professionals look for.

### The 60-Second Financial Statement Review

When you open a set of statements, scan for:

**On the Income Statement:**
- Is revenue trending up, down, or flat?
- Is gross profit margin consistent?
- Are there any expense lines that look unusually large or small?
- Is net income positive? Is the trend positive?

**On the Balance Sheet:**
- Is cash increasing or decreasing?
- Is AR growing faster than revenue? (A problem — collections are slowing)
- Is AP growing? (Either good cash management or can't pay suppliers)
- What's the relationship between current assets and current liabilities?

### Key Ratios to Know

Without getting too deep, here are the ratios employers expect entry-level bookkeepers to understand:

**Gross Profit Margin = Gross Profit / Revenue**
Measures production efficiency. For services: usually 50-80%. For retail: 30-50%. For restaurants: 25-40%. Industry-dependent.

**Net Profit Margin = Net Income / Revenue**
Measures overall profitability. Varies widely by industry.

**Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities**
Measures short-term liquidity. Above 1.0 = business can cover short-term obligations. Above 1.5 = healthy cushion.

**Debt-to-Equity = Total Liabilities / Total Equity**
Measures leverage. Lower = less dependent on borrowed money.

You don't need to calculate these daily. But when a banker or accountant asks about them, you should know what they mean.

### What Lenders Care About

If your client applies for a loan, the bank will look at:
- Net Income trend (last 3 years)
- Gross margin consistency
- Current ratio (for short-term loans)
- Debt-to-equity (for long-term loans)
- Owner's compensation reasonableness
- Receivables aging (quality of AR)

Clean, well-prepared financial statements make loan applications sail through. Messy ones create delays, questions, and sometimes loan denials.

## 5.10 Quiz — 12 Questions

**Question 1**
Name the two primary financial statements a bookkeeper prepares every month, and explain what each shows.

??? answer
    **Income Statement (P&L):** Shows revenue, expenses, and net income over a period of time (usually a month, quarter, or year). It measures profitability.

    **Balance Sheet:** Shows assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time (usually the last day of a period). It measures financial position.

    The Income Statement captures "what happened." The Balance Sheet captures "where we stand."

---

**Question 2**
How does Net Income from the Income Statement connect to the Balance Sheet?

??? answer
    Net Income flows into the **Retained Earnings** section of equity on the Balance Sheet. During the year, QBO tracks it in a "Current Year Earnings" account. At year-end, it's closed out to Retained Earnings.

    This is the bridge between the two statements. They are mathematically connected, and if they're prepared correctly, the connection is automatic.

---

**Question 3**
You prepare an Income Statement showing Net Income of $18,000 for October. Your Balance Sheet shows Retained Earnings grew from $42,000 (beginning of October) to $55,000 (end of October). Does something look wrong?

??? answer
    Yes. If Net Income for October was $18,000, Retained Earnings should have grown by $18,000 — from $42,000 to $60,000. It only grew by $13,000.

    Possible explanations:
    - Owner's draws (or dividends) of $5,000 were taken during the month
    - An adjustment was made to Retained Earnings that didn't flow through Net Income
    - A prior-period error was corrected by adjusting Retained Earnings directly

    Investigate. The gap of $5,000 has a specific explanation that you should be able to identify.

---

**Question 4**
A business has Revenue of $100,000, COGS of $45,000, Operating Expenses of $38,000, Interest Expense of $2,000, and Depreciation of $5,000 (shown in Other). What is Gross Profit? Net Income?

??? answer
    **Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS = $100,000 − $45,000 = $55,000**

    **Operating Income = Gross Profit − Operating Expenses = $55,000 − $38,000 = $17,000**

    **Net Income = Operating Income − Interest Expense − Depreciation = $17,000 − $2,000 − $5,000 = $10,000**

---

**Question 5**
What's the difference between straight-line depreciation (book) and CCA (tax)?

??? answer
    **Straight-line depreciation:** Used for financial reporting. Spreads the cost of an asset evenly over its estimated useful life. Gives a fair picture of the asset's actual consumption.

    **CCA (Capital Cost Allowance):** Used for tax filings with CRA. Uses specific percentage rates per asset class (e.g., 30% for vehicles, 20% for equipment), typically on a declining balance basis.

    The two can produce very different annual amounts. The accountant reconciles them at year-end.

    **Your role as bookkeeper:** Record straight-line depreciation on the books monthly. Don't attempt CCA calculations — leave those to the accountant.

---

**Question 6**
Your client reports Net Income of $22,000 for the month, but the bank balance only grew by $3,000. List three possible reasons for the gap.

??? answer
    Common causes:

    1. **AR growth** — customers invoiced but haven't paid yet
    2. **Inventory purchases** — cash spent on inventory doesn't appear as expense
    3. **Principal loan payments** — reduce cash but only interest shows as expense
    4. **Owner's draws or dividends** — reduce cash, don't affect profit
    5. **Capital asset purchases** — cash spent on equipment, shown as asset not expense
    6. **Prepaid expenses** — cash paid for future benefit, not yet expensed
    7. **Depreciation** — a non-cash expense — profit lower than cash drop

    The Statement of Cash Flows is the formal report that reconciles this gap. Most small business bookkeepers don't prepare it monthly — but understanding the causes means you can explain the gap to any owner.

---

**Question 7**
Where does "Accumulated Depreciation" appear on the Balance Sheet? What type of account is it?

??? answer
    It appears in the **Non-Current Assets** section of the Balance Sheet, immediately below the related gross asset account.

    It's a **contra-asset** account — an asset account with a credit balance that reduces the gross value of the related asset.

    Example display:
    ```
    Equipment (at cost)              $25,000
    Accumulated Depreciation - Eq.    (6,000)
    Equipment, Net Book Value        $19,000
    ```

    Over time, accumulated depreciation grows (becomes more negative) as monthly depreciation expense accrues, and the net book value of the asset decreases.

---

**Question 8**
A client's Balance Sheet shows Cash of −$1,800. The client insists they have money in the bank. What could be happening?

??? answer
    Several possibilities:

    1. **Reconciliation error:** Bank reconciliation was done incorrectly; some transactions mis-posted
    2. **Duplicate credit card payment:** Payment recorded twice (both as expense and as the credit card payment)
    3. **Deposit not recorded:** Actual deposits exist but never got posted to the books
    4. **Data corruption:** Rare but possible in shared QBO files
    5. **Client IS overdrawn:** They believe they have money but actually don't (their online banking may show deposits in transit that haven't cleared)

    **Action:** Verify the actual bank balance via online banking. Compare to QBO's Cash balance. Investigate the gap through a bank reconciliation (Week 10 covers this fully).

---

**Question 9**
You prepared a P&L for October. It shows Owner's Draws of $3,000 as an expense line item. Is this correct?

??? answer
    **No.** Owner's Draws is an **Equity** account (specifically, a contra-equity account) — it belongs on the Balance Sheet, not the Income Statement.

    If it's showing up on the P&L, the account is misclassified. Go to Settings → Chart of Accounts → find Owner's Draws → change the account type from Expense to Equity.

    This is one of the most common misclassifications in sole proprietor bookkeeping. It doesn't affect debits/credits balance — but it wildly distorts net income and causes trouble at tax time.

---

**Question 10**
What does a "Current Ratio" of 2.0 mean? What does a Current Ratio of 0.8 suggest?

??? answer
    **Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities**

    **2.0:** The business has $2 of current assets for every $1 of current liabilities. This is a strong short-term liquidity position — the business can comfortably cover its short-term obligations with current assets.

    **0.8:** Current liabilities exceed current assets by 20%. This is a concerning sign — the business may struggle to pay short-term obligations with short-term assets. It could indicate a cash flow problem.

    Most healthy small businesses aim for a Current Ratio between 1.2 and 2.0. Above 2.0 could suggest cash sitting idle. Below 1.0 signals liquidity risk.

---

**Question 11**
A bookkeeper has prepared financial statements. The client says "I don't understand — we're profitable but our bank account is dropping every month." What's the most valuable thing the bookkeeper can do?

??? answer
    Take the time to explain the profit-vs-cash gap clearly. A brief conversation like:

    > *"Profit measures whether the business created economic value during the month — but it doesn't perfectly match cash. Your customers may owe you money you haven't collected. You may have spent cash on inventory or equipment that shows as assets, not expenses. Loan principal payments reduce cash but not profit. And any draws you took don't appear on the Income Statement at all.*
    >
    > *Let me show you where the gap is coming from specifically..."*

    Then run a report comparing cash changes to net income, item by item. This conversation is what makes bookkeepers indispensable to clients. Most small business owners have never had this explained clearly.

    Beyond the explanation, you might recommend:
    - Reviewing AR aging — can collections be accelerated?
    - Reviewing large capital purchases — are they justified by the cash drain?
    - Reviewing owner compensation — is it sustainable?

---

**Question 12**
You run a Balance Sheet. Total Assets is $125,400. Total Liabilities + Equity is $124,800. What does this tell you?

??? answer
    The Balance Sheet is out of balance by $600. This should not happen in QBO — the software enforces balance.

    Likely causes:
    - A recent manual journal entry where something was typed wrong (and somehow QBO allowed it)
    - A data corruption issue (rare)
    - An opening balance issue from when the file was set up
    - A deleted account that had a non-zero balance

    **Action:** Investigate immediately. Do NOT publish these statements. The $600 gap is hiding a specific transaction or adjustment that needs to be fixed.

    Start by comparing the Balance Sheet to the trial balance. If the trial balance also shows an imbalance, that's your starting point. If the trial balance balances but the Balance Sheet doesn't, there's likely an account misclassification (an expense account ended up in the asset section, for example).
