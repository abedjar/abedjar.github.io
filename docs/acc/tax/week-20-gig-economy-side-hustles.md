## 20.1 The Foundational Question — Business Income or Employment Income?

Before anything else, you need to determine how to classify gig income. The answer determines which form is used, what expenses are available, what self-employment CPP is owed, and what HST obligations exist.

### The Four-Factor Test (Revisited for Gig Workers)

| Factor | Points to Employment | Points to Business |
|---|---|---|
| **Control** | Platform dictates when/where/how you work | Worker sets their own schedule and methods |
| **Tools** | Platform provides equipment | Worker uses their own car, phone, camera, equipment |
| **Financial risk** | Fixed rate, no investment required | Worker bears the cost of bad days, slow periods, returns |
| **Integration** | Worker is integral to one platform's operation | Worker is independent, could serve multiple clients |

### The Reality for Common Gig Platforms

| Platform | Classification | Key Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Uber/Lyft driver** | **Business income (T2125)** | Uses own vehicle, sets own hours, bears financial risk |
| **Uber Eats delivery** | **Business income (T2125)** | Uses own vehicle or bike, independent schedule |
| **Airbnb host** | **Business income (T2125) or rental income (T776)** | Depends on services provided — see Section 20.4 |
| **Etsy seller** | **Business income (T2125)** | Independent selling business |
| **Freelancer (designer, writer, coder)** | **Business income (T2125)** | Independent contractor providing services |
| **Content creator (YouTube, TikTok, Instagram)** | **Business income (T2125)** | Independent business producing content |
| **TaskRabbit / Handy** | **Business income (T2125)** | Independent service providers |
| **Skip the Dishes / DoorDash driver** | **Business income (T2125)** | Uses own vehicle or bike |
| **Amazon Flex driver** | **Business income (T2125)** | Uses own vehicle |
| **Someone who takes on one casual contract** | May be employment | Depends on the facts — assess individually |

!!! warning "Receiving a T4A does NOT automatically mean business income"
    Many gig platforms issue **T4A slips** to their workers. A T4A is used for both self-employment income (Box 48 — fees for services) AND certain employment-type payments. The slip type alone doesn't determine the classification — the facts of the working relationship do. Most gig platform workers are self-employed regardless of how the platform characterizes them. When in doubt, apply the four-factor test.

## 20.2 The T2125 for Gig Workers — The Core Structure

Every gig worker who earns self-employment income files a **T2125 (Statement of Business or Professional Activities)** — one per distinct business activity.

A client who drives for Uber AND sells on Etsy needs **two T2125 forms** — each is a separate business.

### The Gig Worker T2125 Flow

```
GROSS PLATFORM REVENUE
(all payments received — before platform fees)
    − Platform fees / commissions
    − Vehicle expenses (if applicable)
    − Home office (if applicable)
    − Equipment and supplies
    − Business use of phone and internet
    − Other eligible business expenses
══════════════════════════════════════
NET BUSINESS INCOME → Line 13500 on T1
    − CPP on self-employment (50%) → Line 22200
══════════════════════════════════════
NET INCOME for benefit calculations
```

!!! danger "Report GROSS revenue — not what the platform deposited"
    This is the single most common error on gig worker returns. **Gross income** is the total amount the platform charged the customer — before deducting its commission or service fee. The platform fees are then deducted as a **business expense**.

    **Example:** An Uber driver completes $48,000 in customer fares. Uber deducts its 25% service fee ($12,000). The driver receives $36,000 in deposits.

    **Wrong approach:** Report $36,000 as gross income.
    **Correct approach:** Report $48,000 as gross income, deduct $12,000 as platform fee expense. Net: $36,000.

    The difference matters because CRA cross-checks T4A slip amounts (which report gross), HST remittances, and bank deposits. Reporting net income creates a mismatch that triggers reviews.

---

!!! question "Quick Check"
    Kevin drives for Uber evenings and weekends. His T4A Box 48 shows $48,000. He knows from his Uber app that gross customer fares were $48,000, Uber deducted a 25% service fee of $12,000, and deposited $36,000. His logbook shows 38,000 km driven for Uber out of 52,000 total km for the year. What is his gross income on T2125, how is the Uber service fee treated, and what is his vehicle business-use percentage?

??? answer
    **Gross income on T2125: $48,000** — the full customer fare before Uber's deduction. Never use the net deposit amount as gross income. CRA cross-references T4A Box 48 amounts against T2125 gross revenue: if Kevin reports $36,000 gross but the T4A shows $48,000, CRA flags a $12,000 discrepancy immediately. This mismatch is one of the most common gig economy audit triggers.

    **Uber service fee:** $12,000 (25% × $48,000) is claimed as a **business expense** on the T2125. The net effect on taxable profit is identical ($36,000 before other expenses), but the structure correctly matches the T4A slip and avoids the mismatch.

    **Business-use percentage:**
    Business km ÷ Total km = 38,000 ÷ 52,000 = **73.1%**

    All vehicle expenses — fuel, insurance, maintenance, CCA — are multiplied by 73.1% to determine the deductible portion. The Uber app's trip data is a useful supplement but not sufficient alone as a logbook. Kevin must also track personal km and total annual odometer readings.

## 20.3 Rideshare and Delivery Drivers — Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Skip

This is the largest single group of gig workers you will encounter. In Southern Ontario, the rideshare market is massive — hundreds of thousands of drivers operate across the GTA, Hamilton, Kitchener, and London.

### Tax Slips from Rideshare Platforms

| Platform | Slip Issued | Box | What It Reports |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Uber** | T4A | Box 48 | Gross rideshare/delivery fares |
| **Lyft** | T4A | Box 48 | Gross fares |
| **DoorDash** | T4A | Box 48 | Gross delivery income |
| **Skip the Dishes** | T4A | Box 48 | Gross delivery income |
| **Amazon Flex** | T4A | Box 48 | Gross delivery income |

Box 48 on a T4A = "Fees for services" = **self-employment income** → goes on T2125.

### The HST Rule That Catches Every Uber Driver

!!! danger "Uber drivers are registered for HST from DAY ONE — regardless of revenue"
    Standard HST rule: register when revenue exceeds $30,000 in a rolling 12-month period.

    **Uber/rideshare EXCEPTION:** CRA has ruled that rideshare drivers are providing a **taxi service** (broadly defined). Taxi service providers must register for HST **immediately upon commencing** — with **no small supplier exemption**.

    This means a driver who completed their first Uber trip on January 5, 2025 — earning $0.47 on their first fare — is legally required to be HST-registered from that date.

    In practice, CRA has issued administrative relief for drivers who didn't know about this rule and retroactively registered. But going forward — advise every new rideshare driver: **register for HST before your first trip**.

    The good news: Uber collects and remits HST on fares on behalf of its drivers through its own HST account. The driver does NOT charge HST separately on Uber fares. But the driver must still:
    1. Have their own HST registration number
    2. Claim **Input Tax Credits (ITCs)** on vehicle expenses and other business costs
    3. File HST returns (usually annually if revenue is under $1.5M)

    The ITC recovery can be significant — a driver spending $15,000/year on fuel, insurance, and maintenance at 13% HST = $1,950 in HST paid → most of which is recoverable as ITCs.

### Vehicle Expenses — The Backbone of Rideshare Deductions

For rideshare drivers, vehicle expenses are the largest deduction category. They are calculated using the actual expense method:

**Business-use percentage = Business km ÷ Total km**

The logbook is mandatory. For rideshare drivers, the Uber/DoorDash app provides a useful supplementary record (total trips, km driven on platform) — but this alone is NOT sufficient. Drivers must also track:

- Total km driven (odometer readings at year start and year end)
- Personal km driven (commuting, errands, personal trips)
- Platform km while on-trip (from app records)
- Deadhead km while waiting for a trip (also business use)

!!! example "Case — Kevin's Uber Business (Complete T2125)"
    Kevin drives full-time for Uber in the GTA. His 2025 T4A (Box 48): **$62,400** (gross fares).

    Platform data shows 38,000 km driven on the Uber platform during the year.
    His logbook shows 52,000 total km for the year.
    Personal km: 14,000 (weekends, family trips, personal errands).
    **Business-use %:** (52,000 − 14,000) ÷ 52,000 = **73.1%**

    Wait — the correct formula is: Business km ÷ Total km = 38,000 ÷ 52,000 = **73.1%**

    **Vehicle expenses:**

    | Expense | Annual Total | × 73.1% | Deductible |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Fuel | $8,400 | 73.1% | $6,140 |
    | Insurance | $2,800 | 73.1% | $2,047 |
    | Oil changes and maintenance | $1,200 | 73.1% | $877 |
    | Car washes (business trips) | $480 | 73.1% | $351 |
    | Licence and registration | $180 | 73.1% | $132 |
    | CCA (vehicle, Class 10, 30%) | $4,200 | 73.1% | $3,070 |
    | **Total vehicle deduction** | | | **$12,617** |

    **Other business expenses:**

    | Expense | Amount | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | Uber service fee (25% of $62,400) | $15,600 | Platform commission |
    | Cell phone (80% business use) | $1,152 ($1,440 × 80%) | Used for navigation + app |
    | In-car supplies (water, mints, air freshener) | $380 | Passenger amenities |
    | Dash cam | $185 | Safety equipment — Class 8 CCA |
    | Accounting fee | $200 | This year's prep fee |
    | **Total other expenses** | **$17,517** | |

    **T2125 Summary:**

    ```
    Gross income (T4A Box 48):     $62,400
    − Platform fees:               ($15,600)
    − Vehicle expenses:            ($12,617)
    − Other expenses:              ($17,517)
    ═══════════════════════════════════════
    Net business income:           $16,666  → Line 13500
    ```

    **CPP on self-employment:**
    Net income $16,666 − $3,500 basic exemption = $13,166
    Total CPP: $13,166 × 11.9% = $1,567
    Deductible half (Line 22200): $784
    Credit half (Line 31000): $784 × 15% = $118

    **After CPP deduction:** Net income for benefits = $16,666 − $784 = **$15,882**

    Kevin appeared to earn $62,400 from Uber. His actual taxable income is $15,882. His effective tax rate on Uber earnings is dramatically lower than most people imagine — because the vehicle expenses are so substantial. This is why Uber drivers who file correctly often owe far less tax than they fear.

    **But Kevin also owes quarterly tax instalments** — CRA expects self-employed people earning over $3,000 in net tax to pay quarterly. At $15,882 net income, his combined tax is approximately $3,200 — just over the threshold. Advise him to pay quarterly to avoid interest.

## 20.4 Airbnb and Short-Term Rentals — Rental vs. Business

The Airbnb question is one of the most misunderstood situations in gig economy tax. The answer to "which form do I use — T776 or T2125?" depends entirely on what services the host provides.

### The Service Test

| Host Provides | Classification | Form |
|---|---|---|
| Space only — the guest has a key and the host is rarely present | **Rental income** | **T776** |
| Space + cleaning between guests + linens + toiletries + check-in services | **Business income** | **T2125** |
| Space + breakfast + concierge + daily housekeeping | **Business income (B&B)** | **T2125** |

The distinguishing factor: **If it looks like a hotel, it's a business. If it looks like a rental, it's rental income.**

CRA's administrative guidance: if the host provides services beyond basic accommodation (beyond providing a key), the income is business income.

### HST on Airbnb

Unlike regular long-term rentals (which are exempt from HST), **short-term rentals (less than 30 consecutive days)** are a **taxable supply** subject to HST.

Airbnb hosts earning over $30,000 in short-term rental revenue **must register for HST** and remit on their rental income. Airbnb has its own HST account and may collect and remit on some transactions — but this varies and does not eliminate the host's own registration requirement in all cases.

!!! warning "Airbnb hosts who hit $30,000 are one of CRA's stated enforcement targets"
    CRA has formal data-sharing agreements with Airbnb Canada. They receive host revenue data and cross-match it to T1 returns. An Airbnb host earning $32,000 who doesn't register for HST or report the income correctly will eventually receive a letter.

!!! example "Case — Sandra's Airbnb Basement Suite"
    Sandra rents out her finished basement suite on Airbnb year-round in London, Ontario. She provides: weekly cleaning, fresh linens, toiletries, and responds to guest queries.

    Her 2025 Airbnb revenue: **$28,400**

    **Classification:** Business income — she provides services beyond accommodation. Uses **T2125**.

    The basement suite is 30% of her home (1 bedroom, living area = 600 sq ft of a 2,000 sq ft home).

    **Business expenses:**

    | Expense | Total | × 30% Business | Deductible |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Mortgage interest | $18,000 | 30% | $5,400 |
    | Property taxes | $4,800 | 30% | $1,440 |
    | Insurance (home + short-term rental rider) | $2,400 | 30% | $720 |
    | Utilities | $3,600 | 30% | $1,080 |
    | Cleaning supplies | $640 | 100% (direct) | $640 |
    | Linens and towels (replaced this year) | $480 | 100% | $480 |
    | Toiletries for guests | $320 | 100% | $320 |
    | Airbnb platform fee (3%): $28,400 × 3% | $852 | 100% | $852 |
    | Professional photography (listing update) | $250 | 100% | $250 |
    | **Total expenses** | | | **$11,182** |

    ```
    Gross revenue:       $28,400
    − Total expenses:   ($11,182)
    ═════════════════════════════
    Net income:          $17,218  → Line 13500
    ```

    **HST:** Revenue $28,400 — under $30,000 threshold. **No HST registration required** for 2025. But Sandra must monitor closely — if she increases occupancy even slightly next year, she crosses the threshold.

    **CCA on the basement suite:** Sandra should generally **avoid claiming CCA** on the residential portion of her home — it would affect her Principal Residence Exemption on the basement portion when she eventually sells. This is the same principle as the rental landlord (Week 9). Advise her to skip CCA.

## 20.5 Etsy, Shopify, and Marketplace Sellers

Canadians selling handmade goods, vintage items, digital downloads, and physical products online on Etsy, Shopify, and other platforms are operating businesses. Their income is self-employment income — full stop.

### Common Deductions for Etsy/Shopify Sellers

| Expense | Deductible? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Raw materials and supplies | ✅ Yes — Cost of Goods Sold | Materials to make products |
| Packaging and shipping materials | ✅ Yes | Boxes, tissue, bubble wrap |
| Postage and courier fees | ✅ Yes | Actual shipping costs |
| Platform fees (Etsy listing + transaction fees) | ✅ Yes | ~6.5% on Etsy transactions |
| Payment processing fees (Stripe, PayPal) | ✅ Yes | ~2.9% per transaction |
| Photography equipment (business use) | ✅ Yes — CCA | For product photos |
| Prop purchases (for product styling) | ✅ Yes | If exclusively for business |
| Home office (if orders managed from home) | ✅ Yes | Business-use-of-home rules apply |
| Advertising (Etsy Ads, Instagram, Facebook Ads) | ✅ Yes | 100% |
| Software (design tools, accounting, inventory) | ✅ Yes | Canva, QuickBooks, etc. |
| Craft fairs and market booth fees | ✅ Yes | Vendor fees |
| Business portion of phone/internet | ✅ Yes | Proportionate business use |
| Research purchases (buying competitor products) | ✅ Maybe | Must be genuinely for business research |

### The Cost of Goods Sold Calculation

For product-based businesses, the T2125 requires a **cost of goods sold (COGS)** calculation:

```
Opening inventory (January 1)
  + Purchases of raw materials during the year
  − Closing inventory (December 31)
══════════════════════════════════════
= Cost of Goods Sold
```

!!! example "Case — Rachel's Handmade Jewelry Business (Etsy)"
    Rachel started selling handmade silver jewelry on Etsy two years ago. It grew into a real business.

    **2025 T4A Box 48 from Etsy:** None — Etsy Canada may not issue T4As for all transactions. Rachel must report from her own records. She exports her Etsy payment account history to calculate total gross sales.

    **Gross Etsy sales (from account statement):** $38,200

    **Cost of Goods Sold:**
    - Opening inventory (silver, clasps, findings): $1,800
    - Purchases during 2025: $9,400 (silver, beads, wire, clasps)
    - Closing inventory (December 31 physical count): $2,200
    - **COGS: $1,800 + $9,400 − $2,200 = $9,000**

    **Other expenses:**

    | Expense | Amount |
    |---|---|
    | Etsy fees (listing + transaction + payments) | $2,490 |
    | Shipping costs | $3,200 |
    | Packaging materials | $640 |
    | Home office (20% of rent $15,600) | $3,120 |
    | Photography setup (new lightbox and backdrop) | $380 |
    | Canva subscription | $168 |
    | Craft fair booth fees | $450 |
    | Business portion of internet (40%): $960 × 40% | $384 |
    | **Total other expenses** | **$10,832** |

    ```
    Gross income:        $38,200
    − COGS:              ($9,000)
    Gross profit:        $29,200
    − Other expenses:   ($10,832)
    ═════════════════════════════
    Net income:          $18,368  → Line 13500
    ```

    **HST consideration:** Gross revenue $38,200 exceeds the $30,000 threshold. **Rachel must register for HST and charge 13% on all Ontario sales.** She also remits HST on the total. However, she claims ITCs on all HST paid on her business supplies and expenses.

    **This is the conversation Rachel needs:** *"Your Etsy sales crossed $30,000 this year, which means you legally need to be registered for HST. Going forward, you'll add 13% HST to your prices for Canadian customers. The good news is you recover HST you've paid on your materials and supplies — your input tax credits partially offset what you remit."*

## 20.6 Freelancers — The Largest Gig Category

Freelancers (graphic designers, writers, web developers, marketers, photographers, translators, virtual assistants) are the most diverse gig economy category. They typically have multiple clients, variable monthly income, and a mix of working from home and at client sites.

### The T4A Problem — When Clients Issue Slips

When a freelancer earns more than **$500 from a single business client** in a year, that client is required to issue a T4A Box 48 slip to the freelancer.

However:
- Many clients issue T4A slips but the freelancer also has income from clients who didn't issue slips
- Some clients report amounts incorrectly
- Some clients forget to issue the slip

**Your rule:** Verify ALL T4A Box 48 amounts through CRA My Account. Compare to the freelancer's own invoicing records. The total gross income on the T2125 must include income from ALL clients — not just those who issued T4As.

!!! example "Case — Ahmed's Freelance Design Business"
    Ahmed is a self-employed graphic designer in Waterloo. He works from a dedicated home office (200 sq ft of his 800 sq ft apartment).

    **Income verification:**

    | Client | Invoice Amount | T4A Received? |
    |---|---|---|
    | TechCorp (regular client) | $28,000 | ✅ T4A Box 48: $28,000 |
    | Startup XYZ | $9,400 | ✅ T4A Box 48: $9,400 |
    | Three individual business clients | $7,200 total | ❌ No T4A (under $500 each) |
    | Foreign client (US company) | $4,800 USD = $6,576 CAD | ❌ No Canadian T4A |
    | **Total gross income** | **$51,176** | |

    CRA My Account shows T4As totalling $37,400. Ahmed's own records show $51,176. He must report **$51,176** — not just the T4A amounts.

    **Deductible expenses:**

    | Expense | Amount | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | Adobe Creative Suite | $960 | 100% business |
    | Fonts and stock assets | $340 | 100% business |
    | MacBook Pro (Class 50, 55%): $2,200 × 55% × 50% half-year | $605 | CCA |
    | Home office (25% of $15,000 rent + $1,200 utilities) | $4,050 | 25% × $16,200 |
    | Business phone (70% business): $1,440 × 70% | $1,008 | |
    | Professional development (online courses) | $890 | |
    | Advertising (LinkedIn Premium, portfolio hosting) | $720 | |
    | Accounting fee | $300 | |
    | **Total expenses** | **$8,873** | |

    ```
    Gross income:        $51,176
    − Expenses:          ($8,873)
    ═════════════════════════════
    Net income:          $42,303  → Line 13500
    ```

    **HST:** $51,176 gross revenue — exceeds $30,000. Ahmed must be registered for HST, charging 13% on services to Canadian clients (the US client does not pay Canadian HST — zero-rated export of services).

    **CPP on self-employment:**
    $42,303 − $3,500 = $38,803 × 11.9% = $4,618
    Deductible half (Line 22200): **$2,309**
    Credit half: $2,309 × 15% = **$346**

    **After CPP deduction:** Net income = $42,303 − $2,309 = **$39,994**

## 20.7 Content Creators — YouTube, TikTok, Instagram, Podcasting

Content creators represent the most rapidly growing gig segment — and the most complex. Their income streams are numerous, their expenses are unusual by traditional standards, and many don't realize they are operating a business.

### Income Sources for Content Creators

| Source | Platform | Tax Slip | Reporting |
|---|---|---|---|
| AdSense revenue | YouTube/Google | T4A or foreign income | Line 13500 (T2125) |
| Brand deals / sponsorships | Any | T4A (if Canadian payor) | T2125 |
| Affiliate commissions | Amazon, etc. | Sometimes T4A | T2125 |
| Merchandise sales | Own store/Merch shelf | None (self-report) | T2125 with COGS |
| Subscription income | Patreon, Substack | Foreign income | T2125 |
| Platform tips/gifts | TikTok Stars, Twitch Bits | Foreign income | T2125 |
| Course/digital product sales | Teachable, Gumroad | Foreign income | T2125 |

!!! warning "Google pays Canadian creators in USD — convert everything"
    YouTube AdSense pays in US dollars. Use the Bank of Canada annual average rate to convert to CAD. Keep the annual Google AdSense payment summaries as documentation. Many creators have monthly payments — use the annual average rate (not the rate on each payment date) for simplicity.

!!! warning "The personal benefit problem — CRA's main audit target for creators"
    CRA is skeptical of creators who deduct:
    - Luxury travel claimed as "content research" with no content produced
    - Designer clothing claimed as "on-camera wardrobe" but worn daily
    - Restaurant meals claimed as "food content" without a published review
    - Home renovations claimed as "studio upgrades" that primarily benefit the personal living space

    The test is always: **Would this expense have been incurred if you were not a content creator?** If yes — it's personal. If no — it's business.

    A ring light purchased because you're a creator: deductible.
    A new kitchen renovation claimed because you film cooking videos: the incremental cost of making it "studio quality" may be deductible; the general kitchen upgrade is not.

!!! example "Case — Sofia's YouTube Food Channel"
    Sofia has a YouTube channel with 85,000 subscribers focused on Filipino-Canadian recipes. She generates income from YouTube AdSense and two brand sponsorships.

    **2025 Income:**

    | Source | Amount (CAD) | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | YouTube AdSense (USD, converted) | $14,200 | Annual Google payment summary × Bank of Canada rate |
    | Sponsorship — kitchen brand | $4,500 | T4A Box 48 from Canadian company |
    | Sponsorship — food delivery app | $3,800 | T4A Box 48 from Canadian company |
    | **Gross income** | **$22,500** | |

    **Expenses:**

    | Expense | Amount | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | Camera (Sony ZV-E10, purchased in 2025) | $1,100 → Class 8: $1,100 × 20% × 50% = $110 CCA | Half-year rule |

## 20.8 The Hobby vs. Business Question — Why It Matters Enormously

CRA distinguishes between a **business** (operated with a reasonable expectation of profit) and a **hobby** (pursued for personal satisfaction without genuine profit intent).

The difference:
- **Business:** All expenses deductible. Losses can offset other income.
- **Hobby:** Income taxable. Expenses NOT deductible beyond the income earned. Cannot create a loss.

### CRA's "Reasonable Expectation of Profit" Factors

| Factor | Points to Business | Points to Hobby |
|---|---|---|
| Time committed | Significant regular time | Occasional, when motivated |
| Profit history | Made a profit in some years | Chronic losses every year |
| Business planning | Written plan, separate accounts | No plan |
| Expertise | Developed skills intentionally | Personal interest only |
| Nature of activity | Similar to profitable businesses | Primarily recreational |
| Steps to improve profitability | Changed approach when losing | Never changed approach |

!!! example "Case — The Photography Business vs. The Photography Hobby"
    **Scenario A — Business:** Marco shoots weddings and corporate events professionally. He has a website, a booking system, charges market rates, and earned $28,000 in 2025 though his expenses totalled $32,000 (new camera equipment). He has a business plan to reach $60,000 within 2 years. His $4,000 net loss can offset his T4 income from his part-time job.

    **Scenario B — Hobby:** Marco shoots wildlife photography on weekends. He occasionally sells prints on Fine Art America, earning $1,800 in 2025. He's never tried to grow this into a business. His expenses ($4,200 in lens upgrades and park passes) significantly exceed his income. CRA would view this as a hobby — income taxable, expenses NOT deductible beyond $1,800.

    **The line:** Genuine attempt to profit over time = business. Pursuing personal passion with incidental income = hobby. If a client has 3+ years of losses, be prepared to explain why the business still has a reasonable expectation of profit — or CRA may reclassify it as a hobby on audit.

## 20.9 HST — The Gig Worker's Most Common Compliance Gap

Most gig workers are completely unaware of their HST obligations. This is the area where CRA will increasingly focus enforcement.

### The $30,000 Threshold (With the Uber Exception)

| Worker Type | HST Registration Trigger |
|---|---|
| Most freelancers, Etsy sellers, content creators | When revenue exceeds **$30,000** in any rolling 12-month period |
| Airbnb / short-term rental hosts | When revenue exceeds **$30,000** (short-term rental is a taxable supply) |
| Rideshare drivers (Uber, Lyft) | **Day one — immediate registration, no threshold** |

### The Practical Impact of HST Registration

Once registered, the gig worker:
1. **Charges HST** on applicable services/products to Canadian customers
2. **Remits net HST** to CRA (HST collected minus ITCs on business expenses)
3. **Files HST returns** (usually annually for small operators)

**The hidden benefit:** ITCs (Input Tax Credits) recover the HST the worker paid on business expenses. For a freelancer paying 13% HST on $20,000 of deductible expenses = **$2,600 in ITCs** recovered annually. This partially or fully offsets the administrative burden of registration.

!!! example "Case — When Natalie Crossed the Threshold Mid-Year"
    Natalie is a freelance social media manager. She was under $30,000 for her first two years. In 2025, she monitored her revenue:

    | Month | Cumulative Revenue | HST Status |
    |---|---|---|
    | January–June | $22,400 | Under threshold |
    | July | $26,800 | Under threshold |
    | August 15 | **$31,200** | **THRESHOLD CROSSED** |

    From **August 15**, Natalie must register for HST. She must:
    1. Apply for an HST account at CRA (Business Number application — RC1 form or online)
    2. Begin charging 13% HST on all invoices from registration date
    3. Submit her first HST return by the deadline

    **Retroactive correction:** Natalie did NOT charge HST on invoices before August 15 — she was under the threshold. She is not required to retroactively collect HST on those invoices. The obligation starts from registration.

    **Going forward:** Her prices effectively increase by 13% (or she absorbs the HST in her margin). For B2B clients (businesses), HST is neutral — they claim it as ITCs. For individual consumers, the 13% is real additional cost.

    **Your advice to every gig client nearing $30,000:** *"You're at $24,000 in revenue with 4 months left in the year. Based on your trajectory, you'll likely cross $30,000 by October. When you do, you're legally required to register for HST within 29 days. Let's set up your account now so you're not scrambling."*

## 20.10 Record-Keeping for Gig Workers — What Survives a CRA Review

Gig workers are among CRA's highest-audit-frequency categories — cash-light businesses with significant expenses and variable income. Building your client's record-keeping habits in Year 1 protects them for life.

### The Minimum Viable Record System

**For every gig worker, establish from Day 1:**

1. **A separate business bank account** — never commingle personal and business funds. This is the single most important record-keeping discipline. When personal and business are mixed, every transaction must be analyzed at audit time. A separate account makes business income and expenses self-evident.

2. **A simple income log** — a spreadsheet or accounting app (Wave, QuickBooks Self-Employed) recording every payment received: date, amount, client/platform, description.

3. **A receipt system** — digital is fine. Scan or photograph every receipt immediately (thermal paper fades). Organize by year and expense category. Google Drive, Dropbox, or a receipt app like Dext.

4. **A mileage logbook** (vehicle users only) — odometer reading at year start and end. Every business trip: date, destination, purpose, km. Many rideshare drivers use their platform's trip history supplemented by a paper or app-based log for deadhead km.

5. **Annual platform summaries** — download the annual payment summary from every platform (Uber, Etsy, Airbnb, Google AdSense, PayPal, Stripe) at year end. These are your primary income records.

### Platform-Specific Documentation

| Platform | Annual Document | Where to Find It |
|---|---|---|
| Uber | Annual Tax Summary | Uber driver app → Earnings → Tax Info |
| DoorDash | Year-End Statement | Dasher app → Earnings |
| Airbnb | Earnings Summary | Airbnb host dashboard |
| Etsy | Monthly and Annual Payment Account | Etsy seller dashboard → Finances |
| Google AdSense (YouTube) | Annual Payment Summary | AdSense dashboard → Payments |
| Amazon Associates | Earnings Report | Associates dashboard |
| Shopify | Revenue Reports | Shopify admin → Analytics |

!!! tip "Tell every gig client: download your annual summaries in January"
    Platforms update these annually. They are essential for income verification and T2125 preparation. Some platforms only keep records for 12–24 months. A client who doesn't download their Etsy annual summary before it rolls off may have no income verification for a CRA review 3 years later. Build the January download habit.

## 20.11 The Multiple Gig Worker — Stacking Income from Several Platforms

Many gig workers earn from multiple sources simultaneously. This is increasingly common — an Uber driver who also does DoorDash on slow nights. An Etsy seller who also freelances as a photographer. A content creator who also teaches online courses.

**The key rule:** Each distinct business activity on its own **T2125**. However, **shared expenses** (a vehicle used for both Uber and Amazon Flex, a home office used for both freelancing and content creation) are allocated to each business proportionally — or one T2125 is designated as the primary user.

!!! example "Case — Priya's Three Revenue Streams"
    Priya is a versatile gig worker in Kitchener. She does:
    1. **Freelance translation** (English ↔ Punjabi): $18,400 from corporate clients (T4As received)
    2. **Etsy print-on-demand store** (digital file downloads): $8,200 (no T4A — Etsy only)
    3. **Language tutoring (online)**: $6,800 from individual students (no slips)

    **Three T2125 forms:**

    | Business | Gross Income | Key Expenses | Net Income |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Translation | $18,400 | Computer (allocated), phone, professional dictionary software | ~$15,800 |
    | Etsy (digital) | $8,200 | Canva, platform fees (6.5%), PayPal fees | ~$6,900 |
    | Language tutoring | $6,800 | Zoom subscription, teaching materials | ~$6,300 |
    | **Total** | **$33,400** | | **~$29,000** |

    **Shared expenses:** Priya uses one computer for all three businesses. She allocates CCA 60% to translation (primary use), 20% to Etsy, 20% to tutoring.

    **HST:** Total gross $33,400. Exceeds $30,000. **HST registration required.** However: language tutoring services may qualify for **HST exemption** (educational services to individuals). Translation services are taxable. Etsy digital downloads to Canadian customers are taxable. Mixed situation — register for HST on the taxable portions, confirm tutoring exemption.

    **CPP on total net self-employment: ~$29,000 − $3,500 = $25,500 × 11.9% = $3,035**

    Priya has a complex return that most preparers handle incorrectly. Three T2125 forms, HST complexity, shared expense allocation, and CPP on all three income streams. A preparer who handles this correctly earns $350–$500 for Priya's return — and she's a client who refers every person in her multilingual professional network.

## 20.12 Ontario-Specific Gig Economy Considerations

### Ontario Trillium Benefit for Gig Workers

Many gig workers are low-to-moderate income — especially those building a side hustle while still employed. They often rent apartments and qualify for significant OTB benefits. Self-employed income counts for OTB income-testing — so a gig worker's net income (after business expenses and CPP deduction) determines their benefit entitlement.

A driver with $62,400 in gross fares but only $15,882 in net income (like Kevin from Section 20.3) qualifies for a much higher OTB than if gross income were used. The deductions matter for benefits, not just for tax.

### Ontario LIFT Credit — Gig Workers

The LIFT Credit requires **employment income** — self-employment income does not qualify. A full-time gig worker with $35,000 in net self-employment income gets **no LIFT Credit**. But a worker who has both a part-time T4 job AND gig income qualifies for the LIFT Credit on the employment portion.

!!! example "Case — The LIFT Credit for a Hybrid Worker"
    Maria works part-time at Tim Hortons ($12,000 T4 employment) and drives Uber on evenings and weekends (net self-employment income $9,000).

    **LIFT Credit:** Applies to employment income. Her combined income is $21,000. Employment income $12,000 > $0. Ontario basic tax is approximately $1,062. LIFT Credit: Ontario income is low and employment income exists → LIFT Credit eliminates Ontario tax.

    If Maria only had $21,000 from Uber (self-employment only): LIFT Credit = $0. She pays Ontario tax. Same dollar amount of income — completely different Ontario tax outcome based solely on the income source.

    This is one of the most important distinctions for low-income gig workers. When advising clients on structuring their income mix, the LIFT Credit availability is a real consideration.

---

!!! tip "In your tax software"
    - **T2125 — one per distinct business:** If a client drives Uber AND sells on Etsy, enter two separate T2125 forms. Each business is independent. Shared expenses (home office, vehicle, phone, internet) are allocated proportionally between businesses. Label each T2125 clearly with the business type and industry code.

    - **Gross revenue entry:** Always enter the T4A Box 48 amount (gross fares or fees) as income — not the net bank deposit. Enter the platform commission (Uber service fee, Etsy transaction fee, Airbnb host service fee) as a separate business expense line. This structure matches the T4A and prevents the income mismatch that triggers CRA flags.

    - **Rideshare HST registration:** Remind every new Uber or Lyft client to register for an HST account before their first trip — there is no $30,000 threshold for taxi/rideshare services. For all other gig workers, monitor cumulative revenue against the $30,000 threshold. Build a note in the client file for the year they're approaching it and prompt them to register when they cross.

    - **Vehicle CCA:** Enter vehicle cost, class (Class 10 for most; Class 10.1 for vehicles over the prescribed luxury threshold), and acquisition date in the CCA section of T2125. Apply the business-use percentage. The half-year rule (50% of normal CCA in year of acquisition) applies automatically when the acquisition date is entered correctly.

    - **Annual platform summaries:** Uber Tax Summary, Etsy Annual Report, Google AdSense Annual Statement, DoorDash Year-End Statement — advise every gig client to download these in January each year before they archive. These are the primary income records for T2125 preparation and CRA review defence. Store a copy in the client file.

    - **Self-employment CPP:** Software calculates 11.9% on net self-employment income above the $3,500 basic exemption. The deductible half goes on Line 22200 (reducing net income); the credit half (at 15%) goes on Line 31000. Verify both appear — especially for clients with low net income close to the $3,500 exemption level where the calculation may net to near zero.

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - Virtually all gig income is **self-employment business income** reported on **T2125** → Line 13500 — not employment income. The four-factor test determines classification
