## 11.1 What an RRSP Actually Does — The Real Mechanism

Most people describe an RRSP as "tax-free savings." That is imprecise — and the misunderstanding costs clients money.

An RRSP is a **tax deferral** vehicle. Here is exactly what happens:

```
CONTRIBUTE → Get a deduction now    (reduces income at TODAY's marginal rate)
GROW       → Investments compound tax-free inside the plan
WITHDRAW   → Pay full income tax    (at TOMORROW's marginal rate)
```

The genius of this system is the **rate arbitrage**:

- You contribute when you earn the most → large deduction at your highest rate
- You withdraw in retirement when income is lower → smaller tax bill at a lower rate
- The spread between those two rates is your **permanent tax saving** — money that never gets taxed, ever

!!! example "The Rate Arbitrage in Numbers"
    David earns $110,000. His marginal rate is 43.41% (federal 26% + Ontario 17.41%).

    He contributes $15,000 to his RRSP today.
    **Tax saved immediately:** $15,000 × 43.41% = **$6,512**

    In retirement, David's only income is OAS + CPP + RRSP withdrawals, totalling $45,000.
    His marginal rate is now 29.65%.
    **Tax paid on the same $15,000 withdrawal:** $15,000 × 29.65% = **$4,448**

    **Permanent tax saving: $6,512 − $4,448 = $2,064** — on this one contribution alone, before counting a single dollar of tax-free growth inside the plan.

    If those $15,000 grow at 6% annually for 20 years inside the RRSP, they become **$48,122**. The compounding happened entirely tax-free. The value of deferral is enormous.

!!! warning "When the RRSP math works against you"
    The RRSP is less effective — and can actively backfire — when:

    - A client contributes at a **low marginal rate** and withdraws at a **higher one** (a student contributing at 20.05% who later withdraws at 40% has hurt themselves)
    - A client will face **large mandatory RRIF withdrawals** that push income above the OAS clawback threshold (~$90,997), triggering a 15% clawback
    - A client is in the **15% federal bracket** — the TFSA usually wins here because RRSP withdrawals add to income and can reduce income-tested benefits like GIS

    Your job is not to blindly tell every client to "maximize the RRSP." Your job is to understand when it is the right tool — and when it isn't.

## 11.2 RRSP Deduction Limit — Calculating the Exact Room

The **RRSP deduction limit** is the maximum a client can contribute and deduct in a given year. It is printed on every NOA — but you must understand the formula to verify it and explain it confidently.

### The Formula

```
18% of prior year earned income
  (maximum $31,560 for the 2025 tax year)
  − Pension Adjustment (PA) from T4 Box 52
  + Pension Adjustment Reversal (PAR, if applicable)
  + Unused RRSP deduction room carried forward from all prior years
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
= RRSP Deduction Limit (printed on your NOA and in CRA My Account)
```

### What Counts as "Earned Income" for RRSP Purposes?

This is not the same as Total Income — a critical distinction that many preparers miss.

| ✅ Included in Earned Income | ❌ Excluded from Earned Income |
|---|---|
| Employment income (net of union dues and employment expenses) | Interest, dividends, capital gains |
| Net self-employment income (business, professional, commission) | Pension income (DB, DC, CPP, OAS) |
| Net rental income | RRSP/RRIF withdrawals |
| Royalties from work/inventions | EI benefits |
| Taxable alimony/support received | Retiring allowances |
| Research grants (net of expenses) | Scholarships (exempt portion) |

!!! example "Case — Calculating Exact RRSP Room"
    Sandra's 2024 income sources:

    | Item | Amount |
    |---|---|
    | Employment income | $82,000 |
    | Union dues (net from employment income) | ($960) |
    | Net rental income | $4,200 |
    | Capital gains | $8,000 |
    | **Earned income for RRSP** | **$85,240** |

    Note: Capital gains do **not** count as earned income.

    RRSP room generated: $85,240 × 18% = $15,343 (under the $31,560 cap)

    Pension adjustment (T4 Box 52): ($4,800)

    Unused room from prior years (from 2024 NOA): $11,400

    **2025 RRSP Deduction Limit:** $15,343 − $4,800 + $11,400 = **$21,943**

### Where to Find the Limit — Always Verify Both Sources

| Source | What It Shows | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Prior year NOA (last page) | Calculated 2025 deduction limit | Starting point — may not reflect contributions already made this year |
| **CRA My Account** | Real-time updated limit | **Always use this before advising any contribution** |

!!! danger "Never guess the RRSP room — always verify"
    A client who says "I think I have about $20,000 in room" may be off by thousands. An over-contribution penalty is 1% per month on the excess over the $2,000 buffer. One phone call to check My Account takes 3 minutes and can prevent a costly error. Always check.

---

!!! question "Quick Check"
    Sandra earned $78,000 in 2024 from employment (net of union dues already excluded). Her T4 Box 52 shows a pension adjustment of $6,400. She has $9,800 in unused RRSP room carried forward from prior years. What is her 2025 RRSP deduction limit?

    ??? answer
        Earned income for RRSP purposes: $78,000. RRSP room from earned income: $78,000 × 18% = $14,040 (well below the $31,560 cap). Minus pension adjustment: $14,040 − $6,400 = $7,640. Plus prior year carryforward: $7,640 + $9,800 = **$16,840**. Always verify this against CRA My Account before any contribution — the NOA figure reflects the limit calculated at year-end and may not account for contributions already made this year.

## 11.3 The Contribution Deadline — Where Clients Get Confused Every Year

RRSP contributions can be made at any time during the calendar year, OR within the **first 60 days of the following year** — and still count against the prior year's deduction limit.

For the **2025 tax year**: RRSP deadline = **March 1, 2026**
*(In a leap year, 60 days from January 1 may shift the date — verify each year)*

### The February Contribution Decision

This is where sharp planning separates you from the average preparer.

!!! example "Case — Applied to 2025 or 2026?"
    Your client Jennifer contributes $14,000 to her RRSP on February 12, 2026. You are preparing her 2025 return.

    **Option A — Apply to 2025:**
    Jennifer earned $105,000 in 2025. Her marginal rate is 43.41%.
    Deduction value: $14,000 × 43.41% = **$6,077 tax saving**

    **Option B — Apply to 2026:**
    Jennifer is going on parental leave in mid-2026. Her 2026 income will be approximately $42,000.
    Marginal rate at $42,000: ~29.65%
    Deduction value: $14,000 × 29.65% = **$4,151 tax saving**

    **Apply to 2025.** The difference is $1,926 — real money, available immediately as a larger refund.

    **The right question to ask every client who brings you an RRSP receipt dated January or February:** "Do you want this applied to last year or this year?" Then model both scenarios. This conversation alone is worth more than your fee to many clients.

## 11.4 The Pension Adjustment — Why Box 52 Shrinks RRSP Room

The **Pension Adjustment (PA)** in T4 Box 52 reduces a client's RRSP deduction limit. Its purpose is fairness: an employee accumulating a pension through their employer is already building retirement savings. CRA reduces their RRSP room by the notional value of that pension benefit accrued during the year.

| Pension Type | PA Calculation |
|---|---|
| **Defined Benefit (DB)** | (9 × annual pension benefit earned) − $600 |
| **Defined Contribution (DC) / Group RRSP** | Total employer + employee contributions to the plan |
| **DPSP (Deferred Profit Sharing Plan)** | Employer contributions to the plan |

!!! example "Case — The Teacher with Almost No RRSP Room"
    Maria is a teacher earning $95,000. Her DB pension formula: 2% × years × final average salary.

    Pension benefit earned in 2024: 2% × $95,000 = $1,900/year (one year accrued)

    PA (shown in T4 Box 52): (9 × $1,900) − $600 = $17,100 − $600 = **$16,500**

    RRSP room earned: $95,000 × 18% = $17,100
    After PA: $17,100 − $16,500 = **$600 in new room this year**

    Despite earning $95,000, Maria can only contribute an additional $600 (plus whatever unused room she has from prior years). Many government employees and teachers are genuinely shocked by this figure. This is not a mistake — it reflects the enormous value of their defined benefit pension, which is worth more than most people realize.

    The important conversation: Maria doesn't need to maximize an RRSP to retire well — she has a guaranteed pension. Her priority should be TFSA contributions and possibly a non-registered account.

## 11.5 Over-Contributions — The $2,000 Buffer and the 1% Penalty

CRA allows a **lifetime over-contribution buffer of $2,000**. This is a safety net for arithmetic errors — not a strategy. Contributions beyond this buffer attract a **1% per month penalty** on the excess amount, assessed from the month of over-contribution.

### The Penalty Calculation

```
Total RRSP contributions (all time, all plans)
  − Total RRSP deductions claimed (all time)
  − Current year RRSP deduction limit
  − $2,000 buffer
══════════════════════════════════════════════
= Penalized excess → 1% per month penalty
```

!!! example "Case — A Costly Oversight"
    Kevin's 2025 RRSP deduction limit is $14,000. He contributed $17,500, incorrectly believing he had more room. He has no prior year unused room.

    | Item | Amount |
    |---|---|
    | Contributions | $17,500 |
    | Deduction limit | $14,000 |
    | Gross excess | $3,500 |
    | Buffer | ($2,000) |
    | **Penalized excess** | **$1,500** |
    | Monthly penalty | $1,500 × 1% = **$15/month** |

    Kevin over-contributed in January 2025 and doesn't discover this until he files in April 2026 — 15 months of penalties: $15 × 15 = **$225**.

    **The fix:** Withdraw the excess immediately via **Form T3012A** (to avoid withholding tax on the withdrawal of the over-contribution). File **Form T1-OVP** to calculate and pay the penalty for the period the excess existed.

!!! tip "The most dangerous over-contribution scenario"
    It's not someone contributing a large lump sum — it's the client who switched jobs mid-year. Their new employer's pension generates a PA that reduces the RRSP limit below what the prior NOA showed. If they contributed based on the NOA without accounting for the new PA, they may be over the limit without realizing it. Always check My Account at the point of contribution — especially for clients who changed employers.

## 11.6 Spousal RRSP — Income Splitting Across Time

A spousal RRSP is one of the most effective and most underused income-splitting tools in Canada. The contributing spouse uses their own deduction room to contribute to a plan **registered in the spouse's name**. The goal: equalize retirement income between two spouses whose lifetime earnings were very different.

### The Mechanics

| Who | What They Do | Tax Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Contributor** (high earner) | Uses their own room, makes the contribution | Gets the deduction at their high marginal rate — **now** |
| **Annuitant** (lower earner) | Owns the account | Will withdraw in retirement and pay tax at their lower rate — **later** |

!!! example "Case — The Power of Spousal RRSP Over 20 Years"
    James earns $145,000. His wife Rachel was the primary caregiver for their children and will have minimal retirement income — only OAS ($8,300/year) and CPP ($7,500 partial).

    James contributes $20,000/year to a spousal RRSP for Rachel for 15 years.

    **James's tax saving each year:**
    $20,000 × 46.41% (Ontario marginal rate at $145K) = **$9,282/year × 15 years = $139,230 in total deductions**

    **In retirement, Rachel withdraws $35,000/year from the spousal RRSP:**
    Rachel's total income: $8,300 + $7,500 + $35,000 = $50,800
    Combined marginal rate at $50,800: ~29.65%

    **vs. James withdrawing the same from his own RRSP:**
    James has DB pension ($60,000) + CPP ($14,000) = $74,000 already.
    Additional $35,000 RRSP withdrawal → income = $109,000
    Marginal rate: 43.41%

    **Annual tax saving from income splitting: ($35,000 × 43.41%) − ($35,000 × 29.65%) = $15,194 − $10,378 = $4,816/year in retirement.**

    Over a 25-year retirement, this is over **$120,000 in additional after-tax income** — from a planning strategy that costs nothing extra to implement.

### The 3-Year Attribution Rule — Understand This Perfectly

If the **annuitant spouse** withdraws from a spousal RRSP within **3 calendar years** of the contributing spouse making any contribution, the withdrawal is **attributed back to the contributor** and taxed in their hands.

!!! danger "3 CALENDAR years — not 3 full years from the contribution date"
    This is the most commonly misunderstood rule in the entire RRSP framework.

    **The rule:** Attribution applies if a contribution was made in the year of withdrawal **or in either of the 2 preceding calendar years**.

    **Example:** James contributes to Rachel's spousal RRSP:
    - **November 2023**: $15,000 contribution
    - **March 2025**: $18,000 contribution

    Rachel wants to withdraw $12,000 in **December 2025**.

    Attribution test: Was any contribution made in 2025, 2024, or 2023?
    → March 2025 contribution: YES. Attribution applies.

    The lesser of (a) the $12,000 withdrawal and (b) the contributions in the 3-year window ($18,000) = **$12,000 is attributed to James**.

    Rachel must wait until **January 1, 2028** (no contributions in 2025, 2026, or 2027) for attribution to clear entirely.

    **Practical rule for your clients:** Stop contributing to the spousal RRSP at least 3 calendar years before the annuitant plans to make any withdrawal. Mark this date in your client management system.

---

!!! question "Quick Check"
    Thomas contributed $18,000 to his wife Sofia's spousal RRSP in February 2024. He made no contributions in 2023 or 2025. Sofia wants to withdraw $10,000 in December 2025. Will the withdrawal be attributed back to Thomas, and what is the earliest year Sofia can withdraw without attribution?

    ??? answer
        **Yes — attribution applies.** The 3-year rule covers the year of withdrawal (2025) plus the 2 preceding calendar years (2024 and 2023). Thomas contributed in February 2024, which falls within this window. The attributed amount is the lesser of (a) the $10,000 withdrawal and (b) contributions in the 3-year window ($18,000) = **$10,000 attributed to Thomas** and taxed at his marginal rate. Sofia must wait until **January 1, 2027**: a withdrawal in 2027 looks back at 2027, 2026, and 2025. Thomas's 2024 contribution falls outside that window — no attribution applies. No contributions in 2025, 2026, or 2027 means attribution is fully cleared by 2027.

## 11.7 The Home Buyers' Plan (HBP)

The HBP allows a qualifying first-time home buyer to withdraw from their RRSP **without triggering immediate tax** — with an obligation to repay over 15 years.

### 2025 Key Rules

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| Maximum withdrawal per person | **$35,000** ($70,000 per qualifying couple) |
| First-time buyer definition | Cannot have owned a home occupied as principal residence in the current year **or the 4 preceding calendar years** |
| Minimum RRSP seasoning | Funds must have been in the RRSP for **at least 90 days** before withdrawal |
| Home must be purchased by | October 1 of the year following the withdrawal |
| Repayment schedule | Begins **2 years** after the withdrawal year; spread over **15 years** |
| Annual minimum repayment | Total withdrawn ÷ 15 |
| If repayment missed | Shortfall amount added to **income on Line 12900** for that year |

### The Repayment Trap — The Most Common HBP Error You'll See

!!! example "Case — Sophie's Forgotten Repayments"
    Sophie withdrew $30,000 under the HBP in 2021 to buy her first condo. She hands you her tax documents in February 2026. You check her prior NOA — her HBP repayment balance is still $30,000. She has made zero repayments.

    **Repayment should have started:** 2 years after the withdrawal year → **2023 tax year**

    Annual minimum: $30,000 ÷ 15 = **$2,000/year**

    | Year | Required | Repaid | Shortfall Added to Income |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | 2023 | $2,000 | $0 | **$2,000 added to income** |
    | 2024 | $2,000 | $0 | **$2,000 added to income** |
    | 2025 | $2,000 | $0 | **$2,000 added to income — you're fixing this now** |

    At a ~33% combined marginal rate, each missed year costs Sophie approximately **$660 in avoidable tax**. Three missed years: **$1,980 in unnecessary tax paid**.

    **What you do for her 2025 return:**
    Ensure she contributes at least $2,000 to her RRSP **designated as an HBP repayment** on Schedule 7 before March 1, 2026. This prevents the 2025 shortfall from becoming income. The 2023 and 2024 income inclusions have already happened — you cannot reverse those.

    **The client conversation:** "Going forward, set up an automatic $167/month RRSP contribution and designate it as your HBP repayment on your tax return every year. It costs you $2,000 per year but saves $660 in tax annually. Missing it costs more than making it."

    **Set a recurring reminder in your client file system for every client with an active HBP balance.**

### HBP vs. TFSA for a Down Payment — Which Is Better?

This question comes up constantly with younger clients saving for their first home.

| Factor | HBP (RRSP) | TFSA |
|---|---|---|
| Contribution deduction | ✅ Yes — reduces income now | ❌ No deduction |
| Withdrawal tax treatment | ✅ Tax-free — but must repay | ✅ Tax-free — **no repayment obligation** |
| Contribution room impact | ⚠️ Room permanently lost if repayment missed | ✅ Room restored the following year |
| Growth inside account | ✅ Tax-free | ✅ Tax-free |
| Complexity | ⚠️ 15-year repayment obligation to track | ✅ Simple |
| Best for | High-income clients who get a large deduction and will reliably repay | Lower-income clients, or those who want simplicity |

!!! tip "The First Home Savings Account (FHSA)"
    In 2023, the government introduced the **FHSA** — a new account that combines the best of RRSP (tax deduction on contributions) and TFSA (tax-free withdrawals for a first home with no repayment obligation). Maximum: $8,000/year, lifetime $40,000. For clients who haven't bought yet, the FHSA is often superior to the HBP. Know this account exists — we cover it in detail in Week 14.

## 11.8 The Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP)

The LLP allows RRSP withdrawals to fund **full-time education or training** for the account holder or their spouse, with repayment over 10 years.

### Key Rules

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| Maximum withdrawal per year | **$10,000** |
| Maximum total withdrawal | **$20,000** (over up to 4 years) |
| Eligible programs | Full-time enrollment in a qualifying post-secondary program |
| Repayment period | Begins the **earlier of**: 2 years after last eligible withdrawal, or 5 years after the first withdrawal |
| Annual minimum repayment | Total withdrawn ÷ 10 |
| If repayment missed | Shortfall added to income |

!!! example "Case — LLP at Near-Zero Tax"
    Daniel is 38. He is leaving his $95,000 job to return to school full-time for a 2-year MBA. His 2025 income will only be $12,000 from a part-time job.

    He withdraws $10,000 under the LLP for 2025.

    His total 2025 income: $12,000 + $10,000 = $22,000
    After the Basic Personal Amount ($16,129), his taxable income is only $5,871.
    Federal tax on the LLP withdrawal portion: approximately **$880**.

    Compare: if Daniel had made this $10,000 contribution when earning $95,000, he saved $95,000 × 43.41% × (10,000/95,000) = **$4,341** in tax at contribution time.

    Tax saved on contribution: $4,341. Tax on withdrawal: $880. **Net permanent saving: $3,461** — on one $10,000 transaction, achieved by using the LLP to time the withdrawal during a low-income year.

## 11.9 RRSP Maturity — The Conversion at 71

An RRSP must be collapsed by **December 31 of the year the account holder turns 71**. This is not optional. The client has three choices:

| Option | What Happens | Tax Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Lump-sum withdrawal** | Take everything out at once | Entire balance added to income in one year — catastrophic tax hit. Almost never advisable. |
| **Convert to RRIF** | Roll over to a Registered Retirement Income Fund | No immediate tax. Mandatory annual minimums begin next year. |
| **Purchase an annuity** | Use RRSP funds to buy a guaranteed monthly income for life | No immediate tax. Annuity payments taxed as received. |

Most clients convert to a RRIF. Here is what the mandatory minimums look like:

| Age | Minimum Withdrawal % of RRIF Value |
|---|---|
| 65 | 4.00% |
| 70 | 5.00% |
| 71 (first year of RRIF) | 5.28% |
| 75 | 5.82% |
| 80 | 6.82% |
| 85 | 8.51% |
| 90 | 11.92% |
| 95+ | 20.00% |

!!! example "Case — The RRIF OAS Clawback Problem"
    Robert is 69. He has a $1.4 million RRSP, a DB pension paying $62,000/year, and CPP of $14,000/year. He has never touched his RRSP — it has been growing for 35 years.

    At 72, his mandatory RRIF minimum is approximately 5.40%:
    $1,400,000 × 5.40% = **$75,600**

    Total income at 72: $62,000 + $14,000 + $8,300 OAS + $75,600 RRIF = **$159,900**

    OAS clawback threshold: ~$90,997
    OAS clawback: ($159,900 − $90,997) × 15% = **$11,835 in OAS clawed back**

    Robert will lose virtually his entire OAS for that year — and every year after, as mandatory minimums grow.

    **What should have been done:** Between ages 65 and 71, Robert should have taken **voluntary RRSP/RRIF withdrawals** in the 20.5% federal bracket to gradually reduce the RRSP balance before mandatory withdrawals forced the issue. This is called an **RRSP melt-down strategy** and it is one of the highest-value planning conversations you can have with clients in their early 60s.

    **Identify these clients now:** Anyone over 60 with a large RRSP and other guaranteed income (pension, CPP) is a candidate for this conversation.

## 11.10 RRSP Strategy — The 10 Rules Every Expert Follows

These principles separate a tax preparer who fills in boxes from one who adds genuine value.

=== "Rules 1–3: Contribution Decisions"

    **Rule 1 — Match the rate: contribute when your marginal rate is highest**

    A $10,000 RRSP deduction saves $4,341 in Ontario at $110,000 income but only $2,065 at $50,000. Timing contributions to high-income years (or high-income years created by bonuses, severance, or business sales) dramatically increases their value. The contribution earns the same tax-free growth regardless of timing — only the deduction value changes.

    **Rule 2 — At low income, the TFSA usually wins**

    A client in the 15% federal bracket who contributes to an RRSP is saving $0.15 per dollar on the contribution. In retirement, they may well withdraw at the same or higher rate — eliminating the benefit. Worse: RRSP withdrawals add to net income, which can reduce GIS, GST/HST credits, and provincial benefits. For clients earning under approximately $50,000, always model TFSA vs. RRSP explicitly before recommending.

    **Rule 3 — Unused room is an asset — treat it that way**

    Unused RRSP room carries forward indefinitely. A client who earned $85,000 for 10 years but never contributed has accumulated approximately **$135,000+ in room**. When that client has a windfall year — business sale, large severance, inheritance invested and earning income — they can make a massive catch-up contribution at their peak marginal rate. This is one of the most powerful single-year tax reduction strategies in Canada. Always know your clients' accumulated unused room.

=== "Rules 4–6: Tactical Decisions"

    **Rule 4 — Defer the deduction when income will be higher next year**

    You can contribute now and **not claim the deduction** on this year's return. The contribution earns tax-free growth immediately; the deduction is carried forward and claimed in the year it saves the most tax. This is called a **deduction deferral** and it costs nothing except the discipline to not claim it early.

    Ideal scenario: A client who had an unusually low income year (parental leave, sabbatical, health issue) but contributed to their RRSP anyway. Do not claim the deduction this year. Carry it to next year when their income returns to normal.

    **Rule 5 — Never over-contribute intentionally**

    The $2,000 buffer is a safety net for arithmetic errors — not a bonus room. Intentionally using it provides zero tax benefit (no deduction on the over-contribution) while creating penalty exposure if you miscalculate and exceed it.

    **Rule 6 — In-kind contributions require careful handling**

    Contributing securities directly to an RRSP (without selling) triggers a **deemed disposition at fair market value**. If the security has an accrued gain: capital gains tax is owed — but the RRSP deduction offsets it somewhat. If the security has an **accrued loss**: the loss is **denied** (superficial loss rules apply) and is not added to the RRSP's cost base. The loss is simply gone.

    **Practical rule:** Never contribute in-kind positions with accrued losses. Sell them first to crystallize the capital loss, then contribute the cash.

=== "Rules 7–10: Long-Term Planning"

    **Rule 7 — Model RRIF projections for clients aged 60+**

    For any client over 60 with a substantial RRSP, run a projection showing their mandatory RRIF withdrawals at 71, 75, 80. If those withdrawals push income above the OAS clawback threshold, recommend a systematic melt-down strategy starting now. The earlier the conversation, the more time there is to act.

    **Rule 8 — Spousal RRSP is a 20+ year decision**

    The spousal RRSP works best when started early — years of tax-free compounding plus the income splitting at withdrawal amplify the benefit dramatically. Couples in their 30s and 40s where one spouse significantly out-earns the other should be using this strategy consistently.

    **Rule 9 — Stop spousal contributions 3 calendar years before withdrawal**

    Write this in your client files. Set a reminder. The attribution trap costs clients real money every year because no one told them to stop contributing three years before retirement.

    **Rule 10 — Watch the spousal contribution after the contributor turns 71**

    A contributor can contribute to a spousal RRSP only until December 31 of the year **they** turn 71. If a 72-year-old husband contributes to his 66-year-old wife's spousal RRSP, CRA will deny the deduction and assess penalties. The window closes — permanently — at age 71.

## 11.11 Case Studies — The Full Spectrum

### Case A — The Catch-Up Opportunity

**Client:** Linda, 48, was out of the workforce for 12 years raising children. She returned to work 3 years ago and now earns $96,000. Her NOA shows **$71,000 in accumulated unused RRSP room**.

Linda asks: "Should I put in the maximum this year?"

**Your analysis:**

Her marginal rate at $96,000 in Ontario: **43.41%**

A $71,000 contribution would save: $71,000 × 43.41% = **$30,821** — almost entirely eliminating her federal and provincial income tax for 2025.

But does she have the cash? This is where the conversation gets interesting.

If Linda has $71,000 sitting in savings earning 2%, she's paying marginal tax on that interest each year. Moving it into an RRSP earns tax-free growth **and** generates an immediate refund.

**Tax refund from a $71,000 contribution:** approximately $30,821 — cash back in 8 weeks via direct deposit.

**Your recommendation:** If she has the funds, this is a generational tax planning opportunity. Even a partial contribution that brings her income down to the $57,375 bracket boundary (a contribution of ~$38,625) saves every dollar at the full 43.41% combined rate. Contributions that push her below $57,375 are still valuable but slightly less so (she drops into the 20.5% federal bracket).

Show Linda the numbers in writing. Clients who understand what you just did for them become clients for life.

### Case B — The Spousal Withdrawal Disaster (Caught in Time)

**Client:** Marcus, 54, high-income partner at a law firm. He has contributed $22,000 to his wife Claire's spousal RRSP in March 2025.

During your intake call in January 2026, Claire mentions she wants to withdraw $15,000 from the spousal RRSP to renovate their kitchen.

**Red alert.** Marcus contributed $22,000 in **2025**. The 3-year attribution period covers **2025, 2026, and 2027**. Any withdrawal in those years is attributed back to Marcus.

At Marcus's marginal rate of ~53% (Ontario's top rate at partner-level income), $15,000 attributed to him costs approximately **$7,950 in tax**.

If Claire waited until January 2028 (when attribution clears), the same $15,000 would be taxed at her marginal rate — approximately 29.65% — costing **$4,448**.

**Tax cost of withdrawing one year too early: $7,950 − $4,448 = $3,502.**

**Your advice to Marcus and Claire:** Fund the renovation with the home equity line of credit for two years. Pay it off with RRSP withdrawals starting January 2028 when attribution has cleared. The interest cost on the HELOC for two years (approximately $1,200 at current rates on a $15,000 draw) is far less than the $3,502 in avoidable tax.

You just saved them $2,300 with a single phone call.

### Case C — The Deduction Deferral (Underused Strategy)

**Client:** Ahmed, 35, software developer. In 2025 he took a 9-month parental leave. His 2025 income: **$26,000** (combination of EI parental benefits and partial-year employment).

He contributed $9,000 to his RRSP in January 2025.

His 2026 income will return to $112,000.

**Should he claim the $9,000 deduction on his 2025 return?**

| Scenario | 2025 Taxable Income | Deduction Value |
|---|---|---|
| Claim deduction in 2025 | $26,000 − $9,000 = $17,000 | At ~20.05% combined: **$1,805** |
| Defer deduction to 2026 | $26,000 (no change) | At ~43.41% in 2026 on $112K: **$3,907** |

**Difference: $2,102 more tax saved by waiting one year to claim.**

The $9,000 is already inside the RRSP growing tax-free — that benefit starts immediately either way. The only thing deferred is the paperwork.

On Ahmed's 2025 return: report the $9,000 RRSP contribution on Schedule 7 but enter **$0 as the deduction claimed on Line 20800**. The deduction carries forward automatically. On the 2026 return, claim $9,000 on Line 20800 when it saves him more than twice as much.

### Case D — The RRIF Melt-Down Conversation

**Client:** Patricia, 63. She just retired. She has a **$780,000 RRSP**, a defined benefit pension paying **$44,000/year**, and will receive CPP ($13,000) and OAS ($8,300) starting at 65.

You run a quick projection:

At 71: DB pension ($44,000) + CPP ($13,000) + OAS ($8,300) + RRIF mandatory minimum (5.28% × $1,100,000 after 8 years of 6% growth) = approximately $44,000 + $13,000 + $8,300 + $58,080 = **$123,380**

OAS clawback: ($123,380 − $90,997) × 15% = **$4,857 in OAS clawed back per year**

Over 20 years of retirement, that's approximately **$97,000 in lost OAS** — money that should have been hers.

**The melt-down strategy:** Between now and age 71, Patricia should withdraw $20,000–$30,000/year from her RRSP voluntarily. At her current income of $57,300 (pension only), these withdrawals are taxed at ~33% combined. But they reduce the RRSP balance significantly, shrinking future mandatory RRIF withdrawals.

By drawing down strategically now, she reduces her RRIF balance at 71 from ~$1.1M to perhaps ~$650,000 — keeping her total retirement income comfortably below the OAS clawback threshold for most years.

**Estimated lifetime tax saving from this strategy: $40,000–$80,000**, depending on market returns and longevity.

This is the conversation that makes clients realize they needed a tax advisor — not just a tax preparer.

## 11.12 The 10 Most Common RRSP Mistakes

These are errors you will find in client files that were prepared elsewhere — or not prepared at all.

| # | Mistake | Real Cost | Prevention |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Contributing without checking deduction room first | 1%/month penalty on excess over $2,000 buffer | Always verify via CRA My Account before any contribution |
| 2 | Assuming all contributions were deducted | Missed room, CRA confusion, double-deduction risk | Review Schedule 7 on every prior return |
| 3 | Missing HBP repayments | Missed amount added to full income — $660+/year in avoidable tax | Check Schedule 7 HBP balance on every return, every year |
| 4 | Spousal withdrawal within 3 calendar years | Attributed to contributor at their high marginal rate | Know attribution rule cold; set calendar reminders |
| 5 | Contributing at low income when TFSA is better | Minimal deduction; withdrawals reduce benefits in retirement | Ask about income trajectory — not just this year |
| 6 | Withdrawing from RRSP for emergencies | Withholding tax + full income inclusion + RRSP room lost forever | Suggest HELOC, TFSA, or line of credit instead |
| 7 | Not deferring deduction in a low-income year | Deduction claimed when worth the least | Always ask: "Is this year lower income than next year?" |
| 8 | In-kind contribution with accrued loss | Loss denied under superficial loss rule — permanently gone | Sell first, contribute cash, preserve the capital loss |
| 9 | Missing RRSP deadline for prior year | Contribution cannot count for prior year — tax savings lost | Send clients a February reminder every year without exception |
| 10 | No drawdown strategy before 71 | Forced RRIF withdrawals trigger OAS clawback for decades | Model RRIF projections for every client over 60 |

## 11.14 Week 11 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - An RRSP is a **tax deferral** vehicle — the permanent saving comes from contributing at a **higher rate** than you withdraw
    - RRSP deduction limit = 18% of prior year **earned income** (max $31,560) **minus** PA **plus** unused room carried forward — verify in CRA My Account, always
    - Contributions in the first 60 days of the new year can apply to the **prior or current year** — the client chooses based on which year has the higher marginal rate
    - The **Pension Adjustment (Box 52)** can nearly eliminate RRSP room for DB pension members — know this before assuming a client has contribution room
    - Over-contributions beyond the **$2,000 buffer** attract a **1%/month penalty** — fix with Form T3012A + T1-OVP
    - Spousal RRSP withdrawals within **3 calendar years** of any contribution are attributed back to the contributor — 3 **calendar** years, not 3 full years
    - **HBP:** $35,000 tax-free withdrawal; **15-year repayment** starting 2 years after withdrawal year; missed repayments added to income
    - **Deduction deferral:** contribute now, claim the deduction in a future high-income year — the contribution earns tax-free growth immediately
    - **RRIF melt-down strategy:** for clients 60+ with large RRSPs and pension income — model this early and often
    - RRSP must convert to RRIF or annuity by **December 31 of the year the holder turns 71** — no exceptions

## ✅ Week 11 Quiz

When you're ready, ask Claude: **"Quiz me on Week 11"** for an interactive test.

??? question "1. Your client earned $95,000 in 2024 (employment net of union dues $1,200). Their T4 shows a pension adjustment of $8,800. They have $14,500 in unused room from prior years. What is their exact 2025 RRSP deduction limit?"
    Earned income: $95,000 − $1,200 = $93,800. RRSP room: $93,800 × 18% = $16,884 (under $31,560 cap). Minus PA: $16,884 − $8,800 = $8,084. Plus carryforward: $8,084 + $14,500 = **$22,584**. Verify this on the 2024 NOA and CRA My Account before any contribution.

??? question "2. A client contributed $14,000 to their RRSP on February 8, 2026. Their 2025 earned income was $108,000 and their 2026 income will be approximately $38,000 (parental leave). Which year should the deduction be claimed and what is the approximate tax saving difference?"
    **Apply to 2025.** At $108,000, the combined federal/Ontario marginal rate is approximately 43.41%: $14,000 × 43.41% = $6,077. At $38,000 in 2026, the rate drops to approximately 29.65%: $14,000 × 29.65% = $4,151. Claiming against 2025 saves **$1,926 more** — real money available as a larger 2025 refund.

??? question "3. James contributes $20,000 to his wife Claire's spousal RRSP in October 2025. Claire wants to withdraw $8,000 in November 2027. Is this attributed to James? What year must she wait until?"
    **Yes, attributed to James.** Attribution covers the year of contribution (2025) plus 2026 and 2027. A withdrawal in **November 2027** falls within the 3-year window. The lesser of the withdrawal ($8,000) and contributions made in 2025–2027 ($20,000) = **$8,000 attributed to James** at his marginal rate. Claire must wait until **January 1, 2028** for attribution to clear completely.

??? question "4. Your client withdrew $35,000 under the HBP in 2022. She asks if she can skip her repayment this year (2025 return). What is the consequence and what is your recommendation?"
    The first required repayment year is 2024 (2 years after the withdrawal year of 2022). Annual minimum: $35,000 ÷ 15 = $2,333. If she doesn't repay in 2025, **$2,333 is added to her income on Line 12900**. At a 33% combined rate, that costs approximately **$770 in unnecessary tax**. Recommendation: contribute at least $2,333 to her RRSP before March 1, 2026, and explicitly designate it as an HBP repayment on Schedule 7. Automate this — $195/month with a designated repayment instruction so it never happens accidentally.

??? question "5. Ahmed earned $24,000 in 2025 (parental leave year). He contributed $11,000 to his RRSP in January 2025. He returns to his $115,000 salary in 2026. Should he claim the deduction on his 2025 return?"
    **No — defer the deduction.** At $24,000 income in 2025, the marginal rate is ~20.05% combined. The deduction saves approximately $2,205. At $115,000 in 2026, the rate is ~43.41% — the same deduction saves $4,775. By deferring, Ahmed saves an additional **$2,570** with zero effort. The $11,000 already earns tax-free growth inside the RRSP starting in January 2025. On the 2025 return, enter $11,000 on Schedule 7 as contributions made but enter $0 on Line 20800. The $11,000 carries forward automatically.

??? question "6. A client wants to transfer 200 shares of a stock worth $12,000 in-kind to their RRSP. They originally paid $18,000 (accrued loss of $6,000). What do you advise and why?"
    **Do not make the in-kind transfer.** When a security with an accrued loss is transferred to an RRSP, it triggers a deemed disposition at FMV ($12,000). But the resulting $6,000 capital loss is **denied** under the superficial loss rules — it cannot be claimed and it is not added to the cost base inside the RRSP. The loss disappears permanently. Instead: sell the shares in the non-registered account for $12,000, crystallizing the $6,000 capital loss (which can be used to offset other capital gains now or in future years). Then contribute the $12,000 cash to the RRSP. Both the capital loss AND the RRSP contribution deduction are preserved.

??? question "7. Patricia, 64, has a $820,000 RRSP, a DB pension of $48,000/year, and will receive CPP ($13,200) and OAS ($8,300) at 65. What planning issue do you raise and what strategy do you recommend?"
    Patricia faces a serious **OAS clawback risk** starting at 71. With 7 years of 6% growth, her RRSP reaches approximately $1,233,000. At 71, the mandatory RRIF minimum (~5.28%) = $65,100. Total income: $48,000 + $13,200 + $8,300 + $65,100 = $134,600. OAS clawback: ($134,600 − $90,997) × 15% = **$6,540 clawed back annually** — nearly her entire OAS. Recommend a **systematic RRSP melt-down**: withdraw $20,000–$35,000/year voluntarily from now until 71, filling up the lower tax brackets. This reduces the RRIF balance at 71, keeping future mandatory withdrawals below the clawback threshold. Yes, she pays tax now — but at a lower rate than she will if forced to withdraw at 71+, with the added benefit of preserving OAS for life.
