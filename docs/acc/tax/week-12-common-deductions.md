# Week 12: Common Deductions — The $5,000 Most Clients Leave Behind

!!! info "Learning Objectives"
    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    - Apply every major above-the-line deduction that reduces Net Income
    - Calculate childcare expenses correctly — including who claims them and when the exception applies
    - Handle moving expense claims with precision, including the distance test and eligible costs
    - Identify carrying charges and interest deductions most preparers miss
    - Claim support payment deductions correctly and avoid the common misclassification errors
    - Ask the right intake questions to surface deductions clients don't know they have
    - Understand the strategic value of each deduction relative to tax credits

!!! tip "Why deductions are worth more than credits — and why this matters"
    A **deduction** reduces income before tax is applied. Its value scales with your marginal rate — a $5,000 deduction is worth $2,171 to someone at 43.41% and only $1,003 to someone at 20.05%. A **credit** reduces tax directly at a fixed rate (usually 15% federal). Deductions are the higher-value tool for higher-income clients — and that is exactly the client base you are building. Knowing every deduction cold is how you justify a premium fee.

## 12.1 The Architecture of Deductions — What Reduces What

Before diving into specific deductions, understand exactly where each one lives in the T1 flow. This determines how powerful each deduction is.

```
TOTAL INCOME (Line 15000)
    − Union dues and professional fees     Line 21200   ← reduces Net Income
    − RRSP deduction                       Line 20800   ← reduces Net Income
    − RPP contributions                    Line 20700   ← reduces Net Income
    − Childcare expenses                   Line 21400   ← reduces Net Income
    − Disability supports deduction        Line 21500   ← reduces Net Income
    − Moving expenses                      Line 21900   ← reduces Net Income
    − Support payments (eligible)          Line 22000   ← reduces Net Income
    − Carrying charges and interest        Line 22100   ← reduces Net Income
    − CPP on self-employment (50%)         Line 22200   ← reduces Net Income
    − Other employment expenses            Line 22900   ← reduces Net Income
    − Other deductions                     Line 23200   ← reduces Net Income
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
NET INCOME (Line 23600)  ← drives both tax brackets AND benefit entitlements
    − Capital gains deduction              Line 25400   ← reduces Taxable Income only
    − Northern residents deduction         Line 25600   ← reduces Taxable Income only
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TAXABLE INCOME (Line 26000)
```

!!! success "Net Income deductions are doubly powerful"
    Deductions that reduce **Net Income (Line 23600)** are doubly valuable — they reduce the tax you pay AND the income CRA uses to calculate benefit entitlements: GST/HST credit, Canada Child Benefit, OAS clawback threshold, Age Amount, and spousal credit calculations. A client who reduces their net income from $98,000 to $80,000 doesn't just save $18,000 × their marginal rate in tax — they may also unlock or increase benefits worth hundreds of dollars annually. Always think about net income, not just taxable income.

## 12.2 Union Dues and Professional Membership Fees — Line 21200

**The deduction:** Dues paid to a union or professional organization as a condition of employment are deductible on Line 21200.

### What Qualifies

| ✅ Deductible | ❌ Not Deductible |
|---|---|
| Union dues (T4 Box 44, or paid directly) | Voluntary professional association memberships |
| Professional regulatory body fees (required by law to practise) | Fees to organizations unrelated to employment |
| Parity committee dues | Political donations disguised as dues |
| Professional liability insurance required by the employer | Life insurance premiums |
| Employee associations | Recreational club memberships |

!!! example "Case — The Nurse Who Didn't Know"
    Sandra is a registered nurse earning $94,000. She pays $1,840/year directly to the College of Nurses of Ontario (required to maintain her licence) and $960/year in union dues (shown on her T4 Box 44).

    Many preparers only capture the Box 44 amount. The CNO fee is equally deductible — but it never appears on any slip. It requires a single intake question: *"Do you pay any professional licensing fees directly out of pocket?"*

    **Total Line 21200: $1,840 + $960 = $2,800**

    At Sandra's 43.41% combined marginal rate: **$2,800 × 43.41% = $1,215 in tax saved** — from a question that takes 10 seconds to ask.

    **Ask every client with a professional designation:** nurse, engineer, teacher, lawyer, doctor, CPA, social worker, pharmacist, dental hygienist. Regulatory bodies charge hundreds to thousands per year. These fees are almost always deductible — and almost always missed.

## 12.3 Childcare Expenses — Line 21400

Childcare is one of the largest and most frequently misclaimed deductions in Canadian tax. The rules carry more nuance than most preparers realize — and getting them wrong draws CRA attention.

### The Basic Rule

Childcare expenses are deductible when paid to allow the **lower-income spouse** (or the eligible individual, in single-parent households) to:

- Earn income from employment or self-employment
- Attend school full-time or part-time
- Carry on research under a grant

### Eligible Children

| Child Type | Age Limit |
|---|---|
| Biological, adopted, or stepchild | Under 16 at any time during the year |
| Child of any age with a certified disability | No age limit — disability qualifies regardless of age |

### What Qualifies as a Childcare Expense

| ✅ Eligible | ❌ Not Eligible |
|---|---|
| Licensed daycare centres | Clothing, food, or transportation costs for the child |
| Registered nursery schools and preschools | Medical and hospital care |
| Day camps — summer and specialty | Tutoring or purely educational programs |
| After-school programs (childcare component) | Amounts paid to the child's parent or to the taxpayer's spouse |
| Baby-sitters and nannies (with receipts + SIN) | Amounts paid to the taxpayer's own dependants under 18 |
| Overnight boarding schools (childcare portion only) | Full boarding school cost — only the childcare portion qualifies |
| Overnight camps (with annual limits — see below) | Recreational or sports programs (unless childcare is the primary purpose) |

!!! warning "Paying a relative — strict rules apply"
    Childcare paid to a **related person under 18** is **not deductible**. Childcare paid to a **related person 18 or older** (e.g., a university-student sibling babysitting) is deductible — but receipts are required and the caregiver must report the income. CRA cross-matches SINs on childcare receipts. If your client paid a relative, ask their age and obtain the SIN.

### The Annual Per-Child Limits

| Child Status | Maximum Deductible Per Child |
|---|---|
| Under 7 years old at December 31 | **$8,000** |
| Age 7 to 16 | **$5,000** |
| Child with a certified disability (any age) | **$11,000** |

### Special Limit — Overnight Camps and Boarding Schools

| Type | Weekly Limit |
|---|---|
| Overnight camps | $275/week |
| Overnight sports school (where lodging is involved) | $275/week |
| Boarding school | $275/week |

### The Lower-Income Spouse Rule — And Its Exceptions

!!! danger "The most commonly misapplied rule in childcare"
    The deduction must go on the **lower-income spouse's** return — full stop. Not the higher-income spouse, not split between them. The source of the payments is irrelevant. The income level of each spouse is what determines who claims.

    Clients resist this constantly. The higher-income spouse often says: *"I paid for the daycare from my account — can't I claim it?"* The answer is no. Payment source does not determine eligibility. Income level does.

**Exceptions — when the higher-income spouse may claim:**

| Situation | Higher-Income Spouse Can Claim? |
|---|---|
| Both spouses have identical income | Either spouse may claim |
| Lower-income spouse is in school (full-time) | Yes — up to $200/week per child (full-time school) |
| Lower-income spouse is in school (part-time) | Yes — up to $100/week per child |
| Lower-income spouse is hospitalized or has an infirmity | Yes |
| Lower-income spouse is incarcerated | Yes |
| Couple separated for at least 90 days | Each parent claims for periods the child was in their care |

**When the higher-income spouse claims due to the other's schooling, the deduction is the lesser of:**

- The usual annual per-child limit, OR
- $200/week × weeks of full-time enrollment, OR
- $100/week × weeks of part-time enrollment

!!! example "Case — Classic Two-Income Household"
    Victor earns $98,000. Helen earns $54,000. Two children: Emma (age 4) and Liam (age 9). Daycare and after-school costs: $18,400 for the year.

    **Who claims:** Helen — the lower-income spouse.

    | Child | Age | Maximum |
    |---|---|---|
    | Emma | 4 (under 7) | $8,000 |
    | Liam | 9 | $5,000 |
    | **Total maximum** | | **$13,000** |

    Actual expenses of $18,400 are **capped at $13,000**. Helen claims $13,000 on Line 21400.

    At Helen's ~33% combined marginal rate: **$13,000 × 33% = $4,290 in tax saved**.

    Victor claims nothing — even though he earns more and likely paid most of the bills. Explain this clearly at intake. Clients who don't understand the rule question the return and sometimes look for a second opinion. Get ahead of it.

!!! example "Case — Lower-Income Spouse Returns to School"
    Same family. In September, Helen returns to school full-time for 17 weeks (a professional certification). For those 17 weeks, Victor may claim childcare.

    **Victor's claim for the school period:**

    Limit: $200/week × 17 weeks = $3,400 per child (also subject to the annual maximum of $8,000/$5,000)

    | Child | Weekly Limit × 17 wks | Annual Maximum | Victor Claims |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Emma | $3,400 | $8,000 | $3,400 |
    | Liam | $3,400 | $5,000 | $3,400 |
    | **Victor's total** | | | **$6,800** |

    **Helen's claim for January–August:** She can claim the childcare expenses attributable to the period she was working — either by tracing actual expenses or pro-rating the annual total.

    This split-year calculation is time-consuming but delivers a meaningful combined refund. Many preparers skip the Victor portion entirely because they don't know the exception. Doing this right is exactly the kind of thoroughness clients remember and talk about.

!!! question "Quick Check"
    Ahmed earns $74,000; his wife Maria earns $42,000. They paid $16,000 in daycare and after-school programs for their two children — a 3-year-old and an 8-year-old. Who claims the childcare deduction, what is the maximum claimable amount, and what happens to the unclaimed portion?

    ??? answer
        **Maria claims** — she is the lower-income spouse. Per-child annual limits: 3-year-old (under 7) → $8,000; 8-year-old (7–16) → $5,000. **Total maximum: $13,000**. Actual expenses of $16,000 are capped at $13,000 — the remaining $3,000 is not claimable and does not carry forward. Ahmed cannot claim any portion regardless of who wrote the cheques. At Maria's combined ~33% marginal rate: $13,000 × 33% = **$4,290 in tax saved**.

## 12.4 Moving Expenses — Line 21900

Moving expenses are one of the most underused deductions in Canada. A qualifying move can generate $5,000–$50,000+ in deductions — and most clients have no idea they qualify, or what they can claim.

### The Two Qualifying Triggers

| Trigger | What Must Be True |
|---|---|
| **Moving for work** | New job, job transfer, or new business location |
| **Moving for school** | Enrolling full-time at a post-secondary institution |

### The 40 Kilometre Distance Test

The new home must be at least **40 kilometres closer** to the new place of employment or school than the old home was — measured by the **shortest usual route**, not straight-line distance.

```
Distance: OLD home → new workplace
  − Distance: NEW home → new workplace
════════════════════════════════════
Must be ≥ 40 km
```

!!! warning "The test is measured to the WORKPLACE — not between homes"
    Two houses 8 km apart could easily pass the 40 km test if the new workplace is on the far side of town. Clients assume the test is how far they moved. It is not. It measures how much closer they got to work.

!!! example "Case — The Distance Test in Practice"
    David moves from Mississauga to downtown Toronto for a new Bay Street job.

    - Old home (Mississauga) to Bay Street: 42 km
    - New home (downtown Toronto) to Bay Street: 2 km
    - **Difference: 40 km — qualifies exactly**

    Had David moved to Etobicoke instead (25 km from old home, 17 km from Bay Street), the difference would be only 25 km — and he would not qualify. The move must bring you meaningfully closer to where you now work or study.

### The Income Limitation — The Most Overlooked Rule

Moving expenses can only be deducted against **income earned at the new location** in the year of the move. If moving expenses exceed new-location income, the excess carries forward **one year only** — not indefinitely.

!!! example "Case — The New Graduate"
    Priya graduates in April and starts her first professional job in Vancouver in July, having moved from Toronto. Her moving costs were $9,800.

    Income earned in Vancouver (July–December): **$34,500**

    She deducts the full $9,800 — comfortably within her new-location income.

    If she had started in November and earned only $6,000 before year-end: she claims $6,000 in 2025 and the $3,800 remainder carries forward to 2026. **Set a note in the client file to claim the carryforward next year.**

### Eligible Moving Expenses — The Complete Picture

=== "Transportation and Storage"

    | Expense | Deductible? | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | Professional movers | ✅ Yes | Full invoice |
    | Moving truck rental | ✅ Yes | |
    | Packing materials | ✅ Yes | |
    | Storage (up to 30 days) | ✅ Yes | While in transit between homes |
    | Vehicle expenses (personal car) | ✅ Yes | Actual fuel or CRA per-km rate |
    | Flights (if impractical to drive) | ✅ Yes | Economy fare |
    | Insurance on transported goods | ✅ Yes | |

=== "Travel and Temporary Living"

    | Expense | Deductible? | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | Hotel during the move | ✅ Yes | Reasonable duration |
    | Meals during travel | ✅ Yes | 50% of actual, or flat rate |
    | Temporary accommodation at new location | ✅ Yes | **Up to 15 days** |
    | Meals during temporary accommodation | ✅ Yes | 50% |

=== "Selling the Old Home"

    | Expense | Deductible? | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | Real estate agent commission | ✅ Yes | Often the largest single item |
    | Legal fees on the sale | ✅ Yes | |
    | **Mortgage break penalty** | ✅ Yes | Almost universally missed |
    | Advertising and fix-up to sell | ✅ Yes | Reasonable amounts |
    | Lease cancellation penalty | ✅ Yes | If renting |

=== "Establishing at the New Home"

    | Expense | Deductible? | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | Legal fees on the purchase | ✅ Yes | New home only |
    | **Land transfer tax** | ✅ Yes | Almost universally missed |
    | Utility hook-up fees | ✅ Yes | |
    | Driver's licence + vehicle registration (new province) | ✅ Yes | |

=== "What You Cannot Deduct"

    | Expense | Reason |
    |---|---|
    | New furniture or appliances | Personal purchase — not a moving cost |
    | House-hunting trips before the decision to move | Pre-move exploration — not eligible |
    | Loss on the sale of the home | Governed by capital loss rules, not moving expense rules |
    | Mail forwarding beyond 1 year | Too remote from the move itself |
    | Job search costs in the new city | Not a moving expense |
    | Renovations to make the old home marketable | Capital in nature |

!!! example "Case — The Executive Relocation"
    James accepts a promotion and transfers from Calgary to Toronto. His employer pays a $15,000 relocation allowance — taxable (included in T4 Box 40). His actual costs:

    | Expense | Amount |
    |---|---|
    | Professional movers | $9,400 |
    | Real estate agent commission (Calgary home) | $18,200 |
    | Mortgage break penalty | $4,800 |
    | Legal fees — sale and purchase combined | $3,600 |
    | Land transfer tax (Ontario) | $8,475 |
    | Hotel during move (5 days) | $1,200 |
    | Temporary accommodation in Toronto (12 days) | $3,600 |
    | Meals during travel and temporary stay | $1,100 |
    | Ontario driver's licence and vehicle registration | $120 |
    | **Total moving expenses** | **$50,495** |

    James's Toronto employment income from transfer date: **$185,000** (new-location income easily covers it).

    **Deductible: $50,495**

    At James's marginal rate of 53.53% (Ontario top combined): $50,495 × 53.53% = **$27,030 in tax saved.**

    The $15,000 taxable allowance added $15,000 to income. The $50,495 deduction more than offset it — netting James a **$35,495 deduction advantage** from the move.

    He had never heard that the mortgage penalty, land transfer tax, and temporary accommodation were all deductible. Neither had the person who prepared his taxes last year. You found $27,000 in tax savings that were simply sitting there unclaimed.

!!! question "Quick Check"
    Rachel moved from Winnipeg to Toronto for a new job in May 2025. Her eligible moving expenses total $38,000. She earned $22,000 at the new Toronto location from May to December. She also wants to claim a $10,000 RRSP deduction. In what order should the deductions be applied, and how much of each carries forward?

    ??? answer
        Apply **moving expenses first**: $22,000 − $22,000 (moving expenses, limited to new-location income) = $0 remaining income. The $16,000 balance of moving expenses carries forward **one year only** to the 2026 return. The $10,000 RRSP deduction cannot be claimed this year either, but it carries forward **indefinitely**. Why this order matters: moving expense carryforwards are capped at one year and then lost; RRSP unused deductions never expire. Applying moving expenses first protects the most time-sensitive deduction while preserving the RRSP carryforward intact. Flag the 2026 return to claim the $16,000 moving carryforward before it disappears.

## 12.5 Carrying Charges and Interest Expense — Line 22100

This is the single most consistently missed deduction category in Canadian tax practice. Most clients have no idea they can deduct interest and fees paid to earn investment income. Most preparers don't ask.

### The Core Rule

**Interest paid on money borrowed to earn income is deductible** — provided there is a reasonable expectation that the borrowed funds will generate taxable income.

| Expense | Deductible on Line 22100? |
|---|---|
| Interest on a loan to buy non-registered investments (stocks, bonds, funds) | ✅ Yes |
| Interest on a margin account (for income-producing investments) | ✅ Yes |
| **Investment management fees on non-registered accounts** | ✅ Yes |
| Safety deposit box fees (used for investment documents) | ✅ Yes |
| Accounting fees paid specifically for investment record-keeping | ✅ Yes |
| Foreign withholding taxes (alternative to foreign tax credit) | ✅ Sometimes |
| Interest on a loan to buy a rental property | ✅ Yes — but claimed on T776, not Line 22100 |
| Interest on an RRSP loan | ❌ No — RRSP income is sheltered |
| Interest on a loan to fund a TFSA | ❌ No — TFSA income is exempt |
| Interest on personal loans (car, vacation) | ❌ No — no income-earning purpose |
| MERs embedded in mutual funds | ❌ No — already netted from fund returns before income is reported |
| Financial planning fees (for a retirement or financial plan) | ❌ No — personal, not investment management |

!!! example "Case — The Leveraged Investor"
    Patricia borrowed $80,000 on a secured line of credit at 7.2% annually to invest in a non-registered portfolio of dividend-paying stocks and corporate bonds.

    Annual interest: $80,000 × 7.2% = **$5,760**

    Her portfolio generated:
    - Eligible dividends (taxable grossed-up amount): $3,800
    - Bond interest: $1,400
    - Total investment income: $5,200

    She can deduct the **full $5,760** on Line 22100 — even though the interest exceeds the income earned. The net effect is a $560 investment loss that reduces her Net Income.

    At her 43.41% combined marginal rate: $5,760 × 43.41% = **$2,500 in tax saved** from an expense she was paying regardless.

    Ask every client with a non-registered portfolio: *"Did you borrow any money to invest?"*

### Investment Advisory Fees — The Hidden Annual Deduction

Fee-based financial advisors typically charge 0.5%–1.5% annually on the non-registered portfolio value. Their clients receive an annual fee statement — and almost never claim it. This is free money sitting in a document most clients never think to bring.

!!! example "Case — Twenty Years of Unclaimed Fees"
    Robert, 58, has a $450,000 non-registered portfolio managed by a fee-based advisor at 1.0% annually.

    Annual advisory fee: $450,000 × 1.0% = **$4,500** → Line 22100

    At Robert's 46.41% marginal rate: $4,500 × 46.41% = **$2,088 in tax saved — every single year.**

    He has had this portfolio for 11 years. He has never claimed the fee. He has left approximately **$22,968 in tax savings** unclaimed over that period.

    The question that surfaces this: *"Do you pay a separate advisory fee on your investment accounts — not through the fund, but as a direct charge?"*

    If he says yes — ask for the annual fee statement. It takes 2 minutes to add to the return and changes the conversation entirely.

## 12.6 Support Payments — Line 22000

Support payments are one of the most misunderstood deduction categories because the rules changed completely in 1997 — and many clients still operate on the old understanding.

### The Current Rules (Post-May 1997)

| Payment Type | Payer's Tax Treatment | Recipient's Tax Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Spousal / partner support** | ✅ **Deductible** — Line 22000 | ✅ **Taxable** — Line 12800 |
| **Child support** | ❌ Not deductible | ❌ Not taxable |
| **Mixed payments** | Only the spousal portion is deductible | Only the spousal portion is taxable |

!!! danger "Child support is never deductible — full stop"
    Agreements made after April 1997 are unambiguous: child support flows tax-neutral. The payer gets no deduction; the recipient pays no tax. Many clients believe all support payments they make are deductible. They are not. Correcting this misunderstanding at intake — before they give you wrong information — is essential.

### What Makes a Spousal Support Payment Eligible

All four conditions must be met:

1. **Paid to a current or former spouse / common-law partner** (or to a third party specifically directed by a court order)
2. **Made pursuant to a written agreement or court order**
3. **Periodic in nature** — regular, recurring payments; not a lump sum
4. **Made while living apart** due to relationship breakdown

!!! example "Case — The Divorce Settlement"
    Daniel and his ex-wife Sarah have a 2022 court order requiring Daniel to pay:
    - $1,800/month spousal support
    - $2,200/month child support for their two children

    **Daniel's T1:**
    - Line 22000 deduction: $1,800 × 12 = **$21,600**
    - Child support: $2,200 × 12 = $26,400 — **not deductible, does not appear**

    **Sarah's T1:**
    - Line 12800 income: **$21,600** (taxable spousal support)
    - Child support received: **not taxable, does not appear**

    At Daniel's combined 43.41% marginal rate: $21,600 × 43.41% = **$9,377 in tax saved**.

    Sarah pays tax on $21,600 at her lower marginal rate — the tax burden has shifted from Daniel (high earner) to Sarah (lower earner). This is the policy intent. You must also record Sarah's SIN on Daniel's return — CRA cross-matches payer and recipient filings.

### Lump-Sum Payments — Almost Never Deductible

A lump-sum "buyout" settling all future support obligations is a capital settlement — **generally not deductible**. The exception is when a lump sum represents arrears of specifically ordered periodic amounts that went unpaid.

!!! tip "Third-party payments ordered by a court are deductible"
    If a court order specifically directs the payer to pay amounts to a third party on behalf of the recipient (e.g., directly to the landlord or the child's school), those payments may qualify as deductible spousal support — provided the court order specifically designates the third-party payments. A voluntary decision to pay a third party does not qualify.

## 12.7 Disability Supports Deduction — Line 21500

This deduction is routinely confused with the **Disability Tax Credit** (a non-refundable credit we cover in Week 13). They are completely separate — and in some cases, stackable.

### What It Is

A taxpayer with a physical or mental impairment who paid for certain supports **to earn employment income, run a business, attend school, or conduct research** can deduct those costs from income on Line 21500.

### What Qualifies

| Support Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Attendant care | Personal attendant required to enable work or study |
| Communication devices | Bliss boards, voice-activated software, optical scanners |
| Visual aids | Electronic magnification, Braille note-takers |
| Hearing supports | Sign language interpretation, real-time captioning |
| Learning supports | Tutoring (for learning disabilities), talking textbooks |

**Maximum deduction:** the lesser of actual eligible expenses or **two-thirds of the client's earned income** for the year.

!!! tip "Deduction vs. credit — which is better?"
    Some disability-related expenses can be claimed as either the Disability Supports Deduction (Line 21500) OR as Medical Expenses (Schedule 1 credit) — but generally not both for the same expense. The deduction reduces Net Income (scaled to the marginal rate — more powerful for high earners). The medical credit is a flat 15% federal benefit. For clients in the 26%+ federal bracket, the deduction is almost always worth more. Model both and use the better option.

## 12.8 The 12 Questions That Surface Every Deduction

The difference between a good preparer and a great one is not knowing the rules — it is asking the questions that reveal which rules apply. Here is the intake script.

!!! success "Build these 12 questions into every single client meeting"

    1. **"Are you a member of a union? Do you pay any professional licensing fees directly — to a college, regulatory body, or professional association?"** → Line 21200 — catches nurses, engineers, teachers, lawyers, pharmacists

    2. **"Did you pay for childcare — daycare, babysitter, summer camps, after-school programs — so you or your spouse could work or go to school?"** → Line 21400

    3. **"Who earns less between you and your spouse? Because childcare must go on the lower earner's return."** → Prevents misclassification

    4. **"Did your family move for work or school in 2025? Or in early 2024 — you may have a carryforward from last year."** → Line 21900 — the prior-year carryforward is routinely missed

    5. **"Did you pay a mortgage break penalty when you moved or refinanced?"** → Part of moving expenses — almost always missed

    6. **"Do you have a non-registered investment account? Does your financial advisor charge you a fee separately from the fund itself?"** → Line 22100 — the most consistently missed deduction in practice

    7. **"Did you borrow money to invest outside of your RRSP or TFSA?"** → Line 22100 — investment loan interest

    8. **"Do you have a safety deposit box you use for investment or property documents?"** → Line 22100 — small but real

    9. **"Do you pay spousal support under a court order or written agreement?"** → Line 22000 — and confirm child support is NOT being claimed by mistake

    10. **"Were you required to work from home? Does your employer have a signed T2200 for you?"** → Line 22900 / Form T777

    11. **"Do you have a physical or mental condition that required you to pay for attendant care or special equipment to do your job or attend school?"** → Line 21500

    12. **"Is there anything else you spent money on this year that you think might be related to your work, your investments, or a major life change?"** → Open-ended — surfaces things you haven't thought to ask

These 12 questions, asked consistently of every client, will surface meaningful deductions in the majority of cases. Clients who feel you found money they didn't know they had become clients who stay and refer.

## 12.9 Case Studies — Deductions in the Real World

### Case A — The Relocated Engineer

**Client:** Michael, 38, transferred from Winnipeg to Vancouver for his employer in April 2025. His employer contributed nothing toward relocation. Annual salary: $118,000.

**Distance test:**
- Winnipeg home → Vancouver office: 1,870 km
- New Vancouver home → same office: 8 km
- Difference: 1,862 km ✅ Passes easily

**Moving expenses documented:**

| Expense | Amount |
|---|---|
| Professional movers | $14,200 |
| Real estate agent (Winnipeg home) | $11,400 |
| Mortgage break penalty | $3,800 |
| Legal fees (sale + purchase) | $4,100 |
| Land transfer tax (BC) | $7,200 |
| Hotel in Vancouver — 12 days temporary | $2,640 |
| Meals during travel and temporary stay | $1,100 |
| Vehicle gas and km (drive to Vancouver) | $890 |
| BC driver's licence and vehicle registration | $120 |
| **Total** | **$45,450** |

**Vancouver income (April–December):** $118,000 × 9/12 = **$88,500** — covers it fully.

**Deductible: $45,450**

At Michael's combined BC marginal rate of 53.50%: $45,450 × 53.50% = **$24,316 in tax saved.**

Michael knew the movers were deductible. He had never heard that the mortgage penalty, land transfer tax, or hotel were eligible. He nearly threw out his hotel receipts. He will never prepare his own taxes again.

### Case B — The Fee-Based Investor Nobody Asked

**Client:** Helen, 52. Non-registered portfolio of $380,000, fee-based advisor at 1.2% annually. She also borrowed $60,000 at 6.9% interest two years ago to top up her investments when markets dipped.

**What she brings to the meeting:** A T5 showing $9,200 in interest income and $3,100 in eligible dividends. She mentions "some investments and a loan."

**After your intake questions:**

| Item | Amount | Line |
|---|---|---|
| Investment loan interest ($60,000 × 6.9%) | $4,140 | 22100 |
| Advisory fee ($380,000 × 1.2%) | $4,560 | 22100 |
| **Total carrying charges** | **$8,700** | 22100 |

At Helen's 46.41% marginal rate: $8,700 × 46.41% = **$4,038 in tax saved** — from expenses she was paying regardless, completely invisible on her prior returns.

She assumed the advisor fee wasn't deductible because "it's not really an expense — it just comes off my account." That's the exact assumption that costs clients thousands per year.

**The conversation that makes the difference:** *"Helen, does your advisor charge a separate annual fee — not through the fund itself, but billed as a direct charge? Can you get the annual fee statement from them?"*

She emails it the next day. You add $4,560 to Line 22100. She gets an additional $2,116 on her refund. She tells her sister, who has the same advisor.

### Case C — The Separated Parent Who Thought It Was All Deductible

**Client:** David, 44. Separated in 2023 under a court order. He pays $2,400/month spousal support and $1,600/month child support.

**His first question:** *"I paid $48,000 in support this year — that's all deductible, right?"*

**Your answer:** No. Only the spousal support is deductible.

| Payment | Monthly | Annual | Deductible? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spousal support | $2,400 | $28,800 | ✅ Line 22000 |
| Child support | $1,600 | $19,200 | ❌ No |
| **Total paid** | $4,000 | **$48,000** | |

**David's deduction:** $28,800

At his 43.41% combined marginal rate: $28,800 × 43.41% = **$12,502 in tax saved.**

His ex-wife Sarah must report the $28,800 as income on Line 12800. She will pay tax at her marginal rate (likely lower than David's — that's the point of the system).

**What to verify and document:** David's SIN, Sarah's SIN (required on his return), a copy of the court order, and confirmation that payments were made as ordered and that they were living apart throughout.

### Case D — The Contractor with Investment Debt

**Client:** Alex, 41, self-employed consultant earning $130,000 net business income. Three years ago he borrowed $150,000 at 6.5% annually against his home to invest in a non-registered portfolio of ETFs and dividend stocks.

Annual interest: $150,000 × 6.5% = **$9,750**

Alex has been claiming the interest as a home office expense on his T2125. That is **wrong.** The deduction belongs on **Line 22100** (carrying charges) — not as a business expense — because the borrowed funds were invested in a personal non-registered portfolio, not used in the business.

**The correction:**
- Remove $9,750 from T2125 business expenses (no change to business income)
- Add $9,750 to Line 22100 (reduces Net Income on T1)

The tax saving is identical in dollar terms — but the correct line matters for CRA review and for accurate tracking of the loan's investment purpose. If the purpose of the borrowed funds were ever challenged, clear documentation of the investment account funded by the loan is essential.

At Alex's 53.53% combined rate: $9,750 × 53.53% = **$5,219 in annual tax savings** — from an expense correctly classified, consistently documented.

## 12.10 Deduction Optimization — Strategic Sequencing

When a client has multiple deductions available and income-based limits apply to some of them, the order of application matters.

!!! example "Case — Moving Expenses, RRSP, and the One-Year Carryforward"
    Rachel has $46,000 in new-location employment income and $40,000 in eligible moving expenses. She also has a $14,000 RRSP contribution she wants to claim.

    **Option A — Moving expenses first:**
    $46,000 − $40,000 (moving) = $6,000 net. Then RRSP: only $6,000 of the $14,000 can be claimed this year. The $8,000 unused RRSP deduction carries forward indefinitely.

    **Option B — RRSP first:**
    $46,000 − $14,000 (RRSP) = $32,000. Then moving: $32,000 of the $40,000 moving expenses are deductible. The remaining $8,000 moving expenses carry forward — but only **one year.**

    **Which is better?** Option A is safer. RRSP unused deductions carry forward indefinitely; moving expense carryforwards are limited to one year. By claiming moving expenses first, Rachel ensures the time-sensitive carryforward is used, while the RRSP deduction — which never expires — is simply deferred.

    Tax software may not automatically sequence this optimally. Understanding the interaction lets you override the default and protect the client's carryforward.

---

!!! tip "In your tax software"
    Each deduction lives on its own input screen: **union dues (Line 21200)** — enter T4 Box 44 amounts plus any direct professional regulatory fees (these won't appear on any slip); **childcare (T778)** — assign to the lower-income spouse's file, enter each child's birth date so the software applies the correct per-child limit automatically; **moving expenses (T1-M)** — enter the new employer's address and the new-location income earned so the software calculates the deductible ceiling correctly; **carrying charges (Line 22100)** — enter investment loan interest and advisory fees with the lender or advisor name for audit support; **support payments (Line 22000)** — enter the recipient's SIN (required by CRA for cross-matching). For any moving expense carryforward, flag the client file to claim it next year — software does not automatically carry moving expenses forward the way it does RRSP room.

## 12.11 Week 12 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - Deductions reduce **Net Income (Line 23600)** — doubly valuable because they reduce tax AND income-tested benefit calculations simultaneously
    - **Union dues and professional fees (Line 21200):** Always ask about licensing fees paid directly to regulatory bodies — nurses, engineers, teachers, lawyers, pharmacists routinely miss this. T4 Box 44 is only part of the picture
    - **Childcare (Line 21400):** Must be claimed by the **lower-income spouse** — with specific exceptions for schooling, hospitalization, and separation. Limits: $8,000/child under 7, $5,000/child 7–16, $11,000 for certified disability
    - **Moving expenses (Line 21900):** The 40 km distance test is measured to the **new workplace** — not between homes. Mortgage break penalties, land transfer tax, legal fees, and temporary accommodation are all eligible — and almost always missed. Deductible against **new-location income only**; one-year carryforward
    - **Carrying charges (Line 22100):** Investment loan interest and advisory fees on non-registered accounts are deductible — and are the single most consistently missed deduction category in practice. Ask about fee-based advisors on every return
    - **Support payments (Line 22000):** Spousal support under a written agreement or court order is deductible. **Child support is never deductible** — confirm the client is not trying to claim it
    - **Disability supports (Line 21500):** Separate from the Disability Tax Credit; reduces net income; for high-income earners this deduction is more valuable than the equivalent medical credit
    - **The intake questions are as important as the technical knowledge** — build the 12-question script into every client meeting without exception

## ✅ Week 12 Quiz

When you're ready, ask Claude: **"Quiz me on Week 12"** for an interactive test.

??? question "1. Your client is a registered pharmacist earning $108,000. Her T4 Box 44 shows $540 in union dues. She also pays $2,300/year directly to the Ontario College of Pharmacists. What goes on Line 21200 and what is the tax saving at her marginal rate?"
    **Line 21200 total: $540 + $2,300 = $2,840.** The Ontario College of Pharmacists fee is required to maintain her professional licence and is fully deductible as professional dues — it does not appear on any slip and requires a direct intake question to surface. At a combined Ontario marginal rate of approximately 43.41% at $108,000: $2,840 × 43.41% = **$1,233 in tax saved** from a question that takes 10 seconds to ask. Preparers who only capture Box 44 leave $999 on the table for this client alone.

??? question "2. Victor earns $112,000 and his wife Helen earns $64,000. They paid $22,000 in childcare for children aged 2 and 7. Who claims the deduction, what is the maximum, and what happens to the rest?"
    **Helen claims** — she is the lower-income spouse. Maximum per child: age 2 (under 7) → $8,000; age 7 → $5,000. **Total maximum: $13,000.** The actual expenses of $22,000 are capped — Helen claims $13,000. The remaining $9,000 is simply not claimable; it does not carry forward. Victor cannot claim any portion regardless of who paid. At Helen's combined ~33% rate: $13,000 × 33% = **$4,290 in tax saved**.

??? question "3. Michael moves from Halifax to Calgary for a new job in June 2025. His eligible moving costs total $31,400. He earns $19,000 in Calgary before December 31. What does he claim in 2025 and what happens to the balance?"
    He claims **$19,000** in 2025 — limited to income earned at the new location. The remaining **$12,400 carries forward one year** to the 2026 return, where it can be deducted against Calgary employment income. Unlike RRSP deductions (which carry forward indefinitely), moving expense carryforwards last only one year. Flag this in the client file and ensure it is claimed on the 2026 return — it is frequently forgotten.

??? question "4. Helen has a $420,000 non-registered portfolio. Her fee-based advisor charges 1.1% annually ($4,620). She borrowed $70,000 at 6.8% ($4,760 interest) to top up her portfolio two years ago. She has never claimed either on her return. What is the total Line 22100 deduction and the tax saved at her 46.41% marginal rate?"
    **Total Line 22100: $4,620 (advisory fee) + $4,760 (investment loan interest) = $9,380.** Both are deductible — the advisory fee because it is paid for investment management of a non-registered account, and the interest because the loan was used to earn investment income. At 46.41%: $9,380 × 46.41% = **$4,353 in tax saved** — annually, from expenses she was already paying. Note: the MER embedded in any mutual funds in her portfolio is not deductible — it is already netted from fund returns before income is reported.

??? question "5. Daniel pays $3,000/month under a 2023 court order: $1,700 spousal support and $1,300 child support. He asks why he cannot deduct the full $36,000 he paid. Explain the rule and calculate his actual deduction."
    Under rules that apply to all agreements made after April 1997: **spousal support is deductible for the payer and taxable for the recipient**; **child support is neither deductible nor taxable** — it flows tax-neutral. The policy distinction: spousal support replaces income; child support funds child-rearing costs. Daniel's deductible amount: $1,700 × 12 = **$20,400 on Line 22000**. The $1,300 × 12 = $15,600 in child support does not appear on his return. His ex-wife reports the $20,400 as income on Line 12800.

??? question "6. Rachel has $48,000 in new-location employment income and $44,000 in eligible moving expenses. She also wants to claim a $12,000 RRSP deduction. In what order should these be applied and why?"
    Apply **moving expenses first** ($48,000 − $44,000 = $4,000 remaining income), then RRSP ($4,000 of the $12,000 is claimed this year; $8,000 RRSP deduction carries forward indefinitely). Why: moving expense carryforwards are limited to **one year**; RRSP unused deductions carry forward **indefinitely**. By using the time-sensitive moving expense first, the full $44,000 is captured. The $8,000 RRSP deduction, though deferred, is not lost. Reversing the order would risk losing $8,000 in moving expenses to the one-year limit while the RRSP deduction — which faces no such pressure — would have been claimed unnecessarily early.

??? question "7. Name four specific expenses from a real estate move that most preparers miss — and confirm each is deductible."
    Many valid answers — four strong examples: **(1) Mortgage break penalty** — when a fixed-rate mortgage is broken early to facilitate the move, the penalty is a deductible moving expense (often $3,000–$8,000). **(2) Land transfer tax** on the purchase of the new home — a direct moving cost, frequently overlooked. **(3) Temporary accommodation at the new location** — up to 15 days of hotel or short-term rental costs while waiting for possession. **(4) Vehicle registration and driver's licence fee** when relocating to a new province — required by law, directly triggered by the move, deductible.
