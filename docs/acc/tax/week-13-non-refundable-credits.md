!!! tip "Credits vs. deductions — why the distinction shapes every planning decision"
    A **deduction** reduces income — its value scales with your marginal rate. A $5,000 deduction is worth $2,171 at 43.41% and only $1,003 at 20.05%. A **non-refundable credit** reduces federal tax directly — always at a flat **15% federal rate**, regardless of income. This means credits are proportionally more valuable to lower-income earners and relatively less powerful for high-income clients. Understanding this shapes how you advise on everything from charitable donations to the disability tax credit. For high-income clients: maximize deductions first. For low-income clients: credits may be the primary tool.

## 13.1 How Non-Refundable Credits Work — The Mechanics

Non-refundable credits are claimed on **Schedule 1** and reduce **federal tax payable** directly. They cannot reduce federal tax below zero — unused amounts are either forfeited or, in specific cases, transferable to a spouse or supporting individual.

### The Conversion Formula

```
Credit Amount × 15% = Federal Tax Reduction

Example:
Basic Personal Amount:   $16,129
× 15%
= $2,419 reduction in federal tax
```

!!! warning "The credit amount ≠ the tax reduction"
    This confuses clients constantly. When CRA says the Basic Personal Amount is $16,129, that is not $16,129 off your taxes. It is $16,129 × 15% = **$2,419 off your federal taxes**. The $16,129 is the base for the calculation — not the benefit itself. Explain this on every return where a client sees these numbers for the first time.

### Non-Refundable vs. Refundable

| Type | Can reduce tax below $0? | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-refundable** | ❌ No — excess is lost (or transferred) | Basic Personal, disability, tuition |
| **Refundable** | ✅ Yes — paid out even if no tax owing | GST/HST credit, CCB, CWB |

The consequence: a client with zero federal tax owing gets **no benefit** from additional non-refundable credits. This makes it critical to plan which family member claims each credit — the person with the higher tax bill gets the most value.

### Provincial Credits

Every province has its own parallel set of non-refundable credits at its own rates. Ontario's basic personal credit rate is 5.05%; BC's is 5.06%; Alberta has none (flat tax). When this guide refers to "credit rates," it means federal unless stated otherwise. The combined federal + provincial saving is higher — typically 20–27% for the base personal credits.

## 13.2 The Core Credits — Schedule 1 Line by Line

These apply to nearly every return you will prepare. Know them cold.

### Basic Personal Amount — Line 30000

**2025 amount:** $16,129 for most taxpayers
**Federal credit:** $16,129 × 15% = **$2,419**

Every Canadian resident gets this. No eligibility requirements, no forms. It simply exists.

!!! tip "The Basic Personal Amount phases down at very high incomes"
    For taxpayers with net income above **$173,205**, the Basic Personal Amount begins phasing down toward a minimum of $14,538. The phase-out is complete at net income of **$246,752**. At the minimum BPA of $14,538: $14,538 × 15% = $2,181. The difference ($238) is minor but technically accurate. Tax software handles this automatically.

---

### Age Amount — Line 30100

**Who:** Taxpayers who are **65 or older** at December 31 of the tax year.
**2025 maximum:** $8,790
**Federal credit at maximum:** $8,790 × 15% = **$1,319**

**The reduction:** The Age Amount is reduced by 15% of net income above **$44,325**.

```
Age Amount Reduction = (Net Income − $44,325) × 15%
Age Amount = $8,790 − Reduction (minimum $0)
Full elimination at net income: $44,325 + ($8,790 ÷ 15%) = ~$103,925
```

!!! example "Case — Age Amount at Different Income Levels"
    | Net Income | Calculation | Age Amount |
    |---|---|---|
    | $38,000 | Below $44,325 — no reduction | **$8,790** (full) |
    | $60,000 | ($60,000 − $44,325) × 15% = $2,351 reduction | **$6,439** |
    | $90,000 | ($90,000 − $44,325) × 15% = $6,851 reduction | **$1,939** |
    | $103,925+ | Fully reduced | **$0** |

    **Why this matters for planning:** Every dollar of income above $44,325 costs a senior 15 cents in Age Amount — on top of their regular marginal rate. This creates an effective marginal rate surtax for seniors in the $44,325–$103,925 range. Strategic RRSP/RRIF timing, pension splitting, and income-deferral decisions should always be modelled against this threshold for clients aged 65+.

### Spouse or Common-Law Partner Amount — Line 30300

**Who:** A taxpayer who supported their spouse or CLP at any time during the year and their spouse had low or no income.

**2025 maximum:** $16,129 (same as Basic Personal Amount)
**Calculation:** $16,129 − spouse's net income = spousal amount (minimum $0)
**Federal credit at maximum:** $16,129 × 15% = **$2,419**

!!! example "Case — Partial Spousal Credit"
    Robert earns $95,000. His wife Linda earns $8,400 part-time.

    Spousal amount: $16,129 − $8,400 = **$7,729**
    Federal credit: $7,729 × 15% = **$1,159**

    If Linda earned nothing: $16,129 × 15% = $2,419 (maximum).
    If Linda earned $16,129 or more: $0 spousal credit.

    **Planning implication:** For couples where one spouse has low earned income, optimizing the non-earning spouse's income sources to stay below the BPA threshold ($16,129) preserves this credit. Every dollar Linda earns above $0 reduces Robert's spousal credit by $0.15 per dollar. It's a minor effect — but worth knowing when advising on pension splitting amounts.

### Canada Caregiver Credit — Lines 30400 / 30425 / 30450

This credit consolidates what used to be three separate credits. It applies when a taxpayer supports an infirm dependant.

| Who Is Supported | Line | 2025 Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Infirm spouse or CLP | 30400 or 30425 supplement | Up to $7,999 |
| Infirm dependant 18+ (parent, adult child, sibling) | 30450 | Up to $7,999 |
| Infirm dependant under 18 | 30500 | $2,499 |

!!! tip "This credit is routinely missed for clients supporting aging parents"
    If your client financially supports a parent who lives with them (or depends on them) and that parent has a physical or mental impairment, the Canada Caregiver Credit may apply. The impairment does not need to be DTC-certified for some portions — but certification helps. Always ask: *"Are you supporting a parent or other adult family member who has a health condition that affects their daily life?"*

### Canada Employment Amount — Line 31260

**Who:** All taxpayers with employment income.
**2025 maximum:** $1,433
**Federal credit:** $1,433 × 15% = **$215**

This is a flat amount available to any employee — it recognizes employment-related costs that aren't itemized. Capped at the lesser of $1,433 or actual employment income. Your software claims it automatically.

## 13.3 The Disability Tax Credit — The Most Valuable Credit Most Clients Don't Know They Have

The **Disability Tax Credit (DTC)** is the cornerstone of disability-related tax planning in Canada. It is also the most under-claimed credit in the country — CRA estimates that hundreds of thousands of eligible Canadians never apply for it.

### What It Is

The DTC is a non-refundable credit for individuals with a **severe and prolonged** physical or mental impairment that markedly restricts their ability to perform basic activities of daily living.

**2025 federal DTC amount:** $9,872
**Federal credit:** $9,872 × 15% = **$1,481**

**Supplement for children under 18:** Additional $5,758 (subject to reduction by certain childcare amounts)
**Federal credit on supplement:** $5,758 × 15% = **$864** additional

**Total potential DTC credit (adult):** $1,481 federal + provincial equivalent
**Total potential DTC credit (child under 18):** $1,481 + $864 = **$2,345** federal

### What Qualifies — The Eligibility Criteria

The impairment must be **severe** (markedly restricts the ability to perform the activity) and **prolonged** (has lasted or is expected to last at least 12 months).

**Basic activities of daily living:**

| Activity | Examples of Qualifying Conditions |
|---|---|
| **Vision** | Legally blind, severe visual impairment |
| **Speaking** | Severe stuttering, inability to communicate verbally |
| **Hearing** | Profound deafness, severe hearing loss |
| **Walking** | Requires mobility aid, cannot walk more than 50m |
| **Eliminating** | Ostomy, severe IBS requiring significant time |
| **Feeding** | Cannot feed self without extensive support |
| **Dressing** | Cannot dress without extensive assistance |
| **Mental functions for everyday life** | Severe depression, schizophrenia, ADHD (severe), autism spectrum, dementia |
| **Life-sustaining therapy** | Dialysis, insulin therapy (T1 diabetes — see below) |

!!! success "Type 1 diabetes — a frequently missed DTC"
    As of 2021, **Type 1 diabetes** qualifies for the DTC under the life-sustaining therapy category. Individuals who require insulin therapy and spend 14+ hours per week on activities related to managing their diabetes (injections, blood glucose monitoring, diet management, medical consultations) qualify.

    Many T1 diabetics — and their preparers — don't know this. If a client mentions managing Type 1 diabetes, ask immediately: *"Have you applied for the Disability Tax Credit? You likely qualify."* The credit is worth $1,481/year in federal taxes, plus provincial equivalents.

### The Application Process — Form T2201

The DTC is not automatic. It requires:

1. Client (or authorized representative) completes Part A of **Form T2201**
2. A qualified practitioner (doctor, nurse practitioner, psychologist, optometrist, audiologist, etc.) completes **Part B** — certifying the nature, severity, and duration of the impairment
3. T2201 is submitted to CRA for approval **before** claiming the credit
4. CRA reviews and issues an approval letter specifying the eligible years

!!! warning "The DTC must be approved BEFORE you claim it"
    You cannot claim the DTC on a return without prior CRA approval of Form T2201. Filing a return with a DTC claim and no approval will trigger a CRA review and the credit will be denied. The approval must be in hand.

    **However:** once approved, the DTC can be applied retroactively — going back 10 years.

### The 10-Year Carry-Back — Where Expert Preparers Create Real Value

!!! example "Case — The Retroactive DTC Windfall"
    Maria, 52, was diagnosed with a condition affecting her mobility 8 years ago. She has been managing with aids and adaptations. She never applied for the DTC.

    You apply for T2201 this year. CRA approves it, effective 8 years ago.

    **Retroactive DTC credits — federal only:**

    | Year | Federal DTC (approx.) | Savings at ~15% |
    |---|---|---|
    | 8 years ago | $8,870 credit base | ~$1,331 |
    | 7 years ago | $9,000 | ~$1,350 |
    | 6 years ago | $9,100 | ~$1,365 |
    | 5 years ago | $9,200 | ~$1,380 |
    | 4 years ago | $9,428 | ~$1,414 |
    | 3 years ago | $9,586 | ~$1,438 |
    | 2 years ago | $9,713 | ~$1,457 |
    | Current year | $9,872 | ~$1,481 |
    | **Total federal refunds** | | **~$11,216** |

    Plus provincial equivalents — Ontario alone adds approximately 50% more. Total combined refund: approximately **$16,000–$18,000** returned to Maria for years she overpaid.

    You file T1-ADJ for each of the past 10 years (CRA allows adjustments going back 10 years). Maria receives retroactive refunds plus interest on the overpayments.

    This is the kind of result that generates referrals for years. Clients tell everyone. Asking *"Has anyone ever mentioned the Disability Tax Credit to you?"* takes 5 seconds. The outcome can be life-changing.

### Transferring the DTC to a Supporting Person

If the person with the disability does not have enough taxable income to use the full DTC, the **unused portion can be transferred** to a supporting individual — a spouse, parent, grandparent, child, grandchild, sibling, aunt, uncle, niece, or nephew who supports them.

The transfer amount is calculated on **Schedule 2** and flows to the supporting person's Schedule 1.

!!! example "Case — Parent Transferring Child's DTC"
    Kevin's 9-year-old son has autism spectrum disorder (ASD) — a qualifying condition for the DTC. The child has no income and no tax to offset.

    **Child's DTC:** $9,872 (base) + $5,758 (supplement under 18) = $15,630
    Federal credit: $15,630 × 15% = **$2,345**

    Kevin transfers the full unused amount to his own return.
    Kevin's federal tax reduction: **$2,345/year** — plus provincial equivalent (~$1,100 in Ontario)
    **Total annual saving: ~$3,445**

    The child has had ASD since birth. Kevin never applied for the DTC because his doctor never told him to and no preparer ever asked.

    Retroactively — from birth to age 9: 9 years × ~$3,200 average annual saving = approximately **$28,800 in refunds** once T1-ADJs are filed.

---

!!! question "Quick Check"
    Your client mentions that her 64-year-old mother has managed Type 1 diabetes with multiple daily insulin injections for over 20 years — spending approximately 15+ hours per week on diabetes-related activities (injections, blood glucose monitoring, diet management, medical appointments). No one has ever applied for the Disability Tax Credit. What do you do and what is the estimated combined annual tax saving?

    ??? answer
        **Apply for the DTC immediately** using Form T2201 — have the mother's endocrinologist or family doctor complete Part B certifying the life-sustaining therapy requirement (14+ hours/week qualifies for T1 diabetes since the 2021 CRA policy change). Annual combined saving once approved: $9,872 × 15% = $1,481 (federal) + ~$830 (Ontario) = **approximately $2,311/year**. If the mother has low income and can't use the full credit, the unused amount transfers to your client on Schedule 2. File **T1-ADJ for all eligible prior years** (up to 10 years back) — retroactive recovery could total **$20,000–$23,000 in combined refunds**. This question takes five seconds to ask. The outcome changes a family's finances for years.

## 13.4 Medical Expenses — Line 33099 / 33199

Medical expenses are one of the most complex and most underused credits on the T1. The rules have enough nuance that even experienced preparers regularly leave money on the table.

### How the Medical Expense Credit Works

The federal credit is **15% of eligible medical expenses** exceeding the lesser of:
- **3% of net income**, OR
- **$2,759** (the 2025 fixed threshold)

```
Total eligible medical expenses
  − Lesser of (3% × net income) or $2,759
══════════════════════════════════════════
Net eligible amount
  × 15%
══════════════════════════════════════════
Federal medical expense credit
```

!!! example "Case — The 3% Threshold in Action"
    Sandra has net income of $72,000 and paid $5,800 in eligible medical expenses.

    | Threshold | Calculation | Amount |
    |---|---|---|
    | 3% of net income | $72,000 × 3% | $2,160 |
    | Fixed threshold | | $2,759 |
    | **Lesser of the two** | | **$2,160** |

    Net eligible: $5,800 − $2,160 = **$3,640**
    Federal credit: $3,640 × 15% = **$546**

    At a lower net income of $35,000:
    3% threshold: $35,000 × 3% = $1,050
    Fixed threshold: $2,759
    **Lesser: $1,050** — the lower-income person gets a larger credit from the same $5,800 in expenses.

    **Planning implication:** Medical expenses should generally be claimed by the **lower-income spouse** — their 3% threshold is smaller, meaning more of the expenses clear the hurdle. This is the opposite of deductions (which are better for higher earners). For medical expenses: **lower income = lower threshold = larger credit.**

### The 12-Month Window Strategy — The Most Powerful Medical Expense Optimization

Medical expenses don't have to follow the calendar year. CRA allows you to claim any **12-consecutive-month period ending in the tax year** — not just January 1 to December 31.

This creates a powerful planning opportunity: if a client had a cluster of large medical expenses in the second half of one year and the first half of the next, you can combine them into a single 12-month window that exceeds the threshold more effectively.

!!! example "Case — The 12-Month Window in Practice"
    Rachel's medical expenses:
    - March 2024: $1,200 (physiotherapy)
    - September 2024: $800 (specialist visits)
    - February 2025: $3,400 (dental work)
    - April 2025: $600 (prescriptions)

    **Option A — Calendar year 2025:** $3,400 + $600 = $4,000
    Net income $68,000. Threshold: $2,040 (3%). Net eligible: $1,960. Credit: **$294**

    **Option B — 12-month window March 2024–February 2025:** $1,200 + $800 + $3,400 = $5,400
    Same threshold: $2,040. Net eligible: $3,360. Credit: **$504**

    **Option C — 12-month window September 2024–August 2025:** $800 + $3,400 + $600 = $4,800
    Net eligible: $2,760. Credit: **$414**

    **Best option: March 2024–February 2025 → $504**

    The difference between Option A ($294) and Option B ($504) is $210 — from the same expenses, simply by choosing a different 12-month period. Tax software does not automatically optimize this. You must identify the best window manually or ask your software to test all possible 12-month periods.

    For clients with large dental, vision, or paramedical bills spanning two calendar years, this optimization can double or triple the medical credit. Always ask: *"When exactly did each large expense occur?"*

### Claiming for Multiple Family Members

You can pool eligible medical expenses for yourself, your spouse or CLP, and **dependent children born in 2008 or later** (under 18) on one return — then optimize which spouse claims the combined total.

For **other dependants** (adult children, parents, siblings) with low income, you can claim their medical expenses on your return — but the 3% threshold is calculated on **their** income, not yours. This can make it worthwhile to claim a parent's expenses.

!!! example "Case — Claiming a Parent's Medical Expenses"
    David's 74-year-old mother lives on OAS and CPP — total income $20,000. She had $9,400 in dental and vision expenses this year, having avoided the dentist for years.

    **If David claims on his own return:**
    His 3% threshold: $92,000 × 3% = $2,760. Net eligible: $9,400 − $2,760 = $6,640. Credit: $6,640 × 15% = **$996**

    **If his mother claims on her own return:**
    Her 3% threshold: $20,000 × 3% = $600. Net eligible: $9,400 − $600 = $8,800. Credit: $8,800 × 15% = **$1,320**

    His mother claiming her own expenses generates a **$324 higher credit** — because her 3% threshold is so much smaller. Even though she may have low federal tax, the credit may still apply against provincial taxes, or create a refund if she has any tax liability.

    The decision depends on her actual tax owing. If she pays no tax at all, David should claim the expenses on his return (something is better than nothing). Model both scenarios.

!!! question "Quick Check"
    Sandra (net income $34,000) and Robert (net income $95,000) paid $6,800 in eligible medical expenses this year — dental work, physiotherapy, and prescriptions. Which spouse should claim the medical expense credit and why? Calculate the federal credit on each return.

    ??? answer
        **Sandra should claim.** Her 3% threshold: $34,000 × 3% = $1,020. Robert's: $95,000 × 3% = $2,850. **Sandra's return:** $6,800 − $1,020 = $5,780 × 15% = **$867 federal credit**. **Robert's return:** $6,800 − $2,850 = $3,950 × 15% = **$593 federal credit**. Sandra generates $274 more credit — from the same expenses — because her lower income produces a smaller 3% threshold, allowing more of the expenses to clear the hurdle. Medical expense credits work opposite to deductions: **lower income = smaller threshold = larger credit.** Always model on the lower-income spouse first.

## 13.5 Charitable Donation Tax Credit — Lines 34900 / Schedule 9

Charitable donations generate one of the most generous credits in the Canadian tax system — and one that rewards **strategic accumulation** over piecemeal giving.

### The Federal Credit Rates

| Portion of Donation | Federal Credit Rate |
|---|---|
| First **$200** | **15%** |
| Amount **above $200** | **29%** (or 33% if income is in the top federal bracket) |

The provincial credits stack on top, ranging from 5.05% to 16% depending on province. In Ontario:

| Portion | Federal | Ontario | **Combined** |
|---|---|---|---|
| First $200 | 15% | 5.05% | **20.05%** |
| Above $200 | 29% | 11.16% | **40.16%** |

**For top-bracket donors (income over $220,000):**
Above $200: 33% federal + 13.16% Ontario = **46.16% combined** — nearly half the donation returned as tax savings.

### The Donation Stacking Strategy — Why Biennial Giving Saves More

The first $200 of donations is taxed at only 15%. Every dollar after that gets 29%+. This creates a clear incentive: **combine two years of donations into a single return** to spend as little time as possible in the low-rate zone.

!!! example "Case — Annual Giving vs. Biennial Stacking"
    Rachel donates $600/year to charity. She is in the 29% federal bracket.

    **Annual claiming (each year):**
    Year 1: $200 × 15% + $400 × 29% = $30 + $116 = $146 credit
    Year 2: $200 × 15% + $400 × 29% = $146 credit
    **Total over 2 years: $292 in federal credits**

    **Biennial stacking (claim every 2 years):**
    Year 1: claim $0 (carry the $600 forward)
    Year 2: claim $1,200 combined
    $200 × 15% + $1,000 × 29% = $30 + $290 = **$320 in federal credits**

    **Difference: $28 more in federal credits** from the same $1,200 in donations — simply by waiting.

    For higher donors, the math becomes more compelling. A donor giving $2,000/year who stacks every 5 years: $200 × 15% + $9,800 × 29% = $30 + $2,842 = **$2,872** — vs. five annual claims at ($200 × 15% + $1,800 × 29%) = $552/year × 5 = $2,760. Stacking saves **$112** and is completely legal.

!!! tip "Donations can be carried forward 5 years"
    A donation receipt not claimed on this year's return can be carried forward up to **5 years**. This enables both the stacking strategy and also allows timing a large donation deduction against a high-income year (e.g., a year with a business sale or large bonus).

### Spousal Donation Pooling

Donations made by either spouse can be **combined on one return** — whichever spouse has the higher income (or the higher tax bill) should claim them to maximize the above-$200 credit rate.

!!! example "Case — Why Donations Should Be on One Return"
    Victor earns $130,000. His wife Helen earns $32,000. Together they donated $1,400 this year.

    **If each claims separately:**
    Victor: $700 → $200 × 15% + $500 × 29% = $175 federal
    Helen: $700 → $200 × 15% + $500 × 29% = $175 federal
    **Total: $350 federal**

    **If Victor claims both on his return:**
    $1,400 → $200 × 15% + $1,200 × 29% = $30 + $348 = **$378 federal**

    Combining saves $28 in federal credits. Modest — but there is no cost to combining, and for larger donations the savings are more significant. Always pool donations on one return, always on the higher-income spouse.

### Gifts of Securities — The Most Tax-Efficient Donation Method

This is one of the most powerful and underused strategies in Canadian tax planning.

When a taxpayer donates **publicly traded securities directly to a registered charity** (without selling them first), the **capital gain on the donated securities is fully exempt from tax**. Zero percent inclusion rate on the gain.

!!! example "Case — The Donation of Appreciated Shares"
    Margaret owns RBC shares she bought 15 years ago for $8,000. They are now worth $28,000 — an accrued gain of $20,000.

    **Option A — Sell and donate cash:**
    Capital gain: $20,000. Taxable gain (50%): $10,000. Tax at 46.41%: **$4,641**
    Net cash after tax: $28,000 − $4,641 = $23,359
    Donate $23,359 cash: receipt for $23,359
    Donation credit (above-$200 at 29%): ~$6,754
    **Net tax cost after credit: $4,641 − $6,754 = net saving of $2,113 — but she also gave $23,359 away**

    **Option B — Donate shares directly to charity:**
    Capital gain: **$0** (exempt when donated directly)
    Donation receipt: **$28,000** (full fair market value)
    Donation credit: $200 × 15% + $27,800 × 29% = $30 + $8,062 = **$8,092**
    **Net tax saving: $8,092** — no capital gains tax, full FMV receipt

    **Difference: $8,092 vs. $2,113 — a $5,979 advantage** by donating the shares directly.

    Every client who has a non-registered account with long-held appreciated securities and who donates to charity should be having this conversation. It is one of the most financially impactful strategies in the Canadian tax system, and the vast majority of clients — and their financial advisors — never implement it.

### First-Time Donor Super Credit — Historical Note

The First-Time Donor Super Credit (an extra 25% on the first $1,000 of donations for first-time donors) was available from 2013–2017 and has since expired. Know that it existed if you encounter older files — but it does not apply to current returns.

## 13.6 Tuition Tax Credit — Lines 32300 / Schedule 11

The tuition credit rewards post-secondary education costs. It is non-refundable — and crucially, it can be **transferred or carried forward** when the student has no tax to apply it against.

### What Qualifies

| ✅ Eligible | ❌ Not Eligible |
|---|---|
| Tuition fees paid to a Canadian university, college, or designated post-secondary institution | Books, supplies, student activity fees |
| Mandatory fees required for enrollment | Residence, meal plans |
| Examination fees (professional/occupational licensing) | Transportation to school |
| Fees for post-secondary courses at a recognized institution | Voluntary ancillary fees |
| Tuition at a foreign university (full-time, 3 consecutive weeks minimum) | Online courses from non-designated institutions |

**Supported by:** Form T2202 (Tuition and Enrolment Certificate), issued by the institution by the end of February.

### The Credit Calculation

Federal credit: tuition × 15%

A student paying $12,000 in tuition generates: $12,000 × 15% = **$1,800 in federal tuition credit**

### The Four Options for Unused Tuition Credits

A student with little or no income typically owes little or no tax. The tuition credit may exceed their federal tax. Here's what can happen to the excess:

=== "Option 1 — Carry Forward"

    The student carries the unused credit forward indefinitely to future tax years when they have income and tax to apply it against.

    **Best use:** The student expects to earn significant income in the next 1–3 years (graduation approaching, job lined up). The credit is preserved at full value.

    **Reported on:** Schedule 11. The carryforward appears on the student's NOA and in CRA My Account.

=== "Option 2 — Transfer to Spouse or CLP"

    The student can transfer up to **$5,000** of the current year's tuition credit to their spouse or common-law partner.

    **Important:** Only the **current year's** credit can be transferred. Amounts carried forward from prior years cannot be transferred — they must be used by the student.

    **Best use:** Student is married or in a CLP and their spouse has tax owing this year.

=== "Option 3 — Transfer to Parent or Grandparent"

    The student can transfer up to **$5,000** of the current year's tuition credit to a parent or grandparent who is supporting them.

    **Mechanics:** The student designates the transfer on Schedule 11. The parent claims it on Schedule 2 (Federal Amounts Transferred from Your Spouse or Common-Law Partner) or directly on Line 32400.

    **Best use:** Student has a supporting parent with meaningful tax owing. This is the most common transfer scenario.

=== "Option 4 — Use on Own Return"

    If the student has tax owing (from part-time employment, investment income, or RRSP withdrawals), apply the credit directly against their own tax first before deciding whether to carry forward or transfer.

!!! example "Case — The First-Year University Student"
    Tyler, 19, is in first-year university. He paid $11,800 in tuition in 2025. He worked part-time and earned $9,200.

    **Tyler's federal tax on $9,200:**
    After Basic Personal Amount ($16,129), Tyler's taxable income is $0. He owes no federal tax.

    **Tuition credit generated:** $11,800 × 15% = **$1,770**

    Tyler has no federal tax to apply it against. His options:
    1. **Carry forward** $1,770 to use after graduation → best if he expects income soon
    2. **Transfer up to $5,000 base amount** ($5,000 × 15% = $750 credit) to a parent

    His mother earns $88,000 and has federal tax owing. Tyler designates the maximum transfer on his Schedule 11. His mother claims $750 on her Schedule 1.

    **Her tax saving: $750 in federal credit + provincial equivalent (~$375 Ontario) = ~$1,125**

    Tyler retains any unused credit above the $5,000 transferred base as a personal carryforward.

    **Note for your practice:** Always ask: *"Does the student have a spouse or parents who might benefit from the tuition transfer?"* And always verify — on the student's NOA — what accumulated tuition credits they have carried forward. Students who've been in school for 4 years may have $5,000–$8,000 in accumulated federal credits waiting to be used.

## 13.7 Interest Paid on Student Loans — Line 31900

Interest paid on student loans from the **Canada Student Loans program** or provincial equivalents generates a **15% federal credit**.

**Important changes:** As of 2023, the federal government eliminated interest on Canada Student Loans and Canada Apprentice Loans. Many provincial student loans still charge interest. The credit applies to interest actually paid — and with federal interest eliminated, this credit primarily applies to provincial loan interest going forward.

**Carryforward:** Unused student loan interest credits can be carried forward 5 years.

## 13.8 The Home Accessibility Tax Credit — Line 31285

A non-refundable credit for eligible **renovation expenses** that improve accessibility or safety for a qualifying individual (a person 65+ or someone eligible for the DTC) in their home.

**2025 maximum eligible expenses:** $20,000
**Federal credit:** $20,000 × 15% = **$3,000**

**What qualifies:**
- Grab bars and handrails
- Wheelchair ramps
- Walk-in bathtubs or showers
- Widened doorways for wheelchair access
- Non-slip flooring
- Stair lifts and elevators

!!! tip "Stack with the Medical Expense Credit where possible"
    Some home renovation expenses for disability may qualify under **both** the Home Accessibility Tax Credit AND the Medical Expense Credit. Claim as many as possible under HATC (up to $20,000) first; the remainder may qualify under medical expenses. They serve different purposes and can often be combined.

## 13.9 Credit Transfers Between Family Members — The Full Picture

Non-refundable credits can flow between family members in specific, limited ways. Understanding these transfers is where family tax planning becomes powerful.

### What Can Be Transferred

| Credit | Can Transfer To | Maximum Transferable |
|---|---|---|
| **Age Amount** (excess unused) | Spouse only | Amount unused by the senior |
| **Disability Tax Credit** (unused) | Supporting person (spouse, parent, child, sibling, etc.) | Full unused amount |
| **Tuition credit** (current year only) | Spouse or CLP, or parent/grandparent | Max $5,000 base ($750 federal credit) |
| **Pension Income Amount** | Spouse only | Amount unused by recipient |

### What Cannot Be Transferred

| Credit | Transfer Allowed? |
|---|---|
| Medical expenses (excess) | ❌ No — must be claimed by the person who paid or their spouse |
| Charitable donations (excess) | ❌ No — but the other spouse can claim the donations instead |
| Basic Personal Amount | ❌ No |
| Canada Employment Amount | ❌ No |
| Carry-forward tuition credits | ❌ No — only current-year tuition can be transferred |

## 13.10 The 8 Most Common Credit Mistakes

These are the errors that appear in amended returns — filed by other preparers or by clients themselves.

| # | Mistake | What It Costs | How to Prevent |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Never applying for the DTC for an eligible client | $1,481–$3,445/year indefinitely | Ask every client about chronic conditions, disabilities, T1 diabetes |
| 2 | Missing the retroactive DTC opportunity | Up to $18,000+ in recoverable refunds | When DTC is approved, file T1-ADJ for all eligible prior years |
| 3 | Using the calendar year for medical expenses instead of optimizing the 12-month window | $100–$500 per client per year | Always ask when each large expense occurred before claiming |
| 4 | Claiming medical expenses on the higher-income spouse | Reduces the credit by using the larger 3% threshold | Medical credits: lower income = lower threshold = more credit |
| 5 | Failing to pool charitable donations on one return | Small credit leakage on every return | Always combine donations on the higher-income or higher-tax spouse |
| 6 | Transferring tuition credits before checking if the student can use them | Student loses carry-forward value if they'll have income soon | Model the student's expected income for next 1–3 years |
| 7 | Claiming donations in every year instead of stacking | Loses the above-$200 rate advantage on small annual amounts | For donors giving $600/year or less — consider biennial stacking |
| 8 | Missing the Home Accessibility Tax Credit for renovations at a senior's home | Up to $3,000 federal credit lost | Ask every client 65+ or with a disabled family member: "Any home renovations this year?" |

---

!!! tip "In your tax software"
    On Schedule 1: the **Disability Tax Credit** screen will prompt for the T2201 approval date and eligible years — enter exactly what the CRA approval letter states; the software auto-calculates the credit and flags when a transfer to a supporting person is available. For **medical expenses**, use the 12-month window tool if your software offers one — if not, manually test the window that captures the highest-cost cluster of expenses; always assign to the lower-income spouse. For **charitable donations**: pool both spouses' receipts on the higher-income spouse's Schedule 9; the software applies the 15% and 29% tiers automatically. For **tuition transfers**, the student must first enter their T2202 on Schedule 11 and designate the transfer amount — the supporting parent's return then imports it on Line 32400. Verify carried-forward tuition credits from the prior year NOA appear correctly in the student's current return.

## 13.11 Case Studies — Credits in the Real World

### Case A — The DTC Nobody Mentioned

**Client:** James, 46. He was diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes at age 12. He manages his condition with multiple daily injections, blood glucose monitoring 6–8 times per day, strict diet management, and quarterly endocrinologist visits. He estimates he spends 16–18 hours per week on diabetes management activities.

He has never applied for the DTC. His prior preparer never asked.

**DTC eligibility:** Type 1 diabetes with 14+ hours/week of life-sustaining therapy — **qualifies** under the 2021 amendments.

**What you do:**
1. Explain the DTC to James and confirm he qualifies
2. Have his endocrinologist complete Part B of Form T2201
3. Submit to CRA — approval typically takes 8–12 weeks
4. Once approved, file T1-ADJ for all eligible prior years (up to 10 years)

**Annual credit (combined federal + Ontario):** approximately $1,481 + $830 = **$2,311/year**

**10-year retroactive recovery:** approximately **$22,000–$25,000** in combined refunds

James has been overpaying by $2,311/year for over a decade. No prior preparer ever asked the one question that would have started this process.

### Case B — The Medical Expense Window Optimization

**Client:** Patricia, 58. Her medical expenses:

| Date | Expense | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| March 2024 | Major dental work | $4,200 |
| August 2024 | Physiotherapy (12 sessions) | $1,440 |
| January 2025 | Specialist consultation + tests | $890 |
| April 2025 | New glasses + eye exam | $680 |
| September 2025 | More dental work | $2,100 |

Net income: $78,000. Threshold: $78,000 × 3% = **$2,340**

**Testing every 12-month window:**

| Window | Expenses Included | Total | Less $2,340 | Credit (×15%) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Jan–Dec 2025 | $890 + $680 + $2,100 | $3,670 | $1,330 | **$200** |
| Apr 2025–Mar 2026 | $680 + $2,100 + (nothing in 2026 yet) | $2,780 | $440 | **$66** |
| Mar 2024–Feb 2025 | $4,200 + $1,440 + $890 | $6,530 | $4,190 | **$629** |
| Aug 2024–Jul 2025 | $1,440 + $890 + $680 | $3,010 | $670 | **$101** |

**Best window: March 2024–February 2025 → $629 federal credit**

Calendar year 2025 would have given only $200. The optimal window gives **$429 more** — from the same expenses — because the dental cluster in March 2024 is captured.

Patricia gets $629 federal + provincial (~$310 Ontario) = **$939 in total credits** instead of $200+$110 = $310. The difference is $629 in additional refund from choosing the right 12-month window.

### Case C — The Donation of Appreciated Shares

**Client:** Robert, 61. He has been donating $3,000/year to his hospital foundation for 12 years. He always writes a cheque from his bank account.

He mentions his investment portfolio has some RBC shares he bought in 2008 that have tripled in value — purchased for $14,000, now worth $42,000.

**Current method (cheque):**
$3,000 donation receipt. Credit: $200 × 15% + $2,800 × 29% = $30 + $812 = **$842 combined federal credit per year**

**Alternative — donate shares instead:**
Robert donates $42,000 worth of RBC shares directly to the hospital foundation.

Capital gain on the shares: $28,000. With the donation exemption: **$0 tax** on the gain.
Donation receipt: **$42,000**
Credit: $200 × 15% + $41,800 × 29% = $30 + $12,122 = **$12,152 federal credit alone**
At top Ontario rate, combined: approximately **$19,400**

If Robert was planning to donate $3,000/year for the next 14 years anyway ($42,000 total), this single transaction:
- Eliminates $28,000 in capital gains tax he would have eventually paid (~$6,500)
- Generates an immediate $19,400 combined credit
- Completes 14 years of charitable commitments in one move

**Net tax advantage vs. 14 years of cheques:** $19,400 − (14 × $1,420 average combined annual credit) = $19,400 − $19,880 ≈ similar total credits, **but he also saves $6,500 in capital gains tax that would have been owing when he eventually sold the shares.**

For clients with large appreciated positions and charitable intent: donating shares directly is almost always superior to selling and donating cash. Implement this conversation once — the client benefits from it for life.

### Case D — The Family Credit Optimization

**Client:** The Chen family.

- **Victor** (54): Net income $98,000. Federal tax owing (before credits): $16,400
- **Helen** (52): Net income $22,000. Federal tax owing: $870

**Credits available:**
- Combined charitable donations: $2,400 (Victor $1,600, Helen $800)
- Helen's medical expenses: $5,800
- Victor's mother (76, lives with them, severe arthritis — DTC-eligible): DTC = $9,872 base

**Optimizing each credit:**

**Donations — pool on Victor's return:**
$2,400 on Victor: $200 × 15% + $2,200 × 29% = $30 + $638 = **$668 federal credit**
vs. splitting: Victor $464 + Helen $261 = **$725** — actually splitting is better here because both have tax to apply against
→ Test both and use the higher result. In this case, splitting yields $57 more.

**Medical expenses — claim on Helen's return:**
3% threshold: $22,000 × 3% = $660. Net eligible: $5,800 − $660 = $5,140. Credit: $5,140 × 15% = **$771**
vs. Victor's return: $98,000 × 3% = $2,940. Net eligible: $5,800 − $2,940 = $2,860. Credit: $2,860 × 15% = **$429**
**Helen's return wins by $342** — lower threshold enables more of the expense to qualify.

**DTC for Victor's mother — transfer to Victor:**
Mother earns $19,000 (OAS/CPP). She owes minimal tax. DTC transferred to Victor.
Victor's DTC: $9,872 × 15% = **$1,481 federal** + Ontario equivalent (~$830) = **$2,311 combined**

**Before this credit optimization, Victor's federal tax was $16,400.**
After donations ($668) + DTC transfer ($1,481) = **$14,251 net federal tax**
Helen's federal tax: $870 − medical ($771) − shared donation credit = approximately $0

**Total family tax reduction from credit optimization: $2,919 federal alone** — from three decisions that took 20 minutes to model.

## 13.12 Week 13 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - Non-refundable credits reduce **federal tax directly at 15%** — the credit amount × 15% is the tax reduction, not the amount itself. Credits are relatively more valuable to lower-income earners than to higher-income ones (opposite of deductions)
    - **Basic Personal Amount (Line 30000):** $16,129 → $2,419 federal credit. Everyone gets it
    - **Age Amount (Line 30100):** Begins reducing at net income of $44,325 — creates a 15% effective surtax for seniors in the $44,325–$103,925 range. Plan around this threshold
    - **Disability Tax Credit (DTC):** $9,872 base + $5,758 supplement for children under 18. Must be pre-approved via T2201. Can be transferred to a supporting person. **10-year retroactive carry-back** when newly approved — this is where large refunds are recovered
    - **Medical expenses (Line 33099):** Claim on the **lower-income spouse's** return (smaller 3% threshold). Use the **12-month window strategy** — test every possible window to maximize the credit. Pool dependants' expenses across the family
    - **Charitable donations (Schedule 9):** First $200 at 15%; above $200 at **29%** (33% for top-bracket donors). Stack donations every 2–5 years to minimize time in the 15% zone. Always pool on one return. **Donating appreciated securities directly to charity eliminates the capital gain entirely** — one of the most powerful strategies in Canadian tax
    - **Tuition credits (Schedule 11):** Can be carried forward indefinitely or transferred (current year only) to a spouse or parent — up to $5,000 base. Model before automatically transferring — students who will earn income soon may be better off keeping the carryforward
    - **Credit transfers** between family members are limited and specific — know what can transfer (DTC, Age Amount, tuition) and what cannot (medical expenses, BPA)

## ✅ Week 13 Quiz

When you're ready, ask Claude: **"Quiz me on Week 13"** for an interactive test.

??? question "1. Your client has a net income of $58,000 and is 67 years old. What is their 2025 Age Amount and the resulting federal credit?"
    Reduction: ($58,000 − $44,325) × 15% = $13,675 × 15% = $2,051. Age Amount: $8,790 − $2,051 = **$6,739**. Federal credit: $6,739 × 15% = **$1,011**. Note: this client is in the Age Amount phase-out range — every $1 of additional net income costs them $0.15 in Age Amount on top of their regular marginal rate, creating an effective surtax. Any planning decisions that reduce net income (RRSP contributions, pension splitting) are doubly beneficial for this client.

??? question "2. A client's 12-year-old son has been diagnosed with severe ADHD, classified as a marked restriction in mental functions for everyday life. The client has never applied for the DTC. The diagnosis was confirmed 6 years ago. What do you advise and what is the potential recovery?"
    **Apply for the DTC immediately using Form T2201** — have the prescribing psychiatrist or psychologist complete Part B confirming the severity and that it has lasted more than 12 consecutive months. Once CRA approves it, file **T1-ADJ for each of the past 6 eligible years** (going back to when the condition was confirmed). The child has no income, so the DTC transfers to the supporting parent. Annual federal DTC: $9,872 (base) × 15% = $1,481 + child supplement ($5,758 × 15% = $864) = $2,345 federal annually (supplement until child turns 18). Plus Ontario ~$1,100/year. Retroactive recovery for 6 years: approximately **$20,000–$22,000 in combined federal and provincial refunds**, plus CRA interest on the overpayments.

??? question "3. Patricia's medical expenses: $1,800 in October 2024, $3,400 in February 2025, $900 in July 2025. Net income: $65,000. What is the optimal 12-month window and the resulting credit?"
    Test key windows: **Oct 2024–Sep 2025:** $1,800 + $3,400 + $900 = $6,100. Threshold: $65,000 × 3% = $1,950. Net eligible: $6,100 − $1,950 = $4,150. Credit: $4,150 × 15% = **$623**. **Feb 2025–Jan 2026:** $3,400 + $900 = $4,300. Net eligible: $4,300 − $1,950 = $2,350. Credit: $2,350 × 15% = $353. **Calendar 2025:** $3,400 + $900 = $4,300. Same as above = $353. **Best window: October 2024–September 2025 → $623 federal credit**, which is $270 more than the calendar year approach — from including the October 2024 expense in a window that ends in the 2025 tax year.

??? question "4. Victor and Helen donate a combined $3,200 this year — Victor $2,000, Helen $1,200. Victor is in the 29% bracket; Helen is in the 20.05% bracket. Should they split the donations or combine on one return? Calculate both options."
    **Combined on Victor's return:** $200 × 15% + $3,000 × 29% = $30 + $870 = **$900 federal credit**. **Split:** Victor: $200 × 15% + $1,800 × 29% = $30 + $522 = $552. Helen: $200 × 15% + $1,000 × 29% = $30 + $290 = $320. Total split: $552 + $320 = **$872 federal credit**. **Combined on Victor's return wins by $28.** The advantage of combining is that the high-rate ($29%) zone is applied to a larger total, while only one $200 low-rate zone is consumed instead of two. Always combine donations on one return — the higher-income spouse in most cases.

??? question "5. A student paid $14,500 in tuition in 2025. She earned $8,000 from a part-time job. Her father earns $125,000. What are her options for the tuition credit, and what is the maximum that can transfer to her father?"
    Tuition credit generated: $14,500 × 15% = **$2,175 federal**. Her own federal tax (at $8,000 income, below BPA) = approximately $0. She has no tax to apply it against. **Options:** (1) Carry the full $2,175 forward indefinitely to use after graduation. (2) Transfer up to the maximum of $5,000 **base amount** ($5,000 × 15% = **$750 federal credit**) to her father, keeping the remaining $9,500 base ($9,500 × 15% = $1,425) as a personal carryforward. (3) Transfer to a spouse if she has one. **The strategic question:** Will she have significant income in the next 1–2 years? If yes, she may be better off keeping the full carryforward and using it herself at her own marginal rate. If no, the transfer to her high-income father ($750 × his combined rate of ~46%) saves money now. Model before advising.

??? question "6. Robert owns 500 shares of TD Bank purchased for $16,000. They are now worth $44,000. He plans to donate $5,000 to charity this year. Compare the tax outcomes of writing a cheque vs. donating $5,000 worth of shares directly."
    **Option A — cheque:** No capital impact. Donation receipt: $5,000. Credit: $200 × 15% + $4,800 × 29% = $30 + $1,392 = $1,422 federal. **Option B — donate shares (proportionate):** To donate $5,000 worth of shares, transfer approximately 57 shares (57/500 × $28,000 accrued gain = $3,192 of accrued gain). Capital gain on donated portion: **$0** (exempt). Donation receipt: **$5,000**. Credit: same $1,422 federal. **Difference:** Option B gives the same donation credit but **avoids the capital gains tax** Robert would have eventually paid when selling. On a proportionate $3,192 gain: $3,192 × 50% × 46.41% = **$741 in capital gains tax avoided permanently**. Over multiple years of share donation, these savings compound significantly.

??? question "7. A client's mother (72, lives with him, severe arthritis — DTC approved) has OAS and CPP income of $21,000 and pays $1,400 in federal tax. What is the most tax-efficient allocation of her DTC?"
    Mother's federal tax: $1,400. DTC credit: $9,872 × 15% = $1,481. She can use **$1,400** of the credit to eliminate her own federal tax. The remaining **$81 unused credit ($540 base amount)** can transfer to her son as the supporting individual — providing him a modest $81 × (1/15%) = calculated using the actual transfer mechanism on Schedule 2. The key point: **use the credit on the person's own return first, then transfer only the unused remainder.** Don't transfer the full DTC to the son when the mother has tax to apply it against — that would leave the mother paying tax unnecessarily.
