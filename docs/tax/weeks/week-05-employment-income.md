# Week 5: Employment Income — The T4 Decoded

!!! info "Learning Objectives"
    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    - Read and interpret every important box on a T4 slip
    - Correctly report employment income and all related deductions on the T1
    - Identify and handle taxable benefits, allowances, and reimbursements
    - Handle clients with multiple T4s, mid-year job changes, and commission income
    - Claim employment expenses using Form T777
    - Spot the most common T4-related errors before they become CRA problems

---

## 5.1 Why Employment Income Is Your Most Important Topic

Employment income will appear on **90%+ of all returns you prepare**. Before you can handle rental income, capital gains, or self-employment, you need to be flawless with the T4.

The T4 is issued by employers to:

- Every employee who was paid **over $500** in the calendar year, **OR**
- Any employee from whom **CPP, EI, or income tax was deducted**, regardless of amount

Employers must issue T4s by **the last day of February** of the following year. So for the 2025 tax year, T4s are due to employees by **February 28, 2026**.

!!! tip "CRA has your client's T4 before you do"
    Employers file T4 data with CRA electronically. By the time your client hands you their T4, CRA already has it. If you report a different number than what CRA received, their system will flag it automatically during processing.

## 5.2 The T4 Slip — Box by Box

The T4 looks intimidating at first. It isn't. Most boxes apply to very specific situations and many will be blank on a typical return. Here are the ones you'll actually use.

### The Core Boxes — On Almost Every T4

| Box | Description | Where It Goes on T1 |
|---|---|---|
| **14** | Employment income | Line **10100** |
| **16** | Employee's CPP contributions | Schedule 8 / Line **30800** (credit) |
| **17** | Employee's QPP contributions (Quebec) | Schedule 8 |
| **18** | Employee's EI premiums | Line **31200** (credit) |
| **20** | RPP (Registered Pension Plan) contributions | Line **20700** (deduction) |
| **22** | Income tax deducted | Line **43700** (tax already paid) |
| **26** | CPP/QPP pensionable earnings | Used to verify box 16 |
| **44** | Union dues | Line **21200** (deduction) |
| **46** | Charitable donations | Schedule 9 |
| **52** | Pension adjustment | Line **20600** (reduces RRSP room) |
| **85** | Employee-paid health premiums | Medical expense credit |

### The Benefit Boxes — Taxable Amounts Already in Box 14

!!! warning "These boxes don't add income — they explain what's already in Box 14"
    Boxes 30–84 typically report taxable benefits that are **already included** in Box 14. They exist to help you and CRA identify specific credit claims. Do not double-count them.

| Box | Description | What To Do With It |
|---|---|---|
| **30** | Board and lodging | Already in box 14 — no separate action |
| **32** | Travel in a prescribed zone | May qualify for Northern Residents Deduction |
| **33** | Medical travel assistance | Already in box 14 |
| **34** | Personal use of employer's vehicle | Already in box 14 — standby charge benefit |
| **36** | Interest-free / low-interest loans | Already in box 14 |
| **38** | Security options benefits | Already in box 14; triggers Schedule 3 in some cases |
| **40** | Other taxable allowances and benefits | Already in box 14 |
| **41** | Security options deduction | Line **24900** (deduction — complex, refer if unsure) |
| **52** | Pension adjustment | Reduces next year's RRSP room — see NOA |
| **57–60** | COVID-19 benefit amounts (historical) | For reference / amended returns only |

## 5.3 Employment Income — Line 10100

**Line 10100** is the primary employment income line. What goes here:

- Wages, salary, and hourly pay
- Bonuses and vacation pay
- Tips reported through payroll
- Director's fees
- Taxable benefits (already captured in Box 14)
- Commissions (if on a T4 — self-employed commission goes on T2125)

**What does NOT go on line 10100:**
- Self-employment income → T2125, line 13500
- Tips not reported through payroll → line 10400 (Other employment income)
- EI benefits → T4E, line 11900
- Pension income → T4A, lines 11300–11500

### Line 10400 — Other Employment Income

| Situation | Where It Goes |
|---|---|
| Tips/gratuities not on T4 | Line 10400 |
| Casual income (odd jobs, not self-employment) | Line 10400 |
| Royalties from employment-related work | Line 10400 |
| Wage-loss replacement plan benefits (employer-paid premiums) | Line 10400 |

!!! example "Case — The Server's Tips"
    Rosa works as a server. Her T4 shows $28,000 in employment income (her hourly wages). She also received approximately $11,000 in cash tips from customers that were not reported through payroll.

    **What you do:**
    - Box 14 → Line 10100: $28,000
    - Unreported tips → Line 10400: $11,000
    - **Total employment income: $39,000**

    Many servers don't realize their tips are taxable. This is a common conversation you'll have. Tips received directly from customers are income regardless of whether they pass through payroll.

## 5.4 Taxable Benefits — The Invisible Income

A **taxable benefit** is something of value the employer gives the employee beyond their cash pay. CRA requires these to be included in the employee's income — and they show up in Box 14 (and often a specific detail box).

### Common Taxable Benefits

| Benefit | Taxable? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Personal use of company vehicle | ✅ Yes | Standby charge + operating benefit — Box 34 |
| Group life insurance premiums (employer-paid) | ✅ Yes | Box 40 |
| Gifts > $500 total in a year | ✅ Yes | Box 40 |
| Housing allowance | ✅ Yes | Box 30 or 40 |
| Low-interest or interest-free loan | ✅ Yes | Prescribed rate benefit — Box 36 |
| Parking (in most cases) | ✅ Yes | Box 40 |
| RRSP contributions made by employer | ✅ Yes | Box 40; still counts as RRSP room used |
| Stock options exercised | ✅ Yes | Box 38; complex — refer if significant |
| Group health/dental premiums (employer-paid, most provinces) | ✅ Yes | Box 40 or 85 |

### Common NON-Taxable Benefits

| Benefit | Taxable? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Gifts ≤ $500 total per year | ❌ No | CRA's administrative policy |
| Subsidized meals in workplace cafeteria | ❌ No | If employee pays a reasonable amount |
| On-site gym/recreation (employer's facility) | ❌ No | |
| Employee discounts on employer's goods | ❌ No | If available to all employees |
| Transportation to remote work site | ❌ No | |
| Uniforms and special clothing | ❌ No | Required for the job |
| Cell phone (primarily for work use) | ❌ No | If business use is the primary purpose |

!!! example "Case — The Company Car"
    David works in sales. His employer provides him a car worth $45,000. He uses it 70% for work, 30% personal.

    CRA calculates a **standby charge** (for having access to the vehicle) and an **operating benefit** (for personal kilometres driven). These amounts are calculated by the employer and included in Box 34 of his T4, and are already in Box 14.

    **What you do:** Nothing extra — the benefit is already in Box 14 and captured in Box 34 for reference. You do not add it again. However, if David kept a mileage log showing his personal use was less than 50%, he may be eligible for a reduced standby charge — worth investigating.

## 5.5 CPP and EI — Credits, Not Deductions

CPP and EI contributions withheld from a paycheque (Boxes 16 and 18) are **not deductions** — they are **non-refundable tax credits** claimed on Schedule 1.

| Item | Box | Line | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Employee CPP contributions | Box 16 | Line 30800 | Non-refundable credit at 15% |
| Employee EI premiums | Box 18 | Line 31200 | Non-refundable credit at 15% |
| Employer's share of CPP | Not on T4 | N/A | Not claimed by employee |

### 2025 CPP and EI Maximums

| Item | 2025 Rate | Maximum Annual Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| CPP (employee) | 5.95% on earnings between $3,500–$71,300 | **$3,867** |
| CPP2 (second additional CPP) | 4% on earnings $71,300–$73,200 | **$188** |
| EI (employee) | 1.66% on insurable earnings up to $65,700 | **$1,049** |
| EI (Quebec residents) | 1.31% (lower because Quebec has its own parental plan) | **$834** |

!!! warning "Over-contributions — when a client has multiple T4s"
    If a client worked for two employers and each withheld CPP/EI independently, they may have **over-contributed** beyond the annual maximums. CRA will refund the excess CPP automatically on the return. The excess EI is also refunded. Always add up all Box 16s and Box 18s across multiple T4s and check against the maximums.

!!! example "Case — Multiple T4s, Over-Contributed EI"
    Natalie worked two jobs in 2025:

    | Employer | Box 18 (EI) |
    |---|---|
    | Job A | $780 |
    | Job B | $620 |
    | **Total withheld** | **$1,400** |
    | 2025 EI maximum | $1,049 |
    | **Over-contribution** | **$351** |

    The $351 over-contribution is automatically refunded to Natalie on her return. Her tax software will calculate this — but you need to know it exists so you're not confused when you see it.

## 5.6 RPP and Union Dues — Deductions from Box 20 and Box 44

### RPP Contributions — Box 20 → Line 20700

An **RPP (Registered Pension Plan)** is an employer-sponsored pension. Employee contributions to an RPP are **deductible** — they reduce net income.

- Box 20 amount goes directly to **Line 20700**
- Also note Box 52 (pension adjustment) — this reduces the client's RRSP deduction limit for the following year. It's on the NOA, not the T1 itself.

### Union Dues — Box 44 → Line 21200

Union dues paid by the employee are deductible. Box 44 from the T4 goes to **Line 21200**.

!!! tip "Union dues not on T4"
    Sometimes union dues are paid directly by the employee (not through payroll). These can still be deducted on line 21200 if the client has receipts. Always ask: "Were you in a union? Did you pay dues directly?"

## 5.7 Employment Expenses — Form T777

In addition to credits and deductions available to all employees, some employees can deduct **work-related expenses** from their employment income. This is more restrictive than people think — you cannot just deduct everything you spent at work.

### Who Can Claim Employment Expenses?

To claim employment expenses, the employee must have **a signed T2200** (Declaration of Conditions of Employment) from their employer confirming:

- They were required to pay their own expenses as a condition of employment
- They were not reimbursed for those expenses
- They were required to work away from the employer's place of business (for some expense types)

### Common Deductible Employment Expenses (Form T777)

| Expense | Who Can Claim | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Home office expenses | Employees required to work from home | CRA has a detailed/flat-rate method |
| Vehicle expenses | Employees required to use own car for work | Must keep mileage log |
| Supplies | Employees required to supply their own | Reasonable amounts only |
| Meals (travel) | Employees away from home for 12+ hours | 50% of meal cost |
| Accommodation | Employees traveling for work | Full cost if away overnight |
| Cell phone (work portion) | Commission employees or required by T2200 | Must calculate % work use |
| Professional membership dues | If required by employer | |
| Legal fees | To collect employment income owed | |

### Home Office — Two Methods

=== "Temporary Flat Rate (Simplified)"

    Available for employees working from home due to employer direction.

    - **$2/day** for each day worked from home
    - Maximum **$500** per year
    - **No T2200 required**
    - No need to track actual expenses

    Best for: Clients who work from home occasionally and don't have significant home expenses.

=== "Detailed Method"

    Calculates the actual business-use portion of home expenses.

    **Eligible expenses:**
    - Rent (renters only — homeowners cannot claim mortgage principal or interest)
    - Electricity, heat, water
    - Internet (work portion)
    - Maintenance and minor repairs
    - Home internet

    **Formula:**
    ```
    Work space area ÷ Total home area = Business-use %
    Eligible expenses × Business-use % = Deductible amount
    ```

    **T2200 required.** Better for clients who rent and have substantial home costs.

    !!! example "Case — Home Office Detailed Method"
        Sandra rents a 900 sq ft apartment. Her home office is a dedicated room of 135 sq ft.

        Business-use %: 135 ÷ 900 = **15%**

        | Expense | Annual Amount | × 15% | Deductible |
        |---|---|---|---|
        | Rent | $18,000 | 15% | $2,700 |
        | Electricity & heat | $1,800 | 15% | $270 |
        | Internet | $720 | 15% | $108 |
        | **Total deduction** | | | **$3,078** |

        At Sandra's 26% marginal rate, this saves her approximately **$800 in taxes**. Worth claiming.

## 5.8 Commission Employees — Special Rules

Employees paid partly or fully by **commission** have access to additional expense deductions not available to regular salaried employees — but only if their T2200 confirms commission-based employment.

### Additional Expenses Available to Commission Employees

| Expense | Salaried Employee | Commission Employee |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising and promotion | ❌ | ✅ |
| Meals and entertainment | ❌ | ✅ (50%) |
| Office rent (outside home) | ❌ | ✅ |
| Salary paid to an assistant | ❌ | ✅ |

!!! warning "Commission ≠ Self-employed"
    A commission employee receives a T4 and is employed. A self-employed commission agent does not receive a T4 and files on T2125. The distinction matters enormously for how you report income and what expenses are deductible. If there's any ambiguity, ask: "Did you receive a T4 from this company? Were CPP and EI deducted?"

## 5.9 Case Studies

### Case A — The Simple T4

**Client:** Ahmed, 29, single, one employer all year.

**T4 shows:**
- Box 14: $58,000
- Box 16: $3,267 (CPP)
- Box 18: $963 (EI)
- Box 22: $11,400 (tax withheld)
- Box 44: $720 (union dues)

**What you do:**

| Line | Item | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| 10100 | Employment income | $58,000 |
| 20700 | RPP deductions | $0 |
| 21200 | Union dues (box 44) | $720 |
| 30800 | CPP credit (box 16) | $3,267 × 15% = $490 |
| 31200 | EI credit (box 18) | $963 × 15% = $144 |
| 43700 | Tax already withheld (box 22) | $11,400 |

Ahmed's union dues reduce his Net Income by $720 — a small but real saving. Total complexity: low. Time to prepare: 20 minutes.

---

### Case B — Mid-Year Job Change

**Client:** Priya, 35, changed jobs in June.

**T4 from Employer A (Jan–June):**
- Box 14: $41,000
- Box 16: $2,236
- Box 18: $680
- Box 22: $8,100

**T4 from Employer B (July–Dec):**
- Box 14: $48,000
- Box 16: $2,536
- Box 18: $797
- Box 22: $9,800

**Combined:**

| Line | Item | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| 10100 | Employment income | $41,000 + $48,000 = **$89,000** |
| CPP total | $2,236 + $2,536 = **$4,772** | Maximum is $3,867 → over-contribution of **$905** |
| EI total | $680 + $797 = **$1,477** | Maximum is $1,049 → over-contribution of **$428** |
| 43700 | Tax withheld total | $8,100 + $9,800 = **$17,900** |

Both employers withheld CPP and EI as if Priya earned the full annual amount with them alone. The over-contributions ($905 CPP + $428 EI = $1,333 total) are refunded to Priya automatically on her return.

!!! success "Client reaction when they see this"
    Priya will be pleasantly surprised. You'll explain: "Because you had two employers this year, both withheld CPP and EI independently. Together they took more than the annual maximum, so CRA is refunding you the excess — $1,333 — as part of your overall refund." This is exactly the kind of thing a good preparer catches. A client self-filing might never notice.

### Case C — Work-From-Home Employee (T2200)

**Client:** Marcus, 42, works full-time from home (employer requires it, signed T2200). He rents a 1,200 sq ft home; his dedicated office is 180 sq ft.

**Annual home expenses:**
- Rent: $24,000
- Hydro/heat: $2,400
- Internet: $840

**Flat rate calculation:** He worked 220 days from home × $2/day = $440

**Detailed method calculation:**

Business-use %: 180 ÷ 1,200 = **15%**

| Expense | Annual | × 15% | Deductible |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rent | $24,000 | 15% | $3,600 |
| Hydro/heat | $2,400 | 15% | $360 |
| Internet | $840 | 15% | $126 |
| **Total** | | | **$4,086** |

**Which method to choose?** Detailed method: $4,086 vs. Flat rate: $440. **Detailed method wins by $3,646.**

At Marcus's marginal rate (~26% federal + 9.15% Ontario = ~35%), the extra $3,646 saves him approximately **$1,276 in tax**. Always run both calculations for home office clients.

---

### Case D — The Undisclosed Benefit

**Client:** Jennifer's T4 Box 14 shows $75,000. She mentions her company pays for her cell phone and gym membership.

**What you ask:**
- "Is your cell phone primarily for work, or personal use too?"
  - If primarily personal → Box 40 benefit should already be in Box 14. Confirm it's there.
- "Is the gym an on-site company facility or an external membership?"
  - On-site company gym → non-taxable, not in Box 14
  - External gym paid by employer → taxable benefit, should be in Box 40/Box 14

**Why this matters:** If the employer failed to include taxable benefits in Box 14, CRA may assess the employer — but they may also reassess Jennifer. You're not responsible for employer errors, but noting discrepancies protects your client.

## 5.10 The T4 Checklist — Use This for Every Employment Income Client

Before you file any return with employment income, verify each item:

- [ ] Box 14 entered on Line 10100
- [ ] Multiple T4s? All Box 14s added together
- [ ] Box 16 (CPP) — total across all T4s checked against $3,867 maximum
- [ ] Box 18 (EI) — total across all T4s checked against $1,049 maximum
- [ ] Box 20 (RPP) — entered on Line 20700 if present
- [ ] Box 22 (tax withheld) — total across all T4s entered on Line 43700
- [ ] Box 44 (union dues) — entered on Line 21200 if present
- [ ] Box 52 (pension adjustment) — noted for RRSP room impact
- [ ] Box 85 (health premiums) — flagged for potential medical expense credit
- [ ] Any cash tips or unreported gratuities? → Line 10400
- [ ] T2200 on file? → Proceed to Form T777
- [ ] Commission employee? → Additional expenses available

## 5.11 Week 5 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - Employment income goes on **Line 10100** (T4 Box 14); other employment income on **Line 10400**
    - Most T4 boxes beyond Box 14 describe items **already inside** Box 14 — do not double-count
    - CPP (Box 16 → Line 30800) and EI (Box 18 → Line 31200) are **non-refundable credits at 15%**, not deductions
    - Clients with multiple T4s may **over-contribute** to CPP/EI — the excess is automatically refunded
    - Union dues (Box 44 → Line 21200) and RPP contributions (Box 20 → Line 20700) are **deductions** that reduce Net Income
    - Employment expenses require a **T2200** signed by the employer
    - Home office: always calculate both flat-rate and detailed methods and use the larger one
    - Commission employees have access to additional expense deductions — verify via T2200
    - Tips are taxable income whether or not they appear on a T4

## ✅ Week 5 Quiz

When you're ready, ask Claude: **"Quiz me on Week 5"** for an interactive test.

??? question "1. Your client has two T4s. Box 16 on T4-A is $2,900 and Box 16 on T4-B is $1,800. What do you do?"
    Total CPP withheld: $2,900 + $1,800 = $4,700. The 2025 maximum is $3,867. The over-contribution of **$833** is automatically refunded to the client on their T1 return. Enter the total $4,700 for the CPP credit calculation — tax software handles the refund automatically, but you need to know it's happening and why.

??? question "2. A client's T4 Box 14 shows $62,000 and Box 34 shows $4,200 (personal vehicle benefit). What do you report on Line 10100?"
    **$62,000** — the Box 34 amount is already **included** in Box 14. Box 34 is a breakdown, not additional income. Reporting $66,200 would double-count the vehicle benefit and overstate the client's income.

??? question "3. What is the difference between a deduction and a credit, specifically in the context of CPP contributions?"
    CPP contributions are a **non-refundable tax credit**, not a deduction. This means they reduce tax owing (at 15%), not income. By contrast, union dues and RPP contributions are deductions — they reduce net income directly before tax is calculated. The distinction: deductions are more valuable at higher marginal rates; credits are fixed at 15% regardless of income level.

??? question "4. An employee worked from home 200 days in 2025 with a signed T2200. Their home office is 200 sq ft out of a 1,000 sq ft apartment. Annual rent is $20,400, utilities are $1,800. Which method gives a better deduction?"
    **Flat rate:** 200 days × $2 = **$400**. **Detailed method:** (200 ÷ 1,000) = 20% × ($20,400 + $1,800) = 20% × $22,200 = **$4,440**. The detailed method is $4,040 better. Always run both calculations for home office clients.

??? question "5. A client is a real estate agent who receives a T4 from their brokerage. They paid $3,200 in advertising, $1,100 in meals with clients, and $800 in office supplies this year. Can they claim these?"
    Yes — **if** their T4 shows commission income and their employer has signed a **T2200** confirming they were required to pay these expenses. Real estate agents on commission T4s can deduct advertising (100%), meals with clients (50% = $550), and supplies ($800). Total deduction: $3,200 + $550 + $800 = **$4,550** on Form T777.

??? question "6. Your client is a server who received $32,000 in wages (T4 Box 14) and approximately $14,000 in cash tips not reported through payroll. What do you report and where?"
    Box 14 ($32,000) → **Line 10100**. Cash tips ($14,000) → **Line 10400** (Other employment income). Total employment income: $46,000. Cash tips are taxable income under the Income Tax Act — amount received directly from customers, not through payroll, still counts as income. This is one of the most common unreported income situations.

??? question "7. What document must an employee obtain from their employer before claiming any employment expenses on Form T777?"
    A signed **T2200 — Declaration of Conditions of Employment**. Without it, CRA will deny the employment expense claim on audit. The T2200 confirms: the employee was required to pay their own expenses, was not reimbursed, and (for some expenses) was required to work away from the employer's location. Keep this on file — do not attach it to the return, but have it available if CRA requests it.
