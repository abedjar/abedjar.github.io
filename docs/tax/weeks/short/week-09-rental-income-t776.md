## 9.1 Rental Income vs. Business Income — Why It Matters

Most landlords report **rental income** on a T776. But some rental activities cross the line into **business income** (T2125). The distinction affects which expenses are deductible and how income is taxed.

| Factor | Rental Income (T776) | Business Income (T2125) |
|---|---|---|
| Level of services | Passive — providing space only | Active — significant services to tenants |
| Examples | Long-term residential rental, NNN commercial lease | Bed and breakfast, furnished short-term rentals with cleaning/meals, rooming house |
| CCA | Cannot exceed rental income (creates a loss restriction) | Can create a business loss |
| GST/HST | Long-term residential: **exempt** | Short-term (< 30 days) and B&B: **taxable supply** |

!!! warning "Airbnb and short-term rentals"
    Short-term rentals (nightly, weekly) where the host provides services (cleaning, linens, check-in) may be classified as **business income**, not rental income. Additionally, short-term rentals are a **taxable supply** for GST/HST — hosts earning over $30,000 must register. This is increasingly common and increasingly audited. If your client is an Airbnb host, ask detailed questions about what services they provide.

## 9.2 The T776 — Statement of Real Estate Rentals

One T776 per rental property (or group of similar properties). The T776 calculates net rental income, which flows to the T1.

### Where Net Rental Income Goes on the T1

| Situation | Line on T1 |
|---|---|
| Net rental income (profit) | Line **12600** |
| Net rental loss (if allowed — see CCA rules) | Line **12600** (negative number) |

### The T776 Structure

```
Gross Rental Income (all rents received)
  − Rental Expenses (current)
══════════════════════════════════
Net Income Before CCA
  − CCA (Capital Cost Allowance)
══════════════════════════════════
Net Rental Income (or Loss) → Line 12600
```

## 9.3 What Counts as Rental Income?

Everything received from tenants is rental income — not just monthly rent.

| Item | Rental Income? |
|---|---|
| Monthly rent | ✅ Yes |
| Last month's deposit (when applied) | ✅ Yes — in the year it's applied to rent |
| Last month's deposit (when received) | ⚠️ CRA's position: taxable when received, even if not yet "earned" |
| Lease cancellation payment | ✅ Yes |
| Damage deposit (forfeited by tenant) | ✅ Yes — in the year forfeited |
| Parking income | ✅ Yes |
| Laundry machine income | ✅ Yes |
| Subsidy from government (e.g. CMHC) | ✅ Yes |

!!! tip "Last month's deposit — practical approach"
    CRA technically requires the last month's deposit to be reported in the year received. In practice, most preparers report it in the year it's applied (when the tenant leaves), unless CRA specifically challenges it. Be consistent year to year and document your approach.

## 9.4 Deductible Rental Expenses — Current Expenses

Current rental expenses are **fully deductible** in the year incurred.

### The Complete List

=== "Property Operating Costs"

    | Expense | Deductible? | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | Mortgage interest | ✅ Yes | **Interest only** — never the principal repayment |
    | Property taxes | ✅ Yes | Full amount |
    | Home/property insurance | ✅ Yes | Full amount |
    | Utilities paid by landlord | ✅ Yes | Hydro, gas, water — if not charged to tenant |
    | Condo fees / maintenance fees | ✅ Yes | Full amount |
    | Snow removal and landscaping | ✅ Yes | |
    | Repairs and maintenance | ✅ Yes | See distinction from capital below |
    | Property management fees | ✅ Yes | If using a property management company |

=== "Administrative & Professional"

    | Expense | Deductible? | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | Accounting and legal fees | ✅ Yes | Tax prep, lease drafting, eviction legal costs |
    | Advertising | ✅ Yes | Rental listing fees, signage |
    | Office expenses | ✅ Yes | Related to managing the rental |
    | Travel to property | ✅ Yes | To collect rent, supervise repairs — keep records |
    | Interest on money borrowed to earn rental income | ✅ Yes | Loan to purchase or improve rental property |

=== "What Is NOT Deductible"

    | Expense | Deductible? | Reason |
    |---|---|---|
    | Mortgage principal repayments | ❌ No | Capital — building equity, not an expense |
    | Personal portion of expenses (mixed-use) | ❌ No | Must allocate only the rental portion |
    | Land value CCA | ❌ No | Land never depreciates — only buildings |
    | Your own labour | ❌ No | Cannot pay yourself for repairs |
    | Personal meals during property visits | ❌ No | Personal expense |

## 9.5 Repairs vs. Capital Improvements — The Critical Line

This is one of the most common and most important distinctions in rental taxation.

| | Current Expense (Deductible Now) | Capital Improvement (CCA) |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Restores property to original condition | Improves beyond original condition or extends useful life |
| **Tax treatment** | 100% deductible this year | Depreciated via CCA over years |
| **Examples** | Repainting walls, fixing a broken furnace, replacing a broken window, patching drywall | New roof, new HVAC system, kitchen renovation, adding a bathroom, basement finishing |

!!! example "Case — Repair or Capital?"
    Margaret's rental property needed work this year:

    | Item | Cost | Classification | Treatment |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Repainted all rooms | $2,400 | Current repair | **Fully deductible** |
    | Replaced furnace (same capacity) | $4,800 | Current repair | **Fully deductible** |
    | Added new deck | $12,000 | Capital improvement | **CCA — Class 6 (10%)** |
    | Replaced old windows with high-efficiency | $8,500 | Capital improvement (upgrade) | **CCA — Class 1 (4%)** |
    | Fixed broken window (same spec) | $380 | Current repair | **Fully deductible** |

    The total deductible in the current year: $2,400 + $4,800 + $380 = **$7,580**
    The $20,500 in capital improvements goes into CCA classes.

## 9.6 Capital Cost Allowance on Rental Properties

### CCA Classes for Real Estate

| Class | Rate | Asset |
|---|---|---|
| **Class 1** | **4%** | Most residential and commercial buildings acquired after 1987 |
| **Class 3** | 5% | Buildings acquired before 1988 |
| **Class 6** | 10% | Frame/log/stucco buildings; fences; greenhouses |
| **Class 8** | 20% | Appliances (stove, fridge, washer/dryer) in rental |
| **Class 10** | 30% | Vehicle used for rental management |
| **Class 12** | 100% | Small tools, carpets, linens |

!!! warning "Land is NEVER depreciated"
    When you acquire a rental property, you must split the purchase price between **land** and **building**. CCA is only calculated on the **building** value. Land has no CCA class — it does not depreciate. Use the property tax assessment or an appraisal to determine the land/building split.

    **Common split:** In most Canadian cities, use the municipality's assessment ratio. If the assessed value is 30% land and 70% building, apply that ratio to the purchase price.

!!! example "Case — CCA on a Rental Property"
    Kevin buys a rental property for $480,000. Property tax assessment: 25% land ($120,000), 75% building ($360,000).

    Building value for CCA: **$360,000** — Class 1 at 4%

    | Year | Opening UCC | CCA (4%) × 50% first year | Closing UCC |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | 2025 (year of purchase) | $360,000 | $360,000 × 4% × 50% = **$7,200** | $352,800 |
    | 2026 | $352,800 | $352,800 × 4% = **$14,112** | $338,688 |
    | 2027 | $338,688 | $338,688 × 4% = **$13,548** | $325,140 |

### The Rental CCA Loss Restriction — Very Important

!!! danger "CCA on rental property cannot create or increase a net rental loss"
    Unlike self-employment (where CCA can create a loss), **rental property CCA is restricted** to the amount of net rental income **before** CCA. You can use CCA to reduce rental income to zero — but not below.

    **Example:**
    - Net rental income before CCA: $8,400
    - Maximum CCA available: $14,112
    - **CCA claimed: $8,400** (only enough to bring income to $0)
    - Unused CCA ($5,712) carries forward — the UCC simply isn't reduced by the unclaimed amount

    Rental losses from **operating expenses** (mortgage interest, taxes, repairs) are allowed — only CCA is restricted.

## 9.7 When You Sell a Rental Property — Recapture and Terminal Loss

When a rental property is sold, CCA has two important tax consequences beyond the capital gain.

### Recapture

If the **proceeds allocated to the building** exceed the **Undepreciated Capital Cost (UCC)**, the difference is **recaptured** — added back to income as regular income (not a capital gain).

```
Proceeds (building portion) > UCC → RECAPTURE → Added to Line 13600 (regular income)
```

!!! example "Case — Recapture on Sale"
    Sandra bought a rental building for $300,000 and claimed $45,000 in CCA over the years. UCC = $255,000.

    She sells the property. The proceeds allocated to the building are $380,000.

    | Step | Amount |
    |---|---|
    | Building proceeds | $380,000 |
    | UCC | $255,000 |
    | **Recapture** | **$125,000** → added to income (Line 13600) |
    | Original cost | $300,000 |
    | Capital gain (proceeds − original cost) | $380,000 − $300,000 = **$80,000** |
    | Taxable capital gain (×50%) | **$40,000** → Schedule 3 |

    Sandra has **two** taxable items: $125,000 in recapture (taxed at full marginal rate) AND $40,000 in taxable capital gain (taxed at 50% inclusion). Recapture is taxed more harshly because it represents CCA you claimed that turned out not to be a real loss in value.

### Terminal Loss

If building proceeds are **less than the UCC**, the difference is a **terminal loss** — fully deductible against any income (not just rental income).

```
Proceeds (building portion) < UCC → TERMINAL LOSS → Deductible against all income
```

!!! warning "Terminal loss cannot be claimed if you still own other property in the same CCA class"
    If Kevin has two rental buildings in Class 1 and sells one at a terminal loss, the loss is not recognized until he sells ALL properties in that class. This is the "last property in the class" rule.

## 9.8 Partial-Year Rentals and Mixed-Use Properties

### Partial-Year Rental

If a property is rented for only part of the year (seasonal rental, recently purchased, recently converted), expenses must be **pro-rated** for the rental period.

!!! example "Case — Seasonal Cottage Rental"
    Patricia rents her cottage for 3 months (July, August, September) and uses it personally the other 9 months.

    Rental period: 3/12 = **25%**

    | Annual Expense | Total | × 25% | Deductible |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Property taxes | $4,800 | 25% | $1,200 |
    | Insurance | $1,400 | 25% | $350 |
    | Utilities | $3,600 | 25% | $900 |
    | Repairs (during rental period) | $800 | 100% | $800 |
    | **Total rental expenses** | | | **$3,250** |

    Repairs done **specifically** during the rental period may be 100% deductible — not pro-rated.

### The Basement Suite — Most Common Mixed-Use Scenario

This is extremely common in Canadian cities. A homeowner rents out their basement while living upstairs. Both rental income **and** the principal residence exemption are involved.

**Key rules:**

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| Report all rental income | 100% of rent received on T776 |
| Deduct shared expenses by area | e.g. basement is 30% of home — claim 30% of mortgage interest, taxes, insurance, utilities |
| Direct rental expenses | 100% deductible (repairs to basement only) |
| CCA — avoid on principal residence | Claiming CCA on a portion of your home may affect the PRE on that portion when you sell |
| Capital gain on sale | The rented portion may not qualify for the full PRE |

!!! example "Case — The Basement Landlord"
    David owns a home valued at $700,000 (purchased for $450,000). He rents out his basement, which represents 30% of the home.

    **Annual rental income:** $18,000
    **Shared expenses (×30%):**

    | Expense | Total | × 30% | Rental Deduction |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Mortgage interest | $22,000 | 30% | $6,600 |
    | Property taxes | $5,400 | 30% | $1,620 |
    | Insurance | $1,800 | 30% | $540 |
    | Utilities | $3,600 | 30% | $1,080 |
    | **Direct repairs (basement only)** | $1,200 | 100% | $1,200 |
    | **Total expenses** | | | **$11,040** |

    **Net rental income:** $18,000 − $11,040 = **$6,960** → Line 12600

    **Important:** David should NOT claim CCA on the 30% rental portion of his home. If he does, the 30% rented portion loses the Principal Residence Exemption — meaning when he sells for $700,000, 30% of the capital gain becomes taxable. The tax saved from CCA is almost never worth the PRE exposure on a rising market.

## 9.9 Co-Ownership — Joint Rental Properties

Many rental properties are co-owned by two or more people (spouses, partners, investors). Each owner files their **own T776** for their share of income and expenses.

| Ownership Type | How Income is Split | Who Files |
|---|---|---|
| **Joint tenancy (spouses)** | Typically 50/50 | Each spouse files their own T776 for 50% |
| **Tenants in common** | Per ownership percentage | Each owner files their own T776 for their % |
| **Partnership** | Per partnership agreement | T776 (simple) or T5013 (formal partnership) |

!!! warning "Spousal income attribution"
    If a high-income spouse transfers money to a low-income spouse to buy a rental property, the **attribution rules** may apply — attributing the rental income back to the high-income spouse. Proper loan documentation and charging the prescribed interest rate can avoid attribution. This is a planning area worth flagging for spouses with significantly different incomes.

## 9.10 Case Studies

### Case A — The Urban Condo Landlord

**Client:** Jennifer, 41, rents out a condo she no longer lives in. She bought it in 2019 for $420,000 (land $126,000, building $294,000). She's been renting it since 2021.

**2025 income and expenses:**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Gross rent received | $24,000 |
| Mortgage interest | $14,800 |
| Condo fees | $3,600 |
| Property taxes | $3,200 |
| Insurance | $900 |
| Repairs (repainted, replaced faucet) | $1,100 |
| Property management fee (8%) | $1,920 |
| Accounting fee | $300 |
| **Total expenses** | **$25,820** |
| **Net income before CCA** | **($1,820) — a loss** |

Because there is already a net loss before CCA, Jennifer claims **$0 CCA** this year (she cannot worsen the loss with CCA). The available UCC for future years is preserved.

The $1,820 rental loss **is** deductible against her other income — because it's from operating expenses, not CCA. If she earns $90,000 from her job:

```
Employment income:   $90,000
Rental loss:         ($1,820)
════════════════════
Net Income:          $88,180
```

The loss saves her approximately **$637** in federal tax (at ~35% combined rate).

### Case B — The Retiring Landlord Selling the Property

**Client:** Robert, 65, has owned a duplex (rental property, never his residence) since 2005. He bought it for $280,000 (land $84,000, building $196,000). Over 20 years he claimed $87,000 in CCA. Current UCC on building: $109,000. He sells in 2025 for $740,000.

**Step 1 — Allocate proceeds:**
- Land: $84,000/$280,000 = 30% → $740,000 × 30% = **$222,000**
- Building: 70% → $740,000 × 70% = **$518,000**

**Step 2 — Calculate capital gain:**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Total proceeds | $740,000 |
| ACB (original cost) | $280,000 |
| Selling costs (agent, legal) | $24,000 |
| **Capital gain** | **$436,000** |
| Taxable capital gain (×50%) | **$218,000** → Schedule 3 |

**Step 3 — Calculate recapture:**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Building proceeds | $518,000 |
| UCC (building) | $109,000 |
| **Recapture** | **$409,000** → Line 13600 (regular income) |

Robert has an enormous tax bill:
- **$409,000 recapture** taxed at full marginal rate
- **$218,000 taxable capital gain** taxed at marginal rate on inclusion

This is why selling a rental property that's been heavily depreciated is a major tax event. Clients approaching retirement with rental properties need **advance planning**, not a surprise at tax time.

!!! success "Key lesson for your practice"
    Clients who've owned rental properties for 10–20 years and are thinking of selling need a **pre-sale tax consultation** — not just tax return preparation. If you can flag this conversation 1–2 years before they sell, you save them from being blindsided and demonstrate enormous value. This is where relationships are built.

### Case C — The New Airbnb Host

**Client:** Sarah, 34, started renting her spare bedroom on Airbnb in 2025. She earned $9,800 for the year. The bedroom is 15% of her home. Her home expenses:

| Expense | Total | × 15% | Deductible |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rent (she rents her apartment) | $21,600 | 15% | $3,240 |
| Utilities | $1,800 | 15% | $270 |
| Internet | $720 | 15% | $108 |
| Supplies (soap, linens, etc.) | $640 | 100% | $640 |
| Airbnb platform fees (3%) | $294 | 100% | $294 |
| **Total expenses** | | | **$4,552** |

**Classification question:** Is this rental income or business income?

Sarah provides cleaning between guests and supplies linens and toiletries. This level of service likely makes it **business income (T2125)** rather than rental income (T776).

**GST/HST:** $9,800 — below the $30,000 threshold. No registration required yet, but she should watch her revenue if she expands.

**Net business income:** $9,800 − $4,552 = **$5,248** → Line 13500

If classified as business income, Sarah also owes CPP on this net income ($5,248 − $3,500 = $1,748 × 11.9% = $208 in CPP).

## 9.11 Rental Income Red Flags and Audit Triggers

| Trigger | Why CRA Notices |
|---|---|
| Consistent rental losses (5+ years) | Suggests property is not truly a rental or expenses are inflated |
| Claiming 100% of home expenses on partial rental | Should be pro-rated by area or use |
| Large repairs in years 1–3 of ownership | CRA may re-characterize as capital improvements |
| Failing to report last month's deposit | Common omission — CRA cross-references conveyancing records |
| CCA claimed on principal residence | Triggers PRE questions on eventual sale |
| Missing rental income (cash rentals) | CRA compares property ownership records to tax returns |
| Mortgage interest exceeds rental income | Not an automatic flag, but expect scrutiny |

## 9.12 Week 9 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - Rental income is reported on **T776** → net income flows to **Line 12600**
    - Rental income vs. business income depends on level of services — Airbnb with services = likely business income
    - **Mortgage interest is deductible — mortgage principal is not**
    - Repairs restore → **current expense** (fully deductible). Improvements upgrade → **capital expense** (CCA)
    - Land is **never depreciated** — split purchase price between land and building for CCA purposes
    - Rental CCA **cannot create or increase a rental loss** — it can only bring income to $0
    - When selling a rental property: **recapture** (proceeds > UCC = regular income) and **capital gain** (proceeds > ACB) are separate taxable items
    - Basement suite landlords should avoid CCA to protect the **Principal Residence Exemption**
    - **Co-owners** each file their own T776 for their share of income and expenses
    - Selling a rental property with 10–20 years of CCA is a major tax event requiring advance planning

## ✅ Week 9 Quiz

When you're ready, ask Claude: **"Quiz me on Week 9"** for an interactive test.

??? question "1. Your client replaced the entire roof on their rental property for $18,000. Is this a repair or a capital improvement? How is it treated?"
    A full **roof replacement** is a **capital improvement** — it extends the useful life of the building significantly beyond restoring it to its original condition. It goes into **Class 1 (4% CCA)** and is depreciated over many years, not deducted in full this year. If it were patching a small section of an existing roof, that would be a current repair. The distinction is whether the work restores (repair) or improves/replaces a major component (capital).

??? question "2. A client's rental income is $14,000. Expenses before CCA are $11,500. Available CCA is $9,800. How much CCA can they claim?"
    Net income before CCA: $14,000 − $11,500 = $2,500. The CCA is restricted — it can only reduce rental income to zero, not below. So the client can claim a maximum of **$2,500 in CCA** this year. The remaining $7,300 in available CCA is not lost — the UCC simply isn't reduced by the unclaimed portion, and it rolls forward to future years.

??? question "3. What is recapture and when does it occur on a rental property sale?"
    **Recapture** occurs when the proceeds allocated to the building exceed the Undepreciated Capital Cost (UCC). It represents CCA you previously claimed that turned out not to be a real loss in value — the property appreciated instead. Recapture is added to **regular income (Line 13600)** and taxed at the full marginal rate — not at the 50% capital gains inclusion rate. It is calculated separately from, and in addition to, any capital gain on the same sale.

??? question "4. Your client owns a home and rents out the basement (25% of the home). They want to claim CCA on the rental portion. What do you tell them?"
    Advise them to **avoid claiming CCA on the rental portion**. While it would provide a small annual deduction, claiming CCA on a portion of a principal residence causes that portion to lose its **Principal Residence Exemption** protection. In a rising real estate market, the future capital gain tax on that 25% portion will almost certainly far exceed the total CCA savings. The math rarely works in the client's favour, and most experienced preparers recommend never claiming CCA on a home that is also a principal residence.

??? question "5. A client bought a rental property for $550,000 and the municipality's assessment is 35% land, 65% building. What is the maximum building value for CCA purposes?"
    Building value: $550,000 × 65% = **$357,500**. This is the amount that goes into Class 1 for CCA. The land value of $192,500 is never depreciated. In the first year, CCA = $357,500 × 4% × 50% (half-year rule) = **$7,150**.

??? question "6. Maria and her husband co-own a rental property — Maria owns 60% and her husband owns 40%. The property earned $22,000 in net rental income this year. How is it reported?"
    Each spouse files their **own T776** for their proportionate share: Maria reports $22,000 × 60% = **$13,200** on Line 12600. Her husband reports $22,000 × 40% = **$8,800** on Line 12600. Each also claims their proportionate share of all expenses and CCA on their respective T776s.

??? question "7. A client rents their cottage for July and August (2 months) and uses it personally the remaining 10 months. The annual insurance is $1,800 and they replaced the dock during August for $3,400 (a capital improvement). How much can they deduct for insurance and what class does the dock go into?"
    **Insurance:** $1,800 × 2/12 = **$300** (pro-rated for rental months only). **Dock replacement:** Capital improvement — goes into **Class 6 (10% CCA)**, and because it's a capital expenditure associated with a rental property, the half-year rule applies. CCA in the year of purchase: $3,400 × 10% × 50% = $170. Note that if the dock is used only during rental months, there's an argument for further pro-ration, but Class 6 assets used on a seasonal rental are typically depreciated at the standard rate with the rental period pro-ration applied to the net income calculation.
