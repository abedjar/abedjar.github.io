## 4.3 Comprehensive Practice Scenario

Work through this scenario completely before reading the solution. Use a piece of paper or a spreadsheet. This is exactly what you'll do every day during tax season.

---

### Client Profile: Robert & Linda Chen

**Robert**, 58, Ontario resident, full year.

- Employment income from T4: **$92,000** (line 10100)
- Box 22 of T4 (income tax deducted): **$21,000**
- Box 16 of T4 (CPP deducted): **$3,867**
- Box 18 of T4 (EI deducted): **$1,049**
- Interest income from T5: **$850** (line 12100)
- RRSP contribution made in February: **$14,000** (line 20800)
- Charitable donation receipts: **$600**

**Linda**, 56, Ontario resident, full year.

- No income of her own this year
- Robert supports her fully

**Question 1:** Calculate Robert's Total Income.

**Question 2:** Calculate Robert's Net Income.

**Question 3:** Calculate Robert's Taxable Income.

**Question 4:** Calculate Robert's Gross Federal Tax.

**Question 5:** Calculate Robert's non-refundable credits and Net Federal Tax.

**Question 6:** Will Robert get a refund or owe a balance? Estimate the amount (use simplified Ontario tax of ~$5,800 on this income level).

**Question 7:** What is the tax benefit of Robert's RRSP contribution? How much tax did it save him?

---

??? success "Full Solution — Robert & Linda Chen"

    ### Question 1 — Total Income

    | Line | Item | Amount |
    |---|---|---|
    | 10100 | Employment income | $92,000 |
    | 12100 | Interest income | $850 |
    | **15000** | **Total Income** | **$92,850** |

    ---

    ### Question 2 — Net Income

    | Item | Amount |
    |---|---|
    | Total Income | $92,850 |
    | − RRSP deduction (line 20800) | ($14,000) |
    | **Net Income (line 23600)** | **$78,850** |

    ---

    ### Question 3 — Taxable Income

    Robert has no additional deductions beyond RRSP.

    | Item | Amount |
    |---|---|
    | Net Income | $78,850 |
    | **Taxable Income (line 26000)** | **$78,850** |

    ---

    ### Question 4 — Gross Federal Tax

    Apply 2025 brackets to $78,850:

    | Bracket | Income in Bracket | Rate | Tax |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | $0 – $57,375 | $57,375 | 15% | $8,606 |
    | $57,376 – $78,850 | $21,475 | 20.5% | $4,402 |
    | **Gross Federal Tax** | | | **$13,008** |

    ---

    ### Question 5 — Non-Refundable Credits & Net Federal Tax

    | Credit | Amount | × 15% | Tax Reduction |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Basic Personal Amount | $16,129 | 15% | $2,419 |
    | Spouse/CLP Amount (Linda) | $16,129 | 15% | $2,419 |
    | CPP contributions (box 16) | $3,867 | 15% | $580 |
    | EI premiums (box 18) | $1,049 | 15% | $157 |
    | Canada Employment Amount | $1,433 | 15% | $215 |
    | Charitable donation (first $200) | $200 | 15% | $30 |
    | Charitable donation (remaining $400) | $400 | 29% | $116 |
    | **Total Credits** | | | **$5,936** |

    !!! note "Charitable donation credit rates"
        The first $200 of donations is credited at **15%**. Everything above $200 is credited at **29%** (the higher federal rate). This makes larger donations more tax-efficient. Robert's $600 donation: ($200 × 15%) + ($400 × 29%) = $30 + $116 = $146 total credit.

    ```
    Gross Federal Tax:          $13,008
    − Non-refundable credits:   ($5,936)
                               ─────────
    Net Federal Tax (42000):    $7,072
    ```

    ---

    ### Question 6 — Refund or Balance Owing?

    ```
    Net Federal Tax:            $7,072
    + Ontario Provincial Tax:   $5,800  (simplified estimate)
                               ─────────
    Total Payable (43500):     $12,872

    − Tax withheld (T4 box 22): ($21,000)
                               ─────────
    REFUND (48400):             $8,128  🎉
    ```

    Robert gets approximately **$8,128 refund**.

    ---

    ### Question 7 — RRSP Tax Savings

    Without the $14,000 RRSP contribution, Robert's taxable income would have been $92,850.

    | | Without RRSP | With RRSP |
    |---|---|---|
    | Taxable Income | $92,850 | $78,850 |
    | Gross Federal Tax | ~$15,876 | ~$13,008 |
    | Federal tax savings | | **~$2,868** |
    | Ontario tax savings (est.) | | **~$840** |
    | **Total tax savings** | | **~$3,708** |

    Robert's $14,000 RRSP contribution saved him approximately **$3,708 in combined federal and provincial tax** — a 26.5% return on the contribution immediately.

## 4.4 Mock Client Intake — Full Conversation

This is what your first meeting with a new client looks like. Read through this carefully — then practice it out loud.

---

**Setting:** You're meeting a new client, James, for the first time. He was referred by a friend.

---

**You:** Thanks for coming in, James. Before we get started, I'll need to collect a few pieces of information and some documents from you. First — do you have a piece of government-issued photo ID with you? A driver's licence or passport works.

**James:** Sure, here's my driver's licence.

**You:** Perfect. *(You record his name, address, date of birth, SIN from his documents.)* And can you confirm your SIN for me?

**James:** It's 527-XXX-XXX.

!!! tip "SIN starts with 5 — that's a permanent resident or citizen. No expiry to worry about."

**You:** Great. Now, a few questions about your year. Were you living in Ontario for all of 2025, or did you move at any point?

**James:** I've been in Mississauga my whole life.

**You:** Good. Are you married, common-law, separated, divorced, or single?

**James:** Married. My wife works too.

**You:** Got it — I'll be doing your return only today, or both of yours?

**James:** Just mine for now.

**You:** No problem. What was your main source of income in 2025?

**James:** I work full-time at a logistics company. I got a T4 from them.

**You:** Great. Did you have any other income at all — part-time work, freelance, Uber, Airbnb, rental income, investment income, any money from selling stocks or property?

**James:** I have a savings account at TD. And I sold some stocks through my brokerage in November.

!!! warning "Two new items just surfaced — a T5 and a potential capital gain. Always probe."

**You:** Okay, important. For the savings account — you should have a T5 slip from TD showing interest earned. Do you have that?

**James:** I don't think they mailed me one... it was only like $180.

**You:** The amount doesn't matter — it still needs to be reported and TD still filed the slip with CRA. Let's access your CRA My Account to confirm all your slips. Do you have login credentials for that?

**James:** I think so, yeah.

**You:** For the stocks — did you sell at a gain or a loss? And do you know your original purchase price?

**James:** I made money on it. I bought for $8,000 and sold for $13,500.

**You:** Perfect. That's a capital gain of $5,500 — we'll include 50% of that as taxable income, so $2,750 on your return. I'll need the brokerage statement confirming those numbers. Do you have a T5008 or account statement?

**James:** I can download the statement from my brokerage app.

**You:** That works. Now — did you contribute to an RRSP this year, or in the first 60 days of 2026?

**James:** Yes, I put in $6,000 in January.

**You:** Great — I'll need the RRSP contribution receipt from your financial institution. Did you pay any childcare expenses? Union dues? Move for work?

**James:** We have two kids in daycare. My wife pays for it though — is that still mine to claim?

!!! info "Childcare must be claimed by the lower-income spouse in most cases. Ask who earns less."

**You:** Good question. Childcare expenses are generally claimed by the **lower-income spouse**. Who earned less in 2025, you or your wife?

**James:** Probably her — she's part-time.

**You:** Then the childcare would go on her return, not yours. Make a note to discuss this with whoever does her taxes. For your return, we'll focus on your T4, the T5, the capital gain, and the RRSP receipt.

**You:** Last thing — did you receive any government benefits this year? EI, CERB, or any CRA payments?

**James:** No, nothing like that.

**You:** Perfect. Here's what I need from you: your T4, your RRSP receipt, your brokerage statement, and we'll pull the T5 from CRA My Account together. Once I have all of that, I can have your return ready within a few days.

---

### What This Intake Found

| Item | Where It Goes on T1 |
|---|---|
| T4 employment income | Line 10100 |
| T5 interest ($180) | Line 12100 |
| Capital gain ($5,500 × 50% = $2,750 taxable) | Line 12700 via Schedule 3 |
| RRSP contribution ($6,000) | Line 20800 deduction |
| Childcare → wife's return | Not on James's return |

!!! success "The intake conversation is where you earn your fee"
    A less experienced preparer might have filed with just the T4 and missed the T5 and capital gain. Those omissions would have triggered a CRA reassessment. Your job during intake is to be a detective — assume there's always one more piece of information the client hasn't mentioned yet.

## 4.5 Common Beginner Mistakes — Learn From These Now

These are the errors that new preparers make most often. Read this list carefully and revisit it at the start of every tax season.

| Mistake | Why It Happens | How to Avoid It |
|---|---|---|
| Filing with missing slips | Client says "that's everything" | Always verify through CRA My Account |
| Wrong province of residence | Client moved during the year | Always ask "where did you live on December 31?" |
| Forgetting spousal credit | Client is married but wife/husband has low income | Always ask marital status and spouse's income |
| RRSP over-contribution | Not checking RRSP room on prior NOA | Pull NOA from CRA My Account before recording RRSP |
| Childcare on wrong spouse's return | Assumed higher-income spouse claims it | Always ask who earns less |
| Mixing up credits and deductions | Both reduce tax but differently | Deductions reduce income; credits reduce tax directly |
| Capital gain reported incorrectly | Using proceeds instead of gain | Gain = Proceeds − Adjusted Cost Base (ACB) |
| Missing Part-year residency pro-ration | Newcomer treated as full-year resident | Always ask "when did you arrive in Canada?" |
| Not filing because "they don't owe anything" | Client or preparer assumes no benefit | Always file — refunds, RRSP room, and benefits are at stake |
| Using last year's brackets/amounts | Amounts change annually | Download the current year guide from CRA every January |

## 4.6 Phase 1 Comprehensive Quiz

This quiz covers all material from Weeks 1 through 3. Try to answer each question without looking at your notes first.

??? question "1. What is the difference between marginal and effective tax rate? Give a one-sentence explanation you could use with a client."
    **Marginal rate** is the rate applied to your last dollar of income — the bracket you're in. **Effective rate** is the average rate you actually pay across all your income. Client-friendly: *"Your marginal rate is 26%, but because lower rates applied to your first $114,000, you're actually paying an average of about 18% overall."*

??? question "2. A client's T1 shows Total Income of $105,000, Net Income of $88,000, and Taxable Income of $88,000. What caused the $17,000 difference between Total and Net Income?"
    Deductions — most likely an RRSP contribution, but could also include union dues, childcare expenses, moving expenses, or other above-the-line deductions. Net Income = Total Income minus these deductions.

??? question "3. Why is Net Income (line 23600) considered the most important line on the T1?"
    Because it's used to calculate government benefit amounts — including the GST/HST credit, Canada Child Benefit, OAS clawback threshold, and provincial benefit programs. Reducing net income doesn't just lower taxes — it can increase the benefits a client receives.

??? question "4. A client's Basic Personal Amount is $16,129. How much does this actually reduce their federal tax, and why?"
    It reduces federal tax by **$2,419** ($16,129 × 15%). Non-refundable credits are converted to tax reductions by multiplying by 15% (the lowest federal rate). The Basic Personal Amount is not a $16,129 reduction in tax — it's a $16,129 reduction in the income subject to tax at the 15% level.

??? question "5. You have a new client who arrived from the Philippines on June 20. Her income before arrival was $25,000 (equivalent CAD) and her Canadian income after arrival was $38,000. What income goes on her T1 and how are her credits affected?"
    Only the **$38,000 Canadian income** is reported on her T1. Pre-residency foreign income is not taxable in Canada for part-year residents. Her non-refundable credits (Basic Personal Amount, etc.) are **pro-rated** by the number of days she was resident in Canada: 194 days ÷ 365 = 53.1% of the standard credit amounts.

??? question "6. A self-employed client owes $8,000 in taxes and files their return on May 20. Calculate their penalty and describe the interest situation."
    **No late-filing penalty** — self-employed taxpayers have until June 15 to file and May 20 is before that deadline. However, **interest is running**. The $8,000 was due April 30, so CRA has been charging daily compound interest since May 1 (~8-9% annually). At ~8.5% annual rate, that's roughly $1.89/day, so approximately $38–40 in interest by May 20. The client should pay immediately to stop the interest clock.

??? question "7. What are the two things that distinguish a refundable credit from a non-refundable credit?"
    A **non-refundable credit** can only reduce tax to $0 — it cannot create a refund. Its value is capped at your actual tax owing. A **refundable credit** is paid out even if you owe no tax — you receive the full amount as a payment from CRA. Examples of refundable credits: GST/HST credit, Canada Child Benefit, Canada Workers Benefit.

??? question "8. A client filed their return 2 months late with a $6,000 balance owing. They filed late 2 years ago as well. Calculate their penalty."
    Repeat offender rate applies (filed late in one of the previous 3 years): **10% + 2% per month**. Penalty = ($6,000 × 10%) + ($6,000 × 2% × 2 months) = $600 + $240 = **$840**, plus daily compound interest on the entire amount from May 1.

??? question "9. You just finished filing a client's return when they call saying they forgot a T3 slip showing $2,200 in trust income. What do you do — and what do you NOT do?"
    You **do** submit a T1 Adjustment Request (T1-ADJ) through CRA My Account's "Change my return" feature. You **do not** re-file the return — CRA only processes one return per SIN per year. The adjustment adds the $2,200 to income, CRA recalculates, and issues a Revised NOA. The client will owe additional tax plus interest from the original due date.

??? question "10. Name four types of people who should file a tax return even if they have no legal obligation to do so."
    Any four of: (1) Anyone with tax withheld at source who wants a refund, (2) Low-income earners who qualify for GST/HST credit, (3) Parents who want to receive Canada Child Benefit, (4) Young adults building RRSP contribution room, (5) Students with tuition credits to carry forward, (6) Low-income workers eligible for Canada Workers Benefit, (7) Ontario residents eligible for Ontario Trillium Benefit.
