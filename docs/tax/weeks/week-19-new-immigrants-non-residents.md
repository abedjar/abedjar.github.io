## 19.1 The Foundational Question — Is This Person a Canadian Resident?

Before you touch a single line of the T1, you must determine one thing: **Was this person a Canadian tax resident during the year, and for how long?**

This question determines everything — what income is taxable, which credits apply, which forms are required, and what benefits they receive.

### The Three Possible Statuses for a New Arrival

```
ARRIVED THIS YEAR
        │
        ▼
Did they INTEND to stay permanently (or long-term)?
        │
   YES ─┼─ NO
        │         │
        ▼         ▼
  RESIDENT    Likely SOJOURNER or
  (from date  NON-RESIDENT — use
  of arrival) the significant ties test
```

CRA uses the **significant ties test** — not a simple day count — to determine residency.

### The Significant Ties Test

**Primary ties (strongest indicators of Canadian residency):**

| Primary Tie | Description |
|---|---|
| **Home in Canada** | A home available for your use — owned, rented, or made available by a family member |
| **Spouse or CLP in Canada** | Your legal or common-law partner lives in Canada |
| **Dependants in Canada** | Your children or other dependants live in Canada |

**One primary tie is usually sufficient to establish residency** from the date of arrival.

**Secondary ties (supporting evidence):**

| Secondary Tie | Description |
|---|---|
| Canadian bank account | Active account in your name |
| Canadian driver's licence | Province-issued licence |
| Canadian provincial health card | OHIP card in Ontario |
| Canadian vehicle registered in your name | |
| Canadian professional or club memberships | |
| Canadian social ties (extended family, community) | |
| Canadian investments | Brokerage accounts, RRSPs |

Secondary ties are considered collectively. Multiple secondary ties with no primary ties may or may not establish residency — it depends on the totality.

!!! example "Case — Who Is a Resident? Four Arrival Scenarios"

    **Scenario 1 — Maria from the Philippines:**
    Maria lands in Toronto on June 15 with her husband and two children. They rent an apartment. She has a job offer starting July 1.
    **Verdict: Canadian resident from June 15.** She has all three primary ties immediately — home available, spouse in Canada, dependants in Canada.

    **Scenario 2 — Ahmed from UAE:**
    Ahmed arrives in January on a work permit. He rents an apartment. His wife remains in Dubai with their children until the family can join him later.
    **Verdict: Likely resident from January.** He has a home in Canada (rented apartment). His wife and children are abroad — no primary tie for family. But the home tie + work permit + bank account + OHIP card together establish residency. CRA would likely find him resident.

    **Scenario 3 — Wei from China (International Student):**
    Wei arrives in September on a study permit. He lives in a university residence. He has no spouse, no children. His family and home remain in China.
    **Verdict: Likely NOT resident.** He has only a temporary dwelling (residence) and no primary ties to Canada. He may be a **sojourner** — present in Canada but not resident — unless he establishes secondary ties. If he rents a private apartment and opens a Canadian bank account, the analysis shifts.

    **Scenario 4 — Kenji from Japan (Deemed Resident):**
    Kenji is a Japanese citizen who spends 7 months per year in Canada at his cottage and 5 months in Japan. He has no permanent Canadian address beyond the cottage, no Canadian spouse or children.
    **Verdict: Deemed resident** under the 183-day rule — he spent more than 182 days in Canada. He is taxed on worldwide income for the year, but tax treaty provisions may reduce his actual Canadian tax.

!!! warning "Do not rely on immigration status to determine tax residency"
    Immigration status (permanent resident, temporary foreign worker, international student, visitor) and **tax residency** are completely different concepts. A permanent resident who maintains a home and family abroad may not be a Canadian tax resident. A visitor who rents an apartment and moves their family here may be a resident from day one. Always apply the ties test — never assume.

## 19.2 The Part-Year Resident Return — Structure and Mechanics

When a client becomes a Canadian resident partway through the year, they file a **part-year T1** for the period they were resident. This looks like a normal T1 in most respects, with two critical differences:

### Difference 1 — Income

| Period | What Is Taxable in Canada? |
|---|---|
| **Before becoming resident** (non-resident period) | Only **Canadian-source income** (e.g., employment income earned in Canada, Canadian rental income) |
| **After becoming resident** (resident period) | **Worldwide income** — every dollar from every country |

!!! example "Case — Maria's Income Split"
    Maria arrives from the Philippines on June 15. Before arriving, she earned the equivalent of $24,000 CAD in the Philippines (January–June 14). After arriving, she earns $38,000 CAD in Canada (July–December).

    | Period | Income | Taxable in Canada? |
    |---|---|---|
    | Jan 1 – June 14 (non-resident) | $24,000 Philippine income | ❌ No |
    | June 15 – Dec 31 (resident) | $38,000 Canadian income | ✅ Yes |

    **Maria's T1 total income: $38,000**

    The Philippine income before she became resident is not reported on her Canadian T1. This surprises many new immigrants who assume ALL their income for the calendar year goes on the return.

### Difference 2 — Pro-rated Personal Credits

This is where many preparers make a significant error.

**Personal non-refundable credits are pro-rated** for part-year residents based on the fraction of the year they were resident. The formula:

```
Days resident in Canada ÷ 365 × Full annual credit amount = Pro-rated credit
```

!!! example "Case — Maria's Pro-rated Basic Personal Amount"
    Maria became resident on June 15. Days resident: June 15 to December 31 = **200 days**

    Fraction: 200 ÷ 365 = **54.8%**

    Federal Basic Personal Amount:
    Full amount: $16,129 × 15% = $2,419
    Pro-rated: $2,419 × 54.8% = **$1,326**

    **Maria does not get the full $2,419 Basic Personal Amount — she gets $1,326** because she was only resident for 54.8% of the year.

    The same pro-ration applies to: Age Amount, Spouse/CLP Amount, Disability Amount, and all other personal credits.

!!! warning "ONE exception to pro-ration — when Canadian-source income is 90%+ of worldwide income"
    If the part-year resident's Canadian-source income is **90% or more** of their total worldwide income for the year, they may be able to claim the **full annual personal credits** (no pro-ration). This is called the **substantial resident test** and is designed to avoid penalizing people whose entire income was earned in Canada even though they arrived partway through the year.

    Maria earned $24,000 outside Canada + $38,000 in Canada = $62,000 total worldwide. Canadian-source = $38,000 ÷ $62,000 = 61.3%. Below 90%. **Pro-ration applies.**

    If Maria had earned only $3,000 outside Canada and $38,000 in Canada: $38,000 ÷ $41,000 = 92.7%. Above 90%. **Full credits available.**

    Always calculate this percentage before applying pro-ration. When the client's foreign income is small, the full credit may be available — worth thousands of dollars.

### The Ontario Angle on Part-Year Returns

Ontario tax applies at the full Ontario rates but credits are also pro-rated. The Ontario Basic Personal Amount ($11,865) is similarly pro-rated for partial-year residents.

**Ontario Trillium Benefit — partial year:**
The OTB (ON-BEN) covers only the months the client was an Ontario resident paying rent in Ontario. Enter only the rent from the date of arrival to December 31.

## 19.3 Foreign Income — Converting and Reporting Correctly

A new immigrant with worldwide income during their Canadian residency period must report that foreign income on their T1. The mechanics of doing this correctly are critical — and frequently botched.

### Step 1 — Identify What Must Be Reported

During the **Canadian residency period only**, report:

| Income Type | Report on T1? | Line |
|---|---|---|
| Foreign employment income | ✅ Yes | Line 10400 (Other employment income) |
| Foreign business income | ✅ Yes | T2125 / Line 13500 |
| Foreign investment income (interest, dividends) | ✅ Yes | Lines 12000 / 12100 |
| Foreign rental income | ✅ Yes | T776 / Line 12600 |
| Foreign pension income | ✅ Yes | Line 11500 (Other pension income) |
| Foreign capital gains | ✅ Yes | Schedule 3 / Line 12700 |

### Step 2 — Convert to Canadian Dollars

All foreign income must be reported in **Canadian dollars** using the exchange rate at the time the income was received, OR the **Bank of Canada annual average rate** for the year.

**Most common source currencies for Ontario immigrants:**

| Country | Currency | 2025 Approx. Annual Average Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Philippines | PHP | 1 CAD ≈ 41 PHP (i.e., 1 PHP ≈ $0.024 CAD) |
| India | INR | 1 CAD ≈ 62 INR (i.e., 1 INR ≈ $0.016 CAD) |
| China | CNY | 1 CAD ≈ 5.2 CNY |
| UAE/Gulf | AED | 1 CAD ≈ 2.68 AED |
| USA | USD | 1 CAD ≈ 0.73 USD (i.e., 1 USD ≈ $1.37 CAD) |
| UK | GBP | 1 CAD ≈ 0.58 GBP |

!!! tip "Always use the Bank of Canada rate — not Google or your bank"
    The official source is the **Bank of Canada exchange rate lookup tool** at bankofcanada.ca. Use the annual average rate for consistent income (like a monthly salary). Use the specific date rate for one-time transactions (like a property sale). Document the rate used in the client file.

### Step 3 — Claim the Foreign Tax Credit

If the client paid tax in another country on income that is also taxable in Canada, they get a **Foreign Tax Credit** to avoid double taxation.

**Federal Foreign Tax Credit:** Form T2209 → Line 40500
**Ontario Foreign Tax Credit:** Form T2036 → ON428

```
Foreign Tax Credit = Lesser of:
  (a) Foreign tax actually paid on the income (converted to CAD), OR
  (b) Canadian tax that would apply to that same income
```

The credit cannot exceed what Canada would have charged on the income — it just prevents you from paying double. You never get a refund of foreign tax that exceeds Canadian tax on the same income.

!!! example "Case — Ahmed's UAE Salary During Canadian Residency"
    Ahmed becomes a Canadian resident on March 1. He still does remote consulting work for his former UAE employer from April to June, earning 75,000 AED (≈ $28,000 CAD).

    The UAE has **no income tax** — Ahmed paid zero UAE tax on this income.

    Since he paid $0 in foreign tax, there is **no foreign tax credit** available. The $28,000 is fully taxable in Canada as Canadian resident income.

    This surprises clients from zero-tax jurisdictions (UAE, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar). They are accustomed to paying no income tax. Their first Canadian return can be a shock. Prepare them in advance: *"Canada will tax your worldwide income from the day you became resident. Your UAE work income earned after March 1 goes on your Canadian return."*

!!! example "Case — Priya's Indian Salary and the Foreign Tax Credit"
    Priya arrives from India on August 1. During August–December she earns $42,000 in Canada. She also received 180,000 INR (≈ $2,880 CAD) in interest from Indian bank accounts from August to December — income that was taxable in India (she paid 1,500 INR = approximately $24 CAD in Indian tax on it).

    **Reporting on Canadian T1:**
    - T1 line 12100 (foreign interest): $2,880 CAD
    - Federal Foreign Tax Credit (T2209): claim $24 CAD paid to India

    Canadian federal tax on $2,880 at 15%: $432
    Foreign tax credit: $24 (lesser of $24 paid or $432 Canadian tax)

    **Net Canadian tax on the Indian interest: $432 − $24 = $408**

    The foreign tax credit is small because India's tax rate on interest is low. For countries with higher tax rates (UK, Germany, France at 25–45%), the foreign tax credit can eliminate most or all Canadian tax on that income.

## 19.4 The T1135 — Foreign Income Verification Statement

The **T1135** is one of the most consequential and most missed forms in Canadian tax. It is required when a Canadian resident owns **specified foreign property** with a total cost of **more than $100,000 CAD** at any point in the year.

### What Is Specified Foreign Property?

| Qualifies as Specified Foreign Property | Does NOT Qualify |
|---|---|
| Foreign bank accounts | Personal use property (vacation home you use personally) |
| Foreign stocks and bonds (non-registered) | RRSP/RRIF holdings in foreign investments |
| Foreign rental property | Foreign pension plans (CPP equivalents) |
| Foreign corporate interests | |
| Foreign mutual funds and ETFs | |
| Loans to non-residents | |
| Foreign intangible property | |

!!! danger "The T1135 penalty is automatic, severe, and applied even for honest mistakes"
    **Late filing penalty:** $25/day, minimum $100, maximum **$2,500**
    **Failure to file:** $500/month, maximum **$12,000** — can be applied for each year not filed
    **Gross negligence penalty:** Up to **5% of the cost of the foreign property** — on a $500,000 foreign property, this is $25,000

    CRA has been increasingly aggressive in enforcing T1135 requirements as international information sharing under FATCA and CRS matures. Foreign banks now report account information for Canadian residents automatically to CRA.

    **Every new immigrant who arrives with significant foreign assets must have T1135 assessed immediately.**

!!! example "Case — The New Immigrant with Foreign Assets Nobody Asked About"
    Carlos arrives from Brazil on May 1 and becomes a Canadian resident. He has:
    - Brazilian bank account: $45,000 CAD equivalent
    - Brazilian investment portfolio: $68,000 CAD equivalent
    - Brazilian rental property: $210,000 CAD equivalent

    **Total specified foreign property cost: $45,000 + $68,000 + $210,000 = $323,000 CAD**

    This exceeds the $100,000 threshold. Carlos must file **Form T1135** with his T1 for every year he holds this foreign property as a Canadian resident.

    **His prior preparer never asked.** Three years later, CRA writes to Carlos. He has three unfiled T1135 forms.

    **Penalty exposure:**
    - If treated as simple failure to file: $2,500 × 3 years = $7,500
    - If CRA pursues gross negligence: potentially 5% × $323,000 = $16,150/year × 3 years

    Carlos owes potentially $7,500–$48,000+ in penalties — in addition to any income tax on the rental income he forgot to report.

    **The intake question that prevents this:** *"Do you own any bank accounts, investments, real estate, or businesses outside of Canada? What is their approximate value?"*

    Ask it every time. For new immigrants especially — ask it on the first call, before the first meeting.

### The Simplified T1135 — For Accounts Under $250,000

For clients with specified foreign property valued at **$100,001 to $250,000**, CRA offers a **simplified reporting method**:

- Only the country, category of property, and maximum cost during the year are required
- No detailed account-by-account breakdown

For property over **$250,000**, the full detailed method is required.

## 19.5 Common Source Countries — What to Expect from Each

As a tax preparer in Southern Ontario, you will serve immigrants primarily from these regions. Each has unique considerations:

### Philippines

**Tax treaties:** Canada and the Philippines have a **tax treaty** — eliminates some double taxation.

**Common income to report:**
- Philippine bank account interest (small but taxable in Canada)
- Remittances sent TO the Philippines: **not deductible** in Canada (sending money to family abroad is not a deduction)
- OFW (Overseas Filipino Worker) income earned before arrival: not taxable in Canada

**Common question from Filipino clients:** *"I was sending money to my family in the Philippines every month. Is that deductible?"*
**Your answer:** No. Remittances are personal expenditures, not business expenses or deductible support payments. They do not appear on the Canadian return.

**T1135 trigger:** Many Filipino families have small savings accounts in the Philippines — usually well under $100,000. Rarely triggers T1135 for recent immigrants.

### India

**Tax treaties:** Canada–India tax treaty in force. Covers income from employment, business, dividends, interest, and royalties.

**Common income to report:**
- Fixed deposit (FD) interest from Indian banks — taxable in Canada during residency period
- PPF (Public Provident Fund) income — technically taxable in Canada (PPF is a savings plan, not a pension)
- NRE/NRO account interest — once resident in Canada, interest on NRO accounts is taxable in Canada
- Property rental income from India — taxable in Canada, foreign tax credit available

**Common T1135 trigger:** Indian immigrants who arrive with significant savings — NRO accounts, mutual funds, property — frequently cross the $100,000 threshold. The family home in India often represents several hundred thousand CAD equivalent.

**Important:** The **family home** in India (or elsewhere) — if used personally as a residence, even part of the year — is **NOT specified foreign property** for T1135 purposes. Only property held for investment purposes (rental, commercial) triggers T1135. A house the family visits on vacation is personal use property.

!!! example "Case — Deepa's Indian Assets"
    Deepa arrives from Mumbai on April 1. Her assets in India:
    - NRO bank account: $28,000 CAD equivalent
    - Mutual fund portfolio: $45,000 CAD equivalent
    - Family apartment in Mumbai (personal use, visits during Diwali): $280,000 CAD equivalent
    - Rental apartment in Pune: $180,000 CAD equivalent

    **T1135 analysis:**
    - NRO account: $28,000 — specified foreign property ✅
    - Mutual funds: $45,000 — specified foreign property ✅
    - Mumbai apartment (personal use): **Not specified foreign property** ❌
    - Pune rental: $180,000 — specified foreign property ✅

    Total specified foreign property: $28,000 + $45,000 + $180,000 = **$253,000 CAD**

    Exceeds $100,000. T1135 required — simplified method available (under $250,000? $253,000 is just above — full method required. Very close — verify annually as values fluctuate with exchange rates).

    **Income to report on T1:**
    - NRO account interest from April–December: e.g., $680 CAD
    - Mutual fund gains realized: report any dispositions
    - Pune rental income from April–December: e.g., $6,800 CAD
    - Mumbai apartment: no income to report (personal use)

### China

**Tax treaties:** Canada–China tax treaty in force.

**Common income to report:**
- Chinese bank account interest (often at higher rates than Canadian banks)
- WeChat Pay balance — technically a financial account but typically under reporting thresholds
- Chinese stock market investments (if held in non-registered Chinese brokerage accounts)
- Property in China — rental income taxable in Canada; capital gains on sale taxable in Canada

**The major T1135 trigger for Chinese immigrants:** Real estate. Chinese property values (in CAD terms) frequently exceed $100,000 — sometimes dramatically. A condominium in Beijing or Shanghai can easily be $500,000–$2,000,000 CAD equivalent. If held for investment/rental purposes, the T1135 obligation is significant.

**Chinese capital gains complication:** When a Chinese immigrant sells Chinese property after becoming a Canadian resident, the entire capital gain is taxable in Canada (based on the FMV on the date of arrival as the ACB). This can trigger very large Canadian tax bills on property sold years after arrival.

!!! example "Case — Wei Sells His Shanghai Condo"
    Wei arrived from Shanghai on January 15, 2020. At that date, his Shanghai condo was worth $320,000 CAD (his ACB for Canadian purposes).

    In 2025, he sells the condo for $480,000 CAD equivalent.

    **Canadian capital gain:** $480,000 − $320,000 = $160,000
    **Taxable capital gain (50%):** $80,000 → Line 12700

    Wei may also owe Chinese tax on the sale (China taxes capital gains on real estate). He claims the **Foreign Tax Credit** for any Chinese tax paid.

    **The critical detail:** Wei's ACB is the FMV **on the date he became resident** — not what he originally paid for the property in 2015. If he paid $80,000 (RMB equivalent in 2015) and the condo was worth $320,000 CAD on arrival, his ACB is $320,000 — he does NOT owe Canadian tax on the pre-residency appreciation.

    This stepped-up ACB on arrival is one of the most important and least known rules for immigrants with appreciated property.

### Middle East (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain)

**Tax treaties:** Canada has treaties with some Gulf states (varies — verify for each). The UAE has a treaty with Canada (signed 2002).

**Key characteristic:** These are **zero-tax jurisdictions**. Clients from the Gulf have never paid income tax. Their first Canadian return is often a cultural shock.

**Common income situations:**
- End-of-service gratuity received at departure from the UAE: taxable in Canada if received after Canadian residency began
- UAE savings account interest: taxable in Canada (no UAE tax, full Canadian tax applies)
- Investment portfolio from UAE: taxable gains and income

**The "I never paid tax before" conversation:**

When a client from the UAE or Saudi Arabia sits across from you and learns they owe $18,000 in Canadian income tax on their $95,000 salary, their reaction is often disbelief or anger.

Your approach: *"I completely understand this is a significant adjustment. In the UAE you paid no income tax — that's one of the great advantages of living there. In Canada, income tax funds the healthcare system, schools, and public infrastructure you and your family will use every day. Here's the full picture of what you receive in return: your family's healthcare is free, your children's school is free, and you'll receive government benefits including the Canada Child Benefit — which, at your income level with two children, is approximately $X per month. Canada's total compensation including benefits is often comparable to a higher net pay in a lower-tax country."*

Then prepare the return with every legitimate deduction and credit maximized, and show them the net position clearly.

## 19.6 The Deemed Acquisition on Arrival — The Immigrant's ACB Reset

When a person becomes a Canadian resident, CRA deems them to have **acquired their property at fair market value** on the date of residency. This is called the **deemed acquisition** under Section 128.1 of the Income Tax Act.

**What it means in practice:** The ACB of any capital property owned before arriving in Canada is reset to its FMV on the date of arrival. Only appreciation after arrival is taxable in Canada.

This rule protects new immigrants from paying Canadian tax on gains that accrued before they were Canadian residents — it is one of the most generous provisions in the Act for new arrivals.

### How to Establish the FMV on Arrival

| Property Type | How to Establish FMV on Arrival |
|---|---|
| Publicly traded stocks | Use the closing market price on the date of arrival (in CAD) |
| Foreign real estate | Obtain a professional appraisal close to the arrival date |
| Foreign business interests | Business valuation — complex, specialist required |
| Foreign bank accounts | Account balance on arrival date (converted to CAD) |
| Personal use property | Generally not needed — not specified foreign property |

!!! tip "Document FMV on arrival — immediately"
    This is a time-sensitive action. If a client arrives in Canada without documenting FMV, and later sells property years later, establishing FMV retrospectively is difficult and expensive. Advise every new immigrant client on their first meeting: *"For any investments or real estate you own outside Canada, we need to document their value in Canadian dollars as of your arrival date. This protects you from paying tax on gains that happened before you came to Canada."*

    For stocks: pull a closing price from any financial website for the arrival date.
    For real estate: get a local appraisal or comparative market analysis immediately.

!!! example "Case — The Arrival ACB Matters Years Later"
    Sandra arrives from the UK on September 1, 2021. She owns UK shares worth £45,000 (≈ $77,000 CAD) on arrival. She originally paid £18,000 for them in 2015.

    In 2025, she sells the shares for £60,000 (≈ $102,000 CAD).

    **ACB:** $77,000 (FMV on arrival — not the original £18,000 purchase price)

    Canadian capital gain: $102,000 − $77,000 = **$25,000**
    Taxable capital gain (50%): **$12,500** → Line 12700

    **Without the deemed acquisition rule:** $102,000 − $30,000 (original £18,000 converted at today's rate) = $72,000 gain, taxable capital gain $36,000.

    The deemed acquisition **saved Sandra $36,000 − $12,500 = $23,500** in taxable capital gain — more than $5,500 in actual tax savings.

    If Sandra had never documented the FMV on arrival: she would either have to use a reconstructed estimate (potentially challenged by CRA) or use the original cost — paying far more tax than the law requires.

## 19.7 Benefits Available to New Immigrants — The Filing Urgency

New immigrants frequently delay filing because they don't understand what they're entitled to. Here is the complete picture of what a new Canadian resident unlocks by filing:

### Benefits Triggered by Filing — Partial Year

| Benefit | Eligibility | First-Year Amount |
|---|---|---|
| **GST/HST credit** | Canadian resident, age 19+ (or with spouse/child) | Pro-rated from arrival quarter |
| **Canada Child Benefit (CCB)** | Canadian resident with children under 18 | Pro-rated from month after arrival |
| **Ontario Trillium Benefit** | Ontario resident, renting or owning | Pro-rated to Ontario months only |
| **Canada Workers Benefit** | Working income, low-to-moderate income | From date of residency |

!!! example "Case — Maria's First-Year Benefits"
    Maria arrives June 15, 2025. She is 34, married (husband arrived with her), two children ages 4 and 7. They rent an apartment in Brampton for $1,800/month starting July 1. Maria finds work earning $36,000 (July–December).

    **GST/HST credit:** They qualify from Q3 2025 (the quarter after arrival). Annual amount for family of 4: $519 + $519 + $137 + $137 = $1,312. Pro-rated to ~2 quarters: approximately **$656 in 2025**.

    **Canada Child Benefit:** Payable from July 2025 (month after arrival month). Children ages 4 (under 6) and 7 (6–17). At $36,000 AFNI:
    Maximum: $7,787 + $6,570 = $14,357
    Phase-out starts at $36,502 — they're just below it.
    **Approximately $14,357 × 6/12 = $7,179 for July–December 2025.**

    Then from July 2026 onward, full annual CCB based on 2025 income.

    **Ontario Trillium Benefit:**
    Rent July–December: $1,800 × 6 = $10,800. OEPTC: 20% × $10,800 = $2,160 → approximately **$600 partial year OEPTC** + OSTC (pro-rated).

    **Total first-year benefits from filing: approximately $8,435**

    Maria did not know about any of these programs. She came to your office because a friend in her Filipino community said you were the person to see. After filing her first return, she receives:
    - Tax refund from over-withheld employment taxes: ~$800
    - GST/HST credit: $656
    - CCB: $7,179 (paid monthly going forward)
    - OTB: ~$600

    **Total first-year value: approximately $9,235** — from a return that took one meeting to prepare.

    Maria tells everyone in her community about you. This is how a tax practice in a multicultural city grows.

### Applying for CCB — The Separate Step

The CCB does not automatically start when you file. New immigrants must also submit **Form RC66 (Canada Child Benefits Application)** along with:

- Proof of birth for each child
- Proof of immigration status (PR card, visa, work permit)
- Social Insurance Numbers for all family members

This application can be submitted as soon as the family arrives — even before the first tax filing. **Advise every new immigrant family with children to complete RC66 immediately** — every month of delay is a month of lost CCB payments that are NOT retroactively paid (CCB starts from the month the application is approved, not the month of arrival).

!!! danger "CCB is not retroactive beyond 11 months from application date"
    If a family with children arrives in September but doesn't file their taxes or submit RC66 until the following March — they lose approximately 6 months of CCB. At $648/month per child under 6, a family with two young children loses approximately **$7,776** in CCB that can never be recovered.

    Make Form RC66 part of your onboarding package for every new immigrant family with children. Do not wait for tax season.

## 19.8 The Departure Return — When a Client Leaves Canada

Just as arriving in Canada creates a part-year return, **leaving Canada** creates a departure return — a T1 for the period January 1 to the date of departure.

### The Key Rule — Deemed Disposition on Departure

On the day a taxpayer ceases to be a Canadian resident, they are deemed to have **sold all their property at fair market value**. This triggers capital gains (or losses) on all unrealized appreciation on:

- Foreign investments
- Canadian investments held in non-registered accounts
- Real estate (other than Canadian real estate — separate rules)
- Business interests
- Cryptocurrency

**Exempt from deemed disposition:**
- Real estate situated in Canada (separate reporting as non-resident landlord going forward)
- Inventory of a Canadian business
- Employee stock options

!!! example "Case — James Leaves Canada for the UK"
    James, a British-Canadian dual citizen, is being transferred to London permanently. He leaves Canada on November 30.

    He holds:
    - Non-registered Canadian stock portfolio: $285,000 (ACB: $180,000 → unrealized gain: $105,000)
    - British rental property: £280,000 = $476,000 CAD (ACB at original purchase: $220,000 CAD → unrealized gain: $256,000)
    - Principal residence: $850,000 (Principal Residence Exemption applies — no deemed disposition gain)

    **Deemed disposition on departure:**
    Canadian stocks: $105,000 capital gain × 50% = $52,500 taxable capital gain
    British rental: $256,000 capital gain × 50% = $128,000 taxable capital gain
    **Total taxable capital gains on departure: $180,500 → Line 12700**

    At James's top combined rate: $180,500 × 46.16% = approximately **$83,335 in departure tax**

    This is why clients who are planning to leave Canada need tax advice **before** departure, not after. Strategies like donating appreciated securities to charity, timing departure to maximize credit use, or electing to defer the deemed disposition (posting security with CRA) can significantly reduce departure tax.

    **When a client mentions they are moving abroad — bring up departure tax immediately.** It is one of the largest and most avoidable tax surprises in the system.

## 19.9 Common Mistakes on New Immigrant Returns

| Mistake | What Goes Wrong | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Including pre-residency foreign income on the T1 | Foreign income from before arrival is not taxable in Canada | Overstated income — over-payment of tax |
| Using original purchase price as ACB for foreign property | Should use FMV on date of Canadian residency | Overstated capital gains when property is sold |
| Not pro-rating personal credits | Full BPA used when only partial year is entitled | Overstated credits — CRA reassessment |
| Forgetting to file T1135 | Foreign property over $100,000 not disclosed | $2,500–$25,000+ in penalties |
| Treating UAE/Saudi income as non-taxable | Zero-tax country income is still fully taxable in Canada | Underpaid tax, interest, penalties |
| Missing CCB — not filing RC66 | CCB payments not started | Months or years of lost payments |
| Not documenting FMV at arrival | Impossible to establish ACB on future property sale | Pays Canadian tax on pre-residency gains |
| Claiming remittances sent abroad as deductions | Money sent to family is not deductible | False deduction — reassessment |
| Confusing immigration status with tax residency | Assuming work permit holder isn't a resident | Wrong return type — entire return may be incorrect |

## 19.10 The New Immigrant Intake Checklist

This is your must-ask list for every client who arrived in Canada during the year.

### Step 1 — Establish Residency

- [ ] Date of arrival in Canada?
- [ ] Immigration status (PR, TFW, student, visitor)?
- [ ] Where did you live on December 31?
- [ ] Do you have a home in Canada (rented or owned)?
- [ ] Did your spouse/children arrive with you, or separately?
- [ ] Do you still have a home abroad? Are you still paying rent/mortgage abroad?
- [ ] Do you have bank accounts, investments, or property in your home country?

### Step 2 — Income

- [ ] Income earned in Canada after arrival (all slips)
- [ ] Income earned in home country BEFORE arrival (will NOT go on T1 in most cases)
- [ ] Income earned in home country AFTER arrival (WILL go on T1 — worldwide income)
- [ ] Any RESP withdrawals, pensions, foreign pensions?

### Step 3 — Foreign Property

- [ ] Foreign bank accounts — approximate value?
- [ ] Foreign investments (stocks, mutual funds)?
- [ ] Foreign real estate (rental vs. personal use)?
- [ ] Total estimated value of all foreign property exceeds $100,000 CAD? → T1135 required

### Step 4 — FMV Documentation

- [ ] Foreign stocks: closing prices on arrival date pulled and documented?
- [ ] Foreign real estate: appraisal or CMA obtained close to arrival date?
- [ ] All amounts converted to CAD using Bank of Canada rates?

### Step 5 — Benefits Applications

- [ ] Children under 18 in Canada? → RC66 submitted?
- [ ] SINs obtained for all family members?
- [ ] All family members' immigration status confirmed?

### Step 6 — Explain What Comes Next

- [ ] GST/HST credit — when first payment arrives
- [ ] CCB — when first payment arrives (if RC66 submitted timely)
- [ ] OTB — monthly payments, what amount to expect
- [ ] CRA My Account — how to set up, what to monitor
- [ ] T1135 — annual filing obligation explained if applicable

### Case A — The Filipino Nurse Who Arrived in June

**Client:** Maria Santos, 34, arrives from the Philippines on June 15, 2025. Her husband Jose and their two daughters (ages 3 and 6) arrive the same day. They rent a townhouse in Brampton at $2,200/month (starting July 1). Maria starts work as a registered nurse at Brampton Civic Hospital on July 7 at $82,000/year.

**Residency determination:**
- Home available in Canada from July 1 ✅
- Spouse in Canada ✅
- Dependent children in Canada ✅
**Resident from June 15, 2025** (date of arrival — primary ties established immediately)

**Documents received:**
- T4: Box 14 = $40,333 ($82,000 × 6 months/year, approximately July–December)
- Box 22 = $8,200 withheld (employer annualized to full year)
- Box 16 = $2,400 CPP (partial year)
- Box 18 = $671 EI (partial year)
- No T5 or T4A — no investments in Canada yet
- Philippine bank account: 180,000 PHP interest earned August–December ≈ $4,380 CAD (taxable — earned during Canadian residency period)

**Philippine assets:** Savings account 450,000 PHP ≈ $10,900 CAD. **Under $100,000** → No T1135 required.

**T1 calculation:**

Income:
- Employment income (T4 Box 14): $40,333 (Line 10100)
- Foreign interest (Philippine bank, Aug–Dec): $4,380 (Line 12100)
- **Total income: $44,713**

Deductions: None.
**Net income: $44,713**

Canadian-source income: $40,333. Total worldwide income: $44,713. Canadian % = 90.1% ≈ **Just at the 90% threshold**. It is borderline — Maria's case is very close. $44,713 × 90% = $40,242. Her Canadian income is $40,333 — slightly above. She qualifies for **full credits** (no pro-ration) under the substantial resident test.

This saves her approximately: $2,419 × (1 − 54.8%) = $1,090 in additional federal credit. Worth calculating precisely.

| $0–$44,713 | $44,713 | 15% | $6,707 |

- BPA: $16,129 × 15% = $2,419
- CPP: $2,400 × 15% = $360
- EI: $671 × 15% = $101
- Canada Employment: $1,433 × 15% = $215
- **Total credits: $3,095**

**Net federal tax: $6,707 − $3,095 = $3,612**

Ontario tax (ON428):
$44,713 Ontario taxable income:
- $51,446 bracket: $44,713 × 5.05% = $2,258 (under first bracket)

Tax withheld: $8,200
**Refund: $3,082** ✅

OEPTC: 20% × $13,200 = $2,640 → approximately $640/year (partial year)
OSTC: Below threshold — approximately $345 (partial year, pro-rated)

Annual CCB: $14,357 − $1,560 = **$12,797** ($1,066/month)

For the period July–December 2025 (6 months): $12,797 × 6/12 = **$6,399 in CCB** for 2025.

From July 2026 (based on 2025 return): **$1,066/month ongoing**.

### Case B — The Indian Software Engineer with Foreign Assets

**Client:** Rahul Sharma, 31, arrives from Bangalore on March 1, 2025. He is single. He starts at a Waterloo tech firm at $98,000/year on March 15. He rents an apartment at $1,900/month.

**Foreign assets on arrival (March 1 FMV in CAD):**
- Indian savings account (NRO): $18,000 CAD
- Indian mutual funds (equity): $52,000 CAD
- Bangalore apartment (personally used — personal use property): $385,000 CAD
- **Specified foreign property total: $18,000 + $52,000 = $70,000 CAD**

Bangalore apartment = personal use → **NOT specified foreign property**
Total specified foreign property: $70,000 → **Under $100,000. No T1135 required.**

**FMV documentation:**
- NRO account: balance on March 1 from bank statement converted at Bank of Canada rate ✅
- Mutual funds: NAV on March 1 from fund house statement converted at Bank of Canada rate ✅
- Bangalore apartment: no documentation needed (personal use)

**Income during Canadian residency (March 1–December 31):**
- Canadian employment (T4): $98,000 × 10/12 = $81,667
- Indian NRO interest (March–December): 42,000 INR ≈ $677 CAD
- Indian mutual fund dividends declared March–December: $1,240 CAD

**Pro-ration check:**
Days resident: March 1 to December 31 = 306 days. Fraction: 306/365 = 83.8%
Canadian income $81,667 / Total $83,584 = **97.7% Canadian-source** → exceeds 90% → **Full credits, no pro-ration** ✅

**Tax calculation:**
Total income: $81,667 + $677 + $1,240 = **$83,584**
Net income: $83,584 (no deductions)

Federal tax: ~$11,800 gross. After credits (~$3,371): net federal tax ~$8,429
Ontario tax: ~$4,100 (with credits and no surtax at this income)
**Total tax: ~$12,529**

T4 tax withheld (employer annualized 10 months of $98K as if full year): approximately $16,800 withheld.

**Federal + Ontario refund: approximately $4,271**

**Ontario Trillium Benefit:**
Rent March–December: $1,900 × 10 = $19,000 → OEPTC: 20% × $19,000 = $3,800 → approximately **$987/year** for full Ontario year.

Rahul is one of hundreds of thousands of young Indian tech professionals arriving in Ontario every year. His return is clean, his benefits are clear, and he needs proactive planning — RRSP, FHSA for his eventual home purchase, and annual T1135 monitoring as his foreign assets may cross $100,000 as the Indian mutual fund portfolio grows.

### Case C — The UAE Executive Who Owed More Than He Expected

**Client:** Khalid Al-Rashid, 42, arrives from Dubai on February 1, 2025. Wife and three children (ages 8, 12, 15) arrive the same day. He joins a Toronto consulting firm at $145,000/year starting February 15. They purchase a home in Mississauga.

**Foreign assets on arrival (February 1 FMV):**

- Dubai apartment (rental investment, not personal use): $420,000 CAD

**Income during Canadian residency (February 1–December 31):**

- UAE bank account interest (UAE has 0% tax): $2,400 CAD
- Dubai rental income (net, after local expenses): $14,800 CAD
- UAE investment portfolio dividends: $8,200 CAD

**Pro-ration:** Days resident 334/365 = 91.5%. Canadian income $133,000/$158,400 total = 83.9%. Below 90% — **pro-ration of credits applies.**

Days fraction: 334/365 = 91.5%
Federal BPA pro-rated: $16,129 × 91.5% = $14,758. Credit: $14,758 × 15% = **$2,214** (vs. full $2,419)

**Total income: $158,400**
**Net income: $158,400** (no major deductions this year — Khalid needs to open RRSP)

**Tax (approximate):**

Ontario: ~$18,000 + surtax ~$8,500 = ~$26,500 (income in surtax range)
**Estimated total tax: ~$61,300**

**Balance owing: approximately $19,300** (additional tax on the foreign income and the higher income)

**The conversation Khalid needs:**

## 19.12 Week 19 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - **Residency is determined by the significant ties test** — immigration status and day count are not the determinants. One primary tie (home, spouse, or dependants) usually establishes residency from the date of arrival
    - **Part-year T1 income rule:** During the non-resident period, only Canadian-source income is taxable. During the resident period, worldwide income is taxable
    - **Credits are pro-rated** by days resident ÷ 365 — UNLESS the 90% substantial resident test is met (Canadian-source income ≥ 90% of total worldwide income), in which case full credits apply
    - **Foreign income conversion:** Use Bank of Canada annual average exchange rates. Document the rate used in the file
    - **Foreign tax credit (T2209/T2036):** Prevents double taxation. Capped at the lesser of foreign tax paid or Canadian tax on the same income. Clients from zero-tax countries (UAE, Gulf states) get no credit — their income is fully taxable in Canada
    - **T1135 Foreign Income Verification:** Required when total specified foreign property exceeds $100,000 CAD at any point in the year. Personal use property (family home) does not count. Penalties are automatic and severe — up to $25,000+ per year unfiled
    - **Deemed acquisition on arrival:** FMV on arrival date becomes the ACB for all property owned before becoming resident. Only post-arrival appreciation is taxable. Document FMV immediately on arrival
    - **CCB requires Form RC66** — filed separately, not automatic on the tax return. Every month of delay in filing RC66 is lost CCB that cannot be recovered retroactively beyond 11 months
    - **New immigrants from zero-tax countries** (UAE, Gulf) need careful preparation for their first Canadian tax bill — the culture shock is real and your role includes explaining the value received in return
    - **China/India clients:** T1135 often triggered by real estate held in home country — even if used personally in some months, the rental component makes it specified foreign property. The family home used exclusively for personal use is NOT specified foreign property

## ✅ Week 19 Quiz

When you're ready, ask Claude: **"Quiz me on Week 19"** for an interactive test.

??? question "1. Rosa arrives from the Philippines on September 1, 2025. Before arriving, she earned PHP equivalent of $32,000 CAD in the Philippines (January–August). After arriving, she earns $18,000 from a Toronto restaurant job (September–December). What income appears on her Canadian T1 and how are her personal credits treated?"
    **T1 income:** Only the **$18,000 Canadian employment income** from the residency period (September 1–December 31). The $32,000 earned in the Philippines before arrival is **not taxable in Canada** — pre-residency foreign income is excluded for part-year residents. **Credits:** Days resident: September 1–December 31 = 122 days. Fraction: 122/365 = 33.4%. Pro-ration applies UNLESS the 90% test is met. Canadian income $18,000 / Total worldwide $50,000 = 36% — well below 90%. **Credits are pro-rated at 33.4%.** Federal BPA credit: $2,419 × 33.4% = **$808** (vs. the full $2,419). Rosa still gets a meaningful refund of withheld taxes and qualifies for GST/HST credit from Q4 2025, plus OTB for September–December rental period.

??? question "2. Ahmed arrives from Qatar on April 15, 2025. He has a Qatar bank account with $65,000 CAD equivalent, a Qatar investment portfolio worth $48,000 CAD, and a personal apartment in Doha worth $320,000 CAD (which he uses personally when visiting family). Is T1135 required? What is the specified foreign property total?"
    **T1135 analysis:** Qatar bank account ($65,000) — specified foreign property ✅. Investment portfolio ($48,000) — specified foreign property ✅. Doha personal apartment ($320,000) — **personal use property, NOT specified foreign property** ❌. **Specified foreign property total: $65,000 + $48,000 = $113,000 CAD.** This exceeds the $100,000 threshold. **T1135 is required** — simplified method (between $100,001 and $250,000). Ahmed must disclose the bank account and investment portfolio on T1135. The Doha apartment is excluded because it is held for personal use, not investment income. Note: if Ahmed rents the Doha apartment out for any portion of the year, it shifts toward investment property and the analysis changes.

??? question "3. Deepa arrives from India on July 1, 2025. She owns Indian equity mutual funds with an FMV of 3,200,000 INR (≈ $51,200 CAD) on arrival. She originally paid 1,400,000 INR for them in 2019. In December 2025, she sells all the funds for 3,600,000 INR (≈ $57,600 CAD). What is her Canadian capital gain and what is her ACB?"
    **Deemed acquisition on arrival:** On July 1, Deepa is deemed to have acquired her mutual funds at their FMV on that date — **$51,200 CAD** (the arrival FMV). Her original purchase price of 1,400,000 INR (approximately $22,400 CAD) is irrelevant for Canadian tax purposes. **Capital gain on December sale:** Proceeds: $57,600 CAD. ACB: $51,200 CAD. Capital gain: $57,600 − $51,200 = **$6,400**. Taxable capital gain (×50%): **$3,200** → Line 12700. Without the deemed acquisition rule, Deepa would have used the $22,400 original cost, generating a gain of $35,200 and taxable gain of $17,600 — more than five times higher. **This is why documenting FMV on arrival is critical.** Deepa should obtain her fund NAVs on July 1 from her fund house statement and keep them permanently on file.

??? question "4. A new immigrant family from China (husband, wife, two children ages 5 and 9) arrives on May 15. They rent a home in Mississauga at $2,500/month starting June 1. The husband earns $72,000 at his new Ontario job (June–December = 7 months). What is the approximate annual Canada Child Benefit they will receive starting from July 2025 and continuing through June 2026, and what should you remind them to file immediately?"
    **CCB calculation:** AFNI = $72,000 × 7/12 = $42,000 (2025 income). At $42,000 AFNI with 2 children: Maximum = $7,787 (child under 6) + $6,570 (age 9) = $14,357. Phase-out: ($42,000 − $36,502) × 19% = $5,498 × 19% = $1,045. **Annual CCB: $14,357 − $1,045 = $13,312** ($1,109/month). For July–December 2025 (6 months): $13,312 × 6/12 = **$6,656 in 2025 CCB**. From July 2026 onward (based on 2025 return): $1,109/month = **$13,312/year ongoing**. **File immediately:** Form RC66 (Canada Child Benefits Application) must be submitted with proof of birth for each child and immigration documents. Every month of delay is $1,109 in lost CCB that cannot be recovered retroactively (beyond 11 months). This family should walk out of your office with a completed RC66 in hand or submitted that same day.

??? question "5. Khalid arrives from Dubai on March 1. He has a UAE investment portfolio worth $220,000 CAD (FMV on arrival). During March–December (his Canadian residency period), the portfolio earns $11,400 in dividends and capital distributions. The UAE charges zero tax on this income. What are the Canadian tax consequences and is a Foreign Tax Credit available?"
    **Canadian tax consequences:** $11,400 in UAE investment income is **fully taxable in Canada** during his residency period (worldwide income rule). Since it is from a foreign portfolio, it is reported as foreign income — interest/dividends on Line 12100 or 12000 depending on type, capital distributions on Schedule 3 as capital gains. **Foreign Tax Credit:** The UAE charges **zero income tax**. Khalid paid $0 in UAE tax. The Foreign Tax Credit is the lesser of (a) foreign tax paid = **$0** or (b) Canadian tax on the income. **Foreign Tax Credit: $0.** There is no credit available because no foreign tax was paid. The $11,400 is fully taxable in Canada at Khalid's marginal rate. Many Gulf clients are shocked by this. The explanation: the Foreign Tax Credit prevents double taxation — but if only one country is taxing the income (Canada), there's nothing to credit against.

??? question "6. Li Wei is a full-time international student who arrived from China on September 5 and lives in a university residence. He has no spouse, no children, his family remains in China, and he lives in an on-campus residence. His only tie to Canada is his study permit and temporary residence room. Is he a Canadian tax resident for the year?"
    **Almost certainly NOT a resident for the year.** Li Wei has no primary ties to Canada: no permanent home available for his use in Canada (a university residence room is a temporary, communal living arrangement — not a home available for his use), no spouse or CLP in Canada, and no dependants in Canada. His secondary ties are minimal (study permit, campus room). His significant ties are all in China (home, family, bank accounts). Under CRA's significant ties test, Li Wei is likely a **non-resident or sojourner** for 2025. As a non-resident, he is only taxable in Canada on Canadian-source income (which, as a student with no Canadian employment income, may be zero). He does not file a regular T1. If he has no Canadian-source income, he may not need to file at all. **Key intake question:** Does Li Wei have any Canadian employment income (on-campus jobs, co-op)? Does he have a Canadian bank account earning interest? If yes — he may still owe some Canadian tax as a non-resident. For most international students with no income: no filing required.

??? question "7. Sandra moved from the UK to Toronto on August 1, 2025. She brought her UK stock portfolio (worth £85,000 = $144,500 CAD on arrival). She paid £32,000 for these stocks in 2018. In November 2025, she sells all the stocks for £90,000 = $153,000 CAD. Calculate (a) her ACB, (b) her capital gain, and (c) whether T1135 was required."
    **(a) ACB:** Deemed acquisition on arrival at FMV = **$144,500 CAD** (the August 1 value). The original cost of £32,000 is irrelevant for Canadian tax. **(b) Capital gain:** Proceeds $153,000 − ACB $144,500 = **$8,500 capital gain**. Taxable capital gain (×50%): **$4,250** → Line 12700. Without the deemed acquisition rule, the gain would have been $153,000 − ~$54,400 (original £32,000 at current rate) = $98,600, taxable gain $49,300 — more than 11 times higher. **(c) T1135:** Sandra held the portfolio from August 1 (arrival) to November (sale). The cost on arrival was $144,500 — exceeds $100,000 threshold. **T1135 was required for 2025** — even though she sold the portfolio before year-end. T1135 looks at whether the specified foreign property cost exceeded $100,000 **at any point during the year** — not just at year-end. The portfolio was over $100,000 from August 1 to November. T1135 should be filed for the 2025 year.
