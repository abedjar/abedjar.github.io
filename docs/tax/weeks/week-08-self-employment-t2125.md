## 8.1 Who Is Self-Employed?

A self-employed person runs their own business and does not receive a T4 from their clients. They may be:

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Sole proprietor** | Freelancer, consultant, tradesperson, home-based business |
| **Independent contractor** | IT consultant, delivery driver (Uber, etc.), real estate agent (some) |
| **Professional** | Doctor, dentist, lawyer, accountant in private practice |
| **Commission agent** | Insurance agent without T4, some real estate agents |
| **Gig economy worker** | Airbnb host, Etsy seller, content creator, food delivery |

### Employee vs. Self-Employed — The Critical Distinction

This is not always obvious — and getting it wrong has serious consequences for both the worker and the client who hired them. CRA uses a **four-factor test**:

| Factor | Employee | Self-Employed |
|---|---|---|
| **Control** | Employer controls how/when work is done | Worker controls their own schedule and methods |
| **Tools & equipment** | Employer provides tools | Worker provides their own tools |
| **Financial risk** | No financial risk — fixed pay | Takes on financial risk (can profit or lose) |
| **Integration** | Worker is integral to the business | Worker operates independently |

!!! warning "Misclassification has serious consequences"
    If CRA determines a worker classified as self-employed is actually an employee, the hiring company owes **both the employer and employee portions of CPP and EI** — going back years — plus penalties and interest. If a client is uncertain about their status, escalate to a specialist or direct them to CRA's RC4110 guide.

## 8.2 The T2125 — Statement of Business or Professional Activities

The T2125 is the form used to report self-employment income and calculate net business income. One T2125 per business activity.

### The Basic Structure

```
Gross Business Income (all revenue)
  − Cost of Goods Sold (if applicable)
══════════════════════════════════
Gross Profit
  − Business Expenses
══════════════════════════════════
Net Income (or Loss) Before Adjustments
  − CCA (Capital Cost Allowance)
══════════════════════════════════
Net Income (or Loss) from Business → Line 13500 / 13700
```

### Key Lines on the T2125

| Line | Description |
|---|---|
| **Gross income** | Total revenue before any expenses |
| **Cost of goods sold** | Opening inventory + purchases − closing inventory |
| **Gross profit** | Gross income − COGS |
| **Total expenses** | All allowable business expenses (see Section 8.3) |
| **Net income before adjustments** | Gross profit − total expenses |
| **Business-use-of-home expenses** | Home office deduction (calculated on separate section) |
| **CCA** | Capital cost allowance on depreciable assets |
| **Net income (loss)** | Final number → flows to Line 13500 on T1 |

### Where It Goes on the T1

| Business Type | Line on T1 |
|---|---|
| Business income (non-professional) | Line **13500** |
| Professional income (lawyers, doctors, etc.) | Line **13700** |
| Commission income (self-employed) | Line **13900** |
| Farming income | Line **14100** |
| Fishing income | Line **14300** |

## 8.3 Deductible Business Expenses — The Complete List

This is the heart of T2125 work. For every expense your client claims, it must be:

1. **Incurred to earn income** — not personal
2. **Reasonable in amount**
3. **Supported by receipts** — CRA can ask for them

### Current Expenses (Fully Deductible in Year Incurred)

=== "Office & Operations"

    | Expense | Notes |
    |---|---|
    | Advertising and marketing | Website, Google Ads, business cards, social media ads |
    | Office supplies | Paper, printer ink, postage, small tools under $500 |
    | Telephone and utilities | Business portion only — must separate personal use |
    | Internet | Business portion |
    | Software subscriptions | Accounting software, design tools, project management |
    | Bank charges and interest | Business account fees, interest on business loans |
    | Professional fees | Accountant, lawyer, bookkeeper fees paid for the business |
    | Business insurance | Liability, errors & omissions, commercial property |
    | Licenses and dues | Professional association fees, business licenses |
    | Subscriptions | Trade publications, industry databases |

=== "People & Contractors"

    | Expense | Notes |
    |---|---|
    | Salary and wages | Paid to employees of the business |
    | Subcontractor fees | Other self-employed workers hired for specific work |
    | Management fees | If paid to a separate management company |
    | CPP on employee wages | Employer's share of CPP on wages paid |
    | EI on employee wages | Employer's share of EI |

=== "Travel & Meals"

    | Expense | Deductible Amount | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | Business travel (flights, hotels) | 100% | Must be for business, not personal |
    | Vehicle expenses | Business % only | See Section 8.5 |
    | Meals and entertainment | **50%** | CRA hard cap — no exceptions |
    | Client gifts | Up to $50/client/year at 100% | Above $50 treated as entertainment at 50% |

    !!! warning "Meals and entertainment — always 50%"
        Every meal with a client, every hockey game ticket, every dinner — **50% only**. This applies even if the entertainment is 100% for business. CRA has maintained this rule firmly. The 50% limit does not apply to meals provided to employees at a work social event (up to 6 per year).

=== "Rent & Facilities"

    | Expense | Notes |
    |---|---|
    | Office rent | Commercial space rented for business |
    | Business-use-of-home | If working from home — calculated separately (see Section 8.4) |
    | Equipment rental | Rented machinery, tools, vehicles |

## 8.4 Business-Use-of-Home — Self-Employed

Self-employed individuals have **more generous** home office rules than employees (who need a T2200).

### Eligibility

A self-employed person can claim business-use-of-home expenses if:

- The home is their **principal place of business**, OR
- They use the space **exclusively and regularly** to meet clients/customers

### Calculating the Business-Use Percentage

```
Work space area ÷ Total home area = Business-use %
```

!!! tip "Exclusive vs. shared space"
    If the room is **exclusively** used for business (dedicated office, always set up as workspace), the full square footage counts. If the room is shared — a dining room used part-time as an office — CRA expects a reasonable allocation (e.g., hours used for business ÷ total hours in the day × area).

### Eligible Home Expenses for Self-Employed

| Expense | Eligible? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Rent | ✅ | Full rent × business % |
| Mortgage interest | ✅ | Interest portion only — not principal |
| Property taxes | ✅ | × business % |
| Home insurance | ✅ | × business % |
| Electricity, heat, water | ✅ | × business % |
| Internet | ✅ | Business portion |
| Maintenance and repairs | ✅ | × business % — major repairs only |
| **CCA on home** | ⚠️ | **Avoid** — may affect Principal Residence Exemption |
| Mortgage principal | ❌ | Never deductible |

!!! warning "Self-employed get more expenses than employees"
    An employee using the detailed method can claim: rent, utilities, internet. A self-employed person can also claim **mortgage interest, property taxes, and home insurance**. This can make the home office deduction much larger for the self-employed.

### The Home Office Loss Restriction

!!! important "Business-use-of-home cannot create or increase a business loss"
    If the business already has a loss (or net income of zero) before the home office deduction, the home office expenses **cannot make the loss worse**. Unused home office expenses carry forward to future years when the business is profitable.

!!! example "Case — Home Office for a Consultant"
    Maria is a self-employed IT consultant working from a dedicated 200 sq ft office in her 1,000 sq ft owned home. Business use: 20%.

    | Annual Expense | Amount | × 20% | Deductible |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Mortgage interest | $14,400 | 20% | $2,880 |
    | Property taxes | $4,800 | 20% | $960 |
    | Home insurance | $1,800 | 20% | $360 |
    | Electricity & heat | $2,400 | 20% | $480 |
    | Internet | $840 | 20% | $168 |
    | **Total deduction** | | | **$4,848** |

    At a combined federal + Ontario marginal rate of ~35%, this saves Maria approximately **$1,697 in tax** — solely from the home office deduction on a property she would be paying for anyway.

## 8.5 Vehicle Expenses — The Logbook Requirement

Vehicle expenses are one of the most commonly claimed and most commonly audited self-employment deductions. CRA has strict rules.

### Two Methods — Which Applies?

=== "Actual Expense Method"

    Track every vehicle expense and multiply by the business-use percentage.

    **Business-use % = Business kilometres ÷ Total kilometres driven**

    **Eligible expenses:**
    - Fuel
    - Insurance
    - Licence and registration
    - Repairs and maintenance
    - Lease payments (with limits — see below)
    - CCA on the vehicle (with limits — see below)
    - Parking (business trips only)
    - Car washes (reasonable)

    **The logbook is mandatory.** CRA requires a record of:
    - Date of each business trip
    - Destination
    - Business purpose
    - Kilometres driven

    A full logbook must be kept for the **first year** of claiming. In subsequent years, a **3-month sample logbook** may be acceptable if the business-use pattern hasn't changed significantly.

=== "Automobile Allowance (Employees Only)"

    Employees who receive a **per-kilometre allowance** from their employer (e.g., $0.72/km) may not need to claim vehicle expenses separately — the allowance may be non-taxable. This does not apply to the self-employed.

### Luxury Vehicle Limits — 2025

CRA caps the deductible cost of an automobile regardless of actual cost:

| Item | 2025 Limit |
|---|---|
| **CCA cost limit** | **$37,000** (Class 10.1 vehicle) |
| **Monthly lease deduction limit** | **$950/month** |
| **Interest deduction limit** | **$300/month** |

!!! example "Case — Vehicle Expense Claim"
    Kevin is self-employed. He drove 28,000 km total in 2025. His logbook shows 18,000 km for business.

    Business-use %: 18,000 ÷ 28,000 = **64.3%**

    | Annual Expense | Amount | × 64.3% | Deductible |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Fuel | $4,200 | 64.3% | $2,701 |
    | Insurance | $1,900 | 64.3% | $1,222 |
    | Licence/registration | $120 | 64.3% | $77 |
    | Repairs | $650 | 64.3% | $418 |
    | CCA (see Section 8.6) | $3,800 | 64.3% | $2,444 |
    | **Total vehicle deduction** | | | **$6,862** |

## 8.6 Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) — Depreciating Business Assets

Unlike current expenses (which are fully deductible in the year incurred), **capital assets** — equipment, furniture, computers, vehicles — are deducted gradually over time through **CCA (Capital Cost Allowance)**.

### How CCA Works

Assets are grouped into **classes**. Each class has a prescribed depreciation rate.

| Class | Rate | Common Assets |
|---|---|---|
| **Class 8** | 20% | Furniture, equipment, tools > $500, machinery |
| **Class 10** | 30% | General purpose vehicles, taxis |
| **Class 10.1** | 30% | Luxury vehicles (cost > $37,000 — limited) |
| **Class 12** | 100% | Small tools < $500, software (some), linens |
| **Class 14.1** | 5% | Goodwill, customer lists, franchise rights |
| **Class 50** | 55% | Computers, data handling equipment |
| **Class 53** | 50% | Manufacturing/processing equipment (accelerated) |

### The Half-Year Rule

In the **year of acquisition**, only **50% of the normal CCA rate** is allowed — regardless of when in the year the asset was bought.

!!! example "Case — CCA on a New Laptop"
    Sarah buys a new laptop for $2,400 in March 2025 for her consulting business. It belongs to Class 50 (55% rate).

    | Year | Opening UCC | CCA Claimed | Closing UCC |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | 2025 | $2,400 | $2,400 × 55% × 50% = **$660** | $1,740 |
    | 2026 | $1,740 | $1,740 × 55% = **$957** | $783 |
    | 2027 | $783 | $783 × 55% = **$431** | $352 |

    The half-year rule reduces the 2025 claim from $1,320 to $660. CCA is claimed on **Schedule 8** of the T2125.

!!! tip "CCA is optional — claim strategically"
    CCA does not have to be claimed at the full rate. You can claim anywhere from $0 to the maximum. In a low-income year (loss or very low profit), it may be smarter to skip CCA and save it for a year when the client is in a higher tax bracket. This is a real planning opportunity — discuss it with clients.

## 8.7 CPP for the Self-Employed — The Double Contribution

This is one of the most financially significant differences between employees and self-employed individuals.

### How It Works

An **employee** pays CPP at 5.95% — their employer matches it. Together: 11.9% total CPP contribution.

A **self-employed person** pays **both halves**: 11.9% of net self-employment income (between $3,500 and $71,300 for 2025).

| Item | Employee | Self-Employed |
|---|---|---|
| Employee CPP rate | 5.95% | 5.95% (own) |
| Employer CPP rate | 5.95% (employer pays) | 5.95% (also own) |
| **Total CPP** | 5.95% (net cost to individual) | **11.9%** |
| Maximum 2025 contribution | $3,867 | **$7,735** |

### How It's Reported

| Component | Where on T1 |
|---|---|
| CPP contributions on self-employment | **Line 22200** (deduction — 50% of CPP) |
| CPP contributions on self-employment | **Line 31000** (credit — 50% of CPP) |

The self-employed person gets to **deduct 50%** of their CPP (the "employer's half") from net income, and claim a **non-refundable credit on the other 50%** (the "employee's half"). This partially offsets the burden.

!!! example "Case — CPP on Self-Employment"
    Rachel has net self-employment income of $60,000.

    CPP base: $60,000 − $3,500 = $56,500
    Total CPP: $56,500 × 11.9% = **$6,724**

    | Component | Amount | Treatment |
    |---|---|---|
    | Employer half (50%) | $3,362 | **Deducted** from net income (Line 22200) |
    | Employee half (50%) | $3,362 | **Credit** at 15% = $504 off tax (Line 31000) |

    Rachel's actual after-tax cost of CPP: $6,724 − deduction savings (~$1,177 at 35%) − credit ($504) = approximately **$5,043** net.

## 8.8 GST/HST — Awareness Level

As a tax preparer, you won't be filing GST/HST returns for clients (that's a separate engagement), but you must understand the basics to serve self-employed clients properly.

### Registration Threshold

A self-employed person **must register for GST/HST** once their taxable revenues exceed **$30,000 in any single calendar quarter or over 4 consecutive quarters**.

| Revenue | Required to Register? |
|---|---|
| Under $30,000/year | No (small supplier — optional) |
| $30,000 or more | **Yes — mandatory** |

!!! warning "The $30,000 threshold is cumulative"
    A client earning $2,500/month hits $30,000 in month 12. The moment they exceed $30,000, they must register within 29 days and start charging GST/HST on sales after registration. Many new self-employed clients miss this and face back-assessments.

### Input Tax Credits (ITCs)

Registered businesses can claim **Input Tax Credits** — recovering the GST/HST they paid on business expenses. This is a separate filing (GST/HST return) but affects how you record expenses on the T2125.

!!! tip "GST/HST-registered clients: expenses are net of ITCs"
    If your client is GST/HST registered, the business expenses on their T2125 should be the **net amount excluding the HST they recovered** through ITCs. A non-registered client claims the full HST-inclusive amount. Always ask: "Are you registered for HST?"

## 8.9 Common Self-Employment Audit Triggers

Self-employment returns are audited more frequently than T4 employment returns. Know what CRA looks for:

| Trigger | Why CRA Notices |
|---|---|
| Large home office claims relative to income | 40%+ of income as home office = unusual |
| 100% vehicle business use | CRA is skeptical — almost always has some personal use |
| Consistent annual business losses (3+ years) | Suggests hobby, not business — "reasonable expectation of profit" test |
| Round number expenses | $5,000 advertising, $3,000 meals — looks estimated, not tracked |
| Meals and entertainment at 100% | Should always be 50% — software should catch this |
| Large "other expenses" without detail | Vague line items invite scrutiny |
| Cash-based businesses | Restaurant, contractors, hair salons — higher scrutiny |
| GST/HST income vs. T2125 income mismatch | CRA compares HST returns to income tax returns |

!!! success "Your job: keep your clients audit-ready"
    Encourage every self-employed client to keep receipts for everything, maintain a proper logbook, and use accounting software. A client who can produce organized records in a CRA review is a client who keeps you as their preparer for life. A client who can't produce records is a client who will blame you when they get reassessed.

## 8.10 Case Studies

### Case A — The Freelance Graphic Designer

**Client:** James, 32, works full-time as a freelance designer from home.

**Revenue:** $78,000 in client invoices
**Expenses:**

| Expense | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Adobe Creative Cloud | $600 | 100% business |
| New monitor and tablet | $2,200 | Class 50 (55% CCA) |
| Home office (dedicated 180 sq ft of 900 sq ft rental) | 20% of $18,000 rent + $1,200 utilities | = $3,840 |
| Internet | $720 | 80% business = $576 |
| Professional development course | $1,200 | Business education |
| Advertising (Instagram ads, website) | $1,800 | 100% business |
| Accounting fee (your fee!) | $400 | 100% business |
| Meals with clients (receipts) | $1,400 | **50%** = $700 |
| **Total current expenses** | | **$9,116** |
| CCA on monitor/tablet ($2,200 × 55% × 50%) | | **$605** |

**T2125 Calculation:**

```
Gross Income:           $78,000
− Business expenses:    ($9,116)
− Home office:          ($3,840)
− CCA:                    ($605)
══════════════════════
Net Business Income:    $64,439  → Line 13500
```

**CPP on net income:**
$64,439 − $3,500 = $60,939 × 11.9% = **$7,252**
Deduction (50%): $3,626 → Line 22200
Credit (50%): $3,626 × 15% = $544 → Line 31000

After CPP deduction, net income on T1: $64,439 − $3,626 = **$60,813**

### Case B — The Side-Hustle Problem

**Client:** Natalie earns $85,000 from her T4 job. She also sells handmade jewelry on Etsy. Revenue from Etsy: $4,200. She spent $1,800 on materials and $300 on packaging/shipping supplies.

**Is this a business or a hobby?**

CRA's "reasonable expectation of profit" test considers: Does she intend to profit? Has she profited in prior years? Is there an element of personal enjoyment?

Jewelry making could go either way. Since she has clear revenue and expenses and is trying to profit, it qualifies as a business.

**T2125 for Etsy shop:**

```
Gross Income:     $4,200
− Materials:     ($1,800)
− Supplies:        ($300)
══════════════════
Net Income:       $2,100  → Line 13500
```

CPP on $2,100: ($2,100 − $3,500) = **below the $3,500 basic exemption — no CPP owing**

GST/HST: Total revenues $4,200 — under $30,000 threshold, no registration required.

!!! tip "Side-hustle clients are a growth market"
    Gig economy workers, Etsy sellers, content creators, Airbnb hosts — these clients are underserved and often don't realize they need to report this income. They're also often young people who will grow their income over time. Serving them well now builds your client base for the next decade.

### Case C — The Business Loss

**Client:** Patricia opened a home-based coaching business in 2025. Revenue: $8,500. Expenses: $14,200 (coaching certification, website, marketing, home office).

**Net business loss:** $8,500 − $14,200 = **($5,700)**

A business loss can be applied against **other income** on the T1 — including her T4 employment income of $62,000.

```
Employment income (T4):   $62,000
Net business loss:        ($5,700)
══════════════════════════
Net Income:               $56,300
```

The business loss reduces her net income and saves her approximately **$1,995 in tax** (at a combined ~35% rate). Legitimate first-year business losses are a real tax benefit — but CRA will scrutinize losses in year 3+ if the business never turns profitable.

## 8.11 Week 8 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - Self-employed income is reported on **Form T2125** → net income flows to **Line 13500**
    - The employee vs. self-employed distinction is determined by a **4-factor test** (control, tools, risk, integration)
    - Business expenses must be **incurred to earn income**, reasonable, and supported by receipts
    - **Meals and entertainment: always 50%** — no exceptions for business entertainment
    - Home office for self-employed: includes mortgage interest, property taxes, and insurance — more generous than employees
    - **Vehicle logbook is mandatory** — business km ÷ total km = business-use %
    - Capital assets are depreciated through **CCA** — classes, rates, and the half-year rule
    - CCA is optional — claim it strategically based on the client's tax bracket each year
    - Self-employed individuals pay **both halves of CPP (11.9%)** — employer half is deductible (Line 22200), employee half gets a credit (Line 31000)
    - Register for **GST/HST** once revenues exceed **$30,000**

## ✅ Week 8 Quiz

When you're ready, ask Claude: **"Quiz me on Week 8"** for an interactive test.

??? question "1. A client claims 100% business use of their vehicle. What is your response?"
    Treat it with scepticism — almost no vehicle is used 100% for business. Even driving from home to a first client of the day may be considered personal commuting by CRA. Ask the client to review their logbook carefully and verify that personal use (evenings, weekends, vacations) is genuinely zero. If they cannot demonstrate 100% business use with a proper logbook, reduce the claim to a defensible percentage. A 100% vehicle claim is one of CRA's top audit triggers.

??? question "2. A self-employed client earned $55,000 in net business income. Calculate their total CPP contribution, the deductible portion, and the credit portion."
    - CPP base: $55,000 − $3,500 = $51,500
    - Total CPP: $51,500 × 11.9% = **$6,129**
    - Deductible half (Line 22200): $6,129 ÷ 2 = **$3,065** (reduces net income)
    - Credit half (Line 31000): $3,065 × 15% = **$460** (reduces federal tax)

??? question "3. Your client bought a $3,600 camera for their photography business in June 2025. It belongs to Class 8 (20%). How much CCA can they claim in 2025?"
    Half-year rule applies in the year of acquisition: $3,600 × 20% × 50% = **$360** in 2025. In 2026, the full rate applies on the remaining UCC: ($3,600 − $360) × 20% = **$648**.

??? question "4. What is the meals and entertainment deduction limit and does any exception apply?"
    **50% of actual costs** — this is a hard CRA limit on all meals and entertainment expenses incurred to earn business income. The main exception is meals provided to employees at employer-hosted social events (up to 6 per year), which can be fully deductible. Client meals, hockey tickets, restaurant dinners — always 50%, no exceptions.

??? question "5. A self-employed client's home office expenses total $6,200 but her net business income before the home office deduction is only $3,800. How much home office can she deduct and what happens to the rest?"
    She can deduct **$3,800** — the home office deduction cannot create or increase a business loss. The remaining **$2,400** carries forward to future years when the business has sufficient profit to absorb it. This is tracked on Schedule T2125 and carried forward until used.

??? question "6. When must a self-employed person register for GST/HST?"
    When taxable revenues exceed **$30,000** in a single calendar quarter or in four consecutive calendar quarters. Once the threshold is crossed, registration is mandatory within 29 days. Failing to register means CRA can assess HST owing on all sales above the threshold — a significant retroactive liability. Clients approaching $30,000 should be advised to register proactively.

??? question "7. What is the difference between a capital expense and a current expense? Give one example of each."
    A **current expense** is consumed in the year and fully deductible in that year (e.g., office supplies, rent, advertising). A **capital expense** provides benefit over multiple years and must be depreciated through CCA (e.g., buying a laptop, purchasing equipment, a vehicle). The distinction matters because incorrectly expensing a capital item in full provides too large a deduction in year one and triggers a CRA adjustment.
