!!! info "Learning Objectives"

    - Handle the client who hasn't filed in 3, 5, or 10 years — calmly and without judgment
    - Communicate a CRA review letter to a panicked client with authority and reassurance
    - Navigate the grief of an estate client who is also an executor managing a death
    - Handle the client who is angry, crying, or threatening — professionally and firmly

## 24.1 The Psychology of Tax Clients — Why This Business Is Emotional

Tax returns touch the most sensitive dimensions of a person's life: how much they earn, what they spend, whether they're doing financially better or worse than last year, whether they owe money they don't have, and whether they made good decisions.

Most clients arrive with one of these emotional states:

| Client State | What It Looks Like | What They Need |
|---|---|---|
| **Anxious** | "I haven't filed in three years — am I in trouble?" | Reassurance + clear action plan |
| **Embarrassed** | Won't mention the cash income, reluctant to admit they don't understand | Non-judgment + patient explanation |
| **Angry** | "CRA is robbing me" / "My last preparer was useless" | Acknowledgment + competence demonstration |
| **Grieving** | Settling a deceased spouse's estate, never handled finances alone | Compassion + patient step-by-step guidance |
| **Overwhelmed** | New immigrant, gig worker with 6 platforms, first business | Simplification + confidence building |
| **Entitled** | "I pay a lot of taxes so I should get a big refund" | Gentle reality check + education |
| **Trusting** | "Whatever you think is best" | Responsibility + documentation |

Your professional response to each of these states is different from how you handle the tax forms. But it is equally important — possibly more so. **Clients don't leave because you filed their return correctly. They leave because they felt dismissed, confused, or judged.**

## 24.2 Delivering Bad News — The Skill Most Preparers Fear

### The Large Balance Owing

Nothing creates more anxiety in a client meeting than showing a significant balance owing. Many preparers avoid the conversation — they email the result or mumble through it. That is the worst approach. Own the conversation.

### The PREP Framework

Use this framework for every difficult result delivery:

```
P — Prepare the client before the number
R — Reason (explain why)
E — Empathize (acknowledge the impact)
P — Plan (what happens next)
```

!!! example "Case — Delivering a $14,000 Balance Owing"
    **Client:** Khalid, UAE executive who arrived in March. He has $158,400 in income (salary + foreign income). His employer withheld $42,000 in income tax.

    **The result:** Combined federal and Ontario tax of approximately $61,300. Balance owing: **$19,300**.

    **The wrong approach:** "So your total tax comes to $61,300 and you've already paid $42,000 through payroll, so you owe CRA $19,300 by April 30."

    *(Khalid stares. He came from a zero-tax country. He expected a refund. He is now wondering if he made a mistake moving to Canada.)*

    **The PREP approach:**

    **Prepare:** *"Khalid, before I show you the final number, I want to walk you through what happened this year so the result makes sense. Is that okay?"*

    **Reason:** *"Your employment income was $133,000 for 10 months — your employer withheld tax on that correctly. But you also had $25,000 in income from your UAE bank accounts and rental property during your Canadian residency period. Those sources have no withholding. So there's a gap between what was withheld and what's actually owed on your full income. Additionally, because you arrived in March, your personal credits were pro-rated — you only get partial credit for the months you were resident."*

    **Empathize:** *"I know this is a significant amount, and I know it's a big adjustment from what you were used to in the UAE. This is common for executives who arrive mid-year with foreign income — you're absolutely not alone in this situation."*

    **Plan:** *"Here's what we do. The $19,300 is due April 30. Let's talk about how to handle that. For next year, we set up quarterly instalments so this doesn't happen again — I'll calculate the amounts today. We also want to maximize your RRSP — you're generating significant room, and a $25,000 RRSP contribution would save you approximately $11,500 in combined tax next year. So while this year is a surprise, next year we can cut your bill dramatically with planning."*

    **Result:** Khalid understands. He's not happy — but he's not blindsided. He has a plan. He comes back next year.

### The "They Expected a Refund but Owe Money" Conversation

This is the most common bad news scenario. A client who always got a refund now owes $2,400.

**The most likely causes (explain each one):**
1. New income source (side business, rental, investment income) with no withholding
2. Reduced deductions (paid off student loan, RRSP room changed)
3. Employer adjusted payroll withholding (common after a raise)
4. Spousal income changed (reduced credits)
5. Government benefit repayment (OAS clawback if income rose)

**Your script:** *"I want to explain why the result is different from what you may have expected. [Identify the specific cause.] The good news is that this isn't a mistake — it's a reflection of [the change]. Here's what we can do going forward to avoid this next year..."*

The word "mistake" is poison. Avoid it. The word "explain" is gold. Use it.

## 24.3 The Unfiled Years Client — The Most Common "Crisis" You'll Face

Clients who haven't filed for 2, 3, 5, or even 10 years are not rare. They arrive in your office embarrassed, afraid, and often convinced they are about to be arrested. Almost none of them are actually in criminal jeopardy. Almost all of them can be helped.

### The Reality of Unfiled Returns

**What CRA actually does (in order of escalation):**

1. **Sends reminder letters** — multiple, over months and years
2. **Files a "notional assessment"** — estimates your income and sends a bill (often wildly inaccurate)
3. **Contacts you directly** (phone, certified mail)
4. **Applies penalties** to each year's balance owing
5. **Refers to collections** for outstanding balances
6. **Legal action** (garnishment, liens, frozen accounts) — for significant ongoing non-compliance
7. **Criminal prosecution** — reserved for deliberate, long-term evasion with fraud. Extremely rare for ordinary unfiled returns.

**The truth you tell every "unfiled" client:** *"You're not in as much trouble as you think. The vast majority of people who haven't filed simply got behind, not because they were trying to evade tax. CRA's goal is to get returns filed and collect what's owed — not to prosecute people who fell behind. Let's find out exactly where things stand and make a plan."*

### The Assessment Process for Unfiled Years

**Step 1 — Establish the years:**
"When did you last file a return? Do you have your last NOA?"

**Step 2 — Access CRA My Account:**
With the client's authorization, pull up all years on record. CRA My Account shows:
- Years filed
- Years with notional assessments
- Amounts owing (with interest accrued)
- Benefits potentially missed

**Step 3 — Gather documents:**
For each unfiled year, you need: T-slips (request from CRA My Account — usually available for 10 years back), any receipts for deductions still available, prior NOAs if existing.

**Step 4 — Calculate the realistic liability:**
After proper filing with legitimate deductions, the actual tax owing is almost always significantly less than CRA's notional assessment.

**Step 5 — Apply for taxpayer relief:**
CRA's **Taxpayer Relief Program (Form RC4288)** allows waiving or cancelling penalties and interest for clients who have a valid reason for non-compliance (financial hardship, illness, disaster, reasonable reliance on faulty advice). Not automatic — but worth pursuing for clients with legitimate circumstances.

**Step 6 — Consider VDP (if undeclared income):**
If the unfiled returns involve unreported income (not just failure to file), the Voluntary Disclosure Program may be available.

!!! example "Case — Patricia Hasn't Filed in 4 Years"
    Patricia, 44, sits down across from you. She's visibly nervous. "I haven't filed since 2021. I know it's bad. I kept meaning to and then every year I just... didn't."

    Her background: She worked as a nurse from 2021–2024, T4 only. No self-employment, no rental income, no significant investments. She just didn't file.

    **Your assessment:** Low complexity. Four years of T4 employment returns. CRA has all her T-slips. She likely has significant refunds from withheld tax and accumulated GST/HST credits and OTB payments she's never received.

    **Your response:**

    *"Patricia, thank you for coming in. Here's the good news: your situation is much more manageable than you're probably imagining. You have employment income from a T4 — that's the simplest possible situation. All your slips are on file with CRA. We can pull all four years of T-slips through CRA's system today and start preparing the returns right now.*

    *Based on what you've told me, it's very likely you have refunds from these years — your employer was withholding tax all along. You've also missed four years of GST/HST credits and Ontario Trillium Benefit payments. Those can be recalculated and paid retroactively once the returns are filed.*

    *The only penalty that applies is a late-filing penalty if there was a balance owing in any year. If you got refunds each year — and most nurses at your income with standard deductions do — there are no late-filing penalties at all.*

    *Let's start with 2021 and work forward. I'll prepare all four returns. By the time we're done, you'll be fully current, and I'd estimate there's a reasonable chance CRA owes you money rather than the other way around."*

    Patricia leaves 2 hours later with four returns prepared and queued for filing. Her combined refunds from 2021–2024: **$4,840**. Her GST/HST and OTB retroactive payments: **$3,200**.

    She came in convinced she was in serious trouble. She left with $8,040 she didn't know she had.

    She will tell every person she knows about you.

## 24.4 The CRA Review Letter — Managing Client Panic

You've filed the return. Six weeks later, your phone rings. "I got a letter from CRA. Am I being audited? Am I in trouble? Should I call a lawyer?"

**The first thing you do: Listen without interrupting.** Let them finish. Don't answer before you know what the letter actually says.

**The second thing you do: Get the actual letter.** Ask them to read it to you, photograph it, or bring it in. The client's description of CRA correspondence is almost always more frightening than the correspondence itself.

### The Graduated Response Framework

=== "Level 1 — Information Request"

    **What it says:** "We need documentation for [specific claim]."

    **What you say to the client:** *"This is completely routine. CRA reviews a percentage of returns every year to verify specific claims. They're asking for receipts for your medical expenses. This is not an audit — it's a documentation request. I've seen hundreds of these. Here's what we do: I'll prepare a professional response with all the documentation. You don't need to call them or worry. This will be resolved in 4–6 weeks."*

    **Tone:** Calm, confident, in control. The client needs to feel that you have this handled — because you do.

=== "Level 2 — Proposed Reassessment"

    **What it says:** "We propose to reassess your return. You have 30 days to respond."

    **What you say to the client:** *"CRA has reviewed something on your return and they're proposing a change. This is not a final decision — it's a proposal. You have 30 days to respond with your position and documentation. I'll prepare a detailed response. If our documentation is solid, most proposed reassessments are resolved in the client's favour. Let me review exactly what they're questioning."*

    **Tone:** Serious but not alarmed. This requires work — let the client know you're on it.

=== "Level 3 — Formal Reassessment"

    **What it says:** A Notice of Reassessment has been issued.

    **What you say:** *"CRA has completed their review and issued a formal reassessment. This means they've made a change to your return. You now have 90 days to file a Notice of Objection if you disagree with their position. Let me review the reassessment against our filed return and the documentation we have. I'll give you my assessment within [timeframe] on whether we should object or whether the reassessment is appropriate."*

    **Tone:** Professional problem-solver. Your client needs to make an informed decision about objecting — they need your clear recommendation.

!!! example "Case — The Panicked Call About Moving Expenses"
    **Client:** James calls. "I got a letter from CRA. They say they want documentation for my moving expenses. I claimed $22,000. They're asking for receipts. What do I do?"

    **Your response:** *"Perfect — I have all those documents right here in your file. This is a standard documentation request. I'll prepare a response letter with organized tabs: your professional movers invoice, the real estate agent commission on your old home, the mortgage break penalty from your old bank, your hotel receipts for the transition period, and the lease termination letter. Every receipt is verified. I'll have the response package sent to them by next week.*

    *James — this is going to be fine. Every expense we claimed is legitimate and documented. The review process exists to verify that, and we can verify it completely. You don't need to do anything except let me handle it. I'll keep you posted when we receive their response."*

    **Result:** James stops panicking. He trusts you. The review resolves in his favour 5 weeks later. He tells his colleagues at work who also relocated that year.

## 24.5 The Estate Client — Grief, Complexity, and Compassion

Preparing a deceased person's terminal return is one of the most emotionally charged situations in tax preparation. The executor (often a grieving spouse, adult child, or sibling) is managing paperwork while also managing grief — often simultaneously.

### How to Approach the Estate Intake

**Before the appointment:** Send a simple, empathetic email:

*"Thank you for reaching out. I understand this is a difficult time and I want to make the tax filing process as straightforward as possible for you. Please bring whatever documents you have — we'll work through what's needed together. There's no need to have everything perfect before we meet."*

This pre-meeting communication signals that you are not going to overwhelm them.

**At the appointment:** The first 5 minutes are not about taxes.

*"Before we look at any documents — how are you doing? How is your family managing?"*

Listen. Really listen. Don't steer back to the paperwork until the client signals they're ready.

### The Estate Executor Conversation

Many executors have no idea what their obligations are. Your job is to explain the framework calmly and step by step.

!!! example "Case — Helen's Husband Passed in August"
    Helen, 68, calls you. Her husband Robert passed away in August after a short illness. She has never handled their taxes — Robert always did everything.

    She is overwhelmed, exhausted, and doesn't know where to start.

    **Your first call with Helen:**

    *"Helen, I'm so sorry for your loss. I want you to know that we can handle all of this together, one step at a time. You don't need to understand everything today — I'll explain each piece when we get to it. Can I ask a few simple questions first to help me understand the situation?"*

    [Gather basic information: date of death, whether there's a will, who the executor is, approximate income sources]

    *"Here's what we're going to do. Robert's final tax return covers January 1 to August [date of death]. I'll prepare that. You may also need to file prior years if they weren't filed. After the final return is assessed, we'll apply to CRA for a clearance certificate before distributing any estate assets — that protects you personally as executor. I'll walk you through each step. For now, the only thing you need to do is gather the T-slips — I'll help you request anything that's missing from CRA."*

    **The pace:** Slow. Patient. Repeat things. Helen is grieving and information doesn't land the same way as it would in a regular appointment.

    **What you never do:** Hand Helen a checklist of 25 things she needs to do before you can start. She will feel overwhelmed and disappear. Meet her where she is.

### Delivering the Terminal Return Tax Bill

In many estate situations, the terminal return generates a significant tax bill — particularly when there is a large RRSP with no surviving spouse. You are delivering difficult news to someone already under enormous stress.

**The conversation structure:**

1. **Prepare them before the number:** *"Robert's final return has a larger tax bill than a typical year, and I want to explain why before I tell you the amount."*

2. **Explain the mechanism:** *"When someone passes away with an RRSP and there's no surviving spouse to receive it, CRA treats the entire RRSP as income in the final year. Robert's RRSP was $185,000. This adds to his other income for the year — pension, CPP, OAS — which is already taxed. The combined result is a significant tax bill."*

3. **Contextualize it:** *"This is not unusual. Many estates face this. It's why financial advisors recommend drawing down RRSPs gradually before death — but that conversation happens years in advance. There's nothing wrong that was done here."*

4. **Give them time:** *"The bill is $52,000. I know that's a lot to absorb. Take a moment. Do you have any questions before we talk about next steps?"*

5. **Focus on what's actionable:** *"The estate has the assets to cover this. We'll pay CRA from the estate before distributing to the beneficiaries. The clearance certificate process I mentioned protects you as executor throughout this."*

## 24.6 The Angry Client — Maintaining Authority Without Escalating

Angry clients are a reality of any service profession. Tax adds an extra layer of emotional charge. You will encounter clients who:
- Believe CRA is a corrupt institution designed to steal from honest people
- Are furious about a balance owing they didn't expect
- Are convinced the prior preparer "stole" from them
- Are in financial distress and terrified

### The Four-Step De-escalation Method

**Step 1 — Let them speak.**
Do not interrupt. Do not defend yourself. Do not correct them mid-sentence. Let them finish completely. Most anger deflates when it runs out of fuel. If you interrupt to defend yourself, you add fuel.

**Step 2 — Acknowledge.**
Not agreement — acknowledgment. *"I hear you. That's incredibly frustrating. I can see why you feel that way."* This is not capitulation. It is basic human connection.

**Step 3 — Redirect to problem-solving.**
*"Can we look at this together? I want to understand exactly what happened and figure out what we can do."* Shift from emotion to action.

**Step 4 — Establish boundaries if needed.**
If the client becomes verbally abusive: *"I want to help you, and I will. But I need us to be able to have a productive conversation. Can we take a moment and then look at this together?"*

If the behaviour continues: *"I think it might help to take a short break and reconnect when you're ready. I'm going to step out for five minutes."*

You are never required to endure personal abuse. Professionalism does not mean submission.

!!! example "Case — The Client Who 'Got Screwed by CRA'"
    **Client:** David calls in March. "Your return you filed last year is why I owe $4,000 to CRA now. You did something wrong. This is your fault. I want my money back."

    **What actually happened:** CRA reassessed his return to add a T5 slip from his investment account that he forgot to provide. The $4,000 is the tax on that investment income.

    **The conversation:**

    *"David, thank you for calling. I can hear how frustrated you are, and I want to address this directly. Can you tell me what the CRA letter says — specifically what income they added?"*

    [David reads the letter: T5 interest income of $13,800 from his investment account was not on his filed return.]

    *"Okay. I can explain exactly what happened. When we prepared your return, I verified all slips showing in CRA My Account at that time — and this T5 was not yet available. T5 slips from investment accounts sometimes arrive late or get filed by the institution after our filing date. This is not an error in your return — it's a slip that was missing when we filed.*

    *Here's the thing: CRA received this T5 from your investment company. It was always going to show up. If we had filed later, we would have caught it. But the income is real — the $13,800 in interest did happen, and the tax on it is owed. I can file a T1-ADJ to include it properly and avoid any penalties for late reporting.*

    *What I'd suggest: let me pull up the T5 and we'll confirm the amounts, then I'll handle the T1-ADJ at no additional charge. The $4,000 in tax is owed regardless — that's the tax on income you earned. But I want to make sure it's filed correctly and that we avoid any additional penalties."*

    **Key moves:**
    - No defensiveness
    - Factual explanation of what happened
    - Clear delineation of what was an error (nothing — the slip was late) vs. what is owed (the tax on real income)
    - Offer to fix the process issue at no charge
    - Acknowledge his frustration without accepting false blame

## 24.7 The Client in Financial Distress

Sometimes a client owes a balance they genuinely cannot pay. They are afraid. They may be in financial crisis. This is a human moment as much as a tax moment.

### What to Tell a Client Who Can't Pay

**Immediate reassurance:** *"CRA will not arrest you for owing money. Not paying by the deadline generates interest — not criminal charges. Let's talk about your options."*

**The three legitimate options for a client who can't pay:**

=== "Option 1 — Installment Agreement"

    CRA has a formal payment arrangement program. A client can call CRA's collections line and negotiate a monthly payment plan.

    **How it works:** The client proposes a payment amount they can sustain. CRA typically accepts reasonable proposals. Interest continues to accrue on the outstanding balance during the plan — but the collection actions (garnishment, liens) are suspended.

    **Your role:** Prepare the return accurately, file it on time (penalty avoidance), and advise the client to call CRA at 1-800-959-8281 to discuss payment arrangements.

=== "Option 2 — Taxpayer Relief Application"

    If the client is in severe financial hardship (loss of job, illness, disability, natural disaster), they can apply for relief from penalties and interest through CRA's Taxpayer Relief Program (Form RC4288).

    CRA can cancel or waive:
    - Late-filing penalties
    - Failure-to-remit penalties
    - Interest (in exceptional circumstances)

    **Important:** This does not forgive the underlying tax — only the penalties and interest. And CRA is not obligated to grant relief — it is discretionary.

=== "Option 3 — Consumer Proposal / Bankruptcy"

    In severe cases where the tax debt is part of a broader financial crisis, a Licensed Insolvency Trustee (LIT) can negotiate a consumer proposal — paying a fraction of the total debt (including CRA debt) over time, with the balance forgiven.

    CRA is a creditor like any other in a consumer proposal. Tax debts are included.

    **Your role:** This is outside your scope. Refer to a Licensed Insolvency Trustee. But knowing it exists — and telling a desperate client that there are options — is the difference between a client who feels hopeless and one who has a path forward.

!!! example "Case — The Self-Employed Client Who Owes $22,000"
    **Client:** Kevin runs a small landscaping business. His first full year of proper bookkeeping reveals he owes $22,000 in combined federal and Ontario tax plus CPP. He makes $60,000 net but lives paycheck to paycheck.

    *"Kevin, I know $22,000 feels impossible right now. Let me put this in perspective and then give you a plan.*

    *First — you're not going to jail. Not paying CRA means interest accumulates and eventually collection actions, but you have options before we get anywhere near that.*

    *Here's what I recommend: File the return today — on time. Every day after April 30 you don't file, you're adding a 5% penalty plus 1% per month to whatever you owe. That's money you don't have to pay. So we file.*

    *Then, call CRA after we file and tell them you have a balance and you want to set up a payment plan. Say you can pay $800/month. They will almost certainly accept that. On $22,000 at $800/month, you're paid off in about 28 months with interest — not ideal, but manageable.*

    *For next year, we're going to set up quarterly installments so this never surprises you again. I'll calculate the quarterly amount — roughly $5,500/quarter. If you make those payments starting June 15, next April 30 you'll owe nothing.*

    *The situation is hard, but it's not hopeless. You have a clear plan."*

    Kevin files on time. He calls CRA and sets up a plan. He pays it off in 26 months. He never misses a quarterly installment again. He sends you four referrals that year because you were the first person who gave him a path instead of more fear.

## 24.8 The Client Who Reveals Something Sensitive

Tax returns sometimes surface life circumstances that clients haven't told anyone else. A disability. A mental health diagnosis. A marriage breakdown. A gambling addiction that explains why there's no money despite significant income.

### How to Handle Sensitive Disclosures

**The principle:** Your job is tax preparation, not therapy or judgment. But your humanity matters.

**What to do when a client discloses something sensitive:**

1. **Acknowledge briefly and matter-of-factly.** *"Thank you for telling me — that helps me understand your situation better."*

2. **Assess the tax relevance.** Many disclosures have tax implications: a disability may mean a DTC application. A marriage breakdown changes filing status, spousal amounts, and childcare rules. A gambling addiction affecting financial capacity may support a Taxpayer Relief application.

3. **Offer help within your scope.** *"If you'd like, I can apply for the Disability Tax Credit with CRA — that's a straightforward process and it could save you meaningful money each year."*

4. **Don't probe beyond what's relevant.** You don't need to know the full story of the marriage breakdown to determine that the client is now separated. Ask only what you need for the return.

5. **Never share what they've told you.** Client confidentiality is absolute unless legally required otherwise. Never reference a client's personal circumstances in any conversation with anyone — including other clients, your accountant, or family members.

### Recognizing Mental Health Signals

Occasionally a client will display signs that suggest they are struggling beyond normal tax stress:

- Extreme distress disproportionate to the tax issue (crying persistently, expressing hopelessness)
- Statements that suggest financial despair ("I don't know how I'll go on")
- References to self-harm in the context of financial stress

**How to respond:**

*"What you're describing sounds like it's about more than taxes. I want to make sure you're okay. Is there someone you can talk to — a friend, family member, or a counsellor? If you're having thoughts of harming yourself, please call 988 [Canada Suicide Crisis Helpline] — they're available 24/7."*

Then — complete the tax return. Your professional support continues. You don't abandon a vulnerable client. But you are not a mental health professional and it is appropriate to direct them to the right resource.

## 24.9 Handling Prior Preparer Errors

A new client arrives and their prior returns contain errors. You discover them during intake. This is delicate — you need to protect the client's interests without appearing to disparage a colleague or create unnecessary drama.

### The Three-Step Approach

**Step 1 — Identify specifically and factually.**

*"I want to flag something I noticed in your 2022 and 2023 returns. In both years, the investment management fees on your non-registered account weren't claimed on Line 22100. Based on what you've told me about the account size, those fees were approximately $4,200/year — which would have been worth about $1,900 in combined tax credits each year."*

Be specific. Be factual. No characterization of the prior preparer's competence or intent.

**Step 2 — Quantify the opportunity.**

*"In total, looking at 2021, 2022, and 2023, the unclaimed fees represent approximately $5,700 in credits that weren't taken. We can file T1-ADJs for each of those years — going back 10 years is allowed. You'd receive reassessments returning approximately $5,700 in combined federal and Ontario credit plus CRA interest on the overpayments."*

**Step 3 — Offer to act — but let the client decide.**

*"It's your call whether you want to pursue the amendments. If you do, I'll prepare them. They're not complex — it's one additional line on each return. If you'd prefer to just move forward from this year, that's also completely reasonable."*

**What you never say:**
- "Your last preparer made a terrible mistake"
- "I can't believe someone charged you for that return"
- "That's malpractice"

You don't know what the prior preparer knew, what documents the client provided, or what discussions happened. Stay in your lane: identify, quantify, offer solutions. Let the client draw their own conclusions.

!!! example "Case — Five Years of Missed Carrying Charges"
    **Client:** Robert, 58, moves to you from another preparer. During your review of prior returns he shares, you identify that his investment advisory fee ($4,800/year on a $400,000 non-registered portfolio) was never claimed on Line 22100.

    Five years of unclaimed fees: $4,800 × 5 = $24,000.
    At his ~43% combined marginal rate: approximately **$10,320 in missed credits.**

    **Your conversation:**

    *"Robert, I want to show you something I found in reviewing your prior returns. Your investment management fee — the fee you pay [Advisor name] on your non-registered portfolio — is fully deductible on your tax return each year. Over the past five years, that fee was approximately $4,800/year, and it wasn't claimed. I can file amendments going back five years, which would recover approximately $10,320 in combined tax credits.*

    *I don't know why it wasn't claimed — it may not have come up in the intake conversation with your previous preparer. It's a commonly missed deduction. But the good news is we can fix it.*

    *Would you like me to proceed with the amendments?"*

    Robert says yes. You file five T1-ADJs. CRA issues five revised NOAs. Robert receives $10,320 in combined refunds plus interest.

    Robert's reaction: not anger at the prior preparer (at least not directed at you) — but overwhelming gratitude to you. *"No one has ever done that for me before."*

    He refers his business partner, his brother, and two colleagues within the month.

## 24.10 The Conversation Frameworks — Scripts for Every Difficult Moment

These are the exact scripts you'll reach for in your first few years. Read them. Practice them. They will feel awkward at first and natural within one season.

### "Your return won't be as large a refund as last year"

*"Before I show you the result, I want to explain what changed from last year. [Explain the specific reason.] The bottom line is your refund this year is $[amount], compared to $[last year amount]. This is [the normal result / a change driven by X]. Here's what we can do for next year to optimize..."*

### "CRA has selected your return for review"

*"I want to reassure you — this is routine. CRA reviews a percentage of returns every year. They're asking about [specific claim]. We have full documentation for this, and I'll prepare a professional response that addresses exactly what they're asking. You don't need to contact CRA yourself — I'll handle it. Most reviews like this resolve in 4–8 weeks."*

### "You owe more than you can pay"

*"I understand this is difficult. The important thing is: file on time to avoid late-filing penalties on top of what you owe. Then contact CRA to set up a payment arrangement — they have a formal program for exactly this situation. I'll give you the number and what to say. Here's the plan..."*

### "I found errors in your prior returns"

*"I noticed something in reviewing your prior returns that I want to bring to your attention. In [year(s)], [specific deduction/credit] wasn't claimed. Based on your situation, this represents approximately [amount] in recoverable credits. I can file amendments — it's your decision whether to pursue them. What questions do you have?"*

### "Your situation requires a CPA / tax lawyer"

*"I want to be completely transparent with you: this situation involves [corporate restructuring / LCGE claim / T2 corporate return / tax court appeal / criminal tax matter] that goes beyond personal tax preparation. I can continue to prepare your personal T1, but for [the specific issue], you need a CPA / tax lawyer who specializes in this. I have a recommendation — [name, contact]. I'll stay involved on the personal return side."*

### "I can't prepare this return — it would require me to file something incorrect"

*"I need to be honest with you. What you're asking me to include [expense/deduction/income adjustment] isn't something I'm able to file. It would require reporting information that isn't accurate, and that creates legal risk for both of us. I want to help you reduce your tax legally — let me focus on everything that is legitimately available. If that's not what you're looking for, I understand, but I can't prepare a return that contains information I know to be inaccurate."*

## 24.11 The Professional Referral — Knowing Your Limits With Grace

One of the marks of a truly professional preparer is knowing when to refer — and doing it in a way that maintains the client's confidence and trust rather than making them feel abandoned.

### When to Refer (And to Whom)

| Situation | Refer To |
|---|---|
| T2 corporate return | CPA or certified corporate tax accountant |
| Corporate restructuring, income splitting optimization | CPA specializing in owner-managed businesses |
| Estate planning (before death) | Estate planning lawyer + CPA |
| Tax Court appeal | Tax lawyer |
| Voluntary Disclosure (complex, multi-year, fraud risk) | CPA with VDP experience |
| Business sale (LCGE claim) | CPA with M&A and corporate tax experience |
| Bankruptcy / consumer proposal with CRA debt | Licensed Insolvency Trustee |
| International tax / complex treaty situations | International tax specialist |
| Non-resident returns (complex cross-border) | International tax specialist |

### How to Refer Without Losing the Client

**The wrong approach:** *"I can't help you with this. You need a CPA."*

**The right approach:** *"This part of your situation is outside what I handle — it involves [corporate restructuring / international tax / etc.] that requires a specialist. I want to make sure you get the right advice. I'd recommend [specific name] — they specialize in this and I trust their work. I'll continue to handle your personal T1 return, which is within my expertise. The two pieces work together, and I'm happy to coordinate with them."*

**The key elements:**
1. Explain WHY you're referring (not "I can't" but "this requires specialized expertise")
2. Name a specific person you trust (not just "get a CPA")
3. Confirm your continued role ("I'll handle the personal return")
4. Offer to coordinate

A client who is referred with care and specificity trusts you more, not less. It demonstrates that you know the boundaries of your expertise — which means when you say you can handle something, they believe you.

## 24.12 Building the Long-Term Client Relationship

The technical return is what clients pay for. The relationship is what makes them come back every year, refer their family, and forgive the occasional imperfection.

### The Touchpoints That Build Loyalty

**1. The follow-up after filing:**
Two weeks after the return is filed, send a brief message: *"Your return was assessed — your NOA shows [result matches/differs from expected]. Everything looks correct. Let me know if you have any questions."*

Cost: 2 minutes. Value: enormous. Clients who don't hear from you after filing wonder if something went wrong. This eliminates that anxiety.

**2. The year-end planning reminder (October/November):**
*"Wanted to touch base as we head toward year-end. A few things to consider before December 31: [RRSP contribution timing, year-end income, donation receipts, etc.]. Happy to have a quick call if you'd like to talk through anything."*

This positions you as a year-round resource, not just a tax season service.

**3. The RRSP deadline reminder (February):**
*"Just a reminder — the RRSP contribution deadline for your 2025 return is March 1, 2026. Based on what we did last year, you have [X] in available RRSP room. Let me know if you'd like to discuss."*

**4. The major life event check-in:**
When you know a client had a baby, bought a home, got married, or changed jobs: reach out proactively. *"Congratulations on the new baby! A few things change on your tax return when you have a child — I wanted to make sure you're aware of the Canada Child Benefit and how to maximize it. Shall we book a brief call?"*

**5. The birthday note (optional but powerful):**
A brief birthday acknowledgment (text or card) — not an automated one from your software, but a genuine one — makes a client feel valued as a person, not just a client number.

### The Trust Equation

```
Client Trust = Competence × Character / Ego
```

Clients trust you when they see:
- You know what you're doing (competence)
- You have their interests at heart (character)
- You're not in it to show off or make yourself look good (low ego)

Every difficult conversation in this week's material is ultimately about demonstrating all three. When you deliver bad news honestly, you're showing competence and character. When you refer a client to someone better qualified for a specific need, you're showing character and low ego. When you handle an angry client with calm professionalism, you're showing all three.

## 24.13 Case Studies — Difficult Situations Handled

### Case A — The Immigrant Family Who Owed $18,000 They Didn't Expect

**Client:** Ahmad and his wife Fatima arrived from Saudi Arabia in April. Ahmad took a $155,000 job. No withholding on his Saudi bank accounts ($8,200 in interest during Canadian residency). Employer withheld $38,000 in tax.

**The result:** Balance owing: $18,400.

**The conversation:** Ahmad has never paid income tax in his life. The $18,400 is a shock.

You use the PREP framework:

*"Ahmad, before I give you the final number, let me walk you through what happened..."*

[Explains the pro-ration of credits, the Saudi bank interest fully taxable in Canada with no foreign tax credit since Saudi Arabia has no income tax, the employer's estimated withholding not accounting for the foreign income]

*"The balance owing is $18,400. I know that's significant — and I know this is not what you expected coming from a country with no income tax. Here's what I want to show you for next year..."*

[Shows RRSP analysis: $155,000 × 18% = $27,900 RRSP room. $27,900 × 43.41% = $12,110 in tax savings. Quarterly instalments of $4,600 to pre-pay 2026 tax]

*"If we set up quarterly instalments and maximize your RRSP, next year's bill could be approximately $6,000 instead of $18,400. The move to Canada is a financial change, but it's one you can plan for very effectively."*

Ahmad pays the $18,400. He contributes $25,000 to an RRSP. He pays quarterly instalments. His 2026 bill is $4,200. He brings his brother-in-law (who also recently arrived) to you the following February.

### Case B — The Grieving Son Who Became the Executor

**Client:** Marcus, 48, calls. His mother passed away 3 weeks ago. He has been named executor. He has never been an executor before. He doesn't know what he needs to do.

**The first call:**

*"Marcus, I'm so sorry about your mother. I want to help you navigate this as smoothly as possible. Can I ask — do you know if she filed her tax returns up to date? And do you have access to her financial documents?"*

[Marcus confirms she was current through 2023. He has access to her bank statements and T-slips.]

*"Good — that makes this much more manageable than many estates. Here's what we need to do, and I'll guide you through each step. First, we prepare the final return — that covers January 1, 2024, to the date she passed. Second, I'll advise you not to distribute any estate assets until we receive CRA's assessment and obtain a clearance certificate — that protects you personally. Third, we check whether she had any prior year returns outstanding. Let's start with step one."*

[Over two meetings, you prepare the terminal return. Her RRSP of $95,000 goes to Marcus as beneficiary — creating $95,000 in terminal return income. The tax bill is approximately $32,000.]

*"Marcus, I want to prepare you for the terminal return result before I show you the number. Because your mother's RRSP went directly to you as named beneficiary — which is a very common arrangement — it's treated as income on her final return. The total tax is approximately $32,000. This comes out of the estate before you receive your inheritance. It's not a mistake — it's how RRSP treatment works on death without a surviving spouse. The estate has sufficient assets to cover it. After we pay CRA and file for the clearance certificate, the remaining estate is yours."*

Marcus processes this. He asks two questions. You answer clearly.

*"Thank you for explaining that. My mother never mentioned taxes — it's a lot to learn. But I feel like I understand what's happening. What do I do next?"*

### Case C — Discovering Five Years of Missed Deductions

**Client:** Sandra, 52, has been with the same preparer since 2015. She changes to you because he retired. You review her prior returns and find:

1. Investment advisory fees ($4,200/year) never claimed on Line 22100 — 5 years
2. Her rental property carried no CCA claim — 5 years (available but not claimed)
3. Business-use-of-home as a freelancer — she had a T2125 but no home office claimed despite having a dedicated room

**Total potential recovery:**
- Advisory fees: $4,200 × 5 × ~40% = **$8,400**
- Home office (5 years, 20% of $14,000 rent): $1,400 × 5 × 33% = **$2,310**
- CCA recovery is forward-looking (can claim going forward) — not retroactive

**Your conversation:**

*"Sandra, I reviewed your prior returns when you came on board — that's something I do for new clients. I found a few items that weren't claimed that you're legitimately entitled to. I want to walk you through them.*

*First, the advisory fee you pay on your investment account — approximately $4,200/year — is fully deductible on Line 22100. That wasn't claimed in any of the five returns I reviewed. Second, your home office — you have a dedicated room used exclusively for your freelance work. That qualifies for a business-use-of-home deduction, and it also wasn't claimed.*

*Combined, over the five years, I can file amendments that should return approximately $10,700 in refunds plus CRA interest. I want to be clear: I'm not criticizing your previous preparer — these items come up in intake conversations and may simply not have come up. The good news is we can recover them now.*

*Would you like me to proceed with the amendments?"*

Sandra says yes. She receives $10,700 in refunds over the next 8 weeks. She calls her sister, her colleague, and her neighbour. All three become clients the following February.

## 24.14 Week 24 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - **Client emotional states** vary enormously — anxious, embarrassed, angry, grieving, overwhelmed, entitled, trusting. Each requires a different approach. Reading the room and responding to the person before the paperwork is a core professional skill
    - **Delivering bad news:** Use the PREP Framework — Prepare (context before the number), Reason (explain why), Empathize (acknowledge the impact), Plan (what happens next). The word "explain" builds trust. The word "mistake" destroys it
    - **Unfiled years:** The vast majority of unfiled clients are not criminals — they are people who got behind. Most have refunds waiting. Approach with calm reassurance, CRA My Account access, and a clear action plan. Many of your most loyal clients will be people you rescued from this situation
    - **CRA review letters:** Your client needs to hear authority and confidence. You have handled hundreds of these. Calibrate your response to the level (documentation request vs. proposed reassessment vs. formal reassessment). Professional, organized response packages resolve most reviews without escalation
    - **Estate clients:** Pace is everything. Grief changes how people process information. First five minutes: ask how they're doing. Explain filing obligations in plain language, step by step. Never overwhelm with a checklist before you've built rapport
    - **Angry clients:** Listen first. Acknowledge (not agree — acknowledge). Redirect to problem-solving. Set boundaries calmly if abuse escalates. Never become defensive before you understand the full situation
    - **Financial distress:** Three legitimate options — payment arrangement with CRA, Taxpayer Relief application, and Licensed Insolvency Trustee for severe situations. Never leave a client without a path forward
    - **Prior preparer errors:** Identify specifically, quantify the opportunity, offer to act, let the client decide. Never characterize the prior preparer's competence or intent
    - **Referrals:** Name a specific person you trust, explain why you're referring, confirm your continued role, offer to coordinate. Handled well, referrals increase trust — they don't undermine it
    - **Long-term relationships:** Follow-up after filing, year-end planning reminders, RRSP deadline reminders, major life event check-ins. The relationship is built in the moments between tax seasons

## ✅ Week 24 Quiz

When you're ready, ask Claude: **"Quiz me on Week 24"** for an interactive test.

??? question "1. A client calls you in early May. She expected a refund of about $2,000 — the same as every year. Your return shows she owes $3,800. She's upset and says 'something must be wrong.' What do you say and what do you check first?"
    **First — don't defend or dismiss.** Say: *"Let me pull up your return right now and walk through it with you. I want to understand exactly why the result changed."* Then check the most common causes of a refund-to-balance-owing swing: (1) New income source with no withholding — did she start a side hustle, begin receiving rental income, or receive investment income that pushed her above withholding? (2) Reduced deductions — did she stop making RRSP contributions, or did her RRSP room change? (3) Employer adjusted withholding — did she get a raise that wasn't fully reflected in withholding? (4) Lost spousal credit — did her spouse's income change? (5) Benefit repayment — is she in the OAS clawback range or does she owe GST/HST credit repayment? Once you identify the specific cause, explain it using the PREP framework: Prepare her with context, give the Reason, Empathize with the surprise, and Plan for next year to avoid a recurrence. If after checking all of this the result still seems wrong — you may have an entry error. Check the prior year comparison line by line.

??? question "2. A new client tells you he hasn't filed a T1 in 7 years. He's been self-employed the whole time — cash-based cleaning business. He's terrified. What is your first priority, what are his realistic options, and what is your realistic assessment of his situation?"
    **First priority:** Reassure him that he is almost certainly not facing criminal prosecution — that is reserved for deliberate large-scale fraud, not someone who fell behind. Then assess the situation factually: (1) Access CRA My Account to see what years are outstanding, whether any notional assessments have been issued, and what balances are showing. (2) The cash income is the complicating factor — this is not just a failure-to-file situation, it involves unreported income. This means the **Voluntary Disclosure Program (VDP)** is the appropriate path IF CRA has not yet contacted him about any of these years. VDP would allow him to come forward proactively, pay the taxes owing, and receive penalty relief. (3) If CRA has already contacted him about any of these years — VDP is closed for those years. (4) Given the complexity (7 years, cash income, self-employment), this likely requires a CPA with VDP experience, not a basic tax preparer. Your role: explain the situation clearly, explain the VDP option, and refer to a specialist for the VDP application. You may still prepare the actual returns once the VDP framework is established.

??? question "3. You prepared a return for a client last year. He calls now saying CRA sent a proposed reassessment adding $12,000 to his income — a T5 from a GIC that wasn't on the return. He says it's your fault. Walk through your response."
    **Step 1 — Listen completely before responding.** Don't interrupt. **Step 2 — Get the actual letter.** Ask him to describe or send the specific document CRA sent. **Step 3 — Pull your file.** Check your CRA My Account screenshot from when you prepared the return. If the T5 was not showing in CRA's system at filing time — you have documentation. If it was in the system and you missed it — acknowledge the error professionally. **If the T5 was not available at filing:** *"I want to address this directly. When we prepared your return, I verified all slips through CRA My Account — that's a standard step I take for every client. At that time, this T5 wasn't in the system. T5 slips are sometimes filed late by financial institutions. This isn't an error on the return — it's a slip that wasn't available when we filed. The income is real and the tax is owed. I'll file the T1-ADJ at no charge."* **If it was an error:** *"I've reviewed the file, and you're right — this T5 was available and I missed it. I'm sorry. I'll file the amendment immediately, and I'll cover any penalty or interest that results from the delay out of my own practice. The tax itself is owed regardless — that's the tax on real income you earned."* Own genuine errors. Explain clearly when the error wasn't yours.

??? question "4. Helen's husband passed away in October. She calls you in January, grieving and overwhelmed. She says she 'doesn't know anything about taxes' and has never filed. What are your first three sentences to her on that call?"
    Three sentences that open the relationship correctly: (1) *"Helen, I'm so sorry for your loss — thank you for reaching out."* [Human acknowledgment before anything professional.] (2) *"I want you to know that we can handle everything together, one step at a time — you don't need to understand the whole process right now."* [Removes the pressure of competence — she doesn't need to come prepared.] (3) *"Can I ask a few simple questions just to understand the situation — things like when Robert passed and whether he had filed his taxes up to date?"* [Starts gently gathering information without overwhelming her with a checklist.] These three sentences do more for the relationship than any technical knowledge. Helen is being asked to trust a stranger with her late husband's financial affairs while grieving. The first impression sets whether she relaxes or stays anxious for the entire engagement.

??? question "5. A client is pushing you to claim a $15,000 home renovation as a 'home office improvement' when it's clearly a general kitchen renovation. He says 'everyone does it' and 'you'll never get caught.' What is your complete response and what do you document?"
    **Your response:** *"I understand you want to reduce your tax as much as possible — that's exactly what I'm here to help you do. But claiming a kitchen renovation as a home office improvement isn't something I'm able to include. A home office deduction applies to the incremental cost of making a space suitable for business use, not a general renovation. This claim would be denied in any CRA review — and if it came up, it would flag your entire return for closer scrutiny. The 'everyone does it' framing concerns me because it suggests the risk is higher than you might think, not lower — CRA specifically looks for exactly this type of claim. What I can do is make sure we've captured every legitimate deduction you're entitled to — let's focus on those."* **What you document:** A brief note in the client file: *"[Date] — Client asked about claiming kitchen renovation ($15,000) as home office improvement. Explained this does not qualify. Client acknowledged. Proceeding without this item."* If the client insists after this explanation, decline the engagement. Your signed engagement letter and this file note protect you if the client later attempts to involve you in any dispute arising from this.

??? question "6. You discover that a client's prior returns (from another preparer, 3 years) missed claiming the Ontario Disability Tax Credit for their child who has an approved DTC. The annual combined credit (federal + Ontario) is approximately $3,445. What do you do and what do you say?"
    You file T1-ADJ requests for each of the three prior years, adding the DTC and Child Disability Benefit supplement to the parent's return (transferred from the child who has no income). For three years at $3,445/year: approximately **$10,335 in recoverable combined credits**. **The conversation:** *"I want to share something I found when I reviewed your prior returns. Your child's Disability Tax Credit approval was on file with CRA — I can see it in My Account — but it wasn't applied to your returns in 2022, 2023, or 2024. The DTC and the Child Disability Benefit supplement together are worth approximately $3,445/year in combined federal and Ontario credits. For the three years, we can recover approximately $10,335 through amendments. I don't know why it wasn't claimed — it may not have come up in intake. The good news is we can fix it now. I'll file the amendments at no extra charge."* Do not criticize the prior preparer. Do not speculate on negligence. The client will draw their own conclusions. Your job is to find the money and return it to the family.

??? question "7. A self-employed client owes $28,000 in combined federal and Ontario tax. She tells you she simply doesn't have the money and is afraid CRA will 'come after her.' What are her three legitimate options and what specific action should she take in the next 48 hours?"
    **Three legitimate options:** (1) **CRA Payment Arrangement:** Call CRA at 1-800-959-8281 and ask to set up a payment plan. She proposes a monthly amount she can sustain (e.g., $800–$1,000/month). CRA almost always accepts reasonable proposals. Interest continues to accrue, but collection actions are suspended while the plan is active. This is the first and most appropriate option. (2) **Taxpayer Relief Application (Form RC4288):** If her inability to pay is due to circumstances beyond her control (serious illness, job loss, natural disaster, financial hardship), she can apply for relief from penalties and interest. This does not forgive the underlying tax but can significantly reduce the total owing. (3) **Licensed Insolvency Trustee (consumer proposal):** If her overall debt (including CRA) is unmanageable, a consumer proposal allows her to pay a fraction of total debt with the balance forgiven. CRA participates in consumer proposals like any other creditor. **Next 48 hours:** File the tax return TODAY if not already filed — every day past April 30 without filing adds 5% + 1%/month in penalties to the $28,000. Filing stops the penalty clock even if payment is delayed. Then call CRA within the week to establish a payment arrangement. The tax owing with a payment plan is manageable. The tax owing with mounting penalties is not.
