## 18.1 The Student's Tax Reality — Why Most Don't Bother Filing (And Why That's a Mistake)

Most students believe they don't need to file because:

1. "I don't make enough money to owe any tax"
2. "I'm too busy with school to deal with CRA"
3. "There's nothing to claim"

Every one of these beliefs is wrong — and costs them real money.

Here is what a student **loses every year they don't file:**

| Benefit / Credit | Annual Value | Requires Filing? |
|---|---|---|
| GST/HST credit (age 19+) | **$519** | ✅ Yes |
| Ontario Trillium Benefit (renter) | **$500–$1,200** | ✅ Yes |
| Canada Workers Benefit (if working) | **Up to $1,518** | ✅ Yes |
| Tuition credit carry-forward | **Accumulating** | ✅ Must file to establish |
| RRSP contribution room | **Building every year** | ✅ Must file to earn |
| Refund of withheld taxes | **Varies — often $400–$900** | ✅ Yes |

A student who earns $12,000 from a summer job, pays $15,000 in tuition, and rents an apartment in Toronto could receive:

- Full tax refund of withheld employment taxes: ~$650
- GST/HST credit: $519
- Ontario Trillium Benefit: ~$900
- Tuition credit carry-forward established: $2,250 for future use
- RRSP contribution room built: $12,000 × 18% = $2,160

**Total cash or future value from one filed return: approximately $4,319**

This is why you file every student return without exception — and why you explain this clearly to every student client from day one.

## 18.2 The T2202 — Tuition and Enrolment Certificate

The **T2202 (Tuition and Enrolment Certificate)** is the foundation document for all student tax claims. It is issued by every qualifying Canadian post-secondary institution by the last day of February for the prior tax year.

### What It Reports

| Box | Description |
|---|---|
| **Box 20** | Eligible tuition fees paid — full-time months |
| **Box 22** | Eligible tuition fees paid — part-time months |
| **Box 21** | Number of full-time months enrolled |
| **Box 23** | Number of part-time months enrolled |

!!! warning "Students forget to download their T2202 every year"
    Unlike employer T4 slips, most T2202s are **not mailed** — they are available through the student's online school portal (student self-service, OSAP portal, etc.). Many students don't know to look for them. Ask every student client: *"Have you downloaded your T2202 from your school's online portal? It would show your tuition amount for the year."*

    Also ask: *"Did you attend school in January or February? That means you may have a T2202 for the current year too."* January–February tuition generates a T2202 that can be claimed on the prior year's return (if the institution issues it in time) or the current year — an additional planning opportunity.

### What Qualifies as Eligible Tuition

| ✅ Eligible | ❌ Not Eligible |
|---|---|
| Tuition fees at a Canadian university, college, or designated institution | Books, course materials |
| Tuition at a foreign university (full-time, 3+ consecutive weeks) | Student activity fees |
| Occupational, trade, and vocational school fees | Parking |
| Examination fees for professional licensing | Residence and meal plans |
| Mandatory ancillary fees required for enrollment | Gym membership |

## 18.3 The Tuition Tax Credit — Four Strategic Options

The federal tuition credit is 15% of eligible tuition. An Ontario student also generates a provincial tuition credit at 5.05%.

**Example:** $14,000 in tuition generates:
- Federal credit: $14,000 × 15% = **$2,100**
- Ontario credit: $14,000 × 5.05% = **$707**
- **Total tuition credit: $2,807**

This is substantial. But the student with $14,000 in tuition and $9,000 in part-time income has a federal tax bill of approximately **$0** — they owe nothing because their income is below the Basic Personal Amount.

So what happens to the $2,807 in credits they can't use this year?

They have **four strategic choices**, calculated on **Schedule 11**:

=== "Option 1 — Use on Own Return (Current Year)"

    **When:** The student has federal or provincial tax owing from employment, investment income, or self-employment income.

    **Rule:** Apply the credit to your own tax bill first, before deciding what to do with the remainder.

    **Example:** Tyler earned $22,000 from co-op work in 2025. He owes approximately $1,100 in combined federal and Ontario tax. He paid $8,000 in tuition.

    Tuition credit generated: $8,000 × (15% + 5.05%) = $1,604
    Applied against Tyler's tax: **$1,100 used → $0 tax owing**
    Remaining unused credit: **$504**

    Tyler uses $1,100 of his $1,604 tuition credit. He has $504 left. What should he do with it?

=== "Option 2 — Carry Forward (Indefinitely)"

    **When:** The student doesn't need the credit now and will have significant income in the near future.

    **Rule:** Unused current-year tuition credit (and any prior carryforwards) rolls forward to future years — **indefinitely, with no expiry date.**

    **Best use:** A student who will graduate and earn $70,000 starting next year should carry forward. At $70,000 income, the $504 credit is worth the same — but there's no urgency to transfer it now, and keeping it preserves flexibility.

    **How the carry-forward appears:** On the student's NOA, CRA tracks the accumulated tuition, education, and textbook credit available to carry forward. You can see the current balance in CRA My Account.

    !!! tip "Check prior NOAs for accumulated carry-forwards"
        A student who has been in school for 4 years and never claimed their tuition may have accumulated $8,000–$12,000 in federal tuition carry-forwards. When they graduate and file their first employment return, this carry-forward can wipe out 1–2 years of federal tax entirely. Ask every new client who was recently a student: *"Did you ever file returns during school? Can you check CRA My Account for your tuition carry-forward balance?"*

=== "Option 3 — Transfer to a Parent or Grandparent (Up to $5,000 Base)"

    **When:** The student has a supporting parent or grandparent with federal tax owing — AND the student is unlikely to earn significant income in the near future.

    **Rule:**
    - The student can transfer up to **$5,000 of the current year's tuition** to a parent or grandparent
    - The student must first apply any credits against their **own** tax — only the remaining unused portion can be transferred
    - **Prior year carry-forwards CANNOT be transferred** — only the current year's tuition
    - The transfer is designated on **Schedule 11** (student's return) and claimed on **Line 32400** (parent's return)

    **Value of the maximum transfer to parent:**
    Federal credit: $5,000 × 15% = **$750**
    Ontario credit at parent's rate: $5,000 × 5.05% = **$253**
    **Combined value: $1,003**

    !!! example "Case — Tyler Transfers to His Mother"
        Tyler paid $16,000 in tuition in 2025. He earned $9,000 and owes zero federal tax.

        His tuition credit: $16,000 × 15% = $2,400 federal. No tax to apply it against.
        His mother, Diane (Ontario, $88,000 income), has significant federal tax owing.

        Tyler designates the transfer on Schedule 11: he transfers $5,000 of tuition to Diane.
        Diane claims $5,000 on Line 32400 → **$750 federal + $253 Ontario = $1,003 in credits for Diane**

        Tyler keeps the remaining $11,000 in tuition credit ($1,650) as a carry-forward for his own future use.

        **Key question to ask:** Will Tyler have significant income in the next 1–3 years? If yes → carry forward everything (the $1,650 remaining grows in value as his income rises). If no → the $1,003 for Diane is a bird-in-hand.

        Tyler is entering his final year of university. He'll graduate in April and start at $82,000. He should carry forward everything — the $1,650 carry-forward will save him $1,650 × (29.65% / 15%) = more in future years... wait, the credit is already calculated at 15%. He uses the credit directly against his $82,000 income return next year. The $1,650 + the 2026 tuition credit combined will eliminate 1–2 years of federal tax. Much better than giving Diane $750 now.

        **The rule of thumb:** Transfer to parent only when the student does not expect high income for 3+ years. If graduation is imminent — carry forward.

=== "Option 4 — Transfer to Spouse or CLP (Up to $5,000 Base)"

    **When:** The student is married or has a common-law partner with tax owing.

    **Same rules as the parent transfer** — maximum $5,000 base, current year only, unused portion after own tax.

    **Example:** Sarah is a mature student (age 31) enrolled full-time. She paid $11,000 in tuition. Her husband earns $95,000.

    Sarah's own federal tax on $8,000 part-time income: $0.
    She transfers $5,000 base to her husband:
    Husband claims **$750 federal + $253 Ontario = $1,003 in combined credits**.
    Sarah carries forward the remaining $6,000 base ($6,000 × 15% = $900 federal) for herself.

## 18.4 Moving Expenses for Students — The Massively Underused Deduction

!!! success "This is the single most underused deduction in student tax returns"
    CRA allows students to deduct moving expenses when they move to attend school full-time — using the same rules as employment-related moves. This means a student who moved from Sudbury to Toronto to attend university can deduct thousands of dollars in moving costs.

    Most students don't know this. Most preparers don't ask. You will.

### The Rule

Students can deduct eligible moving expenses when:
- They moved to **attend a post-secondary institution full-time**, AND
- They moved at least **40 km closer** to their school, AND
- The deduction is limited to **scholarships, bursaries, fellowships, and research grants** that are included in income

That last point is the critical restriction: moving expenses for students are deducted against **school-related income** (scholarships, research grants, bursaries that are taxable), not employment income. A student with zero taxable scholarship income cannot deduct moving expenses against their summer job income.

!!! example "Case — The PhD Student Who Moved for School"
    Rachel moves from Windsor to Toronto to begin a PhD program at U of T. She receives a $30,000 research grant (taxable). Her moving costs:

    | Expense | Amount |
    |---|---|
    | Moving truck rental | $1,800 |
    | Gas driving to Toronto | $220 |
    | Hotel during move (2 nights) | $320 |
    | First and last month's deposit lost (Windsor lease break) | $1,400 |
    | **Total eligible moving expenses** | **$3,740** |

    Rachel's taxable research grant: $30,000

    **Moving expense deduction:** $3,740 (well within the $30,000 grant income limit)

    At Rachel's 20.05% combined rate: $3,740 × 20.05% = **$750 in tax saved** — from an expense she paid regardless.

    **Ask every grad student or student receiving awards:** *"Did you move for school? Do you receive a stipend, scholarship, or research grant that shows up on a T4A?"* If yes — moving expenses may be deductible.

### Moving Expenses for New Graduates (Employment Move)

This is the bigger opportunity and gets its own detailed coverage because it applies to many more students.

When a student **graduates and moves for their first job** — the full employment moving expense rules apply (covered in Week 12). A new grad who moves from Waterloo to Ottawa for a federal government job can deduct:

- Moving truck / professional movers
- Hotel and meals during the move
- Temporary accommodation at the new location (up to 15 days)
- First and last month's lost deposit if breaking a lease
- Storage costs
- Vehicle km rate to drive to the new city

!!! example "Case — The New Grad Who Moved for Work"
    James graduates from Western University in April 2025 and starts a job at a London-based accounting firm in May — no move needed. Then in October, he's offered a position at a Bay Street firm in Toronto at $95,000. He accepts and moves in November.

    Distance test:
    - London home to Toronto office: 190 km
    - New Toronto home to Toronto office: 4 km
    - Difference: 186 km ✅

    Moving costs:

    | Expense | Amount |
    |---|---|
    | Professional movers | $2,400 |
    | Lease break penalty (London apartment) | $1,800 |
    | Hotel in Toronto (10 days temporary) | $1,600 |
    | Meals during move | $380 |
    | Gas driving to Toronto | $65 |
    | **Total** | **$6,245** |

    Toronto employment income (November–December, 2 months): $95,000 × 2/12 = **$15,833**

    Moving expense deduction: $6,245 (within new-location income)

    At James's combined 29.65% rate: $6,245 × 29.65% = **$1,852 in tax saved**

    **The lease break penalty alone ($1,800) generates $534 in tax savings** — an item almost every new grad pays and almost none of them claim.

    **Ask every client who graduated and moved for work this year:** *"Did you break a lease when you moved? Did you stay in a hotel while looking for an apartment? What were your actual moving costs?"* Then systematically collect every receipt.

## 18.5 Student Loan Interest — Line 31900

Interest paid on **Canada Student Loans (CSL)** or **provincial student loans** generates a **15% non-refundable federal credit** plus an Ontario credit at 5.05%.

**Important update:** As of April 1, 2023, the federal government **eliminated interest** on Canada Student Loans and Canada Apprentice Loans. There is no longer any federal student loan interest to claim going forward.

**However:** Many provinces still charge interest on their provincial loan portions. Ontario's OSAP loans have a provincial component that may still generate interest in some cases — verify the current provincial status annually.

**Carryforward:** Unused student loan interest credits can be carried forward **5 years** — not indefinitely. Unlike tuition carry-forwards (which last forever), student loan interest credits expire.

!!! tip "Check prior NOAs for student loan interest carry-forwards"
    A client who paid OSAP interest in prior years but didn't claim it may have a carry-forward balance. This is visible on the prior NOA. Check the carryforward balance for every new graduate client in your first year serving them.

## 18.6 RESP Withdrawals — The Education Assistance Payment

A **Registered Education Savings Plan (RESP)** is a savings vehicle used by parents or grandparents to save for a child's post-secondary education. When the student withdraws from the RESP, the taxation depends on which component is withdrawn.

### The Two Types of RESP Withdrawals

| Component | What It Is | Taxable? | On Whose Return? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Education Assistance Payment (EAP)** | Investment income + CESG grants accumulated in the plan | ✅ **Yes** — fully taxable | **Student's** return (usually very low/zero tax) |
| **Return of Contributions (PSE)** | The original contributions made to the plan | ❌ **No** — not taxable | No reporting required |

The EAP is reported on a **T4A** slip issued by the RESP provider. Box 40 on the T4A is the taxable EAP amount. It goes on Line **13010** of the student's T1.

!!! success "The RESP tax arbitrage — why it works"
    Parents contribute to the RESP out of after-tax dollars. The investment income and government grants (CESG) grow tax-free inside the plan. When withdrawn as an EAP, that income is taxed in the **student's hands** — who typically has little or no other income and pays minimal or zero tax.

    A student with $18,000 in EAP income and $8,000 in employment income:
    Total income: $26,000
    After BPA ($16,129): taxable income = $9,871
    Federal tax: $9,871 × 15% = $1,481
    Less federal tuition credit (if in school): likely eliminates this entirely

    The government grants and years of compounding growth have been extracted with minimal tax — the entire purpose of the RESP design.

!!! example "Case — Ahmed's First Year of University"
    Ahmed, 18, starts university in September 2025. His parents set up an RESP when he was born. The plan contains $72,000 — split as $22,000 in contributions (non-taxable) and $50,000 in EAP (grants + growth).

    His RESP provider withdraws $16,000 in EAP for his first year (Box 40 on T4A).

    Ahmed's other 2025 income:
    - Part-time cashier job (September–December): $4,200
    - RESP EAP: $16,000
    - **Total income: $20,200**

    **Federal tax:**
    After BPA ($16,129): taxable income = $4,071
    Federal tax: $4,071 × 15% = $611

    **Ontario tax:**
    $20,200 × 5.05% = $1,020 Ontario basic tax
    Less Ontario BPA ($11,865 × 5.05% = $599), CPP/EI credits
    Net Ontario tax: approximately $388

    **LIFT Credit:** Ahmed has employment income and net income below $30,000. LIFT Credit eliminates his Ontario tax → **Ontario tax: $0**

    **Tuition credit generated:** $12,000 in tuition × (15% + 5.05%) = $2,406 total credit
    Applied against federal tax of $611: **Federal tax → $0**
    Remaining tuition carry-forward: $2,406 − $611 = **$1,795 for future years**

    **Benefits:**
    GST/HST credit: $519 (he turns 19 in 2025)
    Ontario Trillium Benefit: ~$900 (renting at residence: $25 student flat amount, OSTC $345)

    Ahmed received $16,000 from the RESP and paid **zero income tax**. He has accumulated $1,795 in tuition carry-forward. He received $1,419 in refundable benefits. His net cost for his first year of university is dramatically reduced — all because of proper tax planning when he was born.

### RESP Withdrawal — What to Watch For

!!! warning "Overwithdrawing EAP in a single year can trigger unexpected tax"
    If a student is not in school (gap year, or school ended mid-year) and the RESP provider still pays out an EAP, it is still taxable — but the student may not have tuition credits to offset it. Time withdrawals to correspond with enrollment years.

    Also: If the student does **not** pursue post-secondary education, the EAP cannot be paid out. The contributions go back to the subscriber tax-free; the accumulated income becomes an Accumulated Income Payment (AIP) — taxed at the subscriber's rate plus a **20% penalty tax**. Avoid this outcome by ensuring the RESP stays active until the student enrolls.

## 18.7 The First Employment Year After Graduation — A Return That Confuses Everyone

New graduates often receive their first T4 in February and are confused by the result. Here's why:

1. They worked for only 8–10 months of the year (started in May/June)
2. Their employer withheld tax based on a full year of that salary
3. They have tuition credits carried forward from school

The result is almost always a **significant refund** — which surprises them.

!!! example "Case — Jennifer's First Job Return"
    Jennifer graduates in April 2025. Starts as a junior accountant at $68,000/year in May 2025.

    **Months worked:** May–December = 8 months
    **T4 Box 14 employment income:** $68,000 × 8/12 = **$45,333**
    **T4 Box 22 tax withheld:** Employer withheld based on a full-year $68,000 salary → approximately $12,800 withheld

    **Jennifer's actual tax on $45,333:**

    | Bracket | Income | Rate | Tax |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | $0–$45,333 | $45,333 | 15% | $6,800 |
    | **Gross federal tax** | | | **$6,800** |

    Less credits:
    - Basic Personal Amount: $16,129 × 15% = $2,419
    - CPP: $3,867 × 15% = $580
    - EI: $1,049 × 15% = $157
    - Canada Employment Amount: $1,433 × 15% = $215
    **Total credits: $3,371**

    **Net federal tax: $6,800 − $3,371 = $3,429**

    Jennifer also has $9,200 in tuition carry-forward from her 4 years at school.
    Tuition credit carry-forward: $9,200 × 15% = **$1,380** applied against federal tax.

    **Federal tax after tuition: $3,429 − $1,380 = $2,049**

    **Tax withheld was $12,800. Tax actually owed: $2,049.**

    **Federal refund: $12,800 − $2,049 = $10,751**

    Plus Ontario refund: ~$2,400 (employer over-withheld Ontario tax too)

    **Jennifer's total refund: approximately $13,151** 🎉

    Jennifer has never had $13,000 arrive in her bank account at once. She will remember who filed her return forever. And she will refer every single one of her friends from school.

    This is the magic of the first-employment-year return. It almost always produces a large refund because:
    1. The employer withheld tax as if the income level continued all year (annualization)
    2. The graduate only worked part of the year
    3. Accumulated tuition credits eliminate even more tax

    **Set the expectation at intake:** *"Jennifer, because you started mid-year and you have tuition credits from school, your refund should be quite large — probably in the $12,000–$14,000 range. Let's make sure we capture everything."* Clients who are surprised positively become loyal clients.

## 18.8 Ontario Student-Specific Considerations

### Ontario Student Assistance Program (OSAP) — Tax Impact

OSAP grants are **not taxable income**. OSAP loans are not taxable (they are borrowed money). Neither appears on the T1.

The only OSAP-related item that appears on the T1 is **interest on the Ontario portion of OSAP loans** — which was historically deductible under Line 31900. With the elimination of federal interest (2023), verify current provincial interest status annually.

### Ontario Trillium Benefit — Students

Students who live in a rented residence (apartment, house, room) in Ontario are eligible for OEPTC based on their rent. Students who live in university residence can claim a **$25 flat amount** as their "property tax equivalent" — not 20% of actual residence fees (which are not considered eligible rent).

| Living Situation | OEPTC Basis |
|---|---|
| Renting privately (apartment, house, room) | 20% of actual rent paid |
| University/college residence | $25 flat amount per year |
| Living with parents (no rent paid) | $0 |
| Co-op/work term housing | Varies — if paying rent, use actual amount |

!!! example "Case — Three Students, Three Different OTB Situations"

    All are Ontario students, 19 years old, attending school in Toronto, net income ~$12,000 each:

    | Student | Situation | OEPTC | OSTC | Annual OTB |
    |---|---|---|---|---|
    | **Ahmed** | University residence (paid $8,200) | $25 flat | $345 | **$370** |
    | **Sara** | Rents private apartment $1,100/month | 20% × $13,200 = $2,640 property equivalent → ~$640 | $345 | **~$985** |
    | **Kevin** | Lives with parents, no rent paid | $0 | $345 | **$345** |

    Sara gets $985/year vs. Ahmed's $370 and Kevin's $345 — because she pays private rent. The 20% of rent rule makes a meaningful difference. Students who move out of residence into private rentals increase their OTB significantly.

### Ontario LIFT Credit — Students Who Work

The LIFT Credit (Ontario) eliminates Ontario personal income tax for low-income Ontarians with employment income. Most students working part-time or co-op qualify:

- A student earning $18,000 from co-op has approximately $1,212 in Ontario basic tax
- LIFT Credit: **$1,212** — Ontario tax reduced to **$0**
- Combined with the federal BPA shelter: the student owes no income tax at all

The LIFT Credit is one reason why many students who work part-time in Ontario end up with their full Ontario tax withheld through the year — then receive it all back at filing, on top of the federal refund.

## 18.9 Co-op and Internship Students — Special Situations

Co-op students face a unique tax situation: they alternate between school terms (tuition, no income) and work terms (employment income, no tuition). This creates planning opportunities.

### Co-op Tax Profile

| Term | Income | Tuition | Credits Generated |
|---|---|---|---|
| School term | Low/zero | High | Large tuition credit (unused — carry forward) |
| Work term | Significant | Zero | Employment income — credits applied against tax |

The optimal strategy: **accumulate tuition credits during school terms, apply them against employment income during work terms.**

!!! example "Case — Michael's Four-Year Co-op Journey"
    Michael is in a 4-year co-op engineering program (8 school terms, 6 work terms alternating).

    **Annual pattern:**
    - September–December (school): Tuition $6,000, income $0 → Tuition credit: $6,000 × 20.05% = $1,203 generated, $0 applied
    - January–April (school): Tuition $6,000, income $0 → Tuition credit: $6,000 × 20.05% = $1,203 generated, $0 applied
    - May–August (work term): Income $18,000, tuition $0 → Employment income, tuition carry-forward applied

    **By Year 3, Michael's accumulated tuition carry-forward: approximately $14,400 × 20.05% = $2,887 federal + Ontario**

    **Year 3 work term tax:**
    Employment income: $22,000
    Federal tax before credits: ~$1,000
    After BPA, CPP, EI: ~$0 federal tax
    Applied tuition carry-forward: $2,887 available → $0 needed (already at $0)

    Michael keeps accumulating. By graduation, he'll have **$7,000–$9,000** in accumulated federal + Ontario tuition carry-forwards.

    First real job: $88,000 salary.
    Federal tax: ~$10,200 before credits.
    Apply $8,000 in accumulated tuition carry-forwards: reduces federal + Ontario tax by **$8,000 × 20.05% = $1,604** → net tax reduced dramatically.

    **The tuition carry-forward from co-op years is one of the most valuable assets a new graduate doesn't know they have.**

### Co-op Relocation Expenses

When a co-op student relocates for a work term to a different city — they may qualify for moving expense deductions if the new location is 40+ km closer to the work placement.

Co-op income is employment income — so the full employment moving expense rules apply.

!!! example "Case — Sara's Co-op Move from London to Ottawa"
    Sara is a third-year Western student. Her May–August co-op placement is in Ottawa at a federal government department. She earns $4,800/month = $19,200 for the term.

    She moves from London (where she rents during school) to Ottawa for the work term. Distance: London to Ottawa = 450 km. Her Ottawa sublet: 4 months.

    Moving costs: $1,200 (rental van + gas both ways counted once for the outward move).

    Distance test: London home to Ottawa workplace (450 km) vs. new Ottawa home to same workplace (3 km). Difference: 447 km. ✅

    **Moving expense deduction: $1,200** (limited to Ottawa employment income of $19,200 — easily covered)

    Tax saving: $1,200 × 20.05% = **$240** — from a single ask at intake.

    Repeat this every co-op term and the cumulative savings over 3 work terms: **$720** — not enormous, but real money for a student on a budget, and the habit of tracking expenses builds good record-keeping practices for the rest of their career.

## 18.10 The Scholarship and Bursary Rules

### What Is Taxable vs. Tax-Free?

| Award Type | Taxable? | Appears on T4A? |
|---|---|---|
| **Scholarship/bursary — full-time student** | ❌ **Tax-free** (full exemption) | Box 105 — but exempt |
| **Scholarship/bursary — part-time student** | ✅ Up to the cost of tuition (remainder tax-free) | Box 105 |
| **Fellowship** | ✅ Yes — taxable | Box 105 or 28 |
| **Research grant** | ✅ Yes — taxable (net of expenses) | Box 104 |
| **Bursary from employer (not school)** | ✅ Yes — employment benefit | Box 40 of T4 |
| **Prize for academic achievement (merit-based)** | ❌ Tax-free | Box 105 |

!!! example "Case — Sophia's Mix of Awards"
    Sophia is a full-time master's student at Queen's University in Kingston, Ontario.

    | Award | Amount | Taxable? |
    |---|---|---|
    | Queen's University entrance scholarship | $6,000 | ❌ Tax-free (Box 105, full-time student) |
    | NSERC research grant | $22,000 | ✅ Taxable (Box 104, T4A) |
    | Teaching assistant stipend | $8,000 | ✅ Taxable (Box 14, T4) |
    | Graduate fellowship | $14,000 | ✅ Taxable (Box 105, Box 28 T4A) |

    **Sophia's taxable income:**
    Research grant: $22,000 (net of research expenses, if any)
    TA stipend: $8,000 (T4 employment income)
    Fellowship: $14,000
    **Total taxable income: $44,000**

    **Moving expenses:** Sophia moved from Vancouver to Kingston. Her eligible moving costs ($3,800) can be deducted against her research grant income of $22,000. ✅

    **RRSP contribution room:** $44,000 × 18% = **$7,920 in new RRSP room** — even as a student. Graduate students with taxable income build RRSP room that most never realize they have.

    **Filing this return properly requires understanding every income source.** A graduate student handing you a pile of T4s and T4As from their university is not a "simple" return. It requires the full application of rules across employment income, fellowship income, research grants, moving expenses, and tuition credits.

### Case B — The Fourth-Year Student: Sarah, The Optimal Tuition Strategy

**Background:** Sarah is in her fourth and final year of a nursing program at Western University (London, Ontario). She turns 22 in 2025. She has been filing since Year 1. Her CRA My Account shows an accumulated tuition carry-forward of **$8,400** (federal) from prior years.

**2025 profile:**
- Part-time hospital placement stipend (T4A): $4,800
- Tuition paid (T2202): $9,200 (September–December)
- Rent: $1,050/month (12 months full year)
- She graduates in April 2026 and has accepted a nursing position at $82,000 starting May 2026

**Tax calculation:**

Income: $4,800
Net income: $4,800
Federal tax: $0 (below BPA)
Ontario tax: $0 (LIFT applies)

**Tuition credit 2025:** $9,200 × (15% + 5.05%) = $1,845 generated
Applied against tax: **$0 needed**
New carry-forward created: $1,845

**Total carry-forward available (federal portion only):** $8,400 existing + $9,200 × 15% = $1,380 new = **$9,780 federal tuition carry-forward**

**Transfer to parent analysis:**
Sarah's mother earns $95,000 and has federal tax owing.
Maximum transfer: $5,000 base × (15% + 5.05%) = **$1,003 in combined credit to mother**

**Your recommendation:** Do NOT transfer. Sarah starts at $82,000 in May 2026. Her 2026 federal tax will be approximately $8,000–$10,000. Her accumulated carry-forward of $9,780 federal + provincial equivalent will **eliminate 1–2 years of income tax after graduation.** The carry-forward is worth significantly more than the $1,003 her mother would get today.

**Show Sarah the math:**
If she transfers $5,000 to mother: mother saves $1,003. Sarah keeps $4,780 federal carry-forward.
If she keeps everything: her 2026 federal tax of $8,000 is reduced by $9,780 in credits → she pays approximately $0 federal tax in her first working year. Total saving: $8,000 vs. $1,003.

*"Sarah, your carry-forward is worth $8,000 in tax savings next year. Your mom would get $1,003 this year. Keep the carry-forward."*

**OTB 2025:**
Rent: $1,050 × 12 = $12,600 → 20% = $2,520 deemed property tax → OEPTC approximately **$640**
OSTC: below threshold → **$345**
**Total OTB: $985/year**

GST/HST credit: $519

**Total 2025 benefits: $985 + $519 = $1,504 in cash payments**
Sarah pays $0 in tax, accumulates $9,780 in carry-forwards, and receives $1,504 in annual benefits.

### Case D — The Mature Student: Daniel, Age 34, Back to School for MBA

**Background:** Daniel, 34, left a $95,000 sales manager job in January 2025 to pursue a full-time MBA at Ivey Business School. He received:
- Severance from his employer: $28,000 (T4 Box 66 — eligible retiring allowance portion)
- MBA scholarship: $12,000 (T4A Box 105 — full-time student, tax-free)
- Part-time consulting work: $18,000 (T4A Box 48 — self-employment)
- Tuition: $38,000 (T2202)
- He rents an apartment in London, Ontario for $1,400/month

**Key tax considerations:**

**Severance:** $28,000 received — eligible retiring allowance. Pre-1996 service component may qualify for RRSP transfer without using room. For post-1996 employees, the $28,000 is fully taxable income. At Daniel's marginal rate, he should consider contributing to RRSP.

**MBA scholarship:** $12,000 — **tax-free** (full-time student). Does not appear on T1 income.

**Self-employment:** $18,000 on T2125 (Line 13500). Subject to CPP at 11.9% on net income above $3,500.

**Daniel's 2025 income:**
Severance (taxable): $28,000
Self-employment net: $18,000
Tuition: $0 (not income)
Scholarship: $0 (tax-free, not reported)
**Total income: $46,000**

**RRSP strategy:** Daniel has $22,000 in RRSP room from prior high-income years. He should contribute to offset the severance income at his 29.65% rate.

$22,000 RRSP contribution → Net income: $46,000 − $22,000 = **$24,000**

Federal tax on $24,000 → approximately $0 (below BPA)
Ontario tax → $0 (LIFT applies — employment + self-employment income, under $30,000)

**Tuition credit:** $38,000 × (15% + 5.05%) = $7,619 generated. No tax to apply against → full carry-forward.

**Moving expenses:** Daniel moved from Toronto to London for the MBA. Eligible under student moving expense rules, deductible against the $18,000 consulting income (earned at the new location) or against any taxable scholarship/fellowship income.

Distance: Toronto home to Ivey (London) = 190 km. New London home to Ivey = 2 km. Difference: 188 km. ✅

Moving costs: $2,800 (truck, hotel, meals). Deductible against consulting income.

**After moving expense deduction:** $18,000 − $2,800 = $15,200 in consulting income.
After RRSP: Net income drops further → **extremely low or zero tax**.

Daniel came from a $95,000 income year to a $24,000 income year. He pays essentially zero income tax. His $38,000 in tuition credits accumulate. When he graduates and returns to $120,000+ income, he has a substantial credit arsenal to deploy.

## 18.13 Week 18 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - **Students must file every year** — even with zero income. Filing establishes tuition carry-forwards, RRSP room, and triggers GST/HST credit, OTB, and CWB. Not filing costs $1,000–$4,000 per year in missed cash payments and accumulated credits
    - **T2202:** The foundation document. Downloaded from school portals (rarely mailed). Ask every student if they have it
    - **Tuition credit (15% federal, 5.05% Ontario):** Four options — use now, carry forward, transfer to spouse, or transfer to parent. Transfer to parent maximum $5,000 base = $1,003 combined credit. **If graduation is imminent — carry forward. The future year value far exceeds the transfer**
    - **Moving expenses for students:** Deductible when moving to attend school full-time (against scholarship/grant income). Full employment rules apply when a graduate moves for their first job. Lease break penalties, hotel stays, and storage are all eligible — and almost always missed
    - **RESP withdrawals (EAP):** Taxable in the student's hands — not the parents'. At a student's income level, often results in near-zero tax. The entire RESP tax arbitrage strategy is built on this
    - **Student loan interest (Line 31900):** Federal interest eliminated April 2023. Provincial interest may still apply. 5-year carryforward
    - **First employment year after graduation:** Almost always produces a large refund — partial year income + employer annualized withholding + accumulated tuition carry-forwards = the most satisfying return you will prepare all season
    - **Co-op students:** Accumulate tuition credits during school terms, apply against co-op employment income. Moving expenses for co-op relocations follow employment rules
    - **Ontario LIFT Credit:** Eliminates Ontario tax for students with employment income under $30,000. Combined with federal BPA — most working students pay zero income tax
    - **Ontario Trillium Benefit:** Students in private rentals get 20% of rent. Students in university residence get $25 flat. Students at home get $0 for OEPTC (but still get OSTC if eligible)
    - **Treat student clients as long-term investments** — they become high-income earners within 5 years and refer everyone they know

??? question "5. A student who filed for three prior years now has $14,200 in federal tuition carry-forward credits on her NOA. She graduates and starts working at $88,000. She files her first employment year return. Her federal tax before applying the carry-forward is $6,800 (after BPA and other credits). What is her net federal tax, and how much carry-forward remains for 2026?"
    Federal tax before tuition: $6,800. Apply tuition carry-forward: $14,200 available, $6,800 needed. **Federal tax: $0**. Remaining federal tuition carry-forward: $14,200 − $6,800 = **$7,400 for 2026**. In 2026 at $88,000 full-year income, her federal tax will be approximately $12,000 before credits. ??? question "6. What is the tax treatment of a $15,000 RESP Education Assistance Payment vs. a $15,000 withdrawal of the original RESP contributions? Whose return does each amount appear on?"
    **EAP ($15,000):** The accumulated government grants (CESG) and investment income inside the RESP. **Fully taxable income in the student's hands.** Reported on the student's T1 via T4A Box 40 → Line 13010. At a student's typically low income, this is often sheltered by the BPA, tuition credits, and LIFT Credit — resulting in zero or minimal tax. **Return of contributions ($15,000):** The original after-tax dollars contributed by the subscriber (parents/grandparents). **Not taxable income for anyone.** The contributor already paid tax on this money before putting it in the plan — it comes back to them (or the student) without any tax consequence. Does not appear on any T1. The RESP's power is that all growth (which went in tax-sheltered) comes out taxed at the student's low rate, while the contributions come back tax-free.

??? question "7. Daniel moved from Toronto to London, Ontario in September to start a full-time MBA program. He receives a $24,000 research grant (T4A Box 104, taxable) as part of his program. His moving costs were $3,200. Can he deduct the moving expenses, and against what income? What is the deductible amount?"
    **Yes** — students can deduct eligible moving expenses when moving to attend school full-time, provided the new home is 40+ km closer to the school. The 40 km test: Toronto home to Ivey Business School (London) minus new London home to same school. Distance is well over 40 km. ✅ **Against what income:** Student moving expenses are deductible against **scholarship, fellowship, bursary, and research grant income** that is included in income. Daniel's $24,000 research grant (Box 104, taxable) qualifies as this income. **Deductible amount: $3,200** — well within the $24,000 grant income limit. At Daniel's 20.05% combined rate: $3,200 × 20.05% = **$642 in tax saved**. Note: student moving expenses cannot be deducted against summer employment income — only against school-related taxable income. This is the key distinction from the employment move rules.
