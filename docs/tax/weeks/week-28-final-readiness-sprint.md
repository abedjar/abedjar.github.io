??? success "The 28 Answers — Your Final Knowledge Confirmation"

    1. **29.65%** (federal 20.5% + Ontario 9.15% — net income $78,000 is in the $57,376–$102,894 combined bracket).

    2. **Repeat offender rate: 10% + 2%/month.** Penalty: ($5,000 × 10%) + ($5,000 × 2% × number of months late). On first month late: $10 + $10 = $600 base. Plus daily compound interest from May 1.

    3. **Net Income (23600)** = Total Income minus above-the-line deductions (RRSP, childcare, moving, etc.). Used for benefit calculations. **Taxable Income (26000)** = Net Income minus a few additional deductions (capital loss carryovers, northern residents, etc.). Used for applying tax bracket rates.

    4. The T4A Box 48 reports **gross fares ($52,000)** — that is the correct gross income on the T2125. Uber's service fee is then deducted as a business expense. Never report net deposits as gross income.

    5. **$2,000 × 1.38 = $2,760** (eligible dividend gross-up at 38%). The $2,760 goes on Line 12000. The DTC is $2,760 × 15.0198% = $414 on Line 40425.

    6. A **$30,000 capital gain × 50% = $15,000 taxable capital gain** reported on Line 12700. Only $15,000 is added to income and taxed at the marginal rate — not the full $30,000.

    7. Rental CCA **cannot create or increase a rental loss**. If net rental income before CCA is $0 or negative from operating expenses, CCA is claimed at $0 (the available CCA is preserved by not reducing the UCC — it simply isn't claimed that year).

    8. Maximum self-employment CPP = $7,735 (both halves, 2025). If two T4 employers each withheld CPP up to the maximum, the total exceeds the $3,867 employee maximum — the **over-contribution is refunded** on the T1 return.

    9. To move from $112,000 to the $114,750 bracket boundary (entering 26% zone): $112,000 is already below $114,750 — so bringing to the boundary of the next lower bracket requires getting to $114,750? No — going DOWN. To move from the 26% bracket into the 20.5% bracket: need to go below $114,751. At $112,000, already in the 20.5% bracket. To move to the 15% bracket: need to reach below $57,375. Required contribution: $112,000 − $57,375 = **$54,625** — but this exceeds a typical RRSP limit. So for a realistic scenario: at $112,000, you're in 20.5%/26% transition. To drop below $57,375: $54,625. To drop below $57,375 and into 15% bracket. More practically: the contribution that reaches the next bracket boundary below your current income.

    10. **The lower-income spouse** claims childcare. Exception: when the lower-income spouse is in full-time school, the higher-income spouse may claim, subject to weekly limits ($200/week per child for full-time enrollment).

    11. **Capital gain: $0** — the capital gain is fully exempt on direct donation of publicly traded securities to a registered charity. **Donation receipt: $18,000** (full FMV). Donation credit: $200 × 15% + $17,800 × 29% = $30 + $5,162 = **$5,192 federal credit**.

    12. The **12-month window** can be any consecutive 12-month period ending in the tax year — not necessarily January–December. It is better than the calendar year when a client had large expenses in the second half of one year and first half of the next — combining them into one window exceeds the 3% threshold more effectively and generates a larger credit.

    13. **No — never rely on T5008 Box 20 as the ACB.** Box 20 is unreliable — it may reflect the brokerage's internal average cost calculation, which differs from CRA's ACB rules (especially for DRIP investors, partial dispositions, and inherited securities). Always obtain original purchase records and calculate ACB independently.

    14. OAS clawback rate: **15%** on net income above **$90,997** (2025). Every dollar above $90,997 costs the senior $0.15 in OAS, on top of their marginal tax rate.

    15. **Income on T1: $48,000** (Canadian income only). The $12,000 Indian income was earned **before** she became a Canadian resident — it is pre-residency income and is excluded. **90% test:** Canadian $48,000 / total worldwide income during residency ≈ $48,000 / $48,100 (assuming small Indian savings interest during the residency period) = 99.8%. **Exceeds 90% — full credits, no pro-ration.** If she had significant Indian income during the Canadian residency period that brought the ratio below 90%, pro-ration would apply.

    16. T1135 is required when a Canadian resident holds **specified foreign property costing more than $100,000 CAD** at any point during the year. Failure to file penalty: **$25/day, minimum $100, maximum $2,500** (simple failure to file). Gross negligence penalty: up to **5% of the total cost of foreign property** — on a $500,000 property, this is $25,000.

    17. **Day one — immediately upon commencing rideshare activities.** Rideshare is classified as a taxi service under the Excise Tax Act. Taxi service providers have no small supplier exemption — they must register for HST before their first trip, regardless of revenue level.

    18. $35,000 actual non-eligible dividend × 1.15 (gross-up) = **$40,250 on Line 12000**. DTC = $40,250 × 9.0301% = **$3,637 on Line 40425**.

    19. Section 15(2): If a corporation loans money to a shareholder and the loan is **not repaid by December 31 of the calendar year following** the year the loan was made — the full loan amount is **added to the shareholder's personal income** for the year the loan was made. Example: loan in 2025, must repay by December 31, 2026, or $X is added to 2025 personal income.

    20. The RRSP (FMV at death) would normally be **fully included in the terminal return income** as a deemed disposition. When there is a surviving spouse who is the beneficiary, you can elect a **spousal rollover (Form T2019)** — the RRSP rolls to the spouse's RRSP with no immediate tax. The spouse pays tax gradually as they withdraw.

    21. **ACK4 = Accepted.** The return was successfully received and processed by CRA. This is the confirmation you want. Record the ACK4 number — it is your proof of filing.

    22. The T183 is the **Authorization for Electronic Filing** — the client's signed authorization for you to submit their return electronically. Without it, you have no documented permission from the client to file. Most software blocks transmission until a T183 signature date is entered. Must be signed before — never after — transmission. Retain for 6 years.

    23. **Two options:** (1) **CRA Payment Arrangement** — call CRA to set up monthly payments. Interest accrues but collection actions suspend while the plan is active. (2) **Taxpayer Relief Application (RC4288)** — if financial hardship justifies it, apply for cancellation of penalties and interest (not the underlying tax). **Immediate action:** **File the return now** — every day after April 30 without filing adds the late-filing penalty (5% + 1%/month for first-time offender). Filing stops the penalty clock even if the balance remains unpaid.

    24. A **superficial loss** is denied when the identical security is repurchased within **30 days before or after** the sale (by the taxpayer or an affiliated person). The denied loss is added to the ACB of the repurchased shares, deferring rather than eliminating it. To avoid: wait 31 days before repurchasing, or buy a different but similar security immediately.

    25. **What you do:** Identify the missed carrying charges specifically and factually. Quantify the recoverable amount. Offer to file T1-ADJ amendments going back up to 10 years. Let the client decide whether to proceed. **What you never say:** Anything characterizing the prior preparer's competence, intent, or professionalism. You don't know what they knew or what the client told them.

    26. **Your response:** *"A kitchen renovation doesn't qualify as a home office improvement — a home office deduction applies to the incremental cost of creating a dedicated workspace, not a general renovation. Filing this claim would be denied in any CRA review and would flag your entire return. I can't include it. Let me make sure we've captured every deduction you legitimately qualify for."* Document the conversation in the client file.

    27. **December 29** is approximately the last trading day for tax-loss harvesting. Equities settle T+2 (trade date + 2 business days). A trade on December 29 settles December 31 — within the tax year. A trade on December 30 settles January 2 — next tax year. Execute all tax-loss harvesting trades by December 29 at latest.

    28. **EFILE authorization is revoked — permanently if CRA pursues it.** Additionally, the preparer faces personal **civil penalties under Section 163.2** of the Income Tax Act: the greater of $1,000 or 50% of the understated tax. For knowing participation in filing false returns, **criminal prosecution** under Section 239 is possible: fines up to **200% of tax evaded** and/or **up to 5 years imprisonment**. Revocation of EFILE is effectively the end of the practice. These are not theoretical consequences. They have been applied to Canadian preparers.

## 28.7 The Professional Mindset Framework — Carrying This Forward

You have 28 weeks of preparation behind you. Before you begin, understand the mindset that sustains excellence over a career.

### The Four Principles of a Lasting Tax Practice

=== "Principle 1 — Accuracy Is Non-Negotiable"

    Every return you file is a legal document. Your client's financial relationship with the Canadian government is on that document. Your EFILE number links you to it permanently.

    **There are no "close enough" returns.** A deduction either qualifies or it doesn't. An income source is either reported or it isn't. The line numbers are either right or they're wrong.

    This is not perfectionism — perfectionism is paralyzing. This is precision. The precision to verify the slip through CRA My Account before filing. The precision to apply the 12-point review before transmitting. The precision to say "I don't know — let me research this" instead of filing based on a guess.

    Accuracy protects your clients. Accuracy protects you. Accuracy is the foundation of every other principle.

=== "Principle 2 — Curiosity Creates Value"

    The average preparer receives documents, enters data, and transmits. They see what the client brings.

    The exceptional preparer asks questions. They wonder what they're not being told. They notice that the T5 interest income seems low for a client with significant savings. They ask: *"Do you have any foreign bank accounts?"* They ask: *"Did you pay any advisory fees on your investment account?"* They ask: *"Did you move at all this year — even just to a new apartment?"*

    Every question has the potential to surface a deduction, a credit, or an income source. The preparer who asks more finds more. The clients who feel found — who leave your office with money they didn't know they were entitled to — become your most loyal advocates.

    **Stay curious. Ask one more question than you think you need to.**

=== "Principle 3 — Integrity Is Your Entire Business"

    Everything you have built — your reputation, your client relationships, your EFILE authorization, your professional standing — is held together by one thing: your integrity.

    Clients trust you with their most sensitive financial information. CRA trusts you to file accurate returns. The tax system trusts preparers to function as a bridge between taxpayers and the government.

    The moment you compromise your integrity — filing something you know is false, claiming deductions you know don't exist, participating in a client's fiction about their income — you have traded the only permanent asset your practice has for a short-term transaction with a client who doesn't deserve your risk.

    **No client, no fee, no relationship is worth your integrity.** The decline is always the right choice. The documentation is always worthwhile. The reputation that comes from 20 years of honest practice is irreplaceable. The reputation that comes from one compromised return is impossible to repair.

=== "Principle 4 — Every Client Is a Long-Term Relationship"

    The student you file for today — for $90 — is the senior executive whose return you prepare for $650 in fifteen years. The new immigrant family you serve with patience and cultural sensitivity today refers thirty members of their community over the next decade.

    Tax preparation is one of the few professional services that people need every single year, for their entire adult lives. The math is extraordinary: a client relationship that begins at age 25 and continues to age 75 represents fifty annual returns. At an average fee that grows with complexity from $125 to $400 — that's a relationship worth approximately $13,000 in lifetime fees from one person.

    The $90 student return is a $13,000 investment.

    **Treat every client as if you already know what they will become.** Because some of them will surprise you.

## 28.8 Your First Tax Season — What to Expect

### January

**Volume:** Slow. The T-slips haven't arrived yet. Use this time to run CRA My Account checks on prior clients, prepare intake forms, and send the January email.

**Common calls you'll receive:** "When do I need to come in?" (Answer: anytime February–April, though sooner is better for peace of mind). "When does my T4 arrive?" (Answer: by February 28, available in CRA My Account).

**Your priority:** Get every new client authorized in CRA My Account before February 1. The AFR functionality requires this authorization.

### February

**Volume:** Picks up. Clients with only T4s can be filed. Hold investment clients until after March 31.

**What will go wrong:** One client will bring a pile of disorganized documents and take 90 minutes instead of 45. One client will call asking about a missed T5 two weeks after you filed their return. One client will be stunned by a balance owing they expected to be a refund.

**How to handle it:** Apply Week 24. Calm. Organized. Solution-focused. These situations are normal — not crises.

### March

**Volume:** Peak. Every return you haven't filed yet needs to be done before April 30.

**The March trap:** Rushing. Returns filed in a hurry make errors. Returns with errors generate CRA letters. CRA letters generate client anxiety that you then spend hours managing.

**Your rule:** Never sacrifice your 12-point review to save 10 minutes. The CRA letter costs 3 hours to respond to. The review takes 12 minutes. The math is obvious.

### April 1–30

**Volume:** Sprint to the deadline. Stop accepting new simple clients after April 15 unless they have all documents ready. Complex returns need extra time — don't book complex clients in the final week.

**April 30:** Deadline day. EFILE servers are heavily loaded. File early in the day.

**After April 30:** The calm arrives. Follow up with clients on their NOAs. Start the post-season debrief.

### The Emotional Rhythm of Tax Season

There is a particular exhaustion that comes from filing 200 returns in 10 weeks. The focus required, the client interactions, the administrative load — it compounds. Experienced preparers plan for it:

- Schedule one completely free day per week (Saturday or Sunday — not both)
- Eat lunch away from your desk, even for 15 minutes
- Keep a "wins" list — returns where you found significant savings for clients. Read it on hard days
- Know that every difficult client today is part of a practice that will serve you for decades

**You are not in a sprint. You are building something that lasts.**

## 28.9 The Five Things You Will Know After Your First Season That You Cannot Learn From Any Course

There are things that only experience teaches. Here is what you will understand after your first real tax season that no curriculum can fully impart:

**1. How clients actually describe their situations.** They will say things like "I have some stocks" (which means anything from $200 in a TFSA to a $400,000 non-registered portfolio with 15 years of unrealized gains). Learning to translate client language into precise tax questions is a skill that only develops through hundreds of actual conversations.

**2. The pattern of errors you specifically make.** Every preparer has their blind spots. Some miss T3s because they don't ask about mutual funds. Some forget to pro-rate ON-BEN rent for mid-year arrivals. Some assume CPP over-contributions happen only with two T4s when a third employer slipped. After your first season, you will know your patterns. Add them to your 12-point review.

**3. What clients actually value.** It is almost never what you expect. The client who got the $12,000 refund barely said thank you — they were used to large refunds. The client who saved $340 from a carrying charge you found cried with gratitude. Value is subjective and relational. Learning to read what matters to each client is a lifelong practice.

**4. How to pace yourself.** You will discover that back-to-back appointments without buffer time is sustainable for about three days before your quality starts to slip. You will find your rhythm. Adjust after the first season. Book sustainably.

**5. That this work matters.** A new immigrant family who filed for the first time and received $9,000 in combined benefits and refunds. A senior whose OAS was preserved through a pension split that saved $2,000/year. A student who discovered a $12,000 tuition carry-forward she didn't know existed. A small business owner who stopped fearing CRA because you handled their review letter with complete professionalism.

These are not just tax returns. They are real improvements in real people's financial lives. Done with integrity and expertise, this work has genuine social value.
