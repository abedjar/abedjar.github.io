## 1.1 The Big Picture — What Income Tax Actually Is and Who Collects It

Income tax is, at its core, a simple idea executed with enormous complexity. The idea: people who earn income contribute a portion of it to fund the public services that make modern economic life possible — roads, hospitals, schools, courts, pensions, social safety nets.

You are about to become one of the professionals who helps Canadians navigate this contribution system correctly, completely, and in their own best interest.

In Canada, income tax is collected at **two levels simultaneously**, applied to the same income:

```
EVERY DOLLAR YOUR CLIENT EARNS
            │
     ┌──────┴──────┐
     ↓             ↓
FEDERAL TAX    PROVINCIAL TAX
(CRA collects) (CRA collects for Ontario)

Funds:         Funds:
• National     • Hospitals
  defence      • Schools
• EI system    • Provincial roads
• CPP          • Social services
• Federal      • Colleges and
  programs       universities
```

Both taxes are applied to the same income. Both are calculated independently — with their own rates, brackets, and credits. Both are filed on the **same single document**: the T1 General Income Tax and Benefit Return.

This is the return you will prepare for every client. Understanding that it simultaneously satisfies two governments' requirements explains why the T1 has so many sections, so many forms, and so many moving parts.

### Who Is the Canada Revenue Agency?

The **Canada Revenue Agency (CRA)** is the federal government agency that sits at the centre of your professional life. Everything you do as a tax preparer involves CRA — either filing with them, accessing their systems, responding to their correspondence, or explaining their rules to clients.

| CRA Function | What It Means for Your Practice |
|---|---|
| **Administers federal income tax** | Receives T1 returns, calculates what clients owe, issues assessments |
| **Administers Ontario provincial tax** | Collects Ontario tax on behalf of Ontario government (a key administrative agreement) |
| **Processes T-slips from employers/institutions** | Has most of your client's income information before they walk in your door |
| **Runs EFILE** | The system through which you electronically file returns for clients |
| **Operates CRA My Account** | The portal where you verify slips, RRSP room, carryforwards, and NOAs as an authorized representative |
| **Issues Notices of Assessment** | CRA's official confirmation of how it processed each return |
| **Administers benefit programs** | GST/HST credit, Canada Child Benefit, Ontario Trillium Benefit — all flow through CRA |
| **Enforces compliance** | Reviews suspicious claims, conducts audits, applies penalties |

!!! success "The single most important thing to understand about CRA as a professional preparer"
    CRA already has most of your client's income information before you touch a single document.

    Every employer files T4 slips directly with CRA by February 28. Every bank files T5 slips. Every investment firm files T3 and T5008 slips. Every pension plan files T4A slips. Every government program files its own slips. This data arrives at CRA's systems before it arrives in your client's mailbox.

    When you file a T1 return, you are not telling CRA something new. You are **confirming and completing a picture CRA has already partly drawn.** If your picture doesn't match theirs — their automated matching system identifies the discrepancy. Almost always, the client owes more tax. Sometimes there's a reassessment. Usually, it was avoidable.

    This is why **verifying all slips through CRA My Account before filing** is not optional professional courtesy — it is the core quality control step that separates professional preparers from people who are just entering data.

### The Quebec Exception — Know It, Then Move On

Quebec is the only province that administers its own income tax completely independently of CRA. Quebec residents file:
1. A **federal T1** with CRA
2. A separate **provincial TP-1** with Revenu Québec

Every other province, including Ontario, has delegated provincial tax administration to CRA. Ontario residents file one T1 that covers both layers.

This course is built around Ontario. Every rule, every credit, every example is Ontario-specific where it differs from federal. For Quebec clients: refer them to a preparer with Quebec expertise.

## 1.2 Canada's Progressive Tax System — The Most Misunderstood Concept in Personal Finance

Understanding this deeply — well enough to explain it clearly to a worried or confused client — is one of the first skills that defines a professional.

### What "Progressive" Means

A progressive tax system taxes higher income at higher rates. But — and this is the misunderstanding point — **those higher rates apply only to the income within that bracket, never to all income.**

The simplest mental model: **buckets.**

Picture a series of buckets stacked vertically. Each bucket has a label — the tax rate — and a capacity — the income range. Income fills the buckets from the bottom up. Each bucket is taxed at its own rate, never at a higher bucket's rate. Income that hasn't reached a higher bucket isn't taxed at that bucket's rate regardless of how much fills the bucket above it.

```
BUCKET 5: 33% rate  ←── Only income above $220,000 goes here
         ─────────
BUCKET 4: 29% rate  ←── Only income $158,520–$220,000 goes here
         ─────────
BUCKET 3: 26% rate  ←── Only income $114,751–$158,519 goes here
         ─────────
BUCKET 2: 20.5% rate ←── Only income $57,376–$114,750 goes here
         ─────────
BUCKET 1: 15% rate  ←── First $57,375 fills here
```

### 2025 Federal Tax Brackets

| Taxable Income Range | Federal Rate | Maximum Tax on This Portion |
|---|---|---|
| $0 – $57,375 | **15%** | $8,606 |
| $57,376 – $114,750 | **20.5%** | $11,762 |
| $114,751 – $158,519 | **26%** | $11,420 |
| $158,520 – $220,000 | **29%** | $17,822 |
| Over $220,000 | **33%** | On the excess |

### 2025 Ontario Tax Brackets

| Taxable Income Range | Ontario Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 – $51,446 | **5.05%** |
| $51,447 – $102,894 | **9.15%** |
| $102,895 – $150,000 | **11.16%** |
| $150,001 – $220,000 | **12.16%** |
| Over $220,000 | **13.16%** |

!!! warning "Brackets change every year — indexed to inflation"
    CRA adjusts bracket thresholds annually by an inflation factor. The figures above are the confirmed 2025 amounts. For every subsequent tax year, verify the current-year brackets from CRA's published T1 General package, released each January. Your tax software updates automatically — but you must understand what it's calculating, not just trust it blindly.

## 1.3 Marginal Rate vs. Effective Rate — Two Numbers You Explain to Clients Every Day

These two concepts are used constantly in tax planning conversations. Confusing them leads to wrong advice and confused clients.

### Marginal Rate — The Planning Number

**Definition:** The rate applied to the next (or last) dollar of income.

If your client's income is $75,000, their top federal bracket is 20.5%. Their **marginal federal rate is 20.5%**. This is the number used for all planning decisions:

- *"How much will my RRSP contribution save?"* → Multiply by marginal rate
- *"What will a $5,000 bonus cost me in tax?"* → Multiply by marginal rate
- *"How much of this freelance income do I keep?"* → Subtract marginal rate from 100%

### Effective Rate — The Perspective Number

**Definition:** The average rate actually paid across all income.

**Formula:** Total tax paid ÷ Total income = Effective rate

The effective rate is always **lower** than the marginal rate — because lower brackets tax the first dollars at lower rates. A client in the 33% federal bracket is not paying 33% on their entire income — only on the portion above $220,000.

### The Critical Distinction in Practice

| | Marginal Rate | Effective Rate |
|---|---|---|
| **Used for** | Planning decisions — RRSP value, raise impact, extra income cost | Understanding overall tax burden |
| **Direction** | Points upward with income | Always lower than marginal |
| **Changes with** | Each dollar that crosses a bracket | Changes gradually with total income |
| **Client question it answers** | "How much will THIS extra money cost me?" | "What percentage of my income do I really pay?" |

!!! example "Case — Sandra Asks Both Questions"
    Sandra earns $82,000 net income. She is thinking about taking on a contract that would pay $12,000. She also wants to understand her overall tax picture.

    **Marginal rate calculation:**
    At $82,000, her top brackets are: federal 20.5%, Ontario 9.15% = **combined 29.65%**
    Tax on the $12,000 contract: $12,000 × 29.65% = **$3,558 in tax**
    She keeps $12,000 − $3,558 = **$8,442 after-tax** from the contract

    **Effective rate calculation:**
    Let's say her total combined tax is approximately $15,800 (before credits — simplified).
    Effective rate: $15,800 ÷ $82,000 = **19.3%**

    Sandra's response: *"So I pay 19% overall but 30% on the new contract?"*

    Your answer: *"Exactly right. The first $51,446 you earn is taxed at about 20%. Everything above that — including this contract — is taxed at nearly 30% combined. This is how progressive taxation works: the more you earn, the higher the rate on the additional dollars — but never on the ones you already earned."*

    Sandra now understands something fundamental about the system she lives in. That understanding builds trust.

## 1.4 The Combined Rate Table — Ontario's Real Tax Rates

Clients always want their "real" rate — combining both layers of tax. This table is what you reference in every planning conversation.

| Taxable Income | Federal | Ontario | **Combined Rate** |
|---|---|---|---|
| $0 – $51,446 | 15% | 5.05% | **20.05%** |
| $51,447 – $57,375 | 15% | 9.15% | **24.15%** |
| $57,376 – $102,894 | 20.5% | 9.15% | **29.65%** |
| $102,895 – $114,750 | 20.5% | 11.16% | **31.66%** |
| $114,751 – $150,000 | 26% | 11.16% | **37.16%** |
| $150,001 – $158,519 | 26% | 12.16% | **38.16%** |
| $158,520 – $220,000 | 29% | 13.16% | **42.16%** |
| Over $220,000 | 33% | 13.16% | **46.16%** |

!!! tip "The four numbers to memorize — you will use them every week"
    These four combined rates form the backbone of almost every planning conversation:

    - **20.05%** → Income below $51,446. Students, part-timers, low-income earners. Every dollar of RRSP saves ~20 cents.
    - **29.65%** → Income $57,376–$102,894. The "working professional" zone. Most T4 employees. Every dollar of RRSP saves ~30 cents.
    - **37.16%** → Income $102,895–$150,000. Senior professionals, managers, business owners. Every dollar of RRSP saves ~37 cents.
    - **46.16%** → Income above $220,000. Top earners. Every dollar of RRSP saves ~46 cents.

    When a client asks "how much will my RRSP save me?" — you need to answer this in under 10 seconds. With this table memorized, you can.

### The Ontario Surtax — The Hidden Additional Layer

Ontario adds a surtax on top of Ontario tax when the Ontario tax bill exceeds certain thresholds:

| Ontario Basic Tax | Additional Surtax |
|---|---|
| Above **$5,315** | 20% on the excess |
| Above **$6,802** | Additional 36% on the excess above $6,802 |

The surtax effectively pushes Ontario's top rates higher than the simple bracket table suggests. For clients earning roughly $90,000+ in Ontario, the first surtax tier kicks in. For clients above approximately $100,000, both tiers apply. This is covered in depth in Week 15 — for now, know it exists.

## 1.5 The T1 Return — The Funnel From Income to Refund

The **T1 General Income Tax and Benefit Return** is the single document that every Canadian individual uses to report their income, claim their deductions and credits, and calculate their final tax result.

Think of it as a funnel: wide at the top, narrow at the bottom. Everything flows in at the top. One number comes out at the bottom.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║    TOTAL INCOME  (Line 15000)                        ║
║    Everything earned from every source               ║
╚═══════════════════════╤══════════════════════════════╝
                        │ Subtract deductions
                        ↓
╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║    NET INCOME  (Line 23600)                          ║
║    The Master Lever — drives tax AND benefits        ║
╚═══════════════════════╤══════════════════════════════╝
                        │ Subtract additional deductions
                        ↓
╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║    TAXABLE INCOME  (Line 26000)                      ║
║    What the tax brackets are applied to              ║
╚═══════════════════════╤══════════════════════════════╝
                        │ Apply federal brackets
                        │ Apply Ontario brackets
                        │ Subtract non-refundable credits
                        ↓
╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║    TOTAL PAYABLE  (Line 43500)                       ║
║    Combined federal + Ontario tax owed this year     ║
╚═══════════════════════╤══════════════════════════════╝
                        │ Subtract tax already withheld
                        ↓
╔═════════════════════════════╦════════════════════════╗
║  REFUND  (Line 48400)       ║  BALANCE OWING         ║
║  If more was withheld       ║  (Line 48500)          ║
║  than owed                  ║  If less was withheld  ║
╚═════════════════════════════╩════════════════════════╝
```

Every T1 you will ever prepare follows this exact flow. The details change. The types of income differ. The deductions and credits vary. But the funnel is identical for every return.

## 1.6 The Six Most Important Line Numbers — Know These Without Looking

Six line numbers appear on virtually every return. Know them — not just their names, but their purpose, what they control, and what they signal when something looks wrong.

### Line 15000 — Total Income

**Every dollar earned from every source, before any deductions.**

This is the widest point of the funnel. Wages, pensions, investment income, rental income, self-employment income — it all aggregates here. Some lenders use this number for mortgage qualification. CRA uses it as the starting point for all calculations.

**As a professional:** When clients say "I made about $65,000 this year," they are describing what they expect on Line 15000. Verify this against CRA My Account before assuming they're right.

### Line 23600 — Net Income ← The Master Lever

**Total Income minus above-the-line deductions.**

This is the most strategically important number on the entire return. It is the number that:

- Determines **federal and Ontario Age Amount** (phased out above $44,325/$42,335)
- Determines **GST/HST credit** amount and eligibility
- Determines **Canada Child Benefit (CCB)** — calculated on Adjusted Family Net Income
- Triggers **OAS clawback** when it exceeds $90,997
- Controls **spousal credit** calculations
- Determines **Ontario Trillium Benefit** amounts
- Affects **LIFT Credit** eligibility for low-income workers

When you reduce Net Income through an RRSP contribution, you don't just reduce tax. You trigger improvements across multiple benefit programs simultaneously. A $10,000 RRSP might generate $2,965 in income tax savings AND $1,900 in additional annual CCB AND restore a partially phased-out Age Amount. This compounding effect — the reason Net Income is called the "master lever" — is the most powerful concept in personal tax planning.

**The professional implication:** Always ask yourself: *"What happens to their benefits if I reduce their Net Income?"* Not just: *"How much tax does this RRSP save?"*

### Line 26000 — Taxable Income

**Net Income minus a few additional deductions.**

This is what the tax brackets are applied to. For most clients, Net Income and Taxable Income are identical — the additional deductions between them (capital loss carryovers, northern residents deduction, LCGE) are not universally applicable.

**The critical distinction:** Benefits are calculated on **Net Income**, not Taxable Income. A deduction that reduces Net Income reduces both tax and benefits simultaneously. A deduction that only reduces Taxable Income (like carrying forward capital losses from Line 25300) reduces tax but does nothing for benefits. Same dollar amount. Very different outcomes.

### Line 43500 — Total Payable

**The combined federal and Ontario tax owing for the year — before credit for withholding.**

This is the total of: federal net tax + Ontario net tax + self-employment CPP (employee+employer halves) + any social benefits repayment.

It is the real tax bill. What comes next (Line 43700) reduces it based on how much was already paid.

### Line 43700 — Total Income Tax Deducted

**Tax already paid at source throughout the year.**

Every paycheque, employers remit a portion directly to CRA on the employee's behalf. Every pension payment, every RRIF withdrawal — tax is withheld. This line is the sum of all Box 22 amounts from every T4 and T4A the client received, plus any instalment payments made.

This is not money the client is paying at tax time. It is money they already paid — continuously, all year. Tax time is when they find out if they paid the right amount.

### Line 48400 / 48500 — Refund or Balance Owing

```
Line 43500 (Total Payable) 
    MINUS 
Line 43700 (Already Paid) 
    = 
POSITIVE = BALANCE OWING (Line 48500) — client owes more
NEGATIVE = REFUND (Line 48400) — client overpaid, gets money back
```

This is the only number most clients think about. Understanding that it's simply the gap between estimated and actual tax — not a judgment about whether they did something right or wrong — is a key client education moment.

## 1.7 Why Net Income Is the Master Lever — The Full Picture

This concept is important enough to examine deeply. It is the insight that separates a basic preparer from a planning-oriented professional.

Canada's tax system is designed so that income-tested benefits phase out as income rises. Most of these phase-outs are calculated on **Net Income (Line 23600)** — not gross income, not taxable income, not take-home pay. Net Income after deductions.

This creates a compounding effect: every dollar you reduce from Net Income simultaneously:
1. Reduces income tax (at the marginal rate)
2. Improves benefit eligibility (at the benefit phase-out rate)

The two effects added together often exceed the marginal tax rate alone — sometimes dramatically.

### The Four Most Common Benefit Phase-Outs Driven by Net Income

| Benefit | Phase-Out Rate | Threshold (2025) |
|---|---|---|
| Canada Child Benefit (1 child) | 13.5% | $36,502 AFNI |
| Canada Child Benefit (2+ children) | 19% | $36,502 AFNI |
| GST/HST Credit | 5% | $44,324 net income |
| Age Amount | 15% | $44,325 net income |
| OAS (seniors) | 15% | $90,997 net income |

### The Multiplier Effect in Practice

!!! example "Case — The RRSP Worth More Than Its Face Value"
    **Client:** Helen, 36. Net income before RRSP: $48,000. Two children ages 5 and 9.

    She is considering a $5,000 RRSP contribution.

    **Direct tax saving:**
    $5,000 × 29.65% combined rate = **$1,483**

    **Indirect CCB improvement:**
    Helen's AFNI drops from $48,000 to $43,000.
    Both levels are above the $36,502 CCB phase-out threshold.
    Phase-out reduction decreases: $5,000 × 19% (2-child rate) = **$950 additional CCB per year**, paid as approximately $79/month going forward.

    **Ontario Trillium Benefit (OEPTC component):**
    Modest improvement from lower income — approximately **$45/year**

    **Total annual value of the $5,000 RRSP:**

    | Benefit | Annual Value |
    |---|---|
    | Income tax saved | $1,483 |
    | Additional CCB (recurring annually) | $950 |
    | OTB improvement | $45 |
    | **Total** | **$2,478** |

    The RRSP contributes $5,000 to a registered plan and immediately generates **$2,478 in annual value** — an immediate effective return of **49.6%**.

    Compare to: putting $5,000 in a savings account earning 4% = $200/year.

    This is not a special case. It is a routine example for any parent with two children earning between $36,502 and $80,000. Millions of Canadians in this exact situation are not making this RRSP contribution — because no one explained the math.

    **That is the professional gap you fill.**

!!! example "Case — The Senior Whose RRSP Saves Far More Than Expected"
    **Client:** George, 71, just converted to a RRIF. His net income: $93,000 (DB pension $55,000, CPP $13,000, OAS $8,300, RRIF minimum $16,700).

    George wants to understand his OAS clawback.

    OAS clawback threshold: $90,997. George's excess: $93,000 − $90,997 = $2,003. Clawback: $2,003 × 15% = **$300 clawed back from his OAS**.

    *"George, your income is only $2,003 above the clawback threshold. If we can reduce your Net Income by $2,003 — through a carrying charge deduction, advisory fee deduction, or any other available item — you preserve your full OAS and save both the clawback and the income tax on that $2,003. Every dollar of deduction above Line 23600 is working double duty here."*

    George has investment advisory fees on his non-registered account that he has never claimed. At $2,400/year — more than enough to eliminate the clawback entirely. This one question saves George $300 in OAS plus approximately $595 in combined income tax savings = **$895 in annual savings from a 2-minute intake question.**

    Net Income as the master lever: this is what it means in practice.

## 1.9 The T1 Filing System — How Returns Are Submitted

### Two Ways to File

| Method | Who Uses It | How |
|---|---|---|
| **EFILE** | Professional tax preparers (that is you) | Submit electronically through CRA-certified tax software |
| **NETFILE** | Individual self-filers | Submit directly from consumer tax software |
| **Paper filing** | Rare — specific situations | Mail T1 to CRA tax centre |

As a professional preparer filing more than 5 returns for compensation, you are **legally required to EFILE.** Paper filing is not permitted for professional preparers.

EFILE requires:
- CRA EFILE registration (an authorization number CRA issues to registered preparers)
- CRA-certified professional tax software (TaxCycle, ProFile, AdvTax, etc.)
- A signed T183 form from the client before each submission (their authorization for you to file)
- An ACK (acknowledgement) number from CRA confirming receipt

### The T183 — Your Authorization to File

The T183 is a one-page form that the client signs before you transmit their return. It confirms:
- They reviewed the return
- The information is accurate to their knowledge
- They authorize you to file electronically on their behalf

**Never transmit without a signed T183.** It is your legal protection if the client later claims they didn't authorize the filing. Store all signed T183s for six years.

### What Happens After You File

```
You transmit via EFILE
        ↓
CRA receives and validates (minutes to hours)
        ↓
CRA sends ACK confirmation (your proof of filing)
        ↓
CRA processes the return (2 days to 2 weeks for EFILE)
        ↓
CRA sends Notice of Assessment (NOA) to client
        ↓
Refund deposited or balance owing due April 30
```

The NOA is CRA's official statement of what they assessed. If their numbers differ from yours — investigate. If you filed correctly and they made an error — file a Notice of Objection within 90 days. If the error was yours — file a T1-ADJ (amendment).

## 1.10 The Four Questions Every Client Asks — Know These Cold

Every new client in your first weeks will ask some version of these four questions. Your ability to answer them clearly, confidently, and briefly determines whether the client feels they are in the hands of a professional.

### Question 1: "Why do I owe money when my employer takes tax off every paycheque?"

**What they're really asking:** "Did I do something wrong?"

**Your answer:**
*"Your employer withholds tax based on an estimate of what you'll earn for the full year. If you had additional income they didn't know about — a side job, investment income, a second job, or an RRSP withdrawal — that extra income had no withholding. Or sometimes an employer withholds at a lower rate after a raise and doesn't catch up through the year. Either way, it's not a mistake you made. It's a gap between estimated and actual tax. Let's find out exactly why and, more importantly, how to prevent it next year."*

**The most common causes of unexpected balances:**
- Side/freelance income (no withholding)
- Investment income (no withholding unless specifically requested)
- Two employers in one year (each withheld as if the other didn't exist)
- RRSP withdrawal (withholding rate often too low)
- Parental EI benefits (withheld at a low flat rate)
- Started a new, higher-paying job mid-year

### Question 2: "I got a raise. Am I going to pay more tax on all my income now?"

**What they're really asking:** "Is this raise actually going to hurt me?"

**Your answer:**
*"No — and this is one of the most common misunderstandings about how Canadian taxes work. Only the income above each threshold is taxed at the higher rate. If your raise pushes some of your income into the next bracket, only that extra portion is taxed at the higher rate. Your previous salary is taxed exactly the same as it was before. A raise always increases your take-home pay — always. You keep every dollar above the bracket threshold minus the higher rate on just that portion."*

**The math to show them (if they want it):**
If someone earning $57,000 gets a $3,000 raise to $60,000 — the first $375 of the raise ($57,375 − $57,000 = $375) stays in the 15% federal bracket. The remaining $2,625 enters the 20.5% bracket. The difference in tax on those $2,625: approximately $143. They take home $2,857 from the $3,000 raise. They are better off. They are always better off taking a raise.

### Question 3: "What's the difference between a deduction and a credit?"

**What they're really asking:** "Which one is better?"

**Your answer:**
*"A deduction reduces your income before tax is calculated — so its value depends on your tax bracket. At a 30% rate, a $1,000 deduction saves you $300. A credit reduces the actual tax you owe directly — a $300 credit saves exactly $300 regardless of your income. The higher your income, the more valuable a deduction is. Credits are equally valuable to anyone who owes tax."*

**The practical example:**
- An RRSP contribution of $10,000 is a **deduction**. At 29.65% combined, it saves $2,965. At 46.16% combined, it saves $4,616.
- The Basic Personal Amount (BPA) is a **credit** — specifically $16,129 × 15% = $2,419 for everyone who owes at least that much federal tax.

### Question 4: "My neighbour gets a huge refund every year. Why is mine smaller?"

**What they're really asking:** "Am I doing something wrong? Am I missing something?"

**Your answer:**
*"A refund isn't a measure of good tax planning — it's simply the difference between how much tax was withheld from your paycheques throughout the year and how much was actually owed. If your neighbour's employer withheld more than necessary, they get a bigger refund — but they also had less money in their pocket every month. A very large refund often means you've been giving the government an interest-free loan all year long. What we're aiming for is accuracy — as close to zero balance as possible. That said, let me look at whether there are deductions or credits you might be missing that would legitimately reduce your tax bill."*

**The reframe:** Shift the client from "size of refund = success" to "accuracy = success" and "minimizing legitimate tax = success." This positions you as someone focused on their actual financial outcome, not on generating a number that feels good but represents their own over-withheld money.

## 1.11 The Complete T1 Flow — A Real Client from First to Last Line

Let's trace every step with Sandra Mitchell, a 42-year-old Ontario engineer.

**Sandra's 2025 situation:**
- Salary: $88,000 (T4 Box 14)
- CPP withheld: $3,867 (T4 Box 16)
- EI withheld: $1,049 (T4 Box 18)
- Tax withheld: $21,000 (T4 Box 22)
- Union dues: $1,200 (T4 Box 44)
- Bank interest: $740 (T5 Box 13)

─────────────────────────────────

### Reading Sandra's Return With Professional Eyes

**Why the large refund?**
Sandra's employer withheld $21,000 in tax — calculated on her full $88,740 income without knowing about her $11,000 RRSP contribution or $1,200 in union dues. Those two deductions reduced her taxable income by $12,200 and her actual tax bill by approximately $12,200 × 29.65% = $3,617. Her refund reflects the combination of over-withholding plus those deductions.

**The RRSP math isolated:**
Without the RRSP contribution, Sandra's net income would be $87,540 and her tax would be approximately $13,139 + ($11,000 × 29.65%) = $13,139 + $3,262 = $16,401.
Tax withheld: $21,000.
Refund without RRSP: $21,000 − $16,401 = $4,599.
Refund with RRSP: $7,861.
**The RRSP is responsible for $3,262 of her refund** — money she gets back that she would have paid in tax.

**What about Sandra's benefits?**
At $76,540 net income:
- GST/HST credit: above $44,324 threshold → partially phased out → approximately $42/year (almost nothing)
- OTB (OEPTC): 20% × $1,800 × 12 = $4,320 deemed property tax → approximately **$987/year** in OTB payments
- Single, no children → no CCB

Sandra's $987 in annual OTB payments — about $82/month — come directly from having filed her return and from her net income being at its current level. Without the return, she gets nothing.

## 1.12 Ontario-Specific Items You Need to Know From Day One

### The Ontario Basic Personal Amount — Not the Same as Federal

Federal BPA: **$16,129** → credit of $2,419
Ontario BPA: **$11,865** → credit of $599

Ontario's BPA is about $4,264 lower. This means Ontario residents pay Ontario tax on more income than they pay federal tax on. Many clients (and some preparers) assume the credits are the same — they are not.

Always use:
- Federal BPA on Schedule 1
- Ontario BPA on ON428
- Never mix them

### The December 31 Province Rule

Where a client lives on December 31 determines their province of residence for the **entire tax year** — not just from that date. A client who moved from BC to Ontario on December 20 is an **Ontario resident** for the full year. They file ON428 and receive all Ontario credits.

This rule surprises clients who moved during the year. The explanation: "Canadian tax is assessed on a calendar year basis. Your province on the last day of the year determines which provincial return you file for that year."

### Ontario-Only Benefits Most Canadians Don't Know They're Entitled To

| Benefit | Who Gets It | Annual Value |
|---|---|---|
| Ontario Trillium Benefit (OTB) | Ontario residents who rent or pay property tax | $345–$2,400+ depending on situation |
| LIFT Credit | Low-income workers with employment income | Up to $900 Ontario tax eliminated |
| Senior Homeowners' Property Tax Grant | Ontario homeowners 64+ | Up to $500 |

These are real cash payments that hundreds of thousands of Ontario residents miss every year because they don't know they need to file Form ON-BEN (for OTB) or that they qualify for LIFT. Every Ontario client should have these assessed — automatically, without them needing to ask.

## 1.13 Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **T1 General** | Canada's individual income tax and benefit return — the document filed for every person |
| **CRA** | Canada Revenue Agency — administers federal and Ontario income tax |
| **EFILE** | The electronic system professional preparers use to submit returns |
| **NOA** | Notice of Assessment — CRA's official response after processing a return |
| **Progressive tax** | System where higher income is taxed at higher rates — but only on the portion within each bracket |
| **Marginal rate** | The rate on the next (or last) dollar of income — the planning rate |
| **Effective rate** | Average rate across all income — total tax ÷ total income |
| **Deduction** | Reduces income before tax is calculated — value scales with marginal rate |
| **Non-refundable credit** | Reduces tax directly at a fixed rate — cannot generate a refund on its own |
| **Refundable credit** | Reduces tax — and generates a cash refund even if no tax is owed |
| **Total Income (15000)** | All income from all sources before deductions |
| **Net Income (23600)** | Total Income minus deductions — the master lever for both tax and benefits |
| **Taxable Income (26000)** | Net Income minus additional deductions — what brackets are applied to |
| **Total Payable (43500)** | Combined federal + Ontario tax owed for the year |
| **Tax withheld (43700)** | Tax already paid at source through employer withholding or instalment payments |
| **Refund (48400)** | Money returned when more was withheld than owed |
| **Balance owing (48500)** | Money owed when less was withheld than owed |
| **T183** | Client authorization for professional to file electronically — mandatory before EFILE |
| **ACK number** | Acknowledgement number from CRA confirming successful EFILE transmission |

## 1.14 Case Studies — Why This System Matters for Real People

### Case A — The Returning Student Who Thought Filing Was Pointless

**Client:** Ahmed, 22, graduated in April, started working at $62,000/year in May. He worked 8 months. He came to you reluctantly — "I don't make much, do I even need to file?"

**The reality:**
- T4 employment income (8 months): $41,333
- Tax withheld: $8,900 (employer annualized the full $62,000 salary)

Actual tax on $41,333 at combined rates: approximately $4,100 after credits.

**Refund from over-withholding alone: $4,800**

But Ahmed also has:
- Prior tuition carry-forward from 4 years of university: $9,800 in accumulated federal tuition credits
- Applied against his $4,100 federal tax: **wipes it out entirely**

**Total refund: $8,900 withheld → $0 tax owing = $8,900 refund**

Ahmed came in thinking he owed nothing and didn't need to file. He left with $8,900 in his bank account that would have disappeared permanently if he had waited another year to claim the tuition credits (they carry forward but don't improve without filing).

He called four university friends that afternoon.

### Case B — The Same Income, Very Different Results

Two clients. Both 34. Both earn $54,000 in employment income. Both are Ontario residents.

| | Maria | Carlos |
|---|---|---|
| Marital status | Single, no children | Married, 2 children ages 3 and 6 |
| RRSP contribution | $0 | $7,500 |
| Income-tested benefits | None significant | CCB, GST/HST |

**Maria's result:**
Net income: $54,000. Combined tax (approx., after credits): $9,800. Refund: depends on withholding.
GST/HST credit: $54,000 > $44,324 threshold → approximately $49/year (nearly phased out)
OTB: approximately $600–$900 depending on rent.

**Carlos's result:**
Net income: $54,000 − $7,500 RRSP = **$46,500**
Tax saving from RRSP: $7,500 × 29.65% = **$2,224**

But Carlos also has two children. With AFNI of $46,500 (still above $36,502 but lower than without RRSP):
CCB for 2 children: maximum $14,357 − [($46,500 − $36,502) × 19%] = $14,357 − $1,900 = **$12,457/year in CCB**

Without RRSP, AFNI would be $54,000:
CCB: $14,357 − [($54,000 − $36,502) × 19%] = $14,357 − $3,325 = **$11,032/year**

**RRSP benefit for Carlos from CCB improvement alone: $12,457 − $11,032 = $1,425/year additional CCB**

**Total annual value of Carlos's $7,500 RRSP:**
- Tax savings: $2,224
- Additional CCB: $1,425
- **Total: $3,649** — a 48.7% return on $7,500

Maria and Carlos earn the same income. Carlos uses the system. The difference in their annual financial outcomes: thousands of dollars — from understanding one concept that takes five minutes to explain.

### Case C — The Non-Filer Who Was Actually Owed Money for Three Years

**Client:** Rosa, 42, cleaned offices for three years as an employee but never filed because "I don't earn enough to pay tax."

She's right that she owes no tax. She earned approximately $22,000/year. After the BPA, she's below the tax threshold. But for three years:

- **GST/HST credit unclaimed:** $519/year × 3 = **$1,557**
- **Ontario Trillium Benefit:** She paid rent. $900/year × 3 = **$2,700**
- **Tax withheld by employer and never refunded:** $1,100/year × 3 = **$3,300** (her employer withheld based on annual projections even though she owed nothing)

**Rosa is owed approximately $7,557 from three years of not filing.**

CRA does not proactively send you money you're entitled to. You must file to receive it. Benefits can be retroactively claimed up to 10 years — but each year you delay is another year of compounding loss.

Rosa is not unusual. Hundreds of thousands of low-income Canadians leave refunds and benefits unclaimed every year because they believe the false narrative that you only file if you owe money.

**Your mission: file everyone.** The tax system has built-in transfers for low-income Canadians — but only for those who claim them.

## 1.15 Week 1 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - Canada has **two layers of income tax** — federal and provincial — both calculated on the same income and filed on the single T1 General return. Ontario delegates provincial collection to CRA
    - **CRA has most of your client's income data before you touch it.** T4s, T5s, T3s, T4As all flow to CRA from issuers. Verification through CRA My Account before filing is professional standard
    - Canada uses a **progressive tax system** — higher rates apply only to income within each bracket. A raise never reduces take-home pay
    - **Marginal rate** = rate on the next dollar of income → used for planning decisions. **Effective rate** = total tax ÷ total income → for understanding the overall picture
    - **Four key combined Ontario rates to memorize:** 20.05%, 29.65%, 37.16%, 46.16%
    - **The T1 flows:** Total Income (15000) → Net Income (23600) → Taxable Income (26000) → Total Payable (43500) → Refund (48400) or Balance Owing (48500)
    - **Net Income (Line 23600) is the master lever.** It drives both income tax AND benefit eligibility — CCB, GST/HST credit, OAS clawback, Age Amount, spousal credit, OTB. Reducing it through an RRSP often generates more total value than the tax saving alone
    - **Every eligible person should file** — including low-income earners with no tax owing. GST/HST credit ($519+), Ontario Trillium Benefit ($600–$2,400+), withheld tax refunds, and RRSP room accumulation are real money left permanently on the table by non-filers
    - **Ontario-specific from day one:** Ontario BPA ($11,865) is lower than federal BPA ($16,129). Province of residence = December 31. Ontario Trillium Benefit, LIFT Credit, and Senior Property Tax Grant exist and are routinely missed

??? question "1. Your client earns $78,000 in Ontario. They want to know their 'real' combined tax rate on the next dollar of income they earn — for example from a freelance contract. What is it, and how do you explain the concept in plain language?"
    Their combined marginal rate is **29.65%** — they are in the federal 20.5% bracket (income $57,376–$114,750) and Ontario's 9.15% bracket (income $51,447–$102,894). Together: 20.5% + 9.15% = 29.65%. Plain language explanation: *"For every additional dollar you earn — from a freelance project, a bonus, or investment income — you keep about 70 cents and pay about 30 cents in combined federal and Ontario tax. Your first $57,375 was taxed at about 20% combined. Everything above that is in the 30% zone. This is why an RRSP contribution at your income level is particularly powerful — every $1,000 you put in saves you about $297 in combined tax."*

??? question "2. Sandra's 2025 net income is $52,000. She contributes $6,000 to her RRSP, reducing net income to $46,000. She has one child, age 7. What is the total annual value of that RRSP contribution — including both the direct tax saving AND the CCB improvement?"
    **Direct tax saving:** $6,000 × 24.15% combined rate (net income $52,000 crosses from the $51,447 boundary — most of the $6,000 is in the 9.15% Ontario bracket overlapping with the 15% federal = 24.15%) = approximately $1,449. **CCB improvement:** Sandra's AFNI drops from $52,000 to $46,000. Both are above the $36,502 phase-out threshold. Phase-out rate for 1 child = 13.5%. Improvement: $6,000 × 13.5% = **$810/year more CCB** (approximately $67.50/month). **Total annual value: $1,449 tax + $810 CCB = $2,259** — a 37.7% return on $6,000. The actual combined rate at this income including the benefit multiplier is approximately 37.7% — significantly higher than the nominal 24.15% rate suggests. This is the Net Income master lever in action.

??? question "3. A client says 'I got a $5,000 raise last month and my take-home went up by less than I expected — I think getting this raise pushed me into a higher tax bracket.' They earn $114,000 after the raise. What do you explain?"
    Their concern about bracket penalty is partially correct but misunderstood. At $114,000, they crossed the Ontario bracket boundary at $102,895 — from 9.15% to 11.16% Ontario — and the federal $114,750 boundary is approaching. But only the income *above* the threshold is taxed at the higher rate. If the raise crossed them into the 11.16% Ontario bracket, only the portion above $102,895 is taxed at 11.16% — their income below $102,895 is completely unaffected. The mathematical result: their take-home is lower per dollar on the raise portion — but higher overall than before the raise. They are always better off with the raise. If they want, show them the math: $5,000 raise at combined 37.16% rate = approximately $1,858 in tax, keeping $3,142 additional take-home. Their anxiety is understandable but the raise made them financially better off, not worse.

??? question "4. Why is a $10,000 RRSP contribution more valuable to a client with two young children at $45,000 income than it is to a single client with no children at the same $45,000 income?"
    Both clients save the same income tax on the $10,000 RRSP: approximately $10,000 × 24.15% = $2,415. But the client with two young children has a second dimension of value: their **Canada Child Benefit is calculated on Adjusted Family Net Income (AFNI)**. At $45,000, they are above the $36,502 CCB phase-out threshold. Their RRSP reduces AFNI by $10,000. At the 2-child phase-out rate of 19%: $10,000 × 19% = **$1,900/year in additional CCB** going forward. The single client with no children has no CCB — so they receive only the $2,415 tax saving. The parent receives $2,415 + $1,900 = **$4,315 in total annual value** — 79% more than the single client on the same income with the same RRSP contribution. This multiplier is why understanding Net Income as a benefits driver — not just a tax driver — is one of the most valuable insights in this profession.

??? question "5. Patricia has never filed a tax return. She is 25, earns $18,000 from part-time work, and had $600 withheld. She says 'I don't earn enough to owe tax so there's no point.' Name five specific financial consequences of not filing."
    (1) **Lost GST/HST credit:** At $18,000 income, Patricia qualifies for the full annual credit of $519/year. Not filing means zero quarterly payments. Each year of non-filing is $519 permanently lost (CRA can retroactively pay for up to 10 years, but only if she files). (2) **Lost Ontario Trillium Benefit:** If she rents an apartment, she qualifies for OTB. At her income, approximately $600–$900/year in OEPTC alone. Not filing = no payments. (3) **Lost tax refund:** $600 was withheld. With $18,000 income and the BPA ($16,129), she owes approximately $1,871 × 15% = $281 in federal tax − credits ≈ $0 federal tax. Most of the $600 withholding is a refund. CRA will not proactively return it — she must file to claim it. (4) **Zero RRSP contribution room accumulating** — incorrect. Room still accumulates based on filed returns. Actually: room is calculated based on filed earned income. CRA assesses RRSP room from filed returns. If she doesn't file, CRA may still track it based on T4 data — but the clean filing history is what confirms it unambiguously. More accurately: (4) **No official RRSP room confirmation** — without a filed return and NOA, her RRSP room isn't formally confirmed, creating risk of over-contribution confusion later. (5) **No filing history for life events** — mortgage applications, immigration sponsorship, benefits programs, income verification for legal proceedings — all require filed NOAs. A gap in filing history creates complications at exactly the moments when documentation matters most.

??? question "6. What is the difference between a non-refundable credit and a refundable credit? Give one example of each from the Ontario T1 system."
    A **non-refundable credit** reduces tax owing — but cannot reduce it below zero. If a client owes $500 in combined tax and has $800 in non-refundable credits, their tax is reduced to $0 — but they do not receive the extra $300. Examples: Basic Personal Amount (creates a credit that shelters the first $16,129 of income from federal tax), Age Amount, Disability Amount, medical expense credit, charitable donation credit — all are non-refundable. A **refundable credit** reduces tax — and if it exceeds the tax owing, the excess is paid out as a refund. Examples: GST/HST credit ($519/year for a single person, paid quarterly regardless of tax owing), Ontario Trillium Benefit (paid monthly), Canada Child Benefit (paid monthly). A client with zero income and zero tax can still receive thousands of dollars from refundable credits and benefits. This is why filing is valuable even for people who owe nothing — the refundable credits are available to anyone who files and meets the eligibility criteria.

??? question "7. The T1 has three key income lines: Total Income (15000), Net Income (23600), and Taxable Income (26000). Which one drives income tax? Which one drives benefit calculations? And if you had to choose only one deduction that reduces both simultaneously, what would it be?"
    **Taxable Income (26000)** drives the income tax calculation — federal and Ontario brackets are applied to this number. **Net Income (23600)** drives benefit calculations — CCB, GST/HST credit, OAS clawback, Age Amount, spousal credit, Ontario Trillium Benefit, LIFT Credit. The distinction is crucial: deductions taken between Lines 23600 and 26000 (like capital loss carryforwards) reduce only the tax calculation — they do NOT improve benefits. Deductions above Line 23600 reduce both simultaneously. The single deduction that most commonly reduces both tax and benefits is an **RRSP contribution (Line 20800)** — it reduces Net Income directly, simultaneously reducing income tax at the client's marginal rate AND improving all benefit eligibility calculations. For a parent with children in the CCB range, this combined effect (tax + CCB) can make an RRSP's real return 40–80%+ in the year of contribution — far exceeding its face value as a simple tax deduction.
