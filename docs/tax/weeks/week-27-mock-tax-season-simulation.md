## 27.1 How to Use This Week

### The Simulation Rules

1. **Work each case as if it is a real client.** Don't skim. Don't skip steps. Treat each one as if the client is sitting across from you.

2. **Time yourself.** After each case, note how long it took. Compare to professional benchmarks provided.

3. **Do NOT look at the answer key until you have completed your own analysis.** The value is in the attempt, not the answer. You learn most from the gap between what you found and what was there.

6. **The errors are real.** In each case, something is missing, misclassified, or incorrect in the client's self-reported information. Finding the error is part of the exercise.

## 27.2 Case 1 — The Simple Employee (Apparent)

**Difficulty:** Medium (appears simple, has hidden complexity)
**Target time:** 35–45 minutes

### Client Profile: James Chen, 34

James calls to book an appointment. He works as a project manager in London, Ontario. He says: "It's a simple return — just a T4 from my job."

**Documents he brings:**

- **T4** from TechCorp Ontario:
  - Box 14: $87,500
  - Box 16 (CPP): $3,867
  - Box 18 (EI): $1,049
  - Box 22 (Tax withheld): $19,200
  - Box 44 (Union dues): $1,320
  - Box 52 (Pension adjustment): $7,800

- **T5** from TD Bank:
  - Box 13 (Interest): $840

- **RRSP receipt:** $8,000 contributed in February 2025

- He rents an apartment at $1,650/month

- He mentions in conversation: "I also did some freelance project consulting for a startup in Waterloo — made about $4,200. They said they'd send me something but I never got it."

**His prior NOA (from 2024 return):** Shows RRSP deduction limit of $14,200

---

### Your Tasks

1. What income sources does James have? List them all with the correct T1 lines.
2. What deductions does he qualify for?
3. What is his correct net income?
4. What should you look up in CRA My Account before filing?
5. What is the error or trap in this case?
6. Does he owe a balance or get a refund? Estimate.
7. What Ontario benefits apply?

---

??? success "Case 1 — Full Answer Key"

    ### The Trap in This Case
    James said "it's a simple return" and "just a T4." But he also has:
    - A T5 for bank interest he brought (good)
    - **Freelance income of $4,200 with no T4A** — he never received a slip but the income is taxable
    - **A pension adjustment in Box 52** of $7,800 that will reduce his future RRSP room
    - **The RRSP limit check is critical:** His prior NOA showed $14,200 room. BUT — his 2024 pension adjustment of $7,800 (from Box 52 of his 2024 T4, which appears on the 2024 NOA) already reduced his 2025 RRSP room. The $14,200 on the NOA already reflects the PA deduction. CRA My Account will show the definitive current limit — verify before confirming the $8,000 deduction is within room.

    ### CRA My Account Check
    - Verify all T-slips: Is there a T4A for the $4,200 freelance income? (Probably not if the client says they never received one — but verify. If the startup issued one, it appears in My Account.)
    - Confirm RRSP deduction limit is at least $8,000

    ### Income Summary

    | Line | Source | Amount |
    |---|---|---|
    | 10100 | T4 employment (Box 14) | $87,500 |
    | 12100 | TD Bank interest (T5 Box 13) | $840 |
    | 13500 | Freelance consulting (T2125) | $4,200 gross → expenses? |

    **For the freelance income:** Create a T2125. Gross: $4,200. Ask James: any expenses? Phone (business use), home office (if he did this from home), equipment? If he has no expenses and no home office claim, net = $4,200 → Line 13500.

    **Total income: $92,540**

    ### Deductions

    | Line | Deduction | Amount |
    |---|---|---|
    | 20800 | RRSP (verify within limit) | $8,000 |
    | 21200 | Union dues (T4 Box 44) | $1,320 |
    | 22200 | CPP on self-employment (50% of freelance CPP) | Calculated below |

    **CPP on freelance income:**
    Net freelance income: $4,200. Less basic exemption $3,500 = $700 × 11.9% = $83.
    Deductible half: $42 (Line 22200). Credit half: $42 × 15% = $6 (Line 31000).

    **Net income: $92,540 − $8,000 − $1,320 − $42 = $83,178**

    ### Federal Tax (approximate)
    $57,375 × 15% = $8,606
    ($83,178 − $57,375) × 20.5% = $25,803 × 20.5% = $5,290
    Gross federal tax: $13,896

    Credits: BPA $16,129 × 15% = $2,419. CPP T4 $3,867 × 15% = $580. EI $1,049 × 15% = $157. Employment $1,433 × 15% = $215. CPP self-emp credit: $6. Total credits: ~$3,377.

    Net federal tax: $13,896 − $3,377 = **$10,519**

    Ontario tax (on $83,178):
    $51,446 × 5.05% = $2,598
    ($83,178 − $51,446) × 9.15% = $31,732 × 9.15% = $2,904
    Ontario basic: $5,502. No surtax (below $5,315? Just above — $5,502 > $5,315).
    Surtax: ($5,502 − $5,315) × 20% = $37.
    Ontario tax: $5,502 + $37 = $5,539. After Ontario credits (~$919): **$4,620**

    **Total tax: $10,519 + $4,620 = $15,139**
    Tax withheld: $19,200
    **REFUND: approximately $4,061**

    ### Ontario Benefits
    - **GST/HST credit:** Income $83,178 — above $44,324 threshold. Phase-out: ($83,178 − $44,324) × 5% = $1,943. Annual credit: $519 − $1,943 = $0 (fully phased out)
    - **Ontario Trillium Benefit:** Rent $1,650 × 12 = $19,800. OEPTC: 20% × $19,800 = $3,960 → approximately **$987/year**. OSTC: $83,178 — above $24,916 → fully phased out. **Total OTB: ~$987**

    ### What This Case Tests
    - Recognizing income not on a slip (freelance cash-equivalent income)
    - T2125 for small self-employment amounts
    - CPP on self-employment even at small amounts
    - RRSP verification through CRA My Account before filing
    - The pension adjustment impact on RRSP room

## 27.3 Case 2 — The New Immigrant

**Difficulty:** High
**Target time:** 60–75 minutes

### Client Profile: Maria Santos, 37

Maria and her husband arrived from the Philippines on **June 1, 2025**. She and her two daughters (ages 4 and 8) came together. Her husband (Jose) will have a separate return.

Maria started work as a personal support worker on **July 1** at $52,000/year. The couple rent a townhouse for $1,900/month starting June 15.

**Documents:**
- **T4** from her employer:
  - Box 14: $26,000 (July–December, 6 months)
  - Box 16: $1,380
  - Box 18: $431
  - Box 22: $4,800

- **Philippine income (January–May 30):** She earned PHP 320,000 (approximately $7,700 CAD) from her Philippines employment before arriving.

- **Philippine savings account:** Balance on June 1 = PHP 180,000 = approximately $4,320 CAD

- She has **no RRSP contributions** and did not file a return before.

- She has submitted **Form RC66** for CCB on arrival.

---

### Your Tasks

1. What is Maria's province of residence? What is her residency status?
2. What income goes on her T1?
3. Do her personal credits get pro-rated? Apply the 90% test.
4. Does T1135 need to be filed?
5. What are her first-year benefits?
6. What is her approximate tax result?
7. What is the most important action you take before this appointment?

---

??? success "Case 2 — Full Answer Key"

    ### Residency Status
    Maria became a Canadian resident on **June 1, 2025** — the date she arrived with her husband and children (primary ties: home available June 15, spouse in Canada, dependants in Canada). **Part-year resident: June 1 to December 31 = 214 days.**

    Province on December 31: Ontario ✅

    ### The 90% Test
    Canadian-source income: $26,000 (employment)
    Total worldwide income: $26,000 + $7,700 (Philippine income during residency? No — the Philippine income was earned January–May 30, BEFORE she became resident)

    Wait — the Philippine income from January to May 30 was earned in the **non-residency period**. This income is NOT included in her Canadian T1 at all. It's not "worldwide income during residency period" — it's pre-residency income.

    **Total worldwide income DURING residency (June 1–December 31):**
    - Canadian employment: $26,000
    - Philippine savings account interest earned June 1–December 31: minimal (savings account, not earning significant income during Canadian residency)

    If the Philippine savings account earned, say, PHP 5,400 in interest June–December = approximately $130 CAD — Maria's total worldwide income during residency: $26,130.

    Canadian income = $26,000 / $26,130 = **99.5% — exceeds 90%.**

    **Full credits apply — no pro-ration needed.**

    ### T1135 Assessment
    Philippine savings account: PHP 180,000 = approximately **$4,320 CAD**.

    $4,320 is far below the **$100,000 threshold.** T1135 is **NOT required.**

    ### Income on T1

    | Line | Income | Amount |
    |---|---|---|
    | 10100 | Employment (T4 Box 14) | $26,000 |
    | (Foreign interest if any) | Philippine account interest during residency | ~$130 |
    | **Total income** | | **~$26,130** |

    Philippine employment income (Jan–May 30): **Not reported** — pre-residency period.

    ### Deductions
    No RRSP contributions. No moving expenses (moved for immigration purposes, not for specific employment — standard immigration moves don't typically qualify unless to a job that creates a 40km improvement).

    CPP from T4: $1,380 (credit on Line 30800)
    EI: $431 (credit on Line 31200)

    **Net income: approximately $26,130**

    ### Federal Tax
    Taxable income $26,130. After BPA $16,129: taxable = $10,001 × 15% = $1,500.
    Less credits: BPA credit $2,419 already applied. CPP $1,380 × 15% = $207. EI $431 × 15% = $65. Employment $1,433 × 15% = $215. Total credits: $2,906.
    Federal tax = $0 (BPA alone shelters all income essentially)

    Ontario tax: $26,130 × 5.05% = $1,320 gross.
    After Ontario BPA and credits: approximately $300 Ontario tax.
    **LIFT Credit:** Employment income = $26,000. Net income ~$26,130. Below $30,000 threshold. **LIFT Credit eliminates Ontario tax → $0**

    **Tax withheld: $4,800. Tax owing: $0. Refund: approximately $4,800** ✅

    ### First-Year Benefits

    **CCB:** RC66 submitted on arrival. CCB starts July 2025 (month after arrival).
    At $26,000 AFNI, 2 children (ages 4 and 8):
    Maximum: $7,787 + $6,570 = $14,357. Below $36,502 threshold — no phase-out.
    **Annual CCB: $14,357. July–December 2025: $14,357 × 6/12 = $7,179.**
    From July 2026 forward: $1,197/month.

    **GST/HST credit:** Income $26,130. Annual: $519 (individual) + $519 (spouse) + $137 + $137 = $1,312 family. Below threshold — **full credit: $1,312/year.**
    Pro-rated from Q3 2025 (arrival quarter + 1): approximately $656 in 2025.

    **Ontario Trillium Benefit:**
    Rent June 15–December 31 = approximately 6.5 months × $1,900 = $12,350.
    OEPTC: 20% × $12,350 = $2,470 → approximately $640 partial year.
    OSTC: $345 (below income threshold).
    **Total OTB: approximately $985/year.**

    **Total first-year value: $4,800 refund + $7,179 CCB + $656 GST + $640 OTB = $13,275** — an extraordinary first-year outcome.

    ### What This Case Tests
    - Part-year residency determination
    - Pre-residency income exclusion
    - The 90% test applied correctly (pre-residency income doesn't count as "worldwide income during residency")
    - T1135 threshold awareness (well under $100,000)
    - CCB and GST credit for new immigrants
    - LIFT Credit for employment income under $30,000

## 27.4 Case 3 — The Gig Worker

**Difficulty:** High
**Target time:** 60–80 minutes

### Client Profile: Kevin Osei, 29

Kevin drives for Uber and does occasional handyman work for a property management company. He lives in Hamilton, Ontario, renting at $1,200/month.

**Documents he brings:**
- **T4A from Uber** (Box 48): $48,600 — this is what he says Uber paid him
- **T4A from Property Management Co.** (Box 48): $8,400 — handyman work
- **Gas receipts:** $4,200 total
- **His logbook:** Shows 42,000 total km driven, 31,500 km on Uber trips
- **He says:** "I use my car for basically all Uber — probably 90% business"
- **Vehicle:** 2018 Honda Accord purchased 3 years ago for $22,000. Opening UCC: $10,780 (Class 10)
- **Insurance:** $2,100/year
- **Oil changes and maintenance:** $780

He also mentions casually: "I made about $3,200 cash from helping neighbours with moving and odd jobs — I never track that."

---

### Your Tasks

1. What is Kevin's actual gross income — and what is the critical error he's presenting?
2. What is his correct vehicle business-use percentage from the logbook?
3. Calculate the vehicle deduction.
4. Should he claim 90% business use as he suggests?
5. What do you do with the $3,200 in cash income?
6. Calculate his approximate net business income and CPP obligation.
7. Does he need HST registration?

---

??? success "Case 3 — Full Answer Key"

    ### Critical Error 1 — The Gross Income Problem
    Kevin says "Uber paid him $48,600." This is likely the **net amount** after Uber's service fee. The T4A Box 48 usually reports **gross fares**. Verify: Is the $48,600 on the T4A the gross (pre-fee) or net amount?

    If the T4A shows $48,600 as gross (which it should — T4A Box 48 reports gross fares), then Uber's service fee (~25%) must be calculated:
    $48,600 / (1 − 0.25) would not be the formula... The T4A should already show gross. If $48,600 IS the gross, then Uber's fee = $48,600 × 25% = $12,150.

    If $48,600 is the **net deposited** (which Kevin thinks is his income), then actual gross = $48,600 / 0.75 = **$64,800** and the T4A should show $64,800.

    **Action:** Verify Kevin's Uber annual tax summary in the Uber driver app. This shows both gross fares and Uber's service fee separately. The T4A should match gross fares. If Kevin's T4A says $48,600 but the Uber summary shows $64,800 in gross fares — the T4A is correct at $64,800 and Kevin has been thinking about his income incorrectly.

    For this case, assume the T4A correctly shows **$48,600 gross fares.** Uber fee: $48,600 × 25% = $12,150.

    **Total gross income: $48,600 (Uber) + $8,400 (handyman) = $57,000**

    ### Critical Error 2 — The $3,200 Cash Income
    Kevin "never tracks" $3,200 in cash from odd jobs. This is **taxable income** that must be reported.

    Your conversation: *"Kevin, that $3,200 is taxable income. It needs to go on your return. I understand it's cash and there's no slip — but CRA requires all income to be reported regardless of whether you received a receipt. We'll include it."*

    If Kevin resists: explain the tax consequences of not reporting vs. the relatively small additional tax at his income level. If he still refuses after your clear explanation — document the conversation and assess whether you can continue the engagement.

    **Total gross income: $48,600 + $8,400 + $3,200 = $60,200**

    ### Vehicle Business Use — The Logbook
    Kevin says "90% business." His logbook says: 42,000 total km, 31,500 km on Uber trips.

    **Logbook-based business use:** 31,500 ÷ 42,000 = **75%**

    Kevin's "90%" is his impression. The logbook says 75%. **Always use the logbook percentage, not the client's guess.**

    Additionally: Deadhead km (driving without a passenger, looking for trips) are also business use — these may not be captured in "Uber trips" which logs only active-trip km. If Kevin can estimate deadhead km, add those. For this case, use **75%** as documented.

    ### Vehicle Deduction

    | Expense | Annual Amount | × 75% | Deductible |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Fuel | $4,200 | 75% | $3,150 |
    | Insurance | $2,100 | 75% | $1,575 |
    | Oil/maintenance | $780 | 75% | $585 |
    | Licence/registration | ~$180 | 75% | $135 |
    | CCA (Class 10, 30%, opening UCC $10,780) | $10,780 × 30% = $3,234 | 75% | $2,426 |
    | **Total vehicle deduction** | | | **$7,871** |

    **Uber platform fee:** $12,150 (deductible business expense)

    **Other expenses:**
    - Phone (70% business): $1,440 × 70% = $1,008
    - In-car supplies (water, cleaning supplies): $350

    **T2125 Summary:**

    ```
    Gross income:         $60,200
    − Uber platform fee: ($12,150)
    − Vehicle expenses:   ($7,871)
    − Phone:              ($1,008)
    − Supplies:             ($350)
    ═══════════════════════════
    Net income:           $38,821  → Line 13500
    ```

    ### CPP on Self-Employment
    $38,821 − $3,500 = $35,321 × 11.9% = $4,203
    Deductible half (Line 22200): $2,102
    Credit (Line 31000): $2,102 × 15% = $315

    **Net income for benefits: $38,821 − $2,102 = $36,719**

    ### HST Registration
    Total gross revenue: $60,200. **Exceeds $30,000.** Kevin must be registered for HST.

    **Critical Uber note:** Rideshare drivers must register for HST from Day 1 (no small supplier exemption). Kevin should have been registered since his first Uber trip. If he isn't registered — this is a compliance issue requiring immediate action and potentially retroactive remittance.

    The handyman work and cash odd jobs ARE subject to HST registration analysis (combined with Uber revenue — all exceed $30,000). Kevin likely needed HST registration this year.

    ### What This Case Tests
    - Gross revenue vs. net deposit — the Uber trap
    - Cash income must be reported
    - Logbook vs. client estimate — always use documented evidence
    - HST registration for rideshare (day one rule) + general threshold crossed
    - CPP on self-employment at full calculation

## 27.5 Case 4 — The Senior Couple

**Difficulty:** Very High
**Target time:** 75–90 minutes

### Client Profile: Robert (72) and Helen (68) Chen

Both Ontario residents all year. You are filing both returns and considering pension splitting.

**Robert's income:**
- T4A (DB pension from former employer): $58,000
- T4A(P) (CPP): $13,200
- T4A(OAS): $8,300
- T4RIF (RRIF withdrawal): $24,000 (Box 16)
- Tax withheld (T4RIF Box 28): $4,800

**Helen's income:**
- T4A(P) (CPP): $7,400
- T4A(OAS): $7,800
- No other income

**Other information:**
- Robert's property tax in 2025: $5,200 (they own their home)
- Medical expenses: Helen had $8,400 in dental work and physiotherapy. Robert had $1,200 in prescription costs.
- Charitable donations: $1,600 (Robert's receipts), $400 (Helen's receipts)
- No RRSP contributions (past age 71 for Robert)

---

### Your Tasks

1. Calculate Robert's net income without any pension splitting.
2. Is Robert in OAS clawback territory?
3. What is the maximum pension split to Helen? How much should actually be split?
4. Recalculate Robert's net income after pension split.
5. Who claims the medical expenses and why?
6. How should donations be handled?
7. What credits does Helen now have access to after the pension split?
8. Complete the ON-BEN analysis for both.

---

??? success "Case 4 — Full Answer Key"

    ### Robert's Net Income Without Splitting

    | Line | Income | Amount |
    |---|---|---|
    | 11300 | OAS | $8,300 |
    | 11400 | CPP | $13,200 |
    | 11500 | DB pension (T4A) | $58,000 |
    | 11500 | RRIF (T4RIF Box 16) | $24,000 |
    | **Total income** | | **$103,500** |

    **No RRSP or other deductions available.**
    **Robert's net income: $103,500**

    ### OAS Clawback Check
    Threshold: $90,997
    Excess: $103,500 − $90,997 = $12,503
    **OAS clawback: $12,503 × 15% = $1,875**

    Robert keeps: $8,300 − $1,875 = **$6,425 of his OAS.**

    ### Pension Income Split Analysis

    **Eligible pension income for splitting (Robert is 65+):**
    - DB pension: $58,000 ✅ eligible
    - RRIF: $24,000 ✅ eligible (65+)
    - CPP: ❌ Not eligible (separate CPP sharing through Service Canada)
    - OAS: ❌ Not eligible
    - **Total eligible: $82,000. Maximum split (50%): $41,000**

    **Strategy: Split enough to bring Robert below the OAS clawback threshold ($90,997).**

    Required reduction: $103,500 − $90,997 = $12,503
    **Minimum split to eliminate clawback: $12,503** — but let's round to $13,000 for buffer.

    **Optimal split decision:**
    Helen's current net income: $7,400 + $7,800 = $15,200.
    Helen's marginal rate at $15,200: approximately 20.05% combined.
    Robert's marginal rate at $103,500: approximately 29.65%–31.66% combined (spans brackets).

    If we split $13,000 to Helen:
    Robert: $103,500 − $13,000 = **$90,500** (just below clawback threshold)
    Helen: $15,200 + $13,000 = **$28,200**

    Robert saves: OAS clawback eliminated ($1,875/year). Plus rate differential on $13,000: $13,000 × (31.66% − 20.05%) = $13,000 × 11.61% = **$1,509.**

    **Total annual saving from pension split: $1,875 + $1,509 = $3,384.**

    ### Medical Expenses

    **Who claims them?** The combined family medical expenses should go on the **lower-income spouse's return** to minimize the 3% threshold.

    Helen's 3% threshold: $28,200 × 3% = $846
    Robert's 3% threshold: $90,500 × 3% = $2,715

    Combined medical: $8,400 + $1,200 = $9,600
    On Helen's return: $9,600 − $846 = $8,754 × 15% = **$1,313 federal credit**
    On Robert's return: $9,600 − $2,715 = $6,885 × 15% = **$1,033 federal credit**

    **Claim on Helen: saves $280 more in federal credits.**

    ### Charitable Donations
    Pool on one return. Who has higher income? Robert at $90,500.
    Combined donations: $1,600 + $400 = $2,000.
    $200 × 15% + $1,800 × 29% = $30 + $522 = **$552 federal credit on Robert's return.**

    vs. splitting: Robert $1,600 = $200 × 15% + $1,400 × 29% = $436. Helen $400 = $200 × 15% + $200 × 29% = $88. Total: $524.
    Combined on Robert: **$552 > $524 — pool on Robert's return.**

    ### Helen's New Credits After Pension Split

    Helen now has $13,000 in RRIF income (transferred from Robert). This is **eligible pension income** for someone 65+.

    Helen now has access to:
    - **Pension Income Amount** (Line 31400): Up to $2,000 of eligible pension income × 15% = **$300 federal credit**
    - **Ontario Pension Income Deduction**: Up to $1,533 × 5.05% = **$77 Ontario credit**

    Helen's pension income ($13,000) well exceeds the $2,000 threshold. Helen gets the maximum pension income credit.

    ### ON-BEN Analysis

    **Robert and Helen (homeowners):**
    Robert's property tax: $5,200.
    OEPTC for homeowner (65+): Based on $5,200 property tax at Robert's income of $90,500.
    The OEPTC formula applies reduction rates at higher incomes — at $90,500, Robert is above the reduction threshold. His OEPTC will be reduced but not eliminated — approximately **$400–$600** depending on exact calculation.
    Both claim their proportionate share — this is a jointly-owned property, each files their own ON-BEN. The property tax is shared between returns.

    **Senior Homeowners' Property Tax Grant:** Robert is 72 (over 64). Income-tested — at $90,500, Robert may receive a partial grant. Helen is 68 — also qualifies but income tested separately. Claim on both ON-BEN forms.

    ### What This Case Tests
    - OAS clawback identification and calculation
    - Pension income splitting optimization (just enough to clear the threshold)
    - Medical expense optimization (lower-income spouse)
    - Donation pooling math
    - New credits unlocked for Helen (pension income amount)
    - Senior homeowners ON-BEN with both OEPTC and Senior Property Tax Grant

## 27.6 Case 5 — The Student Transition Return

**Difficulty:** Medium
**Target time:** 40–50 minutes

### Client Profile: Sarah Tanaka, 23

Sarah graduated from Western University in April 2025. She started her first professional job in May 2025 as a junior engineer at $76,000/year.

**Documents:**
- **T4 from part-time campus job (January–April):** Box 14: $6,200. Box 22: $620 withheld.
- **T4 from engineering firm (May–December):** Box 14: $50,667 ($76,000 × 8/12). Box 22: $12,400 withheld.
- **T2202 from Western for January–April 2025:** 4 months full-time, $9,800 tuition
- **Prior NOA shows:** Federal tuition carry-forward: **$11,400 (credit amount)**
- She lives in London, Ontario, renting at $1,150/month (April 2025 to present — her last 8 months of the year)
- She did NOT move for the job (works in London where she went to school)
- She made an $RRSP contribution of $3,000 in February 2025

---

### Your Tasks

    **Treatment:** $8,500 is NOT currently deductible. It is added to the Class 1 UCC.

For this case, classify as capital (CCA).
