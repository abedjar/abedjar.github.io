# Week 1: How the Canadian Tax System Works

!!! info "Learning Objectives"
    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    - Explain how Canada's federal and provincial tax system is structured
    - Define marginal vs. effective tax rates and calculate both
    - Describe the CRA's role and how it interacts with taxpayers
    - Understand the basic flow of money in a tax return

---

## 1.1 The Big Picture — Who Collects Tax in Canada?

Canada has **two layers** of income tax:

| Layer | Collected By | Goes To |
|---|---|---|
| Federal tax | Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) | Government of Canada |
| Provincial/Territorial tax | CRA (on behalf of provinces*) | Your province or territory |

!!! note "Quebec is the exception"
    Quebec collects its **own** provincial income tax separately through **Revenu Québec**. Residents of Quebec file **two** returns — one federal (T1) and one provincial (TP-1). For now, we'll focus on the other 9 provinces where CRA handles both.

The **T1 General** is the form used by individuals to report income, claim deductions, and calculate how much tax they owe (or how much refund they're owed). When people say "filing their taxes," this is the form they mean.

---

## 1.2 The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA)

The CRA is the federal agency that:

- Receives and processes T1 returns
- Issues **Notices of Assessment (NOA)** after reviewing your return
- Collects taxes owed and issues refunds
- Enforces tax law and conducts audits
- Administers benefit programs (GST/HST credit, Canada Child Benefit, etc.)

!!! tip "The CRA is your most important reference"
    As a tax preparer, you'll use [canada.ca/cra](https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency.html) constantly. Get comfortable navigating it early.

---

## 1.3 How Tax Brackets Work in Canada

Canada uses a **progressive tax system**. This means:

- You pay **different rates** on different portions of your income
- Earning more money does **not** mean all your income gets taxed at the higher rate
- Only the income **within each bracket** is taxed at that bracket's rate

### 2025 Federal Tax Brackets

| Taxable Income | Federal Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 – $57,375 | 15% |
| $57,376 – $114,750 | 20.5% |
| $114,751 – $158,519 | 26% |
| $158,520 – $220,000 | 29% |
| Over $220,000 | 33% |

!!! warning "Brackets change every year"
    The CRA adjusts bracket thresholds annually for inflation. Always verify the current year's brackets before filing season.

---

## 1.4 Marginal Rate vs. Effective Rate

These two terms confuse beginners constantly. Here's the clear distinction:

=== "Marginal Rate"

    **The rate applied to your *last dollar* of income.**

    This is the bracket your top income falls into. It does NOT mean all your income is taxed at this rate.

    **Example:** If you earn $80,000, your marginal federal rate is **20.5%** — but only the income between $57,376 and $80,000 is taxed at that rate.

=== "Effective Rate"

    **The *average* rate you actually pay across all your income.**

    This is your total tax ÷ total income. It is always **lower** than your marginal rate.

    **Example:** On $80,000 of income:

    | Bracket | Income in Bracket | Rate | Tax |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | First $57,375 | $57,375 | 15% | $8,606 |
    | Next $22,625 | $22,625 | 20.5% | $4,638 |
    | **Total** | **$80,000** | | **$13,244** |

    Effective rate = $13,244 ÷ $80,000 = **16.6%**

    Marginal rate = **20.5%**

!!! success "Why this matters for your clients"
    Clients often panic thinking "I got a raise — now I'll pay more tax on everything!" You can reassure them: only the income **above** the bracket threshold gets taxed at the higher rate. This is one of the most common misconceptions you'll correct.

---

## 1.5 Federal + Provincial = Total Tax

Remember: your client always pays **both** federal AND provincial tax. These stack on top of each other.

**Example — Ontario resident earning $80,000:**

| Tax | Amount |
|---|---|
| Federal tax | ~$13,244 |
| Ontario provincial tax | ~$5,500 |
| **Total income tax** | **~$18,744** |

Each province has its own brackets and rates. You'll learn provincial differences in **Week 15**. For now, just know that both layers always apply.

## 1.6 The Flow of a T1 Return

Every T1 return follows this exact logical flow. Memorize this — it's the skeleton of everything you'll do.

```
TOTAL INCOME          (all income from all sources)
    − Deductions
─────────────────
NET INCOME            (used to calculate benefits & credits)
    − Additional deductions (e.g. capital losses)
─────────────────
TAXABLE INCOME        (this is what gets taxed)
    × Tax rates
─────────────────
GROSS TAX PAYABLE
    − Non-refundable credits
─────────────────
NET TAX PAYABLE
    − Tax already withheld (from T4, instalments, etc.)
─────────────────
BALANCE OWING or REFUND  ← the number your client cares about
```

!!! info "Net Income is more important than people think"
    **Net Income** (line 23600) is used to calculate GST/HST credits, Canada Child Benefit, Old Age Security clawbacks, and more. Reducing net income — not just taxable income — can unlock or increase benefits. You'll learn this deeply in Phase 3.

## 1.7 Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **T1 General** | The individual income tax return form |
| **CRA** | Canada Revenue Agency — collects tax, processes returns |
| **NOA** | Notice of Assessment — CRA's response after processing your return |
| **SIN** | Social Insurance Number — required to file; unique to each taxpayer |
| **Tax bracket** | An income range taxed at a specific rate |
| **Marginal rate** | The rate on your highest dollar of income |
| **Effective rate** | Your average rate (total tax ÷ total income) |
| **Deduction** | Reduces your income before tax is calculated |
| **Tax credit** | Reduces the tax you owe directly (more powerful than deductions) |
| **Refundable credit** | A credit you receive even if you owe no tax |
| **Non-refundable credit** | A credit that can reduce your tax to $0 but not below |
| **Withholding tax** | Tax deducted from your paycheque by your employer before you receive it |
| **Balance owing** | Tax owed to CRA after credits and withholdings |
| **Refund** | Amount CRA returns if you overpaid during the year |

---

## 1.8 Week 1 Summary

!!! success "What you learned this week"
    - Canada has **two layers** of income tax: federal (CRA) and provincial
    - The **T1 General** is the individual tax return — the core of your business
    - Canada uses **progressive tax brackets** — higher rates only apply to income within that bracket
    - **Marginal rate** = rate on your last dollar | **Effective rate** = your actual average rate
    - Every return follows the same flow: Total Income → Net Income → Taxable Income → Tax Payable → Balance/Refund
    - **Net Income** (line 23600) is critical — it affects benefits, not just taxes

## ✅ Week 1 Quiz

Try to answer these before checking. When you're ready, ask Claude: **"Quiz me on Week 1"** for an interactive test.

??? question "1. What is the difference between a deduction and a tax credit?"
    A **deduction** reduces your income *before* tax is calculated. A **tax credit** reduces the actual tax you owe *after* it's calculated. Credits are generally more valuable because they reduce tax dollar-for-dollar.

??? question "2. If someone earns $120,000, what is their federal marginal rate?"
    **26%** — their income falls in the $114,751–$158,519 bracket. But their effective rate will be significantly lower because the first $114,750 was taxed at lower rates.

??? question "3. Why does Net Income matter beyond just calculating tax?"
    Net Income (line 23600) is used to calculate benefit eligibility and amounts — including the GST/HST credit, Canada Child Benefit, and OAS clawbacks. Reducing net income can increase benefits a client receives.

??? question "4. A client says 'I don't want a raise because it'll push me into a higher bracket.' How do you respond?"
    Reassure them this is a myth. In Canada's progressive system, only the income *above* the bracket threshold is taxed at the higher rate. A raise always results in more after-tax money — never less.

??? question "5. Which province files two separate tax returns?"
    **Quebec.** Residents file a federal T1 with the CRA *and* a provincial TP-1 with Revenu Québec.
