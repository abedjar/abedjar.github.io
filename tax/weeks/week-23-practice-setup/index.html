    Week 23: Setting Up Your Tax Practice — Legal, Ethical & Business Foundations¶

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    • Register your tax preparation business correctly in Ontario
    • Obtain all mandatory registrations — EFILE, HST, business licence
    • Purchase E&O insurance before accepting your first paying client
    • Draft or adapt a professional engagement letter for every client type
    • Understand your obligations under PIPEDA and Ontario's privacy framework
    • Build a pricing structure that reflects your value without undercharging
    • Market your practice using the strategies that actually generate clients in this business
    • Identify the ethical boundaries that protect your clients and your reputation
    • Navigate the CRA's requirements for registered tax preparers

    The mindset that builds a lasting practice

    Most people starting a tax preparation business focus entirely on the technical side — learning the rules, buying software, studying. That is necessary but insufficient. A practice is a business. A business needs structure, legal protection, professional standards, and clients. This week gives you the operational foundation that the technical knowledge sits on top of. Without it, you are a knowledgeable person doing favours. With it, you are a professional building an asset.

    23.1 Do You Need a Professional Designation to Prepare Taxes in Canada?¶

    This is the first question every aspiring preparer asks — and the answer often surprises them.

    The short answer: No.

    Unlike law (requires a bar call) or public accounting (requires a CPA), tax preparation for individuals in Canada has no mandatory licensing or designation requirement. Anyone can legally prepare T1 returns for compensation, provided they:

    1. Register for EFILE with CRA (required to file electronically for more than 5 paid returns)
    2. Comply with all applicable tax laws and professional obligations
    3. Operate within the scope of services they're qualified to provide

    What this means for you: You can begin your practice as soon as you have: - Technical knowledge (these 28 weeks) - EFILE registration - Software - Insurance - A business registration

    What this does NOT mean: You can prepare complex corporate tax returns (T2), advise on tax planning involving corporate structures, or represent clients at Tax Court — those activities require CPAs or legal counsel.

    Voluntary Professional Associations¶

    While not mandatory, these organizations provide credibility and continuing education:

    Organization What It Offers
    AAPITA (Association of Accounting Professionals and Income Tax Agents) Designation: APIT. Training, professional standards, some E&O group insurance
    IAPC (Income Tax Professionals of Canada) Community, resources, CE credits
    Canadian Tax Foundation Advanced tax education — more relevant as you grow

    These are not regulated — they don't legally authorize anything. But membership signals professionalism to clients and keeps you connected to developments in the field.

    23.2 Business Registration in Ontario — Step by Step¶

    Step 1 — Choose Your Business Structure¶

    As a new solo preparer, you have three options:

    What it is: You operate as yourself. No separate legal entity. Business income is your personal income.

    Pros: - Simplest and cheapest to set up - No corporate tax return required - All business expenses flow directly to your T2125 - Start immediately

    Cons: - You are personally liable for business debts and legal claims - No tax deferral advantage - Harder to bring in partners later

    When to start as sole proprietor: Your first 1–3 years. Under $80,000 net income. Single operator. This is the right starting structure for virtually every new tax preparer.

    Cost to register in Ontario: $60 online at ServiceOntario.

    What it is: Two or more people operating together. Each partner reports their share of income on their personal T1.

    When it makes sense: When you are starting with a partner from day one who brings complementary skills (e.g., you know tax, they know marketing and have a client base).

    Caution: Partnerships require a formal partnership agreement. Each partner is personally liable for the other partner's professional actions. Get a lawyer involved.

    What it is: A separate legal entity. You pay corporate tax on business income, then extract salary and dividends.

    Pros: - Personal liability protection - Tax deferral on retained earnings - More professional appearance at higher volumes

    Cons: - T2 corporate return required annually (you need a CPA or must learn it) - Higher setup and ongoing costs - More complex administration

    When to incorporate: When your net income consistently exceeds $80,000–$100,000 per year. In Year 1 — not necessary.

    Step 2 — Register Your Business Name¶

    If you operate under any name other than your own legal name, you must register a Business Name (Ontario) at ServiceOntario.

    Examples: - "John Chen" — no registration needed (your legal name) - "Chen Tax Services" — must register - "Accurate Tax Pro" — must register

    Cost: $60 online at ontario.ca/serviceontario Duration: 5-year registration, renewable Turnaround: Usually same day online

    Choosing a business name: - Include your city or neighbourhood: "London Ontario Tax" ranks better in Google - Be specific about services: "Individual Tax Returns" or "Personal Tax Preparation" - Avoid generic names: "Tax Services" and "Tax Pro" are both saturated - Check: Ontario Business Name Registry (to ensure no conflict) and Google (to ensure no website conflict)

    Step 3 — Get a Business Number from CRA¶

    A Business Number (BN) is your business identifier with CRA. You'll need it for: - Registering for HST (if your preparation fees exceed $30,000/year) - Registering for EFILE as a business (rather than individual) - Opening a business bank account

    How to get it: Register online at canada.ca/business-registration. Takes approximately 15 minutes. The BN is issued instantly.

    Format: 9 digits (e.g., 123456789). Your HST account would be 123456789 RT 0001.

    Step 4 — Open a Business Bank Account¶

    Separate your personal and business finances from Day 1. This is not optional for professional practice.

    Why it matters: - Makes income and expense tracking trivial (all business in one account) - Required documentation for CRA if audited (business expenses must be demonstrably for business) - Professional appearance (clients pay "Chen Tax Services" not your personal name) - HST tracking (all fees collected in one place)

    What to look for: Most major banks offer business accounts for small businesses. Compare monthly fees ($10–$20/month typical), transaction limits, and online banking quality. The BMO Business Builder, TD Small Business, or RBC Small Business accounts all work. Some credit unions offer better rates for micro-businesses.

    Step 5 — Register for HST (If Applicable)¶

    Your tax preparation fees are a taxable supply — HST of 13% applies in Ontario.

    Required when: Annual revenue from all business activities exceeds $30,000 in a rolling 12-month period.

    Voluntary registration: You can register before reaching $30,000 if you want to claim ITCs on your software, insurance, and office expenses from day one.

    Should you voluntarily register early?

    Revenue Level Register Now? Reason
    Under $20,000/year Wait Administrative burden outweighs ITC benefit at low volume
    $20,000–$30,000 Consider it Approaching threshold; ITCs on software ($1,300+) are real
    Above $30,000 Required Must register immediately

    HST on your services: When registered, add 13% to your invoices for Ontario clients. Most clients don't care about HST — they pay whatever your invoice says.

    23.3 Insurance — The Protection You Must Have Before Client #1¶

    Errors and Omissions (E&O) Insurance — Mandatory¶

    E&O insurance (also called Professional Liability Insurance) protects you if a client suffers a financial loss due to an error or omission in your professional services and sues you for damages.

    What it covers: - Calculation errors that cause a client to underpay tax and owe penalties and interest - Missed deductions you should have claimed - Filing deadlines you missed - Incorrect advice that led to a financial loss

    What it does NOT cover: - Intentional fraud or criminal conduct - Knowing participation in tax evasion - Claims outside the policy period

    Cost: Typically $500–$1,500/year for a solo tax preparer with under $100,000 in annual revenue. Increases with revenue and staff.

    Where to get it: - AAPITA group policy (if you join the association) - BFL Canada (specialist in professional liability) - Intact Insurance - Aviva Canada - Your existing business insurance broker

    Policy limits: Seek at least $1,000,000 per claim and $2,000,000 aggregate for a solo practice. Higher limits are available and cost relatively little more.

    File your first return for a paying client without insurance and you are personally exposed

    A client who owes $8,000 in interest because you missed a filing deadline can sue you personally — especially if you have no corporate liability shield. At $500/year, E&O insurance is one of the best investments in your practice. Buy it before you open. Do not rationalize it as "I'll get it next year."

    General Liability Insurance¶

    Also recommended — protects if a client visits your office and is injured (trips and falls), or if your business activities damage someone's property.

    If you work entirely from home and clients don't visit: lower priority but still worth considering as part of a home business rider on your home insurance.

    Cyber Liability Insurance¶

    Increasingly relevant as you handle sensitive client data. Covers you if client SINs are stolen from your computer, you are hacked, or you accidentally email a return to the wrong person.

    As a new solo practice: check whether E&O policy includes a cyber component, or purchase a rider. Typically $200–$500/year additional.

    23.4 The Engagement Letter — Your First Line of Legal Protection¶

    Every client who pays you for tax preparation services must receive and sign an engagement letter before you begin work. This is not optional. It is your contract.

    What an Engagement Letter Must Include¶

    Section 1 — Scope of Services

    Be specific. What exactly are you being engaged to do?

    "[Your name/business] agrees to prepare the 2025 T1 personal income
tax return for [Client Name] (SIN ending in XXX) based on information
and documents provided by the client."

    Explicitly state what is NOT included: - Prior year returns (unless specifically engaged) - Corporate or partnership returns - Estate administration - Tax planning or advisory services beyond return preparation - Audit representation (unless you offer this service)

    Section 2 — Client Responsibilities

    This is the most important protective clause:

    "The accuracy and completeness of this return depends entirely on
information provided by you. You confirm that you have disclosed all
sources of income, all applicable deductions and credits, and that
all information provided is true, complete, and accurate.

[Your name/business] is not responsible for reassessments, penalties,
interest, or other consequences arising from information you failed
to disclose or provided incorrectly."

    Section 3 — Fees and Payment Terms

    "Fee for services: $XXX [flat rate] or $XXX/hour.
Payment is due upon completion of the return and before filing.
[Your name/business] reserves the right to withhold filing until
payment is received."

    Section 4 — Confidentiality

    "All information provided to [your name/business] will be held in
strict confidence and used solely for the purpose of preparing your
tax return. We will not disclose your information to any third party
without your written consent, except as required by law."

    Section 5 — Limitation of Liability

    "Our liability to you is limited to the fees paid for this engagement.
We shall not be liable for any indirect, special, or consequential
damages arising from our services."

    Section 6 — Signatures

    Both you and the client sign and date. Retain a copy.

    The Clause That Saved a Preparer

    A client failed to mention a $28,000 RRSP withdrawal (T4RSP) they received during the year. The return was filed without it. CRA reassessed with $9,200 in additional tax plus interest.

    The client blamed the preparer and threatened to sue.

    The preparer produced the signed engagement letter containing: "You confirm that you have disclosed all sources of income... [preparer] is not responsible for reassessments arising from information you failed to disclose."

    The matter was dropped. The engagement letter created the legal boundary that protected the preparer.

    Without the engagement letter: a difficult, expensive dispute. With the engagement letter: a documented limitation of liability.

    23.5 Privacy Obligations — PIPEDA and Ontario Law¶

    As a tax preparer, you handle some of the most sensitive personal information that exists: Social Insurance Numbers, income, assets, debts, medical conditions, family situations. Your privacy obligations are both legal and ethical.

    PIPEDA — The Federal Framework¶

    PIPEDA (Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act) is federal legislation that governs how private sector businesses collect, use, and disclose personal information.

    As a tax preparer, PIPEDA requires you to:

    Obligation What It Means in Practice
    Identify purpose Tell clients why you are collecting their information ("to prepare your income tax return")
    Obtain consent Get explicit consent to collect and use their information (the engagement letter achieves this)
    Limit collection Only collect what you need for the stated purpose
    Limit use and disclosure Use information only for tax preparation; never sell or share it
    Safeguard Protect information from unauthorized access (encryption, password protection, secure disposal)
    Individual access Clients have the right to access their own information you hold
    Accuracy Maintain accurate records
    Retention and disposal Keep records only as long as needed; destroy securely when retention period ends

    Ontario-Specific Privacy Considerations¶

    Ontario is currently governed by PIPEDA (no separate provincial privacy legislation for private sector comparable to BC or Quebec — though Ontario has introduced Bill 194 which may create new obligations in future years). For health information: Ontario's PHIPA (Personal Health Information Protection Act) applies, but this affects health-care providers more than tax preparers.

    Practical privacy rules for your practice:

    1. Encrypt your laptop and external drives — use BitLocker (Windows) or FileVault (Mac). If your laptop is stolen and encrypted, client data cannot be accessed.

    2. Never email unencrypted tax returns or documents containing SINs — use a secure portal (TaxFolder, FileInvite, or the secure messaging in your tax software) or password-protected PDFs.

    3. Use strong, unique passwords for CRA My Account, tax software, and email. Use a password manager.

    4. Dispose of paper documents securely — shred everything with client information before disposal. Never put client documents in recycling.

    5. Do not store client SINs in unprotected spreadsheets or email — use your tax software's encrypted client database.

    6. Have a breach response plan — if you believe client data was compromised (lost laptop, hacking), you have an obligation to notify affected individuals and report to the Privacy Commissioner of Canada.

    A SIN in an unencrypted email is a privacy violation waiting to happen

    Email is not secure. Sending a completed T1 (which contains a full SIN, address, income details, and family information) as an unprotected email attachment is a privacy risk. Most professional tax software includes a client portal or secure file delivery system. Use it. If a client insists on email, send password-protected PDFs at minimum, with the password communicated through a separate channel (text message).

    23.6 Setting Your Fees — The Pricing Framework¶

    Pricing is where most new preparers make their first major business mistake: they charge too little. This creates a race-to-the-bottom, attracts price-sensitive clients who switch every year, and leaves no room for professional development.

    The Value-Based Pricing Mindset¶

    You are not selling time. You are selling tax savings, peace of mind, audit protection, and financial confidence. A client who gets a $4,200 larger refund because of your RRSP advice, moving expense claim, and medical window optimization doesn't care if it took you 45 minutes or 2 hours. They perceive enormous value.

    Your fee should reflect outcome value, not time spent.

    The Complexity-Based Fee Structure¶

    Start with this structure in Year 1. Adjust annually as your volume and reputation grow.

    Return Type Ontario Market Range Your Year 1 Price Notes
    Simple T4 only $75–$120 $95 20–30 min
    T4 + RRSP + basic credits $120–$175 $150 30–45 min
    T4 + investments (T5/T3) $175–$280 $225 45–60 min
    T4 + investments + capital gains $225–$350 $280 60–75 min
    Self-employment (T2125) $250–$450 $325 60–90 min
    Rental income (T776) $200–$375 $275 45–75 min
    Self-employed + rental $375–$550 $450 90–120 min
    New immigrant (part-year) $200–$375 $300 60–90 min
    Student (simple) $75–$125 $90 20–30 min
    Senior with pension splitting $250–$400 $325 60–90 min
    Incorporated shareholder $350–$650 $450 90–120 min
    Deceased terminal return $450–$900 $575 120–180 min
    Couple (any complexity) 1.5× individual 1.5× above

    HST: Add 13% HST to all fees once you're registered.

    The Anchoring Strategy¶

    When presenting fees, always describe value before price:

    "I found two deductions you weren't aware of — an advisory fee on your investments and a home office claim that together save you about $2,400 in tax. My fee for your return is $280."

    At that point, $280 feels like a bargain. Without context, $280 feels expensive.

    Always anchor to value first.

    What NOT to Do With Pricing¶

    • Don't charge $50 for any return. You cannot deliver professional quality, maintain insurance, and build a practice at $50 per return. Value your work.
    • Don't be afraid to lose price-sensitive clients. A client who switches to a $49 online service to save $100 was never a good client. Let them go.
    • Don't charge less than H&R Block. You are more qualified than a seasonal employee at a tax franchise. Price accordingly.
    • Don't give discounts promiscuously. One discount becomes the expected price. Offer a one-time new client discount ($25 off) if needed — but that becomes the standard price going forward.

    23.7 Marketing Your Practice — What Actually Works¶

    Tax preparation marketing is fundamentally about trust and community. People don't hire a tax preparer because of a clever Instagram ad. They hire someone their neighbor, coworker, or community member trusts.

    What Actually Works¶

    80% of a strong tax practice comes from referrals. This is not an exaggeration. Happy clients tell their friends. Clients from the same cultural community, the same employer, the same neighbourhood all refer each other.

    How to accelerate referrals:

    1. Ask explicitly at the end of every appointment: "If you know anyone who needs a reliable preparer, I'd genuinely appreciate the referral. I take care of my clients' friends the same way I take care of them."

    2. Follow up after filing: Send a text or email 2 weeks after filing: "Your return was assessed — here's your NOA. Do you have any questions? And if anyone you know needs help, I'm accepting new clients."

    3. Refer back: When you refer clients to mortgage brokers, financial advisors, or CPAs, they refer their clients back to you. Build a referral network.

    4. Don't forget the client's circle: When you file a student's return and get them a $3,200 refund, ask: "Would your parents or roommates like help with their taxes too?"

    The highest-ROI marketing in this business is being present in the community your clients belong to.

    • Filipino community in Brampton/Mississauga: Connect with Filipino community centres, churches (most Filipino communities are Catholic and have active church networks), and Facebook community groups. One respected Filipino family who trusts you will generate dozens of referrals within that community.

    • Indian community in Waterloo/Hamilton: Professional networks (IT, engineering), temple communities, and the South Asian Business Association are powerful channels.

    • New immigrant communities generally: Offer one free "tax basics for newcomers" seminar at a community centre or library. Fifty people in a room learning about the CCB and GST/HST credit from you — ten will book appointments. The other forty will mention your name.

    • Employer relationships: Contact HR departments at large employers (Amazon fulfillment centres, hospital systems, manufacturing plants) and offer an on-site "tax filing day" or informational session. One employer relationship can generate 50–200 annual clients.

    This is your most important digital asset — and it's free.

    Set up a Google Business Profile (formerly Google My Business) at business.google.com. Fill in: - Business name (Chen Tax Services) - Address (home office or virtual, your choice) - Phone and website - Hours (especially February–April) - Services offered - Photos (professional headshot, logo)

    Why it matters: When someone in London Ontario searches "tax preparer near me" or "income tax return London Ontario," Google shows Business Profiles. A profile with 20+ reviews and complete information ranks in the top 3–5 results. Each result costs $0.

    Getting reviews: After every successful filing, send a text: "Thank you for trusting me with your return this year. If you have a moment, a Google review would mean a lot to my practice — it helps others find me. [Google review link]"

    30 five-star Google reviews with specific comments ("Found $2,000 in deductions I didn't know about") creates a referral machine that runs 24/7.

    Facebook: Create a business page. Post tax tips during January–April. Share reminders about deadlines. Answer questions publicly (without sharing client information). Facebook groups for local communities are particularly effective.

    Instagram: Post educational content. Simple infographics: "5 deductions most employees miss." Behind-the-scenes of tax season. Not glamorous — but builds trust and keeps you top-of-mind.

    LinkedIn: Professional audience. Connect with HR professionals, small business owners, and financial advisors. Post content about tax planning, year-end strategies. Position yourself as the go-to individual tax resource.

    What NOT to do: Don't spend $500/month on Facebook ads before you have 100 reviews and a referral engine working. Ads are expensive for tax preparation — the CPL (cost per lead) is high and trust is low for cold traffic.

    One strong referral partnership beats 100 marketing activities.

    Partners who refer individual tax clients: - Mortgage brokers (clients need T1 for income verification) - Real estate agents (home buyers, first-time buyers, estate sales) - Financial advisors (investment clients, RRSP season) - Employment lawyers (severance recipients, wrongful dismissal) - Immigration consultants (new permanent residents) - HR departments (employees of companies, especially after mass hiring) - Settlement agencies (newcomers who need tax help) - Insurance brokers (business owners they serve)

    Approach: "I specialize in individual tax returns and I'm building my practice. If any of your clients need a reliable preparer, I'd be glad to take good care of them — and I'll always refer appropriate clients back to you."

    One active mortgage broker who sends you 20 clients per year is worth more than any advertising campaign.

    23.8 Your Online Presence — The Minimum Viable Website¶

    You do not need an elaborate website. You need a simple, professional, mobile-friendly page that:

    1. Clearly states who you are and what you do
    2. Shows your service area (Ontario, London, GTA, etc.)
    3. Lists services and approximate pricing
    4. Has a clear call to action ("Book an Appointment" or "Call 519-XXX-XXXX")
    5. Shows social proof (Google review widget or quotes)
    6. Has your Google Business Profile link

    Build it in a weekend using Squarespace, Wix, or WordPress.

    Minimum pages: - Home (who you are, what you do, CTA) - Services (with pricing) - About (your background, why people should trust you) - Contact (form + phone + email)

    Domain: Register "ChenTaxServices.ca" at Namecheap or GoDaddy for $15–$20/year. .ca domains rank better for Canadian searches than .com.

    One image that builds trust: A professional headshot. Not a stock photo. Not a cartoon logo. A real photo of you, well-lit, professional background. Clients hire people, not logos.

    23.9 CRA Requirements for Preparers — What the Law Requires¶

    Beyond EFILE registration, CRA has specific expectations of paid tax preparers:

    The EFILE Code of Ethics¶

    When you register for EFILE, you agree to CRA's EFILE Code of Ethics, which includes:

    Obligation What It Means
    Accuracy File accurate returns based on information provided
    Client identity verification Verify the identity of each client
    T183 requirement Obtain signed T183 before each electronic filing
    Record retention Keep copies of all returns filed and T183s for 6 years
    Conflict of interest Avoid situations where your interests conflict with the client's
    Non-use of information Do not use client tax information for your own benefit
    Reporting suspicious activity Report suspected fraud if legally required
    Compliance with CRA requests Respond to CRA enquiries about your filed returns

    The Consequences of EFILE Violations¶

    CRA can revoke EFILE authorization for preparers who: - File returns containing false information - Fail to obtain T183 authorizations - Fail to provide records when requested - Engage in conduct that is not in the client's best interest - Have a pattern of filing returns that result in significant reassessments

    EFILE revocation is effectively the end of your practice. Without it, you cannot file electronically for anyone. It is the most severe professional consequence in this field below criminal prosecution.

    The Preparer Identification Number¶

    Since 2013, CRA has required paid tax preparers to include their EFILE number on every return they prepare for compensation. This creates an audit trail linking returns to preparers.

    Implication: CRA can identify preparers with high rates of reassessment, unusual claim patterns, or compliance concerns. The EFILE number is the mechanism by which preparer accountability functions. Your reputation with CRA is built over years — maintain it.

    23.11 Ethical Boundaries — The Lines You Never Cross¶

    Understanding where your professional obligation ends is as important as knowing where it begins.

    The Bright Lines¶

    Never: - File a return you know to contain false information, regardless of what the client asks - Claim deductions without supporting documentation that you haven't verified exists - Sign T183 forms before completion or on behalf of the client - Share client information with anyone without written consent - Provide tax advice in areas beyond your competence (corporate restructuring, international tax treaties, tax court matters) - Accept payment contingent on the size of the refund ("I'll charge 10% of your refund") — this creates incentives to inflate deductions and is widely considered unethical and potentially fraudulent

    Always: - Advise clients when their situation requires a CPA or tax lawyer - Document unusual claims and client explanations - Maintain a clean record with CRA - Disclose your limitations honestly - Recommend the Voluntary Disclosure Program when appropriate rather than covering up past errors

    Handling a Client Who Asks You to Commit Fraud¶

    This happens. It will happen to you. Here is exactly how to handle it.

    The scenario: A client says: "I heard you can write off a home office even if I don't work from home. Can you just put that in?"

    The professional response: "I understand you'd like to reduce your taxes — that's exactly what I'm here to help with. However, home office expenses for employees require a signed T2200 from your employer confirming you were required to work from home, and for the self-employed, the space must be your principal place of business. Since that's not your situation, we can't claim it legitimately. What I can do is make sure we've captured every deduction you genuinely qualify for — let me walk through your situation and find what we might be missing."

    You have: 1. Declined the fraudulent claim 2. Explained why (not preachy — factual) 3. Redirected to finding legitimate alternatives 4. Protected yourself legally

    The client who persists after this explanation: Thank them for their time, decline the engagement, and move on. This client is not worth the professional and legal risk they represent.

    23.12 Case Studies — Practice Setup in the Real World¶

    Case A — Starting From Zero: James's First Practice¶

    Background: James, 35, has been a bank teller for 10 years. He completed this tax course and wants to build a side practice that becomes his primary income in 3 years.

    His starting situation: - Living in London, Ontario - Has connections in his workplace and Filipino community - Starting budget: $3,000

    His launch plan:

    Item Action Cost
    Business name "Santos Financial Services" registered $60
    Business number Applied online $0
    Bank account RBC Small Business account $6/month
    EFILE Applied in October $0
    Software TaxCycle T1 Professional (Year 1 bundle) $600
    E&O Insurance AAPITA group policy $650/year
    Website Squarespace 5-page site $240/year
    Google Business Profile Created and verified $0
    Domain SantosFinancialServices.ca $20/year
    Total first-year setup $1,570

    His first 30 clients: - 12 from his Filipino church community (referrals from his aunt who spread the word) - 8 from coworkers at the bank - 4 from a free tax session at a newcomers' centre - 3 referrals from a friend who is a mortgage broker - 3 organic Google searches (his profile had 7 reviews by February)

    First tax season revenue: - 30 returns × average fee $175 = $5,250 gross - Costs: Software ($600) + Insurance ($650) + admin ($200) = $1,450 - Net first-year profit: $3,800

    Not retirement money — but it covers the setup costs, builds skills, and generates a client base. Year 2: 60–80 clients at higher fees. Year 3: 120–150 clients. Year 5: full-time practice.

    The key: James didn't wait until he had 500 clients to set up properly. He set up properly before client #1. Every client arrived to a professional engagement letter, a secure portal, organized filing, and a responsive preparer. That professionalism IS the marketing.

    Case B — The Pricing Conversation¶

    Client: A potential client calls. "How much do you charge for a simple tax return?"

    Unprepared response: "Um... $50? I'm trying to build my practice..." What happens: You attract $50 clients. Your reputation becomes "the cheap one." You do 500 returns to make $25,000. You burn out.

    Prepared response: "My fee depends on the complexity of your situation. For a straightforward employment return with standard deductions and credits, it's $150. If you have investments, self-employment, or rental income, the fee reflects the additional complexity — typically $225–$350. Can I ask a few questions about your situation to give you an accurate quote?"

    What happens: You attract clients who value professional service. The few who hang up because you're "too expensive" were not your clients anyway. You build a quality practice at sustainable fees.

    The script that reframes the conversation: "My fee includes verifying all your slips through CRA's system, optimizing any credits and deductions, handling any CRA correspondence for this return, and your peace of mind that it's done correctly. Most clients find I identify $500–$2,000 in savings they weren't aware of. In that context, the fee is usually the best investment you make all year."

    Case C — The Privacy Incident (Prevention)¶

    Scenario: Ahmed, a new preparer, sends a completed T1 to his client via regular email. His email provider is hacked three days later. The client's full name, SIN, address, income, and family details are now potentially accessible to attackers.

    The consequences: - Ahmed has a PIPEDA obligation to notify the client and the Privacy Commissioner - The client is at risk of identity theft - If the SIN is used fraudulently, it could take years to resolve - Ahmed's professional reputation is damaged

    What should have happened: 1. The T1 was delivered via Ahmed's secure client portal (most tax software includes one) 2. The client received a notification: "Your tax documents are ready in your secure portal. Click here to access them with your password." 3. No unencrypted documents were ever in email

    The cost difference: Zero. The secure portal is included in professional tax software. The risk difference: Catastrophic vs. protected.

    The rule: Treat every document containing a client's SIN as if it were their bank password. Because in the hands of a fraudster, it effectively is.

    23.13 Week 23 Summary¶

    What you learned this week

    • No licence required to prepare individual T1 returns in Canada — but EFILE registration, insurance, and engagement letters are all mandatory before your first paying client
    • Business structure: Start as a sole proprietorship. Register your business name in Ontario for $60. Get a Business Number from CRA. Open a business bank account. These four steps establish your practice legally
    • HST: Required once revenue exceeds $30,000. Voluntary registration is worth considering earlier for ITC recovery on software and insurance
    • E&O insurance: $500–$1,500/year. Minimum $1,000,000/$2,000,000 coverage. Buy before client #1. Non-negotiable
    • Engagement letter: Defines scope, makes client responsible for completeness, limits your liability. Have every client sign it. The clause that makes the client responsible for disclosing complete information is your most important legal protection
    • PIPEDA: You handle sensitive data. Encrypt everything. Use secure portals for document delivery. Never email unencrypted returns. Have a breach response plan
    • Pricing: Charge based on complexity and value — not time. The market range in Ontario is $95–$600+ depending on return type. Value-based framing before price disclosure changes every conversation
    • Marketing: 80% of a strong practice comes from referrals. Google Business Profile with reviews is your most powerful digital asset (and free). Community presence in new immigrant communities builds practices faster than any advertising. One referral partner (mortgage broker, immigration consultant) beats 100 marketing activities
    • CRA requirements: EFILE code of ethics. Include your EFILE number on all filed returns. T183 mandatory before every transmission. Records retained 6 years. EFILE revocation is the professional death penalty — avoid conduct that risks it
    • Ethical bright lines: Never file what you know to be false. Never accept fee-as-percentage-of-refund arrangements. Always decline and document when a client asks you to cross the line

    ✅ Week 23 Quiz¶

    When you're ready, ask Claude: "Quiz me on Week 23" for an interactive test.

    1. James wants to start preparing tax returns professionally in Ontario. A friend tells him he needs a CPA designation to charge for tax preparation. Is this correct? What does James actually need before his first paying client?

    Incorrect. There is no mandatory professional designation required to prepare individual T1 personal tax returns for compensation in Canada. James needs: (1) EFILE registration with CRA — required to file more than 5 returns for compensation. (2) E&O insurance — minimum $1M/$2M, purchased before client #1. (3) Engagement letter — signed by every client defining scope and limitations. (4) Business registration — if operating under a business name other than his own legal name ($60 at ServiceOntario). (5) Tax software — EFILE-certified professional software. (6) CRA My Account RepID — to access client information as an authorized representative. CPAs and licensed professionals have additional regulated powers (T2 corporate returns, audit representation, public accounting) — but T1 individual preparation does not require them.

    2. Maria has been preparing returns for 8 months. Her annual gross preparation fees are now $28,000. Should she register for HST now or wait until she crosses $30,000? What are the practical considerations?

    At $28,000, she is not legally required to register (below the $30,000 threshold). However, voluntary registration is worth considering because: (1) She is approximately $2,000 from the mandatory threshold — she may cross it within weeks or months at current growth. Registering proactively avoids the scramble of registering mid-client-season. (2) Once registered, she can claim Input Tax Credits (ITCs) on HST paid on business expenses: her tax software (~$600 + 13% HST = $78 ITC), E&O insurance (exempt, no HST), office supplies, and other taxable business costs. (3) Administratively, it's easier to set up the system now than after a chaotic tax season crossing the threshold. If she registers: she begins charging 13% HST on new fees. Most business clients are indifferent (they claim it as ITCs). Individual clients pay 13% more — consider whether to absorb this in your fee or add it explicitly.

    3. A client hands you their documents and you prepare their return. At the end, before you ask them to sign the T183, you notice your engagement letter template is back at your office. Can you have the client sign the T183 now and send the engagement letter later? What is the correct process?

    Do not file before the engagement letter is signed. The engagement letter defines the scope of your engagement and — critically — makes the client responsible for the completeness of information they provided. If you file before it's signed and a problem arises (client omitted income, a claim is disputed), you have no documented limitation of liability. The T183 specifically authorizes electronic filing; the engagement letter governs the entire professional relationship. Correct process: (1) Send engagement letter to client (email is fine — ask them to sign and return via email, DocuSign, or in person). (2) Once signed engagement letter is received, have the client sign T183. (3) Transmit the return. Never skip or reverse this order.

    4. Ahmed charges his clients a fee equal to 15% of their refund — the bigger the refund, the more he earns. He reasons that clients like it because they only pay if they get money back. What are the ethical and legal problems with this arrangement?

    This is a contingency fee arrangement tied to the size of a tax refund — widely considered unethical in tax preparation for two reasons: (1) Creates a perverse incentive: Ahmed is financially motivated to maximize the refund regardless of whether deductions are legitimate. This creates pressure to file questionable or false claims. Under Section 163.2 of the Income Tax Act, a preparer who knowingly participates in false statements faces civil penalties of up to $100,000. (2) Conflicts with the client's interest: A legitimate refund is good; an inflated refund based on fabricated deductions creates future CRA reassessments, penalties, and interest — harming the client. CRA's EFILE Code of Ethics and professional standards in this industry universally prohibit percentage-of-refund fee arrangements. Ahmed should charge flat fees or hourly rates based on the complexity of the return — not contingent on the outcome.

    5. Sandra operates her tax practice from her home in London, Ontario. She emails completed tax returns (as PDF attachments) directly to clients. A friend who works in cybersecurity tells her this creates a privacy risk. What specific risk does email transmission create and what should Sandra do instead?

    Email is not a secure transmission channel. Completed tax returns contain: full legal name, SIN, date of birth, home address, employer information, income details, family situation, medical information, and financial account details. An unencrypted email attachment is: readable if the email is intercepted in transit, accessible if either email account is compromised, stored permanently in sent/received folders (which are frequent hack targets), and potentially misdirected if Sandra types a wrong address. Under PIPEDA, Sandra has an obligation to protect client information using appropriate security measures. What she should do instead: (1) Use the secure client portal included in professional tax software (TaxCycle, ProFile) — clients receive a password-protected link to access their documents. (2) If the client insists on email — send a password-protected PDF and communicate the password through a separate channel (text message). (3) Never email SINs or full returns as unprotected attachments under any circumstances.

    6. Kevin just finished his first tax season. He prepared 35 returns at an average fee of $130 each — $4,550 gross. His software cost $600, insurance $650, and business registration $60. He has no reviews on Google yet. What should his top three priorities be in May and June to set up Year 2 as a significantly stronger season?

    (1) Get Google reviews immediately. Send a personalized message to each of his 35 clients: "Thank you for your trust this tax season. If you have 2 minutes, a Google review would genuinely help my practice grow — it helps other families find a reliable preparer. [Google link]." Even 10–15 reviews transforms his visibility in local search. Reviews from this season are most authentically written while the experience is fresh. (2) Ask for referrals explicitly. Follow up with every client: "I'm building my practice and would love to serve your family and friends. If anyone you know needs a reliable preparer for next year, I'd be grateful for the introduction." Converting even 5 of his 35 clients into referral sources who each send 2–3 new clients = 10–15 new clients for Year 2. (3) Raise his prices. At $130 average, Kevin is below market and cannot sustain quality at this rate. For Year 2, he should set $150–$175 as his minimum and $225–$350 for complex returns. Year 2 with 60 clients at $195 average = $11,700 gross — 2.6× Year 1 revenue from better pricing alone.

    7. A potential client calls and says their current preparer charges $65 and they want to know if you can match it. How do you respond?

    A professional response that converts the call: "My fee for a return like yours is $150 [or whatever is appropriate]. I know that's more than $65 — let me explain what the difference means to you. Before I file, I verify all your slips directly through CRA's system — I've caught missing T5s and investment slips that would have triggered reassessments. I optimize your medical expenses across the best 12-month window, make sure credits are claimed on the right family member's return, and check your RRSP room. Most clients find I identify $400–$2,000 more in savings than they expected. My E&O insurance also covers any errors — yours and mine. The $85 difference is usually recovered in the first ten minutes of our appointment. I'd love to show you that. Would you like to book?" If they decline, wish them well. The client who chooses the $65 option was not your ideal client. Your ideal client chooses you because they understand value.
