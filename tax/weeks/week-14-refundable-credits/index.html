    Week 14: Refundable Credits and Benefits — Cash Payments Driven by Net Income¶

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    • Explain why refundable credits are fundamentally different from non-refundable ones
    • Calculate the GST/HST credit and identify who is missing it by not filing
    • Calculate the Canada Child Benefit with precision — including the phase-out mechanics
    • Explain the Canada Workers Benefit and identify every client who should claim it
    • Navigate the Ontario Trillium Benefit and its three component programs
    • Apply the First Home Savings Account (FHSA) — the newest registered account in Canada
    • Understand how Net Income (Line 23600) is the master lever controlling all these benefits
    • Identify the net income thresholds that trigger benefit cliffs — and how to plan around them

    The most important insight of this entire week

    Every refundable credit and income-tested benefit in this week flows from a single number: Net Income on Line 23600. A $1,000 reduction in net income — through an RRSP contribution, a childcare deduction, or any above-the-line deduction — does not just save the client their marginal tax rate. It may also increase their GST/HST credit, increase their Canada Child Benefit, reduce OAS clawback, and unlock or increase provincial benefits. The combined effect can be worth two to three times the face value of the deduction. This multiplier effect is one of the most powerful and least understood concepts in Canadian tax planning.

    14.1 Refundable vs. Non-Refundable — The Critical Distinction¶

    Feature Non-Refundable Credit Refundable Credit / Benefit
    Reduces tax below $0? ❌ No — excess is lost or transferred ✅ Yes — paid out in full regardless of tax
    Value if no tax is owing? $0 — worthless Full amount — paid as a direct payment
    Driven by? The credit amount and tax rate Net Income (Line 23600) — family situation
    Examples Basic Personal, DTC, medical GST/HST credit, CCB, CWB
    Who benefits most? Anyone with federal tax to reduce Low- and middle-income earners

    The consequence of this distinction is profound: a client with zero tax owing — a student, a caregiver, a part-time worker — gets nothing from additional non-refundable credits. But they may receive thousands in refundable benefits simply by filing a return. Many low-income Canadians never file because they think they "don't owe anything." They are leaving real cash on the table.

    Filing is the trigger — not having tax owing

    The GST/HST credit, Canada Child Benefit, and Canada Workers Benefit are all triggered by filing a tax return, not by having income tax to pay. A single mother earning $28,000 who doesn't file loses her entire CCB — potentially $5,000–$9,000 per year — because no return was filed. One of your highest-value services is identifying these clients and ensuring they file every year without exception.

    14.2 The GST/HST Credit — Line 45200¶

    The GST/HST credit is a tax-free quarterly payment that offsets the regressive nature of sales tax for low- and middle-income Canadians. It is administered by CRA and paid automatically once a return is filed — no separate application is needed.

    Who Qualifies¶

    • Canadian resident age 19 or older (or has/had a spouse or CLP, or is/was a parent)
    • Below the income phase-out threshold
    • Filed a tax return — this is the trigger

    2025 Credit Amounts (Annual)¶

    Component Amount
    Base (individual) $519
    Spousal/CLP supplement $519
    Per child under 19 $137

    A family of four (couple + 2 children): $519 + $519 + $137 + $137 = $1,312/year — paid in four quarterly installments of $328.

    The Phase-Out¶

    The credit begins reducing once adjusted family net income exceeds approximately $44,324 (2025 — indexed annually).

    Reduction = (Adjusted Family Net Income − $44,324) × 5%

    Full elimination threshold: approximately $54,704 for a single person; higher for families with children.

    Case — GST/HST Credit at Different Income Levels

    Single individual, no children:

    Net Income Annual Credit Calculation
    $25,000 $519 Below threshold — full credit
    $44,324 $519 At threshold — still full
    $48,000 $334 ($48,000 − $44,324) × 5% = $184 reduction
    $54,704 $0 Fully phased out

    The GST/HST credit is invisible to high earners — but for a client earning $32,000, $519 is two weeks of groceries. Never overlook it. Never assume a client is "too high-income" without checking the phase-out thresholds.

    Practical Planning — Net Income Is the Lever¶

    Case — The RRSP That Unlocks the GST/HST Credit

    Marcus earns $46,000. His GST/HST credit: Reduction: ($46,000 − $44,324) × 5% = $84 Annual credit: $519 − $84 = $435

    Marcus makes a $3,000 RRSP contribution. New net income: $43,000 — below the $44,324 threshold. Annual GST/HST credit: $519 (full amount restored)

    The RRSP saved him $3,000 × 20.05% = $601 in income tax. It also recovered $84 in additional annual GST/HST credit. Total value of the $3,000 RRSP contribution: $601 + $84 = $685 — a 22.8% immediate return.

    This multiplier effect — where a deduction triggers benefit recovery — is what separates sophisticated planning from simple tax filing.

    14.3 Canada Child Benefit — The Largest Benefit Program¶

    The Canada Child Benefit (CCB) is the most significant income-transfer program for families with children. Introduced in 2016, it replaced the UCCB and CCTB with a fully income-tested, tax-free monthly payment. For many families, it is worth more than any tax deduction or credit on their return.

    Who Qualifies¶

    • Primary caregiver of a child under 18 who is a Canadian resident
    • In shared custody: each parent receives 50% of what they would have received as sole custodian
    • Applied for automatically when a child is registered with CRA — but the primary caregiver must file a return every year for payments to continue

    2025 Annual CCB Amounts (Before Phase-Out)¶

    Child's Age Annual Amount Per Child
    Under 6 $7,787 ($648.92/month)
    Age 6 to 17 $6,570 ($547.50/month)

    Child Disability Benefit (CDB) supplement: $3,322/year per child who is DTC-approved (added to the base CCB amount).

    The Phase-Out — Two Tiers¶

    The CCB phases out in two stages based on adjusted family net income (AFNI):

    Tier 1 — Phase-out begins at $36,502 AFNI:

    Number of Children Reduction Rate
    1 child 13.5% of AFNI above $36,502
    2 children 19.0%
    3 children 22.0%
    4+ children 23.0%

    Tier 2 — Second phase-out begins at $79,087 AFNI:

    Number of Children Additional Reduction Rate
    1 child 5.7% of AFNI above $79,087
    2 children 8.0%
    3 children 9.5%
    4+ children 10.0%

    Case — CCB Calculation for a Family of Three

    Helen and Victor have two children — ages 3 and 9. Their combined adjusted family net income (AFNI) is $72,000.

    Maximum CCB (before phase-out): - Child age 3 (under 6): $7,787 - Child age 9 (6–17): $6,570 - Total maximum: $14,357/year

    Tier 1 phase-out: Reduction rate for 2 children: 19.0% Phase-out on ($72,000 − $36,502) = $35,498 × 19.0% = $6,745

    Annual CCB: $14,357 − $6,745 = $7,612/year ($634/month)

    At $72,000 combined income, they still receive a meaningful benefit. At $120,000, the benefit would be largely phased out. At $200,000+, it would be zero.

    The CCB Net Income Multiplier — Where Planning Pays Off¶

    Case — The $5,000 RRSP That Generates $1,950 More in CCB

    Patricia is a single mother earning $48,000. She has two children, ages 4 and 7.

    Current CCB (AFNI = $48,000): Maximum: $7,787 + $6,570 = $14,357 Tier 1 phase-out: ($48,000 − $36,502) × 19.0% = $11,498 × 19% = $2,185 Annual CCB: $14,357 − $2,185 = $12,172

    Patricia contributes $5,000 to her RRSP. New AFNI: $43,000.

    New CCB: Phase-out: ($43,000 − $36,502) × 19.0% = $6,498 × 19% = $1,235 Annual CCB: $14,357 − $1,235 = $13,122

    CCB increase from $5,000 RRSP: $13,122 − $12,172 = $950/year (ongoing, every July)

    Plus the income tax saving from the RRSP deduction: $5,000 × 29.65% = $1,483

    Total value of the $5,000 RRSP contribution: $1,483 (tax) + $950 (CCB) = $2,433 Effective return: 48.7% on a $5,000 investment — the CCB multiplier at work.

    This is not a hypothetical. This is the math behind every single-parent or dual-income family with children in the $37,000–$80,000 AFNI range. Every RRSP contribution, every deduction, every dollar of net income reduced generates both a tax saving and a CCB increase. Clients who understand this become your most loyal clients.

    The CCB July Reset — Why Prior Year Income Determines Current Benefits¶

    The CCB is recalculated every July 1 based on the prior year's tax returns. This means:

    • July 2025 to June 2026 CCB is based on 2024 tax returns
    • Filing on time matters — late filers have their CCB suspended until the return is assessed

    CCB suspension — a real and common problem

    If a client fails to file their prior year return on time, CRA suspends CCB payments after the July reset until the return is filed and assessed. A family receiving $1,200/month who misses the filing deadline may go 2–3 months without CCB before it is reinstated.

    Retroactive payments are issued once the return is filed — but the cash flow disruption is real and stressful. Alert every CCB-receiving client: filing on time is not optional when CCB is involved.

    Shared Custody¶

    In shared custody arrangements (at least 40% of time with each parent), each parent receives 50% of the CCB they would have received as the sole custodian, based on their own net income.

    Case — Shared Custody CCB Split

    Daniel and his ex-wife Sarah have shared custody of their 8-year-old son (50/50).

    If Daniel were sole custodian (his AFNI $68,000, 1 child): Max: $6,570. Phase-out: ($68,000 − $36,502) × 13.5% = $4,252. Full CCB: $6,570 − $4,252 = $2,318 Daniel receives 50%: $1,159/year ($96.58/month)

    If Sarah were sole custodian (her AFNI $42,000, 1 child): Max: $6,570. Phase-out: ($42,000 − $36,502) × 13.5% = $742. Full CCB: $6,570 − $742 = $5,828 Sarah receives 50%: $2,914/year ($242.83/month)

    Combined family CCB: $1,159 + $2,914 = $4,073/year — less than if either had sole custody at their income levels, but reflecting the shared arrangement. This is correctly calculated and filed automatically once both parents file their returns and the custody arrangement is documented.

    14.4 Canada Workers Benefit — Line 45300¶

    The Canada Workers Benefit (CWB) is a refundable credit for low-income working Canadians. It replaced the Working Income Tax Benefit (WITB) in 2019 and was significantly enhanced in 2021 and again in 2023.

    The CWB rewards employment and self-employment — it is only available to people who worked during the year. It does not apply to passive income.

    2025 Basic CWB Amounts¶

    Marital / Family Status Phase-In Begins Maximum Amount Phase-Out Begins Phase-Out Rate
    Single, no dependants $3,000 earned income $1,518 $23,495 15%
    Family (with spouse or eligible dependant) $3,000 earned income $2,616 $26,805 15%

    CWB Disability Supplement: Additional amounts for individuals eligible for the DTC — up to $737 for singles, $737 for families.

    How the Phase-In and Phase-Out Work¶

    Phase-In: Earned income between $3,000 and ~$13,120
  CWB = (Earned income − $3,000) × 27% [approx. — actual rates vary slightly]

Maximum: Between ~$13,120 and $23,495 (single)
  CWB = $1,518

Phase-Out: Earned income above $23,495
  CWB = $1,518 − (Net income − $23,495) × 15%
  Zero at approximately: $33,607 (single)

    Case — The CWB for a Low-Income Worker

    Rosa works part-time, earning $18,000/year. She has no dependants and is single.

    She is in the maximum range (above $13,120, below $23,495). CWB: $1,518 — paid as a refundable credit even if she owes no tax.

    Rosa has never heard of the CWB. She thought her tax return would be zero since she doesn't earn much. Her return is actually: - Federal tax owing: $0 (income below BPA) - CWB refundable credit: $1,518 - GST/HST credit: $519 - Total refund: $2,037 — from a return she nearly didn't bother filing.

    This is why you file for every client, regardless of income level.

    The Advance CWB Payments¶

    Starting in 2023, CRA began paying 50% of the estimated CWB in advance during the year — in three equal payments (July, October, January). The advance payments are reconciled on the following year's return.

    Advance CWB creates a potential repayment on the return

    If a client received advance CWB payments during 2025 (July and October 2025, January 2026) but their actual 2025 income made them ineligible or entitled to less than the advance, they must repay the difference on their 2025 return. Always ask clients: "Did you receive any advance Canada Workers Benefit payments this year?" CRA My Account will show these. Failure to reconcile creates a balance owing.

    14.5 The Ontario Trillium Benefit — Three Programs in One¶

    The Ontario Trillium Benefit (OTB) is an Ontario-specific refundable credit that combines three credits into a single monthly payment:

    1. Ontario Energy and Property Tax Credit (OEPTC) — for rent paid or property tax paid in Ontario
    2. Northern Ontario Energy Credit (NOEC) — for residents of northern Ontario
    3. Ontario Sales Tax Credit (OSTC) — similar to the GST/HST credit for Ontario sales tax

    Eligibility Requirements¶

    Credit Key Requirements
    OEPTC Ontario resident, age 18+, paid rent or property tax on a principal residence in Ontario
    NOEC Ontario resident in a northern district (defined list), paid energy costs
    OSTC Ontario resident, age 19+ (or has/had spouse/CLP, or is/was a parent)

    The OEPTC — Where the Real Money Is¶

    The OEPTC has two components:

    Energy component: Up to $277/year (higher in northern Ontario) Property tax component: Based on property tax or rent paid

    For renters: 20% of rent is deemed "property tax equivalent" For homeowners: actual property tax paid

    Case — The Renter's Trillium Benefit

    Sandra rents an apartment in Toronto for $1,800/month ($21,600/year). She is 28, single, no children. Net income: $36,000.

    OEPTC property tax component: 20% × $21,600 = $4,320 (deemed property tax) OEPTC formula (simplified): $277 (energy) + property tax credit (calculated on ON-BEN) ≈ $1,100 total OEPTC

    OSTC: Approximately $345 (based on income)

    Total annual OTB: approximately $1,445 — paid monthly ($120/month)

    Sandra has been filing her own taxes and has never claimed her rent payments. She thought the Trillium Benefit was only for homeowners. She has missed approximately $1,445/year for several years.

    Ask every Ontario renter: "Did you claim your rent on your Ontario Trillium Benefit form?" Many don't know it applies to them.

    Claiming the OTB — Form ON-BEN¶

    The OTB is claimed on Form ON-BEN (Application for the Ontario Trillium Benefit) filed with the T1. The form requires:

    • Principal residence address
    • Monthly rent paid (or property tax billed)
    • Whether the client owns or rents

    Payment timing: OTB is paid monthly from July to June. If the annual amount is under $360, it is paid as a single lump sum in July.

    Landlord's name and address are required on ON-BEN

    CRA may request verification of rent paid. Best practice: retain copies of lease agreements and rent receipts. For clients in informal rental arrangements (renting from relatives), encourage written documentation.

    14.6 The First Home Savings Account (FHSA) — Canada's Newest Registered Account¶

    The FHSA launched in April 2023. It is the most significant new savings vehicle introduced in decades — combining the best features of both the RRSP and TFSA specifically for first-time home buyers.

    The Core Rules¶

    Feature FHSA
    Annual contribution limit $8,000
    Lifetime contribution limit $40,000
    Contributions are Tax-deductible (like RRSP)
    Withdrawals for home purchase are Tax-free (like TFSA)
    Qualifying withdrawal repayment? ❌ None — unlike the HBP
    Who qualifies First-time home buyer, Canadian resident, age 18–71
    First-time buyer definition Did not own a principal residence in the current year or any of the preceding 4 calendar years
    Account lifespan Maximum 15 years (or until age 71)
    Unused annual room Carries forward — up to $8,000 maximum carryforward
    Investment income inside Tax-free while in the account

    The FHSA is superior to the HBP in almost every case

    The HBP requires repayment over 15 years. A missed repayment is added to income. The FHSA has no repayment obligation. For a client buying a home: - FHSA: deduction now + tax-free withdrawal forever - HBP: deduction when contributed + tax-free withdrawal + 15-year repayment obligation

    For clients who haven't yet opened an FHSA, open one now — even if the home purchase is years away. Room accumulates (up to $8,000 carryforward), investments grow tax-free, and the deduction is available immediately.

    How the Carryforward Works¶

    The FHSA carryforward works differently from the RRSP: - You must open the account before room begins accumulating - Unused room from a year (up to $8,000) carries forward to the next year - Maximum carryforward at any one time: $8,000 (one year's worth)

    Case — FHSA Carryforward Strategy

    Rachel opened her FHSA in June 2023 but contributed nothing that year. In 2024 she contributed $8,000. In 2025 she contributes $8,000 again.

    Year Annual Room Carryforward Available Total Room Contributed Remaining CF
    2023 $8,000 $0 (first year) $8,000 $0 $8,000 CF
    2024 $8,000 $8,000 CF $16,000 $8,000 $8,000 CF
    2025 $8,000 $8,000 CF $16,000 $8,000 $8,000 CF

    Rachel has contributed $16,000 and deducted $16,000 over two years. Her FHSA balance (assuming 6% growth) is approximately $17,300. This is all available as a tax-free withdrawal for a qualifying home purchase.

    If she had waited until 2025 to open the account: Zero carryforward from prior years — she would have only $8,000 in room for 2025. Opening the account early — even without contributing — is one of the best low-effort tax moves for any client who might buy a home within 15 years.

    FHSA + HBP — They Can Be Combined¶

    A qualifying first-time buyer can use both the FHSA and the HBP on the same home purchase.

    FHSA maximum tax-free withdrawal: $40,000 HBP maximum tax-free withdrawal: $35,000 Combined: up to $75,000 per person ($150,000 per qualifying couple)

    Case — The Maximum First Home Strategy

    Ahmed and his partner Sandra are both first-time buyers saving for a home. Each has maximized their FHSA ($40,000 each) and has RRSP room for the HBP ($35,000 each).

    Combined tax-free home down payment: FHSA: $40,000 + $40,000 = $80,000 (no repayment obligation) HBP: $35,000 + $35,000 = $70,000 (15-year repayment obligation for each) Total: $150,000 tax-free withdrawal for the down payment

    Each also received a tax deduction on both the FHSA contributions and the RRSP contributions. They've essentially received a government subsidy of tens of thousands of dollars on their home purchase — entirely through the proper use of registered accounts.

    What Happens If They Don't Buy a Home¶

    If the account holder hasn't used the FHSA to buy a home by the end of 15 years (or age 71), the account must be closed. The balance can be:

    • Transferred to an RRSP (without using RRSP contribution room) — the fallback is excellent
    • Withdrawn as taxable income (less optimal, but available)

    The risk of opening an FHSA is essentially zero. If the client never buys a home, they roll the balance into their RRSP tax-free. The deductions they claimed still stand.

    14.7 Net Income as the Master Lever — The Benefit Multiplier Framework¶

    This is the most important synthesis in this entire week. Every benefit in Sections 14.2 through 14.5 is driven by Net Income (Line 23600). Reducing net income creates a cascade of benefits that compounds the value of every deduction.

    The Benefit Cascade for a Middle-Income Family¶

    Case — The Full Multiplier Effect

    Patricia is a single mother with two children (ages 5 and 9). She earns $52,000 as an employee in Ontario. She has $6,000 in RRSP contribution room.

    Current situation (no RRSP): | Benefit | Annual Amount | |---|---| | Federal income tax | ~$5,100 | | Ontario tax | ~$2,200 | | CCB (AFNI $52,000, 2 children) | ~$10,800 | | GST/HST credit | ~$519 | | OTB (renter, $1,400/month rent) | ~$1,350 | | Net position | |

    After $6,000 RRSP contribution (net income: $46,000):

    Benefit Change Amount
    Federal income tax reduction $6,000 × 20.5% = −$1,230
    Ontario income tax reduction $6,000 × 9.15% = −$549
    CCB increase (AFNI drops $6,000 × 19% rate) +$1,140/year
    GST/HST credit increase $6,000 × 5% (below threshold) +$300
    OTB increase (modest) +$60
    Total annual value of $6,000 RRSP $3,279
    Effective return on $6,000 54.7%

    Patricia gets a 54.7% immediate return on her RRSP contribution — not 20.5%. The difference is the benefit multiplier. A preparer who only explains the income tax saving is giving her less than half the picture.

    The conversation: "Patricia, your $6,000 RRSP contribution doesn't just save you $1,779 in income tax. It also increases your Canada Child Benefit by $1,140 and your GST credit by $300. Your total benefit is $3,279 from a $6,000 contribution — that's more than a 50% return. Would you like to contribute the maximum?"

    14.8 The Benefit Cliffs — Where $1 of Income Costs Far More Than $1 in Tax¶

    A benefit cliff occurs when a small increase in net income causes a disproportionately large reduction in benefits — creating an effective marginal rate far above the nominal tax bracket.

    The Major Benefit Thresholds to Know¶

    Threshold Benefit Affected Effect of Crossing It
    $36,502 CCB begins phase-out 13.5–23% benefit reduction rate on every $1 above
    $44,325 Age Amount begins phase-out 15% effective surtax for seniors
    $44,324 GST/HST credit phase-out 5% reduction rate above this
    $79,087 CCB second phase-out Additional 5.7–10% reduction rate
    $90,997 OAS clawback threshold 15% OAS clawback on every $1 above

    Case — The Hidden 70% Marginal Rate

    Helen is 38, single mother, two children, net income of $38,000 (just above the CCB phase-out threshold of $36,502).

    Her nominal marginal rate: 20.5% federal + 9.15% Ontario = 29.65%

    But every extra $1,000 she earns above $36,502 costs her: - Income tax: $1,000 × 29.65% = $297 - CCB reduction: $1,000 × 19% = $190 (for 2 children) - GST/HST reduction: $1,000 × 5% = $50 (if near threshold) - Effective marginal cost: $537 per $1,000 of extra income = 53.7%

    For a single mother with three children, the CCB reduction rate at this level is 22% — pushing the effective rate even higher.

    This is why overtime work can feel pointless for some families. They earn $100 more gross and end up with less in benefits than they gained in net pay. Understanding benefit cliffs lets you give clients honest, complete information about the real financial impact of income changes.

    Strategies to Manage Benefit Cliffs¶

    The most powerful lever. Every dollar contributed reduces AFNI, which increases CCB and other income-tested benefits. For a family near the Tier 1 CCB phase-out threshold of $36,502, every $1,000 of RRSP contributions generates approximately $200–$230 in additional CCB on top of the income tax saving.

    Target: Contribute enough to keep AFNI below the relevant threshold — or at the most efficient point in the phase-out range.

    FHSA contributions are deductible and reduce net income exactly like RRSP contributions. For a young family saving for a home, the FHSA delivers double benefit: the home savings goal AND net income reduction that increases CCB.

    Childcare deductions directly reduce Net Income — and therefore directly increase CCB. A family that forgets to claim $10,000 in childcare expenses doesn't just lose $10,000 × their marginal rate in tax savings. They also lose the CCB increase that would flow from the reduced net income.

    For retired couples, allocating pension income to the lower-income spouse reduces the higher-income spouse's net income — potentially below OAS clawback thresholds, Age Amount reduction thresholds, and other income-tested benefit breakpoints.

    14.9 The Climate Action Incentive (CAI) — Automatic for Most¶

    The Canada Carbon Rebate (formerly Climate Action Incentive Payment) is a tax-free refundable credit paid quarterly to residents of provinces that do not have their own carbon pricing system. As of 2025, this applies to Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba, and New Brunswick.

    Ontario, BC, and Quebec residents do not receive the federal CAI (they have their own provincial carbon systems, though most of those rebates flow through provincial mechanisms rather than the federal T1).

    2025 annual amounts (Alberta, largest province):

    Household Type Annual Amount
    Individual $900
    Spouse/CLP $450
    Per child under 19 $225
    Rural supplement (10% additional) Automatic if rural address

    This payment is automatic — no form required. Filing a return is the only trigger. Rural residents receive the 10% supplement automatically if their postal code is designated rural.

    Clients who moved from Alberta mid-year

    The CAI is based on province of residence on December 31 — the same rule as provincial tax rates. A client who moved from Alberta to Ontario in November receives no CAI for that year (Ontario residents don't get it and they were an Ontario resident on December 31). Alert clients about this in the year of a cross-province move.

    14.10 Benefit Recovery — The Filing-as-Trigger Concept¶

    A core theme of this entire week is that filing the return is the trigger for every benefit described here. Many of these benefits are not automatically determined — CRA needs the return to calculate them.

    Clients Who Should File Even With Zero Income¶

    Client Type Why They Must File Annual Value
    Low-income individual (19+) GST/HST credit $519
    Single parent with children CCB + GST/HST $7,000–$15,000+
    Low-income worker CWB Up to $1,518
    Ontario renter Ontario Trillium Benefit $500–$1,500
    New immigrant (first year) All of the above from the moment they become resident All of the above

    Case — The New Immigrant Who Didn't Know to File

    Mei arrived from Hong Kong on August 15, 2025. She has no employment income for the rest of 2025. Her husband is working and they have a 3-year-old child.

    She assumes she doesn't need to file — she earned nothing.

    What she's missing by not filing: - GST/HST credit: prorated from arrival date — approximately $175 for the partial year - CCB: from the month after her arrival until June 2026 — approximately $3,893 at her husband's income level - Ontario Trillium Benefit: partial year — approximately $400

    Cost of not filing: approximately $4,468 — from a return that takes 20 minutes to prepare.

    Filing for new Canadians in their first year — even with zero personal income — is one of the highest-return services you offer. The return establishes residency, triggers all income-tested benefits, and builds the filing history that CRA requires for ongoing eligibility.

    14.11 Case Studies — Benefits in the Real World¶

    Case A — The Single Mother's Return (Complete Benefit Picture)¶

    Client: Jennifer, 34. Single mother, two children (ages 2 and 6). Ontario resident. Annual income: $44,000 from employment. Pays $1,600/month rent. Pays $12,000/year in daycare (claimed by Jennifer as the lower-income/sole parent).

    After childcare deduction: Net income: $44,000 − $12,000 (childcare, capped at $8,000 under 6 + remaining eligible) = let's say effective deduction of $13,000 → Net income: $31,000

    Benefits at $31,000 AFNI:

    Benefit Calculation Annual Amount
    CCB (2 children) Below $36,502 threshold — full amount: $7,787 + $6,570 $14,357

    14.13 Week 14 Summary¶

    What you learned this week

    • Refundable credits are paid out even if no tax is owing — they are triggered by filing a return, not by having tax liability. Never allow a client to skip filing because they "don't owe anything"
    • GST/HST credit (Line 45200): $519/year for individuals; phase-out begins at $44,324 AFNI at 5%. Automatic on filing. Every low-to-middle income client gets this
    • Canada Child Benefit: The most significant benefit for families — up to $7,787/child under 6 and $6,570/child 6–17. Two phase-out tiers starting at $36,502 and $79,087 AFNI. July reset based on prior year returns — late filing suspends payments
    • The CCB multiplier: A $5,000 RRSP contribution at the right income level can generate $950+ in additional annual CCB on top of the income tax saving — producing effective returns of 40–55% on the contribution
    • Canada Workers Benefit (Line 45300): Up to $1,518 for low-income workers; $2,616 for families. Advance payments reconciled on the return. Disability supplement available for DTC-eligible clients
    • Ontario Trillium Benefit (ON-BEN): Combines OEPTC + NOEC + OSTC. Renters get 20% of rent treated as property tax. Most Ontario renters have no idea this applies to them
    • First Home Savings Account (FHSA): $8,000/year, $40,000 lifetime. Deductible contributions + tax-free qualifying withdrawals + no repayment obligation. Open immediately for any client who might buy a home within 15 years
    • Benefit cliffs: Effective marginal rates for low-income families with children can reach 50–65% once income tax and benefit phase-outs are combined. Understanding this is essential for giving clients complete financial advice
    • Net Income (Line 23600) is the master lever — every dollar of net income reduced through deductions creates a cascade of benefit increases that can be worth 2–3× the face value of the deduction

    ✅ Week 14 Quiz¶

    When you're ready, ask Claude: "Quiz me on Week 14" for an interactive test.

    1. A client earns $29,000 and has never heard of the Canada Workers Benefit. They don't think they need to file because they owe no tax. What do you tell them and what is the approximate dollar value of benefits they are missing?

    Filing is essential — not because they owe tax, but because filing is the trigger for all refundable credits. At $29,000 income (single, no children), they receive: CWB approximately $1,518 (in the maximum range), GST/HST credit $519, and Ontario Trillium Benefit approximately $800 (if Ontario renter). Total annual value of filing: approximately $2,837 — paid as cash refunds and quarterly payments regardless of any tax owing. Not filing costs them nearly $3,000 per year in government payments they have already earned the right to receive.

    2. Helen is a single mother with two children (ages 4 and 8) and an AFNI of $55,000. Calculate her approximate annual CCB.

    Maximum CCB: $7,787 (child under 6) + $6,570 (child 6–17) = $14,357. Phase-out: AFNI above $36,502 = $18,498. Reduction rate for 2 children = 19.0%. Tier 1 reduction: ($55,000 − $36,502) × 19% = $18,498 × 19% = $3,515. Remaining after Tier 1: $14,357 − $3,515 = $10,842/year ($903.50/month). Tier 2 doesn't apply until $79,087. If Helen makes a $5,000 RRSP contribution (AFNI drops to $50,000): Tier 1 reduction: ($50,000 − $36,502) × 19% = $2,565. New CCB: $14,357 − $2,565 = $11,792 — an increase of $950/year just from the RRSP, on top of the income tax saving.

    3. Marcus and his partner opened their FHSAs in January 2024 and contributed nothing in 2024. In 2025, they each want to contribute the maximum. How much can each contribute in 2025 and what is the total deduction available to the couple?

    Each had $8,000 in room for 2024 (unused). The carryforward from 2024 is $8,000 (maximum carryforward is one year's worth). Adding 2025's $8,000 room: each can contribute $16,000 in 2025 ($8,000 carryforward + $8,000 current year). Combined couple contribution: $32,000. At a combined average marginal rate of 33%: $32,000 × 33% = $10,560 in combined deductions in a single year — plus the money grows tax-free toward their home purchase with no repayment obligation on withdrawal.

    4. A client earned $46,000 this year and received advance CWB payments of $759 (July and October 2025). What happens on their 2025 return?

    The advance CWB payments (50% of the estimated annual amount) are reconciled on the annual return. At $46,000 net income (single, no dependants), the CWB is in the phase-out range: $1,518 − ($46,000 − $23,495) × 15% = $1,518 − $3,376 = $0 (fully phased out). The client received $759 in advance payments but is entitled to $0. They must repay $759 on their 2025 return — this appears as a balance owing. This surprises clients who didn't realize the advance payments would be reconciled. Always ask: "Did you receive advance Canada Workers Benefit payments this year?" and check CRA My Account.

    5. Patricia and Victor have a combined AFNI of $72,000 and two children (ages 3 and 9). Victor's employer offers him significant overtime that would push their combined AFNI to $80,500. Describe what happens to their CCB at each income level and calculate the benefit cliff.

    At $72,000: CCB = $14,357 − ($72,000 − $36,502) × 19% = $14,357 − $6,745 = $7,612/year. At $80,500 (just above the $79,087 Tier 2 threshold): Tier 1 reduction: ($79,087 − $36,502) × 19% = $8,091. Remaining: $14,357 − $8,091 = $6,266. Tier 2 reduction: ($80,500 − $79,087) × 8% = $113. Final CCB: $6,266 − $113 = $6,153/year. CCB reduction from overtime: $7,612 − $6,153 = $1,459/year less CCB. Combined with income tax on the $8,500 overtime: $8,500 × 29.65% = $2,520 tax. Total cost of earning $8,500 more: $2,520 + $1,459 = $3,979. Victor keeps $8,500 − $3,979 = $4,521. Effective marginal rate: 46.8%.

    6. Rosa, 26, moved to Toronto from Vancouver in March 2025. She rents an apartment for $1,900/month. She earns $38,000 from a contract job. What Ontario benefits might she qualify for and what information does she need to provide?

    Rosa qualifies for the Ontario Trillium Benefit — specifically the OEPTC (property tax equivalent on rent) and OSTC (Ontario Sales Tax Credit). She files Form ON-BEN with her 2025 T1. Required information: Ontario address as of December 31, 2025; monthly rent amount ($1,900); landlord name and address; confirmation she paid rent on a principal residence in Ontario. OEPTC property component: 20% × ($1,900 × 9 months she was in Ontario) = 20% × $17,100 = $3,420 deemed property tax, subject to the OEPTC formula (approximately $900–$1,100 annual credit). OSTC: approximately $345 based on income. Total OTB: approximately $1,300–$1,500/year — something Rosa had no idea she was entitled to.

    7. Explain the FHSA carryforward rule and why advising a client to open an FHSA this year — even if they can't contribute anything — is valuable advice.

    The FHSA generates $8,000 in annual contribution room from the moment the account is opened. Unused room (up to $8,000) carries forward to the following year. By opening the account now without contributing, the client accumulates $8,000 in carryforward room. Next year, they can contribute up to $16,000 ($8,000 current year + $8,000 carryforward) instead of just $8,000 — allowing them to catch up and maximize the deduction in a higher-income year. The cost of opening the account: zero. The cost of not opening it: permanently losing the $8,000 in room that could have accrued. For any client aged 18–71 who might buy a first home in the next 15 years, the right advice is always: open the FHSA today, even if you can't contribute for months.
