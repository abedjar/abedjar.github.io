    Week 4: Phase 1 Review — Stress-Testing Your Foundation Before Building Higher¶

    What This Week Is

    Week 4 is not a new topic. It is a deliberate pressure test of everything Weeks 1–3 built. Every concept you've learned is challenged here — through realistic client scenarios, an unguided complete return worked from scratch, a series of embedded errors you must find, and a 15-question comprehensive quiz that spans the entire Phase 1 foundation.

    The standard is high. If you can complete this week accurately and within professional time benchmarks, your foundation is solid enough to build Phase 2 income types on top of it. If specific sections reveal gaps, you know exactly where to return before proceeding.

    This week's format: - 4.1 — The Phase 1 knowledge map: confirm every concept before testing - 4.2 — Three client intake conversations: practice the professional questions - 4.3 — The unguided complete return: Victor Mensah's full T1 from scratch - 4.4 — The error hunt: eight returns with deliberate mistakes — find them all - 4.5 — Ontario-specific consolidation: the province-level concepts that appear on every return - 4.6 — The Net Income master lever in seven scenarios - 4.7 — Phase 1 comprehensive quiz: 15 questions, full answers - 4.8 — Phase 1 readiness checklist

    Work every section before reading the answers

    The gap between what you find and what was there is your most honest learning signal. Resist the urge to check answers mid-analysis. Complete your work. Then compare. Note what you missed. Go back to the source week for anything that wasn't solid.

    4.1 The Phase 1 Knowledge Map — Confirm Everything Before Testing¶

    Work through this checklist honestly. For each item: can you explain it in plain language to a client? Can you calculate it without looking anything up? If yes — checkmark and move on. If no — return to that section before the simulation.

    Week 1 — The Canadian Tax System¶

    • [ ] Two-layer system: Federal CRA + Ontario provincial, both calculated on the same income, both filed on the single T1
    • [ ] CRA's pre-knowledge: Employers, banks, and government agencies file T-slips with CRA before you file. CRA's system has most of your client's income data already. When your return doesn't match — their matching system flags it automatically
    • [ ] Progressive brackets — the bucket model: Higher rates apply only to income within each bracket. A raise never reduces take-home pay. Income doesn't "get pushed back" to a lower bracket
    • [ ] Four combined Ontario marginal rates: 20.05% / 24.15% / 29.65% / 37.16% / 46.16% at their respective thresholds
    • [ ] Marginal rate vs. effective rate: Marginal = rate on the next dollar (used for planning decisions). Effective = total tax ÷ total income (used for perspective). Marginal is always higher than effective
    • [ ] Net Income (23600) as the master lever: Every dollar of reduction in Net Income simultaneously reduces income tax AND improves benefit eligibility (CCB, GST/HST credit, OAS clawback, Age Amount, spousal credit, OTB, LIFT). Deductions above Line 23600 work on both dimensions. Deductions below Line 23600 work only on tax
    • [ ] Why everyone should file: GST/HST credit ($519+), Ontario Trillium Benefit ($600–$2,400+), withheld tax refunds, RRSP room establishment. These are foregone every year a return isn't filed

    Week 2 — The T1 Return Structure¶

    • [ ] T1 four sections: Identification → Total Income (15000) → Net Income (23600) / Taxable Income (26000) → Tax Calculation and Credits (43500) → Refund (48400) or Balance (48500)
    • [ ] Province = December 31. Never the work province, never the prior province, never "where they mostly lived." December 31. Period.
    • [ ] Six critical line numbers: 15000 / 23600 / 26000 / 43500 / 43700 / 48400 or 48500 — purpose and difference between them
    • [ ] T4 slip mastery: Box 14 → Line 10100. Box 16 → credit Schedule 8. Box 18 → credit 31200. Box 22 → Line 43700. Box 40 → already inside Box 14, never add separately. Box 44 → Line 21200. Box 52 → records pension adjustment, does not reduce income
    • [ ] T5 dividend rule: Always report the grossed-up (taxable) amount — Box 11 for eligible, Box 25 for non-eligible. Never Box 10 or Box 24 (actual amounts)
    • [ ] T4A Box 48 → T2125 always: Never Line 10100. Box 48 is self-employment income. Must create a T2125 and calculate CPP on self-employment
    • [ ] T3 timing: Due March 31. Never file any client with mutual funds, ETFs, or trust investments before April 1
    • [ ] T5008 Box 20: Not reliable as ACB. Always verify against original purchase records
    • [ ] Schedule 1: Federal tax calculation + federal non-refundable credits at 15%
    • [ ] ON428: Ontario tax calculation + Ontario non-refundable credits at 5.05%. Ontario BPA = $11,865 (not $16,129). Ontario Age Amount phase-out starts at $42,335 (not $44,325)
    • [ ] Ontario surtax: 20% on Ontario basic tax above $5,315. Additional 36% on Ontario basic tax above $6,802
    • [ ] NOA: Confirms CRA's assessment. Four sections. 90-day window to object if CRA changed something incorrectly

    Week 3 — Filing Mechanics and Compliance¶

    • [ ] Must file vs. should file: Legal obligation triggered by owing tax, selling capital property (including principal residence since 2016), realizing capital gains, OAS/EI repayment, CPP on self-employment. Should file even without legal obligation when entitled to refundable benefits
    • [ ] Principal residence must be reported even when fully exempt — Schedule 3 + T2091. Failure = potential $100/day penalty (max $8,000) and possible PRE denial
    • [ ] Residency — significant ties test: Primary ties (home, spouse, dependants in Canada) vs. secondary ties. One primary tie usually establishes residency. Day count alone is insufficient
    • [ ] Deemed resident: 183+ days in Canada → treated as full-year resident
    • [ ] Part-year returns: Pro-rate credits at days/365 UNLESS Canadian income is 90%+ of worldwide income during residency — then full credits
    • [ ] Filing deadlines: April 30 (most). June 15 (self-employed filing only — balance owing still April 30). This distinction causes hundreds of dollars in avoidable interest every year
    • [ ] Late-filing penalty: 5% + 1%/month (first offense, max 17%). 10% + 2%/month (repeat, max 50%). Only applies when balance is owing — no penalty for refund returns regardless of how late they're filed
    • [ ] Interest: ~9% annual compounded daily from May 1 on unpaid balances. Accrues on tax owing AND on penalties. Cannot be avoided once incurred
    • [ ] EFILE: T183 mandatory before every transmission. ACK4 = success. ACK3 = already filed — investigate. Record ACK number as proof
    • [ ] T3 timing repeat: Do not file investment clients in February — wait for T3 slips after April 1
    • [ ] T1-ADJ: Corrects prior years up to 10 years back. Highest value uses: DTC retroactive recovery, unclaimed advisory fees, ACB corrections, medical expense window optimization
    • [ ] VDP: For unreported income or unfiled returns with balance owing — before CRA makes contact. Four conditions: voluntary, complete, involves penalty risk, and older than normal assessment period. Not needed for simple unfiled-but-refund T4-only returns
    • [ ] SINs starting with 9: Temporary — check expiry date on the card. Transmitting with expired SIN = ACK2 rejection

    If all 35 items above are solid, proceed. If any are uncertain, return now.

    4.2 Three Client Intake Conversations — Practice the Professional Questions¶

    The intake conversation is where every return begins. You cannot file accurately without asking the right questions. These three simulated conversations test whether you know which questions to ask and — critically — what each answer means technically.

    Work through each one. After the client's response, write your technical interpretation before reading the note.

    Intake Simulation 1 — Victor Mensah¶

    Victor, 38, walked in with a stuffed envelope. "I moved from Calgary to London for a new job. I have investments, a common-law girlfriend, and I want to make sure everything's done right."

    You: "What province were you living in on December 31, 2025?"

    Victor: "London, Ontario — we moved here August 1 and that's where we were on December 31."

    Technical interpretation

    Victor is an Ontario resident for the full 2025 tax year. Despite living in Alberta January through July, December 31 determines province. He files ON428. Ontario rates apply to his full year's income. His Alberta employer's withholding may not have accounted for Ontario's different rates — this may create a balance or refund difference. He qualifies for Ontario Trillium Benefit for August–December rent in Ontario only.

    You: "Tell me about your employment — who did you work for and when?"

    Victor: "Alberta Energy Corp January to July 31 — they paid me $62,000 for those 7 months. Ontario Tech Solutions August 1 to December 31 — they paid me $42,000 for 5 months."

    Technical interpretation

    Victor has two T4 slips. Combined employment income: $104,000. Critical flags: (1) CPP over-contribution: Each employer withheld CPP independently as if Victor would earn their salary rate for the full year. Alberta: $62,000 × 7/12 annualized = $106,286 — employer withheld close to $3,867 max. Ontario: $42,000 × 5/12 annualized = $100,800 — employer also withheld close to $3,867 max. Combined CPP withheld: likely approaching or exceeding $7,000. Maximum employee CPP (2025): $3,867. Over-contribution approximately $3,133 — returned to Victor as a credit on his T1. (2) EI over-contribution: Same pattern. Combined likely exceeds $1,049 maximum. Excess returned. (3) Both employers withheld tax on their portion only. On $104,000 combined, the actual marginal rate is ~31.66% (crossing the $102,894 Ontario bracket boundary). Neither employer knew about the other. The combined withholding may not be adequate — potential balance owing depends on total withholding from both T4 Box 22 amounts.

    You: "Do you have an RRSP? What's your deduction limit from last year's NOA?"

    Victor: "Yes — I contributed $7,500 to a personal RRSP in February. My limit was $18,200 from my Alberta NOA."

    Technical interpretation

    $7,500 is well within the $18,200 limit — no over-contribution. The RRSP deduction reduces Victor's net income from $104,000 to $96,500. This has two effects: (1) Tax saving at 31.66% combined rate: $7,500 × 31.66% = $2,375 (at the combined rate for income in the $102,895–$114,750 range — the RRSP moves him down from that bracket into the 29.65% range, creating a blended saving). (2) Net income $96,500 — just above $90,997 OAS clawback threshold, but Victor is 38, so OAS is not applicable. Important: check T4 Box 52 (pension adjustment) from either employer — if Victor has a group RRSP or RPP through work, the PA will further reduce his 2026 RRSP room.

    You: "You mentioned a common-law girlfriend — what does she earn?"

    Victor: "She's a nurse — earns about $84,000. We've been living together since June 2024, so we're definitely common-law."

    Technical interpretation

    Victor and his girlfriend are common-law partners (CLP) — 12 continuous months of cohabitation since June 2024 means they became CLPs in June 2025. Victor's return must reflect married/CLP marital status. Implications: (1) No spousal credit for Victor — her net income ($84,000) far exceeds the $16,129 threshold. (2) CCB calculation — if they have children, AFNI includes both incomes. They don't appear to have children based on what Victor said. (3) GST/HST credit — calculated as a couple. At combined income they will be above phase-out thresholds. (4) The girlfriend's T1 must also reflect CLP status with Victor's net income entered — even if her return is filed separately.

    You: "Tell me about the investments you mentioned."

    Victor: "I have a TD brokerage account. I sold some Microsoft shares in April — bought them in 2019 for about $12,000 USD and sold for $18,000 USD. I also have a mutual fund ETF that pays quarterly dividends. The T5 arrived but I haven't received a T3 yet."

    Technical interpretation

    Three distinct issues: (1) Capital gain on Microsoft shares: Both amounts in USD — must convert to CAD using Bank of Canada rates at purchase date (2019 average) and sale date (April 2025 rate). CAD amounts will differ from USD amounts. The ACB in CAD is the important figure. The T5008 may report Box 20 in USD or may be blank — do not use it without verification. (2) Mutual fund ETF dividends: T5 slip for quarterly dividends — report the taxable (grossed-up) amount on Line 12000. (3) T3 timing: Victor doesn't have the T3 yet. The ETF will issue a T3 reporting capital gains distributions, interest, and other income for the year. Do not file Victor's return until after April 1 — run AFR to confirm all T3 slips are in CRA's system. Explain this to Victor: "We need to wait for your mutual fund's T3 slip before we can file accurately."

    Intake Simulation 2 — Maria Santos (New Client, Quick Read)¶

    Maria, 32, arrived from the Philippines on September 15, 2025. She started work as a PSW on October 1 at $46,000/year salary. She rents a room for $800/month in Brampton. She sends $600/month to her parents in Manila. She has a Philippine savings account with approximately $8,000 CAD equivalent.

    Write your technical notes before reading: - Province of residence on December 31? - Part-year or full-year resident? - What income goes on the T1? - Do personal credits get pro-rated? - Does T1135 apply? - Are the monthly remittances to her parents deductible? - What benefits does she qualify for?

    Technical Analysis — Maria Santos

    Province: Ontario — December 31 ✅

    Residency: Part-year resident from September 15. Days resident: Sept 15–Dec 31 = 108 days.

    Income on T1: ONLY the Canadian employment income from October 1 onward — approximately $46,000 × 3/12 = $11,500 (October–December). Philippine income from before September 15 is pre-residency income — NOT reported on the Canadian T1.

    Credit pro-ration: 108/365 = 29.6%. Pro-rated BPA: $16,129 × 29.6% = $4,774. Credit: $4,774 × 15% = $716 (vs. the full $2,419). UNLESS the 90% test applies. Maria's Canadian income during her residency period: $11,500. Any Philippine income during her residency period (Sept 15–Dec 31): $0 (she was working in Canada). 90%+ test: $11,500 / $11,500 = 100% Canadian source during residency → full credits apply, no pro-ration.

    T1135: Philippine savings account ≈ $8,000 CAD. Well below the $100,000 threshold. T1135 not required.

    Monthly remittances to parents: $600/month to Manila. These are personal expenditures — not deductible. Sending money to family abroad is not a business expense, not a support payment (support payments deductible only for legal arrangements with former spouses), not any recognized deduction. Maria may believe these should be deductible ("I'm supporting my family"). They are not.

    Benefits Maria qualifies for: - GST/HST credit: From Q1 2026 (first quarter after her arrival quarter). At $11,500 net income — full annual credit $519. Pro-rated for partial year: approximately $260 in 2025. - Ontario Trillium Benefit: Rent October–December: $800 × 3 = $2,400. OEPTC: 20% × $2,400 = $480 deemed property tax → approximately $130 for the partial year. OSTC: approximately $260 (partial year below threshold). OTB starts July 2026 based on 2025 return. - CWB (Canada Workers Benefit): Working income $11,500. Net income $11,500. At this income and working status — qualifies for CWB. At $11,500 employment income: approximately $1,518 maximum × income phase-in rate = modest CWB amount.

    Intake Simulation 3 — The Estate Call¶

    Karen calls you. Her father Edward passed away on October 8, 2025, at age 74. He had a T4 from January through October 8 (retired professor's DB pension, prorated), CPP, OAS, a $145,000 RRSP with Karen named as beneficiary, and a jointly-owned home with her mother (FMV $540,000, purchased 1991 for $160,000).

    Write your technical notes before reading: - What returns need to be filed? - What is the RRSP treatment? - What happens to the jointly-owned home? - What is the principal residence situation? - What is the filing deadline? - What should Karen NOT do yet?

    Technical Analysis — Edward's Estate

    Returns required: (1) Terminal T1 for January 1 – October 8, 2025 (the year of death). (2) Prior years if any outstanding — verify through CRA My Account. (3) Rights or Things return (optional) — any income earned but not received at death (October pension payment received after October 8, any outstanding CPP payment). (4) T3 Trust return if the estate earns income while being administered — different form, different filing system.

    RRSP treatment: Edward's RRSP FMV at death: $145,000. Karen is the named beneficiary — but Karen is a daughter (adult), not a spouse. No spousal rollover available. The full $145,000 is added to Edward's terminal return income as a deemed disposition. At the combined income level on the terminal return, this creates a substantial tax bill — potentially $145,000 × 46.16% (at top rate) = approximately $67,000 in tax from the RRSP alone, payable from the estate before Karen receives her inheritance.

    Jointly-owned home: Home is owned jointly by Edward and his wife (mother). On Edward's death, his 50% interest passes to his wife by right of survivorship — automatically, without going through the estate. The home is NOT part of the estate to be distributed.

    Principal residence: Edward's 50% share of the home is deemed disposed at FMV: $540,000 / 2 = $270,000. ACB of his 50%: $160,000 / 2 = $80,000. Capital gain: $190,000. Fully exempt under the Principal Residence Exemption — he and his wife designated it as their principal residence throughout ownership. However, the sale (deemed disposition) must STILL be reported on Schedule 3 with T2091. Even $0 gain — must report.

    Filing deadline: Death on October 8 → within the October 1–December 31 window for deceased taxpayers. Terminal T1 deadline: April 30, 2026 (standard April 30 applies when death is October 1 or later). Rights or Things return deadline: Later of April 30, 2026 or 1 year after date of death (October 8, 2026).

    What Karen must NOT do: Do not distribute any estate assets to beneficiaries until: (1) Terminal T1 filed and assessed, (2) All prior years confirmed current, (3) CRA Clearance Certificate (Form TX19) received. If Karen distributes the estate and CRA later assesses additional tax, Karen as executor is personally liable for that additional tax from her own assets.

    4.4 The Error Hunt — Eight Statements With Embedded Mistakes¶

    These are statements from client files, colleague conversations, or prior returns. Each contains at least one error. Find it. Explain why it's wrong. State the correct treatment.

    Work through all eight before checking answers.

    Statement 1: "My client rents in Hamilton, Ontario. His net income is $48,000. His refund is $1,200. I claimed the Ontario Trillium Benefit on ON-BEN — he pays $1,300/month and has been there since August. I entered $15,600 as the total annual rent."

    Error 1 — Answer

    The error: The client moved in August. He was an Ontario resident only from August onward (assuming December 31 residency). The ON-BEN should reflect only the rent paid for Ontario months — not a full calendar year. Entering $15,600 (12 × $1,300) overstates the eligible rent by 7 months ($1,300 × 7 = $9,100). The correct entry: August–December = 5 months × $1,300 = $6,500. The OEPTC is calculated on 20% of eligible rent — entering the inflated $15,600 vs. the correct $6,500 overstates the deemed property tax equivalent by $1,820. This leads to a slightly overstated OEPTC claim and a potential OTB adjustment when CRA's residency data doesn't match.

    Statement 2: "Sarah has a T4 with Box 14 = $78,000 and Box 40 = $5,200 (representing her company car benefit). I entered $83,200 on Line 10100 to capture all employment income including the benefit."

    Error 2 — Answer

    The error: Box 40 is already included inside Box 14. The employer calculated the taxable value of the car benefit ($5,200), added it to Sarah's base salary, and reported the combined total in Box 14. Entering $78,000 + $5,200 = $83,200 on Line 10100 double-counts the car benefit by $5,200. The correct entry is $78,000 (Box 14 only). The $5,200 is not additional income — it is a component of income already captured. Filing with $83,200 overstates income by $5,200 and generates approximately $5,200 × 29.65% = $1,542 in excess combined tax. CRA's automated matching will also eventually flag the discrepancy between the slip ($78,000) and the return ($83,200).

    Statement 3: "My client is self-employed and earned $52,000 net. I have his return ready. He came in June 10 — well within the June 15 deadline for self-employed filers. He owes $8,400. I'm transmitting today and he'll send the payment through his bank today as well."

    Error 3 — Answer

    The error: The self-employed filing extension is June 15. But the payment deadline is April 30 — for everyone. If today is June 10 and the client just now pays $8,400, interest has been accruing since May 1 (41 days). At 9% annual: approximately $8,400 × 0.02466% × 41 days = $85 in avoidable interest. The return can be filed June 10 within the deadline. But the payment should have been made on or before April 30. The client should have been advised in April (or at the November year-end planning call) to estimate their balance and make the payment by April 30 even if the return wasn't ready. The June 15 date covers filing, not payment.

    Statement 4: "James's T4A Box 48 shows $14,200 in consulting fees. I entered this on Line 10100 as additional employment income. He also had a T4 from his regular job. Both are now on the return."

    Error 4 — Answer

    The error: T4A Box 48 is "fees for services" — it is self-employment income, not employment income. It must go on a T2125 (Statement of Business or Professional Activities), with net income flowing to Line 13500. Entering it on Line 10100 causes three problems: (1) No T2125 is created — CRA expects one when T4A Box 48 appears. CRA's matching system flags T4A Box 48 with no corresponding T2125 as an anomaly. (2) Self-employment CPP is not calculated. James must pay CPP at 11.9% on his net consulting income minus $3,500 basic exemption — approximately ($14,200 − $3,500) × 11.9% = $1,273 in CPP. Neither the deductible half ($637) nor the credit is applied. (3) Legitimate business expenses are not deducted. Any home office proportion, phone allocation, or other business costs James paid to earn the consulting income are deductible on T2125 — but not if the income is misclassified as employment.

    Statement 5: "Patricia is 68 and retired. She lives in a rented condo in Mississauga — $1,800/month. Her only income is OAS and CPP. Total income is $19,600. I didn't bother with ON-BEN because she doesn't owe any tax."

    Error 5 — Answer

    The error: ON-BEN and the Ontario Trillium Benefit are completely independent of whether tax is owed. Patricia is a renter in Ontario with low income — she qualifies for significant OTB. At $19,600 net income: OEPTC: 20% × $21,600 annual rent = $4,320 deemed property tax → approximately $1,247/year (senior rate at 68). OSTC: approximately $345 (below phase-out at $24,916). Senior Homeowners' Property Tax Grant: does not apply (she rents). Total annual OTB: approximately $1,592/year paid as approximately $133/month. By not completing ON-BEN, Patricia loses $1,592/year indefinitely. If this error repeated for 3 years before correction: $4,776 in forfeited benefits. ON-BEN is required for every Ontario renter and homeowner regardless of their tax owing. It is the gateway to the OTB — not a tax reduction tool.

    Statement 6: "My client sold his house last year. He bought it in 2009 for $280,000 and sold it for $890,000. He lived in it the whole time. He got the Principal Residence Exemption and owes zero tax on the gain. I didn't include the sale on his return — there's nothing to report if there's no tax."

    Error 6 — Answer

    The error: Since 2016, every disposition of a principal residence must be reported on the T1 — on Schedule 3 with Form T2091 designating the property as principal residence — even when the gain is fully exempt and zero tax is owed. The reporting obligation and the tax obligation are separate. Not reporting the sale: (1) CRA receives land registry data about the transaction. They identify the unreported disposition and send an inquiry. (2) A late-filed T2091 designation (after the original assessment date) can trigger a penalty of $100/day late, minimum $100, maximum $8,000. (3) In extreme cases, CRA may deny the PRE entirely if the designation is not filed and documentation cannot be provided retroactively. The client owes $0 in tax. They still must file Schedule 3 and T2091. These are two completely separate obligations.

    Statement 7: "Marcus has a T5 from his brokerage: Box 10 = $3,200 (actual eligible dividends) and Box 11 = $4,416 (taxable eligible dividends). I entered $3,200 on Line 12000 — the amount he actually received. I don't see why he should report more income than he got."

    Error 7 — Answer

    The error: The taxable (grossed-up) amount must always be used for dividends — Box 11 ($4,416), not Box 10 ($3,200). The gross-up from $3,200 to $4,416 (×1.38) represents the pre-tax corporate income from which the dividend was paid. The Canadian tax system applies an integration mechanism: eligible dividends are grossed up to represent pre-tax corporate income, then the Dividend Tax Credit (Box 12 on the T5) partially compensates for the corporate tax already paid. Entering the actual amount ($3,200) instead of the taxable amount ($4,416): (1) Understates income by $1,216. (2) Generates an incorrect DTC calculation (the DTC is calculated as a percentage of the taxable amount — not the actual amount). (3) CRA's automated matching will compare the T5 Box 11 (which they received as $4,416) against Line 12000 ($3,200) and flag the discrepancy. Always use Box 11 for eligible dividends and Box 25 for non-eligible dividends — never Box 10 or Box 24.

    Statement 8: "My client is self-employed and her net business income is $48,000. She filed her return April 15. Her combined tax owing was $6,200 but she had only $0 withheld at source. She paid the $6,200 in full on April 15. I'm confused why CRA is now sending her an instalment letter for next year."

    Error 8 — Answer

    No error in the past — but the CRA letter makes complete sense. When a taxpayer owes more than $3,000 in combined federal and provincial tax in the current year AND in at least one of the two prior years, CRA requires quarterly tax instalments for the following year. Your client owed $6,200 — well above the $3,000 threshold. If she also owed over $3,000 in prior years, CRA will send an instalment schedule for the following year. Instalments are due: March 15, June 15, September 15, December 15. This is not a penalty notice — it is a demand for advance payments. Failure to make instalments results in instalment interest (same prescribed rate as late-balance interest). Your planning action: Calculate her expected 2026 tax now. Set up quarterly payments. Advise her: "CRA now expects you to pre-pay your tax in four installments throughout the year. This is normal for self-employed people — it prevents a large April balance. We'll calculate the quarterly amount together."

    4.5 Ontario-Specific Consolidation — The Province-Level Concepts on Every Return¶

    These Ontario concepts appear on virtually every return you prepare for an Ontario resident. Confirm each one before proceeding to Phase 2.

    The Five Ontario Concepts That Create Errors Most Often¶

    1. Ontario BPA vs. Federal BPA

    Federal BPA: $16,129 → credit of $2,419 (×15%) on Schedule 1. Ontario BPA: $11,865 → credit of $599 (×5.05%) on ON428.

    These are different forms, different amounts, different credit rates. The Ontario BPA generates approximately one quarter of the federal credit value. Never use the federal amount on ON428 or vice versa.

    2. Ontario Age Amount vs. Federal Age Amount

    Federal Age Amount: $8,790, phase-out above $44,325. Ontario Age Amount: $5,812, phase-out above $42,335.

    Ontario's phase-out begins $1,990 lower than federal. A senior at $43,000 net income gets the full federal Age Amount but a reduced Ontario Age Amount. The calculations are independent.

    3. Ontario Surtax — Why Two Tiers Matter

    Ontario Basic Tax Surtax Applied
    Under $5,315 No surtax
    $5,315 – $6,802 20% on excess above $5,315
    Above $6,802 20% on excess above $5,315 PLUS additional 36% on excess above $6,802

    For clients above approximately $95,000 net income, both tiers apply and add meaningfully to the effective Ontario rate. The surtax is the reason Ontario's actual effective rate is higher than the simple bracket table suggests at higher incomes.

    4. Ontario LIFT Credit

    Available to Ontario residents who have employment income and whose combined net income is below approximately $38,500 (individual). At lower levels, it can completely eliminate Ontario income tax — replacing it with zero. For low-income workers (e.g., students working part-time, new workers, seasonal workers), the LIFT Credit is one of the reasons they owe zero Ontario tax even after withholding. Does NOT apply to self-employment income alone.

    5. ON-BEN is not automatic

    The Ontario Trillium Benefit is only triggered by completing Form ON-BEN (included in the T1 package). If ON-BEN is not completed — no OTB payments, regardless of eligibility. Every Ontario renter, homeowner, senior, and northern resident should have ON-BEN completed. Never skip it.

    4.6 The Net Income Master Lever — Seven Scenarios¶

    Net Income (Line 23600) simultaneously drives income tax AND benefit eligibility. This section puts that concept through seven distinct client situations. For each scenario: what happens to tax? What happens to benefits? What is the combined effect?

    The Setup: Each client considers a $6,000 RRSP contribution that would reduce their Net Income by $6,000.

    Scenario 1 — Kevin, 32, single, $42,000 net income, Ontario, no children

    Tax saving: $6,000 × 24.15% combined (in the $37,376–$51,446 range): = $1,449 Benefits affected: GST/HST credit slightly improved (both levels below $44,324 — additional benefit minimal). OTB slightly improved. Combined annual value: approximately $1,500

    Scenario 2 — Helen, 34, two children ages 2 and 5, $52,000 net income, Ontario

    Tax saving: $6,000 × 24.15% (crossing from second to first Ontario bracket): ≈ $1,449 CCB improvement: AFNI drops $6,000 within the phase-out range. Rate for 2 children: 19%. $6,000 × 19% = $1,140/year more CCB. Combined annual value: $1,449 + $1,140 = $2,589 — 78% more than Kevin's result from the same RRSP on the same income.

    Scenario 3 — Margaret, 66, $47,000 net income, Ontario, no children

    Tax saving: $6,000 × 24.15%: ≈ $1,449 Age Amount effect: Federal phase-out at $44,325. Margaret's income drops from $47,000 to $41,000 — below the federal $44,325 threshold. Full federal Age Amount restored. $8,790 × 15% = $1,319 (vs. her reduced amount before: ($8,790 − [($47,000−$44,325) × 15%]) × 15% = ($8,790 − $401) × 15% = $1,259). Additional credit: $60. Ontario Age Amount: $41,000 below Ontario $42,335 threshold — full Ontario Age Amount also restored. Additional ~$40 Ontario credit. Combined annual value: approximately $1,549 — the restored Age Amount adds $100 to the basic RRSP saving.

    Scenario 4 — George, 73, $93,500 net income, Ontario — near OAS clawback

    Tax saving: $6,000 × 31.66% (in $102,895+ bracket? No — let's check): At $93,500, he's between $57,376 and $102,894 — combined 29.65%. $6,000 × 29.65% = $1,779. But wait — George is above the $90,997 OAS clawback threshold! OAS clawback: ($93,500 − $90,997) × 15% = $2,503 × 15% = $375 being clawed back.

    After $6,000 RRSP: net income = $87,500 — below the $90,997 OAS clawback threshold. OAS clawback reduced to $0. OAS preserved: $375/year saved. Combined annual value: $1,779 tax + $375 OAS = $2,154 — from the same $6,000 RRSP.

    Scenario 5 — Sandra, 28, earning $24,000, Ontario, single, renting at $1,000/month

    At $24,000 net income before RRSP: Federal tax: essentially $0 (below BPA + credits). Ontario tax: LIFT Credit eliminates it. Tax saving from RRSP: $0 — already at zero tax.

    However: GST/HST credit slightly improved (already at full amount below $44,324). OTB slightly improved.

    Should Sandra make this RRSP contribution? Not for immediate tax benefit — she has no tax to save against. She should maximize her TFSA first (tax-free growth, no deduction needed). However, building RRSP room at 24 is not wasted — contributions deferred until higher income are worth significantly more at 30+ when she's earning $70,000+. The contribution now, deduction deferred to a higher-income year, would then save $6,000 × 29.65% = $1,779. RRSP room is permanent — it can wait.

    Scenario 6 — Carlos, 44, $155,000 net income, Ontario, incorporated client taking $155,000 salary

    Tax saving: $6,000 × 38.16% combined (in the $150,001–$158,519 range): = $2,290 No major benefit programs in play at this income (all phased out above $115,000). Pure tax saving. Combined annual value: $2,290 — the highest direct tax saving of the seven scenarios. Ontario surtax also reduced slightly.

    Scenario 7 — Patricia, 64, $38,500 net income, Ontario, widowed, homeowner

    Tax saving: $6,000 × 24.15%: ≈ $1,449 Ontario LIFT Credit: Patricia has employment income and is below $30,000 net income threshold? No — $38,500 is above. No LIFT Credit effect. Age Amount: She turns 65 in December — not yet 65 on December 31. No Age Amount this year. Senior Homeowners' Property Tax Grant: At $38,500 income — she may be close to phase-out range. A small income reduction might slightly increase her grant. Modest additional benefit. Combined annual value: approximately $1,490 — primarily the direct tax saving.

    What the Seven Scenarios Reveal:

    Client Marginal Rate RRSP Tax Save Benefit Effect Total Annual Value
    Kevin (single, $42K) 24.15% $1,449 ~$50 ~$1,500
    Helen (2 kids, $52K) 24.15% $1,449 +$1,140 CCB $2,589
    Margaret (senior, $47K near Age Amount) 24.15% $1,449 +$100 Age Amount ~$1,549
    George (senior, OAS clawback zone) 29.65% $1,779 +$375 OAS $2,154
    Sandra (low income, $24K) 0% $0 minimal ~$0 now
    Carlos (high income, $155K) 38.16% $2,290 $0 $2,290
    Patricia ($38K, widowed) 24.15% $1,449 ~$40 ~$1,490

    The lesson: The same $6,000 RRSP generates between $0 and $2,589 in annual combined value depending entirely on the client's situation. Knowing where your client sits in the income landscape — and what benefits their Net Income controls — is what converts data entry into genuine financial advice.

    4.7 Phase 1 Comprehensive Quiz — 15 Questions¶

    These 15 questions cover every concept from Weeks 1–3. Work through all 15 before checking answers. Target: 13+ correct before moving to Phase 2. Below 11 — return to the relevant weeks.

    Q1. What is the combined Ontario marginal rate for a client with $78,000 in net income? What does this rate mean for a $9,000 RRSP contribution?

    Combined rate: Federal 20.5% + Ontario 9.15% = 29.65% (income $57,376–$102,894 federal bracket, $51,447–$102,894 Ontario bracket). For a $9,000 RRSP: tax saving = $9,000 × 29.65% = $2,669 in combined federal and Ontario income tax — the immediate return on the RRSP contribution.

    Q2. A client says their T4 shows Box 14 = $96,000 and Box 40 = $7,400 representing a taxable benefit. What goes on Line 10100 and why?

    $96,000 on Line 10100. Box 40 ($7,400) is already inside Box 14. The employer calculated the taxable benefit, added it to base salary, and reported the combined total in Box 14. Box 40 is an informational breakdown — not additional income. Adding both would double-count $7,400 and generate approximately $7,400 × 29.65% = $2,194 in excess tax.

    Q3. List three specific situations where a person with zero tax owing SHOULD still file a return, with the specific dollar value of what they're missing each year.

    Any three of: (1) Ontario renter at $25,000 income — OTB approximately $870/year (OEPTC + OSTC). (2) Any person 19+ — GST/HST credit $519/year. (3) Low-income worker — Canada Workers Benefit up to $1,518/year (single). (4) Person with withheld employment taxes — full refund of over-withholding. (5) New immigrant with children — CCB up to $14,357/year (2 children). Every one of these is forfeited permanently for years where no return is filed.

    Q4. Maria moved from Alberta to Ontario on December 20. She was employed in Alberta all year at $72,000. What provincial tax form is used, and does she qualify for Ontario Trillium Benefit for the year?

    ON428 — Ontario provincial tax. Province of residence = December 31. Maria was an Ontario resident on December 31. Ontario rates apply to her full year's $72,000 income. OTB: She may qualify, but ON-BEN should reflect only Ontario rent (December 20–31 = approximately 11 days of rent). Alberta rent for January–December 19 does not count. Her OTB for this year would be minimal due to only days of Ontario rent, but she should still file ON-BEN to establish the record and begin future eligibility.

    Q5. A T4A slip shows Box 16 = $36,000 (pension income) and Box 48 = $8,500 (consulting fees). Where does each box go on the T1 and what additional obligation does Box 48 create?

    Box 16 → Line 11500 (Other Pensions and Superannuation). Eligible for Pension Income Credit (up to $2,000 × 15% = $300 federal credit). Box 48 → T2125 (self-employment income, Line 13500). Additional obligations from Box 48: (1) T2125 must be prepared with gross income and any business expenses. (2) Self-employment CPP calculated on net consulting income: ($8,500 − $3,500) × 11.9% = $595 total CPP, deductible half $298 at Line 22200, credit half $298 × 15% = $45 at Line 31000. (3) If total self-employment revenue exceeds $30,000 in a rolling 12 months, HST registration required.

    Q6. What is the late-filing penalty for a first-time offender who files 5 months late with a $9,200 balance owing? What would it be for a repeat offender with the same facts?

    First-time (5 complete months late): 5% × $9,200 + (1% × 5 × $9,200) = $460 + $460 = $920. Repeat offender (late in at least one prior 3 years): 10% × $9,200 + (2% × 5 × $9,200) = $920 + $920 = $1,840. The repeat-offender penalty is exactly double the first-time penalty with these facts. Interest also accrues on the $9,200 + on the penalty itself from the payment due date.

    Q7. Why is the distinction between deductions above Line 23600 (Net Income) and below Line 23600 (Taxable Income) so important for a family with two young children?

    Deductions above Line 23600 reduce Net Income — which drives both income tax AND the Canada Child Benefit (calculated on Adjusted Family Net Income). For a family with two children at $55,000 AFNI, every $1,000 of RRSP contribution both saves $1,000 × 29.65% = $297 in tax AND increases annual CCB by $1,000 × 19% (2-child phase-out rate) = $190/year. Combined effect: $487 in value per $1,000 contributed. A capital loss carry-forward (below Line 23600) reduces only Taxable Income — saves $297 in tax but does nothing for CCB. Same $1,000 deduction, $190/year difference in outcome. The location on the return determines the magnitude of the benefit.

    Q8. Patricia has two T4s from two jobs she held in 2025. Combined CPP withheld: $5,100. Combined EI withheld: $1,520. What happens on her return?

    CPP over-contribution: $5,100 − $3,867 max = $1,233 returned as a credit on Line 44800. EI over-contribution: $1,520 − $1,049 max = $471 returned as a credit on Line 45000. Combined additional credit from over-contributions: $1,704. This is not a refund of tax — it is a return of over-contributed CPP and EI premiums. Both employers withheld independently without knowledge of the other employment. The T1 corrects this automatically.

    Q9. What is VDP, when is it needed, and what is the key timing risk that closes the door on it?

    VDP (Voluntary Disclosures Program) allows taxpayers to proactively disclose unfiled returns or unreported income before CRA contacts them — receiving reduced penalties and prosecution protection. Needed for: unreported business income, foreign income, multi-year non-filing where balances are owing. Key timing risk: the disclosure must be voluntary — once CRA has made contact about the specific period (written inquiry, audit notice, phone call requesting specific documentation), the voluntary condition is no longer met and VDP closes for that period. A client who received a CRA letter about 2022 cannot use VDP for 2022 — only for years not yet mentioned by CRA. This is why acting immediately when a client discloses a non-filing situation is critical.

    Q10. A client says 'I'm a part-year resident who arrived in June. I only earned Canadian income while I was here — I had nothing before I arrived.' Do their personal credits get pro-rated?

    No — full credits apply. The 90% test: if a part-year resident's Canadian-source income is 90% or more of their total worldwide income during the entire period of Canadian residency — they receive the full annual personal credits with no pro-ration. If this client had zero income before arriving and 100% Canadian income during their residency period, they clearly exceed 90%. The pro-ration rule (days/365) exists to prevent full credits when significant foreign income was also earned during the residency period. When a client arrives with no prior income, the 90% test is almost always satisfied — full credits apply.

    Q11. What is the Ontario surtax on a client whose Ontario basic tax (after Ontario non-refundable credits) is $9,800?

    Tier 1: ($9,800 − $5,315) × 20% = $4,485 × 20% = $897. Tier 2: ($9,800 − $6,802) × 36% = $2,998 × 36% = $1,079. Total Ontario surtax: $897 + $1,079 = $1,976. Total Ontario tax: $9,800 + $1,976 = $11,776. The surtax adds approximately 20% on top of the Ontario basic tax at this level — significantly higher than the nominal bracket rates suggest.

    Q12. A client sold their mutual fund ETF in April and also received quarterly dividend distributions from it all year. They hand you a T5 and ask when they'll get a T3. What do you tell them, and what does this mean for filing timing?

    T3 slips from mutual funds and ETFs are issued by March 31 — a full month after T4 and T5 slips (due February 28). The T3 will report capital gains distributions, eligible dividends, interest, and other income from the fund for the year. Even if the client sold the fund in April and received a T5 for dividends, the T3 reports additional income allocations that existed inside the fund during their ownership. Filing timing: Do not file this client's return before April 1. Run Auto-fill my return (AFR) on or after April 1 to confirm the T3 is in CRA's system. Filing in February without the T3 produces an incomplete return that CRA's matching system will identify when the fund issuer files the T3 in March. The result: a reassessment adding the T3 income, additional tax, and interest from April 30.

    Q13. What are the three things a non-filer client with T4 employment income from prior years most wants to hear from you, and what question determines whether they owe any late-filing penalties?

    Three things they want to hear: (1) "You are not facing criminal prosecution — that is reserved for deliberate fraud, not people who fell behind." (2) "CRA's goal is to get the returns filed and collect what's owed — not to punish ordinary people who got behind." (3) "Because you had T4 employment income and your employer was withholding tax all year, there's a reasonable chance some of these years have refunds — which means no late-filing penalty at all." The question that determines penalties: "Did you have a balance owing in any of those years, or do you think you had refunds?" Late-filing penalties ONLY apply to years with a balance owing. If every unfiled year had a refund — no penalties apply regardless of how many years late.

    Q14. On the final calculation step of a T1 return: Total Payable is $14,600. Total income tax deducted (from all T-slips) is $18,200. The client also had CPP over-contributions of $1,400 and EI over-contributions of $520. What is the total refund?

    Refund = Tax withheld − Tax owed + Over-contribution credits. $18,200 − $14,600 = $3,600 (from tax perspective) + $1,400 (CPP over-contribution) + $520 (EI over-contribution) = $5,520 total refund. The CPP and EI over-contributions are returned at Lines 44800 and 45000 — they are separate from the tax refund but both come back in the same assessment result.

    Q15. Margaret and her husband George are CLP and file separate returns. George has a net income of $9,400 (below the BPA). Margaret earns $88,000. How does George's income affect Margaret's T1, even though they file separately?

    George's net income must be entered on Margaret's return to calculate the Spouse/Common-Law Partner Amount (Line 30300). The spousal credit formula: ($16,129 − George's net income) × 15% = ($16,129 − $9,400) × 15% = $6,729 × 15% = $1,009 in federal spousal credit for Margaret. Additionally: (1) Their combined family net income ($88,000 + $9,400 = $97,400) affects any CCB they might receive for children. (2) Margaret and George's individual and combined incomes affect GST/HST credit calculations (as a couple, the phase-out threshold and credit amount are both different). (3) If medical expenses are claimed on Margaret's return for the couple, George's lower income produces a smaller 3% threshold — reducing what is subtracted from the medical expense claim. Even though the returns are filed separately, they are financially linked through multiple calculations.
