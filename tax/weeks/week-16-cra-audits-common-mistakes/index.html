    Week 16: CRA Audits, Reviews & Common Mistakes — Protecting Your Clients and Your Reputation¶

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this week, you will be able to:

    • Understand the difference between a CRA review, a desk audit, and a full field audit
    • Identify the 20 most common errors that trigger CRA attention
    • Build returns that are accurate, defensible, and audit-ready from day one
    • Respond professionally to CRA review letters on behalf of a client
    • Advise clients on record-keeping before a review happens
    • Understand your liability exposure as a paid preparer — and how to limit it
    • Recognize when to refer a client to a tax lawyer or CPA rather than handle it yourself
    • Know the Ontario-specific triggers that draw CRA scrutiny

    The single most important mindset shift this week

    Most new preparers think about CRA audits as something that happens to clients. Experienced preparers think about CRA audits as something they prepare for on every single return. The difference is this: a well-prepared return filed with proper documentation is not just correct — it is defensible. When CRA asks questions, you respond with evidence, not apologies. Building every return as if it will be reviewed transforms your quality and your confidence. That mindset is what this week is about.

    16.1 The CRA Review Spectrum — Not All "Audits" Are the Same¶

    The word "audit" creates panic in clients. Most of the time, what they're experiencing is far less serious — but requires professional handling regardless. Understanding the spectrum helps you calibrate your response.

    LEAST INVASIVE                                              MOST INVASIVE
     │                                                           │
     ▼                                                           ▼
Processing    →    Review Letter    →    Desk Audit    →    Field Audit
 (automatic)      (specific claim)      (mail/phone)      (in-person, CRA)

    Level 1 — Automated Processing Review¶

    CRA's computer system cross-references your filed return against: - T-slips filed by employers, banks, and brokerages - Prior year returns (unusual changes flagged) - Industry averages for self-employed returns - Information from third parties (real estate registries, foreign financial institutions)

    What happens: CRA issues a Notice of Assessment (NOA) with adjustments, or asks for clarification. Your response: Verify the NOA matches the filed return. If CRA changed something, investigate immediately.

    Level 2 — Review Letter (Most Common)¶

    CRA selects specific claims for documentation. This is not a full audit — it is CRA asking you to prove a specific deduction or credit.

    Common triggers for review letters: - Medical expenses that seem large relative to income - Home office expenses - Moving expenses - Childcare expenses (especially with cash payments to relatives) - Business expenses for self-employed returns - Donation amounts

    Your response: Gather the documentation, write a clear cover letter, and submit within the deadline. Most review letters resolve without further action.

    A review letter is an opportunity, not a crisis

    Clients who receive CRA review letters often panic and call you in distress. Your job is to reframe it: "This is routine. CRA reviews millions of returns every year. You claimed a medical expense credit — they want to see the receipts. I'll prepare the response. We have solid documentation. This will be fine."

    Clients who see you handle a review letter calmly and professionally become your most loyal referrals. They tell everyone: "My preparer dealt with CRA for me and it was no problem."

    Level 3 — Desk Audit (Correspondence Audit)¶

    A more detailed examination conducted by mail or phone. CRA may request: - Multiple years of documentation - Detailed explanations of business income and expenses - Bank statements to verify income - Contracts and invoices to support business claims

    What triggers it: Returns with repeated losses, significant rental losses, large business expenses relative to gross income, or discrepancies between HST returns and income tax returns.

    Your response: Compile organized, tabbed documentation. Respond factually and specifically. Never volunteer information beyond what is asked. If the scope expands, consider involving a CPA or tax lawyer.

    Level 4 — Field Audit (Rare — But Real)¶

    A CRA auditor visits the business or requests an in-person meeting. This is reserved for larger businesses, suspected fraud, or complex situations requiring physical verification.

    As a new preparer: You will rarely encounter a field audit in your first years. If a client receives a field audit notice, refer them to a CPA or tax lawyer who specializes in CRA dispute resolution. The stakes are too high for on-the-job learning.

    16.2 How CRA Selects Returns for Review — The Selection Triggers¶

    CRA uses a combination of automated screening, random selection, and targeted programs. Understanding what draws attention allows you to prepare returns that are accurate and low-profile when everything is legitimate.

    Automated Red Flags — The Algorithm¶

    CRA's computer system flags returns that deviate significantly from statistical norms. Think of it as a scoring system: each unusual element adds points. When the score exceeds a threshold, the return is pulled for human review.

    Red Flag Why CRA Notices
    Large business losses (3+ consecutive years) Pattern suggests hobby, not business
    Home office claim > 30% of employment income Statistically unusual
    Vehicle claim at 100% business use Almost always some personal use
    Meals and entertainment > 2% of gross revenue Industry benchmark
    Cash-based business with declining revenue Suggests unreported income
    Rental income with no income (all losses) Pattern suggests inflated expenses
    Self-employment with no HST registration above $30,000 Cross-reference with CRA HST database
    Large donation amounts relative to income $10,000 donation on $40,000 income
    Medical expenses claimed in multiple years near threshold Gaming the 3% threshold
    Prior year reassessments CRA flags returns where errors were found before

    Third-Party Information Matching¶

    CRA receives data from sources the taxpayer may not realize are reporting:

    Source What CRA Receives
    Employers T4 slips — employment income, CPP, EI, tax withheld
    Banks and brokerages T5, T3, T5008 — all investment income and transactions
    Real estate registries Property purchases and sales — matched to capital gains reporting
    Foreign financial institutions Foreign account balances and income (via FATCA and CRS agreements)
    HST/GST returns Business revenue cross-matched to T1 income
    Social media and online marketplaces Unreported income from Airbnb, Etsy, Facebook Marketplace
    Other government programs EI claims, CERB payments, social assistance

    CRA knows more than your client thinks

    When a client says "I sold a few things on Kijiji — does that count as income?", the answer is: it depends on frequency and intent. But more importantly — CRA may already know. Online platforms increasingly report transaction data. The cost of underreporting discovered by CRA is far higher than the tax on properly reported income.

    16.3 The 20 Most Common Errors — Know Every One¶

    These are the errors that generate the most CRA letters, reassessments, and avoidable client stress. Many are made by clients filing their own returns. Many are also made by inexperienced preparers.

    Category 1 — Missing or Wrong Income¶

    Error 1 — Missing T-slips

    The situation: Client says "that's everything" and hands you a T4 and one T5. CRA has three T5s and a T3 on file.

    The cost: Automatic reassessment. Additional tax plus daily compound interest from the original filing deadline. If it looks intentional — penalties.

    Prevention: Always access CRA My Account and verify all slips on file before filing. Never file based solely on what the client provides. This takes 5 minutes and prevents the most common reassessment in Canadian tax.

    Error 2 — Unreported Cash Income

    The situation: A self-employed tradesperson, server, or freelancer earns cash and doesn't include it. Or a landlord receives rent in cash.

    The cost: If discovered — 50% gross negligence penalty on top of the tax and interest. If a pattern is established — potential criminal prosecution.

    Prevention: Ask every self-employed client directly: "Did you receive any cash payments for work this year that weren't deposited to your business account?" Document their response. If they say yes, include it. If they say no and CRA later finds otherwise, it is their responsibility — not yours, as long as you asked and documented.

    Error 3 — Forgetting Foreign Income

    The situation: A Canadian resident with a bank account in India, China, the Philippines, or elsewhere earns interest on foreign savings. They either forget about it or assume it's not taxable in Canada.

    The cost: CRA receives foreign account information through FATCA and CRS agreements with over 100 countries. The T1135 (Foreign Income Verification Statement) is required for foreign property over $100,000 CAD. Missing foreign income generates reassessments plus a possible T1135 penalty of $25/day up to $2,500 for late filing.

    Prevention: Ask every client: "Do you have any bank accounts, investments, or property outside of Canada?" If yes — assess whether T1135 is required and ensure all foreign income is reported in Canadian dollars at the Bank of Canada average annual exchange rate.

    Error 4 — Wrong Year for RRSP Deduction

    The situation: Client contributes to RRSP in February 2026 and wants it claimed on the 2025 return. The receipt is entered but the year of deduction is incorrectly marked or the deduction is claimed twice in subsequent years.

    The cost: Over-deduction of RRSP on one year, possible under-deduction the next. CRA cross-references contribution room and deductions annually.

    Prevention: Always confirm with the client which year the contribution applies to. Mark the file clearly. Check Schedule 7 carefully — the form distinguishes between contributions made during the year and those made in the first 60 days of the following year.

    Error 5 — Capital Gains Not Reported (Principal Residence Sale)

    The situation: Client sells their home, assumes it's fully exempt, and doesn't mention it. The sale is not reported on Schedule 3 with Form T2091.

    The cost: Since 2016, ALL principal residence sales must be reported — even if fully exempt. Failure to report = potential penalties and possible denial of the exemption. CRA matches real estate transactions through provincial land registries.

    Prevention: Ask every client, every year: "Did you sell any real estate this year?" Not just "did you sell your home" — ask about all real estate. Include rental properties, cottages, and inherited properties. A missed Schedule 3 is one of the most common errors with the largest potential consequences.

    Category 2 — Overclaimed Deductions and Credits¶

    Error 6 — Home Office Claim Without T2200 (Employee)

    The situation: An employee claims home office expenses without a signed T2200 from their employer.

    The cost: CRA denies the entire employment expense claim. The T2200 is mandatory — no exceptions.

    Prevention: Before claiming any employment expenses, confirm the T2200 exists. Ask: "Do you have a signed Declaration of Conditions of Employment from your employer?" If not — they cannot claim. Do not claim expenses and hope CRA won't ask.

    Error 7 — 100% Vehicle Business Use

    The situation: A client claims 100% business use of a personal vehicle with no logbook.

    The cost: CRA audit invariably reduces the claim. Personal use (commuting, evenings, weekends, vacations) is almost always present. Without a logbook, CRA sets the business-use percentage wherever they choose — often 50% or lower.

    Prevention: Require a logbook before claiming vehicle expenses. Explain the logbook requirement in writing during onboarding. If a client claims 100% business use, document their explanation and advise them in writing of the audit risk. A defensible 80% is better than an indefensible 100%.

    Error 8 — Meals and Entertainment at 100%

    The situation: A self-employed client or commission employee claims meals at full cost rather than 50%.

    The cost: CRA denies 50% of the claim. Depending on amounts, this could also trigger a broader review.

    Prevention: The 50% rule is absolute and has no exceptions for client entertaining. Your software should apply it automatically, but always verify. A $4,000 meals expense becomes $2,000 — always.

    Error 9 — Childcare Claimed by the Wrong Spouse

    The situation: The higher-income spouse claims childcare because they "paid for it."

    The cost: CRA denies the claim or reassesses it to the lower-income spouse. If the lower-income spouse has insufficient income to support the full claim, the deductible amount may be reduced.

    Prevention: Always determine which spouse earns less before assigning childcare. Explain the rule clearly. If the client insists the higher earner should claim, show them the CRA rule in writing and document the conversation.

    Error 10 — RRSP Over-Contribution Missed

    The situation: A client contributed based on a prior year NOA without accounting for a new employer's pension adjustment, or they simply contributed without checking their limit. They now have an over-contribution beyond the $2,000 buffer.

    The cost: 1%/month penalty on the excess. If not caught for 18 months — $270 penalty on a $1,500 over-contribution. Ongoing until the excess is withdrawn.

    Prevention: Always verify the RRSP deduction limit from CRA My Account — not just the prior year NOA — before recording any RRSP contribution. The real-time My Account balance reflects contributions already made during the year.

    Error 11 — Moving Expenses Claimed Without Meeting the 40 km Test

    The situation: Client moved but the distance test wasn't met. Or they moved for personal reasons (relationship, lifestyle) rather than for work or school.

    The cost: Full denial of moving expenses. If large amounts were claimed — interest on the tax owing from the original filing date.

    Prevention: Before claiming any moving expenses, confirm: (1) Was the move for work or school? (2) Does the move meet the 40 km test (distance to NEW workplace − distance to NEW workplace from old home ≥ 40 km)? Document the calculation in the file.

    Error 12 — Disability Tax Credit Claimed Without CRA Approval

    The situation: A preparer claims the DTC on a return without the client having a CRA-approved T2201 on file.

    The cost: CRA denies the credit and reassesses. Interest from the filing date. If it appears deliberate — preparer penalties under the Income Tax Act.

    Prevention: Before claiming the DTC on any return, confirm that CRA has approved Form T2201 for the relevant years. Check CRA My Account — the approved DTC will be visible. Never claim based on the client saying "I applied for it" without confirmation of approval.

    Category 3 — Ontario-Specific and Administrative Errors¶

    Error 13 — Wrong Province on the Return

    The situation: A client who moved to Ontario in November is filed as a resident of their prior province. Or vice versa.

    The cost: Wrong provincial tax rates, wrong credits, wrong benefit calculations. The entire provincial portion of the return is incorrect.

    Prevention: Ask every client: "What province were you living in on December 31?" This is the single question that determines provincial tax treatment. For the Ontario Trillium Benefit — also ask when they moved to Ontario.

    Error 14 — ON-BEN Rent Amount Covers Non-Ontario Months

    The situation: A client who moved to Ontario in July enters their full year's rent (12 months) on ON-BEN.

    The cost: Overstated OEPTC claim. CRA may disallow the non-Ontario portion and reassess.

    Prevention: ON-BEN rent should reflect only rent paid for an Ontario principal residence. If the client moved mid-year, enter only the Ontario months.

    Error 15 — Missing T-Slip Reconciliation Before Filing

    The situation: Preparing a return based only on what the client provides, without cross-referencing CRA My Account.

    The cost: This is the root cause of Error 1 (missing slips). It's listed separately because it's a process failure, not just a data entry error. CRA reassessments for missing T-slips are entirely avoidable.

    Prevention: Build the CRA My Account verification step into your workflow for every return, every year. Access it before you start the return, not after. This is non-negotiable professional practice.

    Error 16 — Support Payments — Child Support Deducted

    The situation: A client deducts child support payments under a post-April-1997 court order, believing all support is deductible.

    The cost: CRA denies the child support deduction. If the claim is large, interest from the filing date.

    Prevention: When a client mentions support payments, immediately ask: (1) Is there a written agreement or court order? (2) What portion is spousal vs. child support? (3) When was the agreement made (pre or post-April 1997)? Only deduct spousal support. Never deduct child support.

    Error 17 — Deducting the Wrong Portion of a Loan as Investment Interest

    The situation: A client borrowed money partially for investment purposes and partially for personal use (e.g., refinanced a mortgage and used some proceeds for investments, some for a renovation). They deduct all the interest.

    The cost: CRA requires the interest deduction to be proportional to the investment use of the borrowed funds. Deducting personal-use interest generates a reassessment.

    Prevention: When a client mentions investment loan interest, trace the exact proceeds. If $200,000 was borrowed, $120,000 went to investments, and $80,000 went to home renovation: only 60% of the interest is deductible. Document the allocation precisely.

    Error 18 — Not Filing When Required (Self-Employed)

    The situation: A self-employed client with a balance owing files late, thinking they have until June 15 (self-employed filing deadline).

    The cost: The balance owing was due April 30. Interest runs from May 1. The late-filing penalty doesn't apply until after June 15 (for the filing itself) — but the interest on the unpaid balance starts April 30 regardless.

    Prevention: Communicate this every year to every self-employed client: "You have until June 15 to file, but any money you owe must be paid by April 30. If you don't know the exact amount, pay your best estimate by April 30 and we'll true it up when we file."

    Error 19 — Charitable Donation Without an Eligible Receipts

    The situation: Client donates to a GoFundMe, a foreign charity, or a non-registered organization and claims the donation.

    The cost: CRA denies the claim. Eligible donations must be to a CRA-registered charity. The receipt must contain the charity's registration number.

    Prevention: Verify every donation receipt has a CRA charity registration number (15-digit number in format XXXXXXXXX RR XXXX). GoFundMe, personal fundraisers, foreign charities, and non-registered organizations do not qualify. If a client can't produce a proper receipt — the donation cannot be claimed.

    Error 20 — Gross Negligence vs. Simple Error — Knowing the Difference

    The situation: A client has been significantly under-reporting self-employment income for years. Or a preparer has repeatedly claimed expenses they know aren't eligible.

    The cost: CRA distinguishes between honest mistakes (which attract interest and possibly standard penalties) and gross negligence or tax evasion (which attract penalties of 50% of the understated tax — and potentially criminal prosecution).

    The critical line: A preparer who knowingly files a false return faces personal penalties under Section 163.2 of the Income Tax Act — fines up to $100,000 or imprisonment for participating in tax evasion. This is not theoretical. It happens.

    Prevention: Never file a return you know to be false. If a client asks you to omit income or fabricate expenses — decline the engagement in writing. Keep a record of the refusal. Your professional reputation and legal exposure are not worth any client fee.

    16.4 When CRA Writes to Your Client — Responding to Review Letters¶

    This section teaches you exactly what to do when a client calls saying "I got a letter from CRA."

    Step 1 — Read the Letter Carefully¶

    CRA review letters are specific. They ask for documentation supporting a specific claim. Before doing anything else:

    • Identify exactly what CRA is asking for
    • Note the deadline for response (usually 30 days — do not miss it)
    • Note the CRA contact name and phone number
    • Note the reference number on the letter

    Missing the response deadline has serious consequences

    If your client does not respond to a CRA review letter by the deadline, CRA will disallow the claim and issue a reassessment. This is automatic. Once the deadline passes, the burden of proof shifts entirely to the taxpayer to contest the reassessment. Always respond before the deadline — even if just to request an extension.

    Step 2 — Gather Documentation¶

    Assemble every document supporting the claimed amount:

    Claim Under Review Documentation to Gather
    Medical expenses Receipts, EOB statements from insurance, prescription records
    Charitable donations Official tax receipts with registration numbers
    Moving expenses All receipts — movers, hotel, real estate agent invoices, mortgage penalty statement
    Childcare expenses Receipts, cancelled cheques, caregiver's SIN
    Home office T2200, utility bills, lease or mortgage statement, floor plan or measurement
    Business expenses Receipts, bank statements, invoices, contracts
    Vehicle expenses Logbook, all fuel and repair receipts, insurance certificate
    RRSP contributions RRSP contribution receipts from financial institution

    Step 3 — Write the Cover Letter¶

    A CRA response is not just a pile of receipts. It is an organized, professional submission. Structure it as follows:

    CRA RESPONSE LETTER FORMAT

[Date]
[CRA contact name]
[CRA address from review letter]

Re: Response to Review — [Client Name] — SIN: XXX-XXX-XXX
    Tax Year: [Year] — Reference: [CRA reference number]

Dear [CRA contact],

I am responding on behalf of [Client Name] regarding your review of
the [specific claim] claimed on the [year] T1 return.

SUMMARY OF CLAIM:
[The client claimed $X,XXX on Line XXXXX representing [brief description].]

SUPPORTING DOCUMENTATION:
Attached please find:
Tab 1 — [Description of document]
Tab 2 — [Description of document]
...

EXPLANATION:
[Clear, factual explanation of why the claim is valid and how it meets
the statutory requirements.]

Please contact me directly if you require any additional information.

Sincerely,
[Your name]
[Your contact information]
[Your EFILE number or representative identifier]

    A professional, organized response resolves most reviews without escalation

    CRA auditors review hundreds of files. A clear, tabbed, well-organized response that directly addresses the question — with no extraneous information — gets resolved quickly and favourably. A disorganized pile of receipts with no explanation takes weeks and may lead to follow-up requests.

    Every response you write should be something you'd be comfortable submitting to a judge. Be factual. Be organized. Be professional.

    Step 4 — Submit and Confirm¶

    • Submit by the deadline — mail with tracking, or via CRA My Account if available
    • Retain a complete copy of everything submitted
    • Note the submission date in the client file
    • Follow up if no response within 8 weeks

    Step 5 — If CRA Disagrees (Objections)¶

    If CRA reviews the documentation and still disallows the claim, the client has the right to file a Notice of Objection within 90 days of the reassessment date. This is a formal dispute process.

    For small amounts — assess whether the cost of pursuing the objection exceeds the tax at stake. For large amounts — recommend a CPA or tax lawyer who handles CRA disputes.

    16.5 Record-Keeping Standards — What Every Client Needs to Know¶

    CRA requires records to be kept for 6 years from the end of the tax year to which they relate. A 2024 return must be supported by records kept until at least December 31, 2030.

    Minimum Record-Keeping Requirements by Income Type¶

    Income Type Records Required Minimum Retention
    Employment T4 slips, T2200 (if employment expenses claimed) 6 years
    Investment T5, T3, T5008, purchase records for ACB 6 years after disposition
    Self-employment All revenue records, all expense receipts, bank statements, mileage logbook 6 years
    Rental Lease agreements, rental receipts, all expense invoices, mortgage statements 6 years
    Capital property Original purchase price documentation 6 years after the year of sale
    Charitable donations CRA-compliant donation receipts 6 years
    Home purchase/sale Purchase and sale agreements, legal fees, renovations (for ACB) 6 years after sale

    Capital property records — keep forever until sold + 6 years

    A client who bought their rental property in 2005 and sells in 2031 must have the 2005 purchase records to establish the ACB — even though that was 26 years ago. The clock doesn't start until the year of disposition. Many clients throw away old property records thinking they no longer matter. Advise every property owner client: keep real estate purchase records permanently until the property is sold, plus 6 additional years.

    Digital Records¶

    CRA accepts digital records (scanned receipts, PDF statements, electronic records). Best practice for your clients:

    1. Scan receipts immediately — thermal paper fades within 2–3 years; the receipt you have in a shoebox today may be unreadable in 6 years
    2. Organize by year and category — one folder per tax year, subfolders by income type and expense category
    3. Back up to cloud storage — one hard drive failure should not destroy 6 years of records
    4. Keep original documents for anything over $1,000 — even if you have a scan

    16.6 Your Liability as a Paid Preparer — Know Your Exposure¶

    As a paid tax preparer, you have legal obligations and personal exposure that amateur filers do not.

    What You Are Responsible For¶

    Your Responsibility Description
    Accuracy You are responsible for the accuracy of information you enter based on documents provided
    Reasonable inquiry You must ask reasonable questions to verify the information given to you
    Known false statements You cannot knowingly file a return containing false information
    Due diligence You must apply the technical knowledge expected of a competent preparer

    What You Are NOT Responsible For¶

    Not Your Responsibility Description
    Undisclosed client fraud If a client lies to you and you file in good faith — you are not liable
    Subsequent legal changes If the law changes after you file — you filed correctly at the time
    CRA interpretation CRA sometimes disagrees with reasonable tax positions — this is not preparer error

    Section 163.2 — Third-Party Civil Penalties¶

    CRA has the power to assess civil penalties against tax preparers who: - Knowingly make false statements on a client's return, OR - Make statements in gross negligence (wilfully blind to obvious falsehood)

    Penalty amounts: - For false statements: the greater of $1,000 or the false statement penalty on the client - For gross negligence: the greater of $1,000 or 50% of the understatement of tax

    These penalties are applied to the preparer personally — not the client.

    The preparer penalty is real and it has been applied

    CRA has assessed third-party penalties against preparers who repeatedly filed returns with fabricated expenses, inflated charitable donations, or unsupported business losses. The fact that the client asked for it is not a defence. The fact that it's a common practice in certain communities is not a defence. The Income Tax Act holds the preparer to a professional standard.

    Your protection is a paper trail: document every unusual claim, keep copies of the supporting documents provided by the client, and — if a claim seems implausible — ask additional questions and document the answers. If a client insists on a position you believe is wrong, decline to include it and document that you advised against it.

    Errors and Omissions (E&O) Insurance¶

    As noted in Week 1, E&O insurance is essential before you file your first return for a paying client. It protects you if a client suffers a financial loss due to an error you made in good faith.

    • Typical E&O insurance for a small tax practice: $500–$1,500/year
    • Covers errors made in preparing returns — not intentional fraud
    • Does not cover CRA third-party penalties (those require intentional or negligent conduct)

    16.7 Intake and Engagement Documentation — Your First Line of Defence¶

    The best protection against client complaints and professional liability is clear documentation from the start. Before you file a single return for a paying client:

    Engagement Letter — Use One, Always¶

    An engagement letter is a written agreement between you and your client that sets out:

    • Scope of services: "I will prepare your 2025 T1 personal income tax return based on documents you provide."
    • Client responsibilities: "You are responsible for providing complete and accurate information."
    • Fee: Your preparation fee and payment terms.
    • Limitations: "My work product is based on information provided by you. I am not responsible for errors resulting from incomplete or incorrect information."
    • No audit representation: If you don't offer audit representation, say so clearly.

    The clause that protects you most

    Include this language in every engagement letter:

    "The accuracy of this return depends entirely on the completeness and accuracy of the information you provide. By signing below, you confirm that you have disclosed all income received, all applicable deductions and credits, and that all information provided is true and complete. [Your name] is not responsible for reassessments, interest, or penalties arising from information not disclosed to us."

    Have the client sign and date it. Keep a copy.

    The Preparer's Copy — Keep Everything¶

    For every return you file, retain:

    • A complete copy of the filed return (PDF)
    • Copies of all T-slips and supporting documents provided
    • The signed engagement letter
    • Notes from the intake conversation (especially any unusual claims and the client's explanation)
    • CRA My Account screenshot confirming slips verified
    • Confirmation of EFILE transmission

    Retain for 6 years minimum. You never know when a client will claim they gave you different information than they actually did.

    16.8 The Voluntary Disclosure Program (VDP) — When a Client Comes to You With a Problem¶

    Occasionally, a new client will come to you because something has gone wrong in their tax history:

    • They haven't filed for several years
    • They omitted significant income
    • They claimed deductions they weren't entitled to

    If CRA has not yet contacted them about the specific issue, they may qualify for the Voluntary Disclosure Program — which allows taxpayers to correct their tax affairs with reduced penalties.

    VDP Key Rules¶

    Requirement Detail
    Voluntary CRA cannot already be pursuing the issue. If they've received a T-slip verification or audit notice already — VDP is closed
    Complete Must disclose everything, for all years affected. Partial disclosure disqualifies the application
    Payment Must pay all taxes owing (and applicable interest) at the time of application
    Penalty relief Gross negligence penalties typically waived. Criminal prosecution typically waived. Interest may be partially waived
    How to apply Form RC199 (Taxpayer Agreement) — submit to CRA Voluntary Disclosures Program

    VDP is a specialized service — refer when in doubt

    In your first tax season, if a new client walks in with 5 years of unfiled returns and significant unreported income, assess your comfort level carefully. VDP applications require calculating taxes owing across multiple years, negotiating penalty relief, and coordinating with CRA. Refer these clients to a CPA or tax lawyer experienced in VDP if the situation is complex. You can still prepare the actual returns once the VDP application is under way — that is within your scope.

    16.9 Ontario-Specific Audit Triggers¶

    CRA's Ontario Tax Centre reviews Ontario-specific items with the same rigor as federal items. These are the areas that most commonly surface in Ontario audit-related correspondence:

    Ontario Issue Common Trigger
    OEPTC claimed without landlord information ON-BEN filed with rent but no landlord name/address
    Full year rent claimed after mid-year arrival Ontario arrival date visible from T4 address vs. full-year rent claim
    LIFT Credit claimed on investment-only income Low income + no employment income source
    Ontario surtax not remitted Software error or manual return with incorrect surtax
    Senior Homeowners' Grant with incorrect ownership information Renter claims grant instead of homeowner
    Trillium claim for vacation property or secondary residence Only principal Ontario residence qualifies
    Ontario disability amount without federal DTC approval Provincial DTC cannot be claimed without federal approval

    16.10 The Audit-Ready Return — A Checklist¶

    Build this into your closing process for every return.

    Before Filing — 10 Verification Steps¶

    • [ ] CRA My Account T-slip verification: All slips filed by third parties are on the return. None were omitted
    • [ ] Province of residence confirmed: Client confirmed December 31 address — province is correct
    • [ ] RRSP room verified: Deduction claimed does not exceed CRA My Account deduction limit
    • [ ] Business vehicle claim: Logbook exists and supports the percentage claimed. Not 100% unless documented
    • [ ] Home office: T2200 on file if employee. Self-employed: principal place of business confirmed
    • [ ] Principal residence sale: If property sold, Schedule 3 and T2091 completed
    • [ ] Support payments: Only spousal support claimed. Child support not included
    • [ ] Donation receipts: CRA registration number confirmed on every receipt
    • [ ] Moving expenses: 40 km test documented. New-location income supports the claim
    • [ ] DTC: CRA approval letter on file before claiming on any return

    Documentation Filed in the Client File¶

    • [ ] Signed engagement letter
    • [ ] Copy of filed return (PDF)
    • [ ] All T-slips and supporting documents
    • [ ] CRA My Account slip verification screenshot
    • [ ] Notes on any unusual claims and client explanations
    • [ ] EFILE confirmation number

    16.11 Case Studies — Audits and Errors in the Real World¶

    Case A — The Missing T3 That Triggered a Review¶

    Client: James. You filed his 2025 return in February. He had a T4, two T5s, and an RRSP receipt — all confirmed through CRA My Account at the time of filing.

    In April, his mutual fund company issues a T3 dated March 28. James didn't tell you — he thought it was just paperwork.

    In July, James calls: "I got a letter from CRA asking about investment income."

    What happened: CRA received the T3 showing $2,400 in capital gains and $890 in eligible dividends. These are not on James's filed return.

    What you do: 1. Calm James down: "This is routine. The T3 arrived after your return was filed. We'll correct it." 2. File a T1-ADJ to add Line 12000 ($890 grossed-up dividends) and Line 12700 ($1,200 taxable capital gains = 50% of $2,400) to the 2025 return 3. Include a cover letter explaining the late-arriving T3 4. CRA reassesses James with the additional income. Additional tax: approximately $820 plus modest interest

    The lesson: Always tell clients: "If you receive ANY new tax documents after we file your return — especially T3s which arrive late — call me immediately."

    For future years: CRA My Account shows all T-slips CRA has received — but T3s from foreign or delayed filers may arrive late. Confirm with the client every year: "Do you own any mutual funds, ETFs, REITs, or trust investments? If so, wait for your T3 before we file."

    Case B — The Home Office Review Letter¶

    Client: Patricia, self-employed marketing consultant. She claimed $8,400 in business-use-of-home expenses on her T2125 (20% of home costs for a dedicated 200 sq ft office in a 1,000 sq ft home she owns).

    CRA sends a review letter requesting documentation for her home office claim.

    Your response package:

    Cover letter: Explains that Patricia is self-employed (T2125, Line 13500), that her home is her principal place of business, and that she uses a dedicated 200 sq ft room exclusively for business. Describes each expense category.

    Tab 1 — Floor plan: Simple sketch showing the 200 sq ft office and total 1,000 sq ft = 20% business use.

    Tab 2 — Mortgage statement: 2025 annual statement showing interest paid = $14,400 (20% = $2,880 claimed)

    Tab 3 — Property tax bill: 2025 City of London property tax bill = $4,800 (20% = $960 claimed)

    Tab 4 — Insurance certificate: $1,800 annual premium (20% = $360 claimed)

    Tab 5 — Utility statements: Monthly hydro/gas statements totalling $2,400 (20% = $480 claimed)

    Tab 6 — Internet bill: Annual total $840 (20% = $168 claimed)

    Tab 7 — T2125: Copy of the filed T2125 showing the home office calculation section

    Result: CRA accepts the claim without adjustment. The review closes within 6 weeks.

    What made this work: Organization. Completeness. A cover letter that anticipated CRA's questions and answered them before they were asked. Patricia never had to speak to CRA directly — you handled it entirely.

    Case C — The Self-Employed Client Who Admitted to Cash Income¶

    New client: Kevin comes to you in March. His prior preparer "retired." He is a landscaper who operates entirely in cash. He earns approximately $65,000/year but his prior returns show $28,000–$32,000.

    He asks: "Can you just do what my last guy did?"

    What you say:

    "Kevin, I understand what you're describing. Here's the situation: I'm legally required to file based on your actual income. If we file with $30,000 when you earned $65,000, that's tax evasion — and I face personal penalties under the Income Tax Act in addition to you. I won't do that."

    "What I can help you with is getting your tax affairs in order. You have two options: we file correctly going forward and you pay the tax on $65,000 with all your legitimate deductions (equipment, vehicle, fuel, insurance, sub-contractors, home office — all of it, which will significantly reduce the net tax). Or, if you want to come clean on prior years, there's a Voluntary Disclosure Program that can reduce your penalties for past under-reporting. I can walk you through both."

    Documentation: Write a brief note to the file: "March 12, 2026 — discussed income reporting requirements with Kevin [last name]. Explained legal obligations. Client will return to confirm engagement scope."

    The outcome: Some clients walk away. That is fine. The ones who stay and file correctly become long-term clients who refer others — because they know their preparer has integrity. The client who pressures you to commit tax fraud is not a client you want. They will eventually be audited, and they will blame you.

    Case D — The Successful Review Defence¶

    Client: Rachel. CRA sends a review letter requesting documentation for: 1. $14,200 in moving expenses (Line 21900) 2. $4,200 in charitable donations (Line 34900)

    For the moving expenses: Rachel moved from Mississauga to Ottawa for a new federal government job in April 2025.

    Distance test documented: Mississauga home to Ottawa office = 421 km. New Ottawa home to same office = 8 km. Difference: 413 km. ✅

    Documentation submitted: - Professional movers invoice: $8,400 - Real estate agent commission (Mississauga home): $3,800 (2.5% of sale price) - Hotel in Ottawa during possession wait (8 days): $1,200 - Meals during move: $340 - Vehicle registration change to Ontario: $120 (moved from Ontario to Ontario — but stayed for illustration) - Utility connection fees: $340

    Total: $14,200 ✅

    New-location income April–December: $67,000 ✅ (exceeds moving expenses)

    For the donations: Three donation receipts presented, each with CRA registration numbers: - Heart and Stroke Foundation of Canada: $1,800 (BN 106211493 RR 0001) - United Way East Ontario: $1,600 (BN 108209673 RR 0001) - Ottawa Food Bank: $800 (BN 122006489 RR 0001)

    Result: CRA accepts both claims in full. Review letter resolved.

    The receipts were organized, the registration numbers were verified, and the moving expense documentation was comprehensive. Rachel didn't worry — because you told her at intake: "Keep every receipt from your move. CRA sometimes asks for documentation on moving expenses and we want to be ready."

    16.12 Week 16 Summary¶

    What you learned this week

    • CRA reviews exist on a spectrum — from automated processing reviews to field audits. Most clients encounter review letters for specific claims, not full audits. Handle them professionally and calmly
    • CRA selects returns using algorithmic scoring — unusual deductions, cash businesses, industry outliers, and third-party information mismatches all raise the score. Accurate returns with documentation are both correct and low-profile
    • The 20 most common errors fall into three categories: missing income, overclaimed deductions, and administrative failures. CRA My Account verification before filing prevents the majority of them
    • Responding to review letters: Read carefully, meet the deadline, organize documentation by tab, write a clear cover letter. Professional responses resolve most reviews without escalation
    • Record-keeping: 6 years from the tax year end. Capital property records until disposition + 6 years. Digital records accepted — but scan immediately (thermal paper fades)
    • Your liability as a paid preparer: Section 163.2 penalties are real. Knowingly filing false returns can result in personal fines and imprisonment. Your protection is documentation, due diligence, and refusal to file what you know to be false
    • Engagement letters are your first line of defence. Use them for every client. Include the clause making the client responsible for the completeness of information they provide
    • Voluntary Disclosure Program: Available when CRA hasn't yet contacted the client about the issue. Reduces penalties for correcting past errors. Refer complex VDP cases to a specialist
    • The audit-ready return: Ten verification steps before filing + complete documentation in the client file = returns you can defend with confidence

    ✅ Week 16 Quiz¶

    When you're ready, ask Claude: "Quiz me on Week 16" for an interactive test.

    1. A client calls in a panic saying they received a letter from CRA. The letter asks them to provide documentation for their medical expenses claimed on the 2024 return. The deadline is in 25 days. Walk through the five steps you take.

    Step 1 — Read the letter carefully: Identify exactly what CRA is asking for, note the deadline (25 days), the CRA contact name, reference number, and what specific claim is under review. Step 2 — Gather documentation: Collect all medical expense receipts, any insurance EOB statements, prescription records, and any other supporting documents for the amounts claimed. Verify the total matches what was filed on the return. Step 3 — Write the cover letter: Prepare a professional, organized letter with a subject line citing the client's name, SIN, tax year, and CRA reference number. Describe the claim clearly. Tab the documentation. Step 4 — Submit before the deadline: Mail with tracking or submit through CRA My Account. Retain a complete copy of everything sent. Note the submission date in the client file. Step 5 — Follow up if no response within 8 weeks. If CRA disallows the claim after reviewing, assess whether to file a Notice of Objection within 90 days of the reassessment.

    2. Your new client, a restaurant owner, mentions that about 40% of his revenue is cash. He says his prior preparer 'always found a way to keep the numbers reasonable.' What do you do and what do you document?

    You explain clearly that you are required by law to report actual income — including cash. Underreporting is tax evasion, which carries penalties of 50% of understated tax for the client, and potential Section 163.2 civil penalties for you as the preparer if you knowingly participate. You offer two legitimate paths: (1) file correctly going forward, claiming all legitimate business deductions (food costs, labour, rent, utilities, insurance, vehicle, etc.) which will significantly reduce the net tax; or (2) if he wants to correct prior years, the Voluntary Disclosure Program may be available. Document in writing: a brief file note dated today stating that you discussed income reporting requirements with the client, explained the legal obligations, and that the client has been advised that you cannot prepare returns that do not reflect actual income. If he engages you, the engagement letter should explicitly state that the client warrants all income is disclosed.

    3. A client claims $9,800 in home office expenses as a salaried employee. When you ask for the T2200, she says 'my employer didn't give me one but I definitely worked from home.' Can you claim the expenses?

    No. The T2200 is a mandatory prerequisite for any employment expense claim under the formal method. Without it, the claim is not eligible regardless of whether the client actually worked from home. The only alternative is the flat-rate method ($2/day, maximum $500/year), which does not require a T2200. Explain this to the client: the formal detailed method requires a T2200. If she requests one from her employer and they provide it, you can file an amended return. If the employer refuses to provide a T2200, she is limited to the flat-rate method. Document this conversation in the file.

    4. You file a return in February. In June, the client calls saying CRA sent a Notice of Reassessment adding $3,200 to their income — a T5 they hadn't given you. The client is angry and blames you. What is your professional response and what protects you?

    Your professional response: "I understand this is frustrating. When we prepared your return, we verified all T-slips through CRA My Account. At that time, this T5 was not showing. This sometimes happens when issuers file slips late. We'll need to file a T1-ADJ to confirm the correct amounts, and any additional tax will be due with interest from April 30." What protects you: (1) Your file should contain a CRA My Account screenshot dated when you prepared the return, showing the T-slips that were on file. This proves you performed due diligence. (2) Your engagement letter, signed by the client, confirms that they are responsible for providing complete information and that you are not responsible for reassessments arising from information not available at filing. With both of these, you can demonstrate that you acted professionally and that the T5 not being on CRA My Account at time of filing was beyond your control.

    5. Rachel paid $25,000 in support payments this year: $14,000 spousal support and $11,000 child support, under a 2021 court order. She asks if all $25,000 is deductible. What is the correct answer and what do you enter on the return?

    Only the spousal support of $14,000 is deductible on Line 22000. Child support paid under post-April 1997 agreements is never deductible — it flows tax-neutral. The $11,000 in child support does not appear anywhere on Rachel's return. You must also: (1) confirm there is a written court order or agreement; (2) confirm Rachel and her ex-spouse were living apart due to relationship breakdown throughout the year; (3) record the recipient's SIN on the return; (4) keep a copy of the court order in the file. The recipient must report the $14,000 spousal support as income on Line 12800 of their own return.

    6. A client wants to include a $2,000 donation to a GoFundMe campaign for a neighbour's medical expenses, and a $1,200 donation to a foreign university. Can either be claimed? What is required for a charitable donation to qualify?

    Neither qualifies. The GoFundMe campaign is a personal fundraiser — it is not a CRA-registered charity and does not issue an official tax receipt. The foreign university, while a legitimate educational institution, is not a CRA-registered organization (foreign universities are not eligible donees under the Income Tax Act unless specifically designated by the federal government, and most are not). To qualify, a donation must be made to a registered Canadian charity or other qualified donee as defined by CRA. The receipt must contain the organization's CRA Business Number in the format XXXXXXXXX RR XXXX. Without a compliant receipt from a qualified organization, the donation cannot be claimed regardless of its charitable nature.

    7. You discover that on a client's prior return (prepared by another preparer), the Ontario Basic Personal Amount used was $16,129 (the federal amount) instead of $11,865 (the Ontario amount). The error created a $215 overstatement in Ontario credits. What do you do and what is the process?

    File a T1-ADJ (T1 Adjustment Request) to correct the error. The T1-ADJ corrects the ON428 to reflect the correct Ontario BPA of $11,865. The client will receive a Revised NOA showing additional Ontario tax of $215, with interest from the original filing date. Explain this to the client proactively: "I found an error in your prior year return. The Ontario Basic Personal Amount was entered incorrectly. I'll correct it now — it will result in a small additional amount owing with interest, but it's better to correct it voluntarily than have CRA find it later and potentially flag your account for future scrutiny." The fact that it was a prior preparer's error does not prevent CRA from assessing interest — the tax was underpaid regardless of whose fault it was.
